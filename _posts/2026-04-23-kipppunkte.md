## WHAT'S IN IT FOR YOU: A LOOK BACK AT THE 1990S – THEIR MISTAKES, FAILURES, AND MISSED OPPORTUNITIES.

Disappointment in politics, extremism, fake news, not to forget extreme wealth, turbo capitalism, and climate disaster.

Phew!

The overall state of the world is indeed worrying.

But how did we get into this situation?

Georg Diez looks back at the 1990s to find answers.

In this decade, important foundations were laid and directions chosen. We can see these choices more clearly today than we could back then when we were still in the middle of it.

Diez points out several turning points where the fate of the world was decided.

What if we had chosen a different path back then?

What can we learn today from those moments and decisions?

And how can we use these lessons to face the social problems becoming more urgent today?

Let’s take a closer look!

## THE DECADE OF DECISIONS

To understand the developments of the 90s, it is helpful to look at the situation at the start of this important decade.

Since the 1950s, wealth had spread in the global West.

There was an agreement between the state, democracy, and the market.

This changed in the 70s with the oil crisis.

Suddenly, the economy and the state were not a team but opponents.

This also caused a crisis for democracy.

One of the leading thinkers of this time was Milton Friedman. He saw the free market as the best way to organize the economy and society – a perfect, all-knowing system.

Supply and demand would make decisions much better than voters or experts ever could.

According to Friedman, a free market benefits everyone.

Many believed in these simple promises of neoliberalism, and with Margaret Thatcher and Ronald Reagan in power, it soon became the state policy.

At the same time, by the late 80s, people already knew that many things were deeply wrong: Acid rain, Chernobyl, and the ozone hole had heavily shaken the hope for the future.

But in the 90s, this did not lead to new thinking. Instead, social democrats like Tony Blair, Gerhard Schröder, and Bill Clinton, who came to power then, accepted neoliberal ideas and used their political tools: deregulation, liberalization, privatization, tax cuts, and budget discipline.

So many governments gave up their power step by step in the 90s – and laid the foundation for many crises and collapses we see today.

How can they get back the power they gave up?

And which decisions and events exactly started the current breakdown?

Let's look at this next.

## THE MISSED OPPORTUNITIES OF REUNIFICATION

One of the biggest turning points was surely the fall of the Berlin Wall and the reunification of Germany that followed.

What would have happened if the East German government had reacted to protests with military force, like in Tiananmen Square that same year?

We clearly see today that Helmut Kohl owed his re-election to German unity – without it, his conservative era in Germany might have ended earlier.

If instead Oskar Lafontaine had been elected chancellor in 1990, environmental protection and ecological market economy might have come onto the public agenda much earlier.

But Kohl's image as a strong unity chancellor gave him more time: the East voted for the CDU.

The CDU's 1990 election program was a celebration of "more of the same."

Economy and prosperity were the motto.

In West Germany, people believed social market economy was the best system and imposed it too quickly on the East.

Looking back, German reunification was a chance to not only rebuild the state order but also to modernize the constitution.

But this chance was missed, and the East was quickly aligned to the West German system.

State companies were privatized, environmental protection was ignored – no one had the energy for that in such busy times.

“Flourishing landscapes?”

Yes.

Biodiversity?

Doesn't matter.

This reunification happened too fast and caused many disappointments and traumas for East German families.

Careers ended, social support broke down, many lost their property.

This caused many East Germans to feel resigned and politically passive.

At the same time, public talk began to see the East as a problem and the West as the norm.

In this setting, racist attitudes found it easy to grow, which helps explain why racism is still stronger in the East today.

There was an understanding in the 90s that racism was a real social problem, but little was done to stop it.

The police were often ineffective or even made things worse, as they also held racist views.

And politics?

Politicians made offers to right-wing groups instead of stopping them.

Instead of punishing neo-Nazis and arsonists, immigration laws were tightened.

All this shaped German identity deeply and also affected how Europe sees itself – a point we will look at next.

## THE NEW WORLD ORDER AFTER THE COLD WAR

The end of the Cold War is closely connected to reunification.

With the fall of the Eastern bloc, it seemed that right and left were no longer useful political categories.

Everything was somehow in the middle.

The big conflicts seemed solved, and until September 11, 2001, some believed we were moving toward world peace.

At the same time, Europe and even political action lost some importance.

Before, everything was based on the Cold War.

This balance – even if a fearful one – was now gone.

The economy became stronger than politics.

Before, economic foreign policy was part of geopolitical strategy, now the new idea was: economy first.

The thought was that if the former Eastern bloc countries' economies worked well, democracy would follow.

Political freedom was seen as a result of economic freedom; capitalism as a prerequisite for democracy.

According to this idea, all international conflicts were basically trade conflicts.

The World Trade Organization (WTO) and the International Monetary Fund (IMF) started work in the 90s, institutionalizing this and reducing politics’ role further.

We should also remember: after the Cold War, there was no official peace.

No conference or symbolic end where a new international order was created.

The institutions made for the Cold War, like NATO, just continued unchanged.

Could people have guessed how deeply NATO’s eastward expansion hurt Russia?

Or EU enlargement?

How peace is made decides the future.

The best example is the Treaty of Versailles, seen by German nationalists as a "shameful peace" and used to justify World War II.

The war in Ukraine today follows this pattern too.

What can we learn?

Anyone who wants to build a shared future must consider the losers’ perspective.

## A LACK OF IDEAS IN EUROPE

One problem that is becoming clear today with bad results is this: Europe lacks ideals – a shared idea.

If human rights are our idea, how do we justify the dead refugees in the Mediterranean?

What are our values?

One reason for this lack is that the EU was made less as a political union and more as an economic one.

This also explains the democratic deficit in its institutions: the executive is too strong, and the Commission is not elected democratically.

So most Europeans don’t see the EU as representing their interests, but as something forced on them from above.

Here too, after the Cold War in the 90s, we could have rethought and built differently.

For example, the rule of unanimity, which makes many decisions impossible, could have been dropped.

A European constitution would have been a good step forward.

But even crises like the debt crisis or Russia’s war in Ukraine have not caused big reforms in Europe’s basic rules.

This leads to growing selfishness and countries acting alone – and sadly, to a rise of right-wing and EU-critical parties.

The only thing really uniting Europe today seems to be fear of too much immigration.

Inside we are free thanks to the Schengen Agreement, but outside, the borders are closed.

And the causes for this once again go back to the 90s.

If Europe had taken steps towards more democracy and efficiency back then, it would be more successful and open today.

That would be an important step, because cross-border problems like climate change, migration, or security cannot be solved by single countries – we need a strong community for that.

It is not too late for a united Europe.

But first, we must agree on a shared idea.

## THE DARK AND BRIGHT SIDES OF GLOBALIZATION

Globalization sped up in the 1990s.

Many saw it as a force of nature that could not be stopped.

Those who criticized it and asked for more rules were labeled "anti-globalization."

Globalization was a turning point but was presented as completely normal.

One result was that policies about where businesses locate became more important than social policies.

The WHO and IMF worked to strengthen this trend wherever they could.

If a country wanted to win, it had to follow the neoliberal textbook.

Countries needing financial help became test cases for radical liberalism.

The question changed from what the economy can do for people and countries to what people and countries can do for the economy.

The idea that capital should fund a functioning state and social systems was lost.

What did these 90s events bring us?

The global crisis of 2008 and, on a larger scale, Brexit and Donald Trump’s presidency.

Globalization in its current form plays people’s interests against those of corporations.

Russia also lost control in the race for global profits.

Nobel winner Joseph Stiglitz once said stable democratic institutions must come first, then free markets.

If this order is not kept, oligarchs automatically take over.

This happened in Russia: economy and society became a playground for the elite, and democracy was crushed.

In the 90s, protests against uncontrolled globalization were criminalized more and more.

This reached a tragic peak when Carlo Giuliani was shot by police during protests against the G8 summit in Genoa in 2001.

Good ideas from the anti-globalization movement, like a tax on financial transactions, were never seriously considered by any country.

Today, mainly right-wing groups oppose global free trade and support national interests.

The center and left seem to lack the will or language to offer their own answers.

How about we start working on this now?

What stops us from rethinking globalization and making it stable economically and democratically?

Another change that started in the 90s and deeply changed society is the internet.

It had mixed beginnings and many possible directions: first made by the military, then taken over by nerds dreaming of a free, equal, and fair online world.

## THE INHUMANE INTERNET

But who won in the end?

The market.

Tech idealism had many interesting ideas.

For example, it thought governments acted like parents making too many rules for their children.

The internet was the treehouse the parents could not enter.

Cyberspace offered a freedom never known before: people could connect worldwide and talk without hierarchy.

Communities formed quickly and became important parts of many people’s lives.

A big problem in the relationship between state and technology is incompetence.

Governments are simply too slow.

Companies are fast – no wonder they shape the internet today.

The modern internet serves not the people, but mostly profit, except for rare examples like Wikipedia.

Many tech believers, like US investor Marc Andreessen, are market radicals today.

They believe progress will solve all problems if markets are left alone.

Ideas like sustainability and social justice are seen by this group, including Elon Musk and Peter Thiel, as useless obstacles to remove.

The most valuable companies started their success in the 90s: Apple, Microsoft, Google.

After the dotcom bubble burst in 2000, more focus was put on profit.

Creativity, community, connection, beauty, fun, and information quickly became goods to sell on the internet.

There was also a big trend to monopoly, which governments could hardly stop.

So, more and more power moved from states to capital.

If we want a more humane internet, the first step is to understand how it developed.

The same goes for new technologies today: first we must understand how they work – then create space for all to decide how to use them.

Technology offers endless possibilities to improve society and democracy.

We just need to use it well.

## DENIAL OF CLIMATE CHANGE

Sadly, the 1990s were a lost decade in the fight against climate change.

Even though the dangers of global warming were well known, no action was taken.

This was partly because lobbyists stirred doubt, distorted reality, and shifted responsibility to consumers.

The oil, gas, and coal producers created pressure, but that was not all – there was also a huge failure from countries, societies, and governments to work together and solve the problem.

Global inequality also played a role here.

Countries in the South rightly argued they did not want to give up prosperity to fix a problem caused by the Global North.

In the 90s, the world missed chances like leapfrogging for fast-growing countries.

This means skipping outdated technology in some countries.

For example, large parts of Africa never had a landline phone system – people went from no phone straight to mobiles.

The same could have happened in energy: from no electricity straight to solar, wind, or water power.

The technology was ready in the 90s, but no one wanted to make it happen.

Instead, the market was left in charge, as usual.

And this had terrible effects on the environment.

For example, global emission certificates turned into a type of indulgence trade: those who pollute too much buy certificates from poorer countries with lower emissions.

The result is still too many greenhouse gases, but everyone feels allowed and good about themselves.

Like the anti-globalization protests in the 90s, today’s climate protests are also being criminalized.

And lobbyists were so successful that a large part of the world still doesn’t believe in climate change.

So what can we do?

Adapt and keep fighting.

Giving up is not an option.

Now we have reached the end of our summary.

Let’s quickly review.

## CONCLUSION

The 1990s were not a harmless transition decade but a time of important decisions whose effects we feel today.

In politics, economy, technology, and climate protection, the same mistake was made again and again: responsibility was given to markets, corporations, or "facts" instead of being politically debated.

So the big crises of our time are not sudden breaks but logical results of this way of thinking.

The good news is: since people made these choices, they can be changed.

Diez’s look back is not nostalgic but strategic.

He shows that alternatives did exist – and still do.

The future doesn’t happen by itself; it is made.

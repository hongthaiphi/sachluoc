## What you will learn: How to negotiate with confidence and control.

Negotiations are part of our whole daily life. We negotiate with family at breakfast. We negotiate with sellers when buying a car. We negotiate with colleagues at work. We also negotiate with our partner about movies and holiday plans. Our negotiation skills help us solve problems, make choices, and build relationships.

Sadly, many talks follow the same pattern. Our good points are not heard. People's views become fixed. In the end, we feel like we accepted too little or lost.

In *Durchsetzungsstark verhandeln*, we will explore advanced communication skills. The goal is not to trick others or use power games. Instead, it is about using clear ideas, courage, and good communication to convince people.

## Blink 1 - Why Negotiating is Often So Hard

In autumn 1962, the world was close to a nuclear war. John F. Kennedy and Nikita Khrushchev negotiated through letters and messages. They had to find a solution where no one officially won. This peaceful end to the Cuban Missile Crisis is still a great example of successful negotiation.

Negotiating is seen as a very skilled part of communication. In almost no other situation do so many things come together at once. You must state your own needs clearly. You must listen carefully to the other person. You must manage conflicts and control your feelings. Also, you need to keep the relationship stable. To negotiate, you need to know about the topic and also understand people.

No wonder many talks do not bring the hoped-for result. This is because negotiations are not just about facts and logic. They are shaped by hopes, power differences, inner stress, and how you feel that day. Here, communication is more an art than a technical skill.

For a long time, people thought one of two things. Either you are tough and get what you want, or you give in and lose. There were only two types of people: wolves and sheep. Wolves fight for what they want. Sheep are used and pay a price.

Good negotiators understand that this way of thinking is too simple. They stay on good terms even in hard situations. They know the difference between real concerns and just excuses. They also know that a lasting result is better than a quick win. Being firm is about being clear, not loud.

A key step for this is to know yourself well. No one negotiates the same way all the time. When we are tired, hungry, or stressed, we act differently. We act differently than when we are calm and balanced. Stress makes our view narrow. Calmness opens it.

So, it is helpful to know your own habits. At what times of day and in what situations are you most open to new ideas? When do you make good choices? When are you more likely to be impatient or give in too quickly? If you know these times, you can plan important talks better.

Clear basic rules are also important. Be tough on the issue, but kind to the person. Interests matter more than fixed positions. Facts are more important than opinions. If you put pressure on yourself, teamwork and openness will suffer.

To negotiate firmly means to manage this inner pressure. It means to build relationships carefully and go into talks prepared. A successful negotiation starts not with an argument, but with the person.

## Blink 2 - What Makes a Good Negotiation Result

Hardly a day goes by without a negotiation. Who will take the children to school? What's for dinner? How long can we watch TV? Or it's about dates, duties, money, or what's most important. Even if we don't call it negotiating, much of our daily life includes talks like this.

But what does a good negotiation result actually mean? Many people would quickly say: to get as much as possible. A better price, a quick deal, a clear win. But in real life, it soon becomes clear that a result is only truly good if it lasts. This means it must be good not just for a short time, but also for future meetings.

The most important tool is preparation. What you miss here can hardly be fixed during the talk. Collecting information is only the first step. It is just as important to look at the situation around you. What is the history between the people involved? Were there problems before, or good teamwork? What hopes are unspoken?

Most business talks are not one-time events. Over eighty percent are part of a relationship's history. Everything that happened before affects the next talk, whether we know it or not. So, it is useful to make this history clear. For example, use a simple table that marks different parts of talks with colors. Good experiences are green. Bad ones are red. This quickly shows how the relationship has grown. If there are mostly green entries, there is trust. More red entries mean growing tension. Even good offers may fail if you ignore this history. So, you should first focus on the relationship before you talk about the details.

Also, a good result rarely comes only from facts. If only numbers mattered, computers could negotiate. But people don't decide only based on logic. They have goals, react to hopes, and have personal needs. Learn to see the difference between these levels.

Goals show the way. Needs provide the energy. Two people can want the same thing but act for very different reasons. For one person, success means a good result for the company. For others, it's about personal safety or being recognized. This explains why talks that seem to be about facts can become emotional.

Your point of view also matters. Sellers worry about losing a deal, losing bonuses, or losing rank at work. Buyers worry more about problems with delivery, quality, or breaks in the production line. The price is important, but often not the most important thing.

This is where 'leverage topics' appear. These are not always numbers or discounts. They are things that touch on important needs. Someone who really wants to buy a flat will care more about speed and knowing things are sure than about small price differences. So, the real chance for negotiation is often beyond the clear demands.

Therefore, it is often smarter not to show all your cards at once. Your main goal should usually be at the end of the negotiation, not the start. If you begin too early with your main demand, you fix positions. You quickly get stuck in a situation where you can only leave by losing face.

Good negotiators first agree on the basic rules. They understand interests and build a shared story. Only when this is strong will many single points become a result that makes sense for both sides.

## Blink 3 - How to Create a Smart Negotiation Strategy

A negotiation plan is like an architect's drawing. It shows how everything should fit together. But whether the building will stand strong is only decided on the building site. Numbers, presentations, and ideas form the base. Real strength comes only from how people work together at the negotiation table.

Anyone who only argues with facts negotiates blindly. Even good ideas are lost if they are aimed at the wrong person. Or if important interests are missed. Remember this: In negotiations, there are no companies, but people. Each person brings their role, their job, and their history.

So, first, get clear about who is involved. Who really decides? Whose opinion is important? Who takes the risk inside the company if things fail? Who might be competing with others and sees the outcome differently? These connections often decide more than numbers about whether a negotiation succeeds or fails.

Nothing is more frustrating than working for hours or days to find solutions. Only to find out later that the other person has no budget or power to decide. Good negotiators find out early how and by whom decisions are made in the company. They ask about internal processes, people involved, and formal rules. They do this not because they don't trust, but to understand the real situation.

Personal experiences also play a part in negotiations. What past projects, conflicts, or successes shape what people expect? Emotional history often affects negotiations more than facts.

Open questions can help to show this. What worked well in similar projects, and what didn't? What is needed in the company for a plan to succeed? What is personally important to the other person when working together?

The answers to these questions give you useful information about values, priorities, and possible problems. This is the base for a strong negotiation strategy. All you have to do now is connect the facts with the relationship parts to form a storyline. The better you link your messages, the higher the chance of being understood and agreed with. Because people don't follow lists of arguments. They follow clear connections.

## Blink 4 - The Five Steps to a Perfect Negotiation

Negotiations fail less because of bad arguments. They fail more because of a lack of structure. Talks jump from one small point to another. Objections appear before anyone knows what the main topic is. In the end, everyone talks past each other. In these cases, you quickly lose track and lose influence.

This is where the five-step script comes in. It is not a strict set of rules. It is a mental framework that helps you lead talks clearly, understandably, and effectively. Instead of just listing arguments, you create a story that the other person can follow.

First comes the *Situation*. Before you talk about solutions, everyone involved should have the same picture in mind. What is the exact topic? What is the starting point? Who is involved? How long has the current situation been like this, and why are we talking about it now? This step helps everyone understand and prevents misunderstandings.

From the situation, the *Problem* comes. Why do we need to do anything at all? What bad things will happen if everything stays the same? Where are there risks, losses, or costs? It is important not to judge the problem, but to describe it based on facts. It forms the bridge between now and change.

Next comes the *Goal*. It describes what you want to achieve. What exactly should be reached? How will you know when the goal is met? Who is responsible and by when? A clearly stated goal gives direction to the negotiation. It does this without saying what the solutions will be.

After that, the *Benefit* comes. The goal and problem mainly offer direction. But the benefit creates motivation. What does the desired solution bring to the company, the team, or the person? What good effects will there be? This step often decides if the plan gets support or faces resistance.

The final step is the *Call to Action* (Appell). Now, understanding becomes action. What are the best next steps? Who does what by when? The call to action makes sure talks don't end vaguely. It makes sure they truly start something new.

The five-step script works so well because it follows how we naturally think. We want to understand first, then put things in order, then judge, and only then decide. If you follow this order, you increase the chance of being heard.

Also, the script can help you understand objections. If they come up early, often a step has been skipped. If you are already arguing about solutions before the goal or problem is clear, you lack a shared base.

## Blink 5 - How to Spot and Avoid Negotiation Traps

Not every negotiation goes as planned. Even with good preparation, clear structure, and strong arguments, talks can go wrong. Emotions rise, accusations appear, or you go in circles. Then, what matters is not who is right, but how you can make the situation stable again.

One of the most common traps is feeling that things are not fair. Most people react emotionally if they feel treated unfairly. Then anger quickly rises. People's views become fixed, and it's hard to see solutions. The problem is: fairness is personal. What seems fair to one side can seem unfair to the other. Then the talk is no longer about facts.

Skilled negotiators see this moment as a warning. Instead of fighting about what's fair, they focus on what can be done. Feelings don't decide. The question is: what solution is real under the current conditions?

Closely related is the compromise trap. We make promises we didn't really want to make. We do this because we want to keep things friendly. Here, it is important to stay kind, but also clear and firm. If you quietly stick to your defined limits, you protect the result and your own trustworthiness.

When talks become emotional, changing the facts often helps. Turn unclear statements into neutral ones. For example, change an accusation into a factual point. This way, you address bad behavior without being unkind.

In unclear or confusing situations, the *Columbo* principle helps. It is named after the detective who found criminals by asking seemingly innocent questions. By acting as if he didn't quite understand, he made the other person explain their statements more clearly. In negotiations, this principle works similarly. Friendly clarifying questions like „I don't quite understand that yet“ or „Can you explain how you came to this view?“ weaken vague claims without creating pressure.

Sometimes the problem is less about the topic and more about the situation. If you notice in a negotiation that focus is dropping, arguments are repeating, and no progress is made, it's time for a break. Or even to postpone the talk. But only if both sides promise to find new information or create other solutions. Otherwise, you will just repeat the same things.

And a last tip: always be kind! In the heat of negotiation, it's easy to lose control. Then people become tougher. Kindness keeps your options open. It keeps the relationship good. It helps you stay calm, collected, and firm even in hard moments.

## Blink 6 - How to Stay Confident in Negotiations

Many negotiations fail not because of arguments. They fail because of inner pressure. People know how they should negotiate. But they still act differently. They give in too early, lose their calm, or stick to bad positions. The reason is rarely the talk itself. It is hidden dependencies.

Economic dependence especially makes your position weaker. If you badly need an order, you negotiate under stress. Every objection or delay feels like a threat and a risk. So, confidence does not start at the negotiation table. It starts with a real look at your financial situation.

Career pressure works in a similar way. Good deals are seen as a way to get praise and move up. But if your personal progress depends on the result of one negotiation, you lose sight of what's important. This also makes your negotiation power weaker.

Status can also be a trap. If you care too much about seeming skilled, better, or quick-witted, you listen less well. Vanity takes your attention away from the other person and onto yourself. Calmness often comes only when we allow ourselves not to be perfect.

And finally, even a good relationship can become a burden. Closeness creates trust, but it also holds you back. For fear of harming the relationship, we no longer clearly address important points. Confidence here means the ability to separate the relationship from the issue.

If you recognize these four chains, you can consciously free yourself from them. You will develop confidence. Mental preparation does not mean thinking away problems. It means actively guiding your focus. Thoughts cannot be forbidden, but they can be directed. Instead of thinking about money, career, status, or keeping relationships, focus your attention on structure, goals, and how you lead the conversation.

After every negotiation, a clear ending is also useful. Firm agreements create clarity. A clear end point strengthens the relationship and shows progress. This is true even if you have not yet reached a final solution.

Thinking about it afterward is just as important. What worked? Where did inner pressure build up? What dependence was felt? If you ask yourself these questions regularly, you turn single talks into learning steps. To negotiate firmly means, in the end, to lead yourself first.

## Conclusion

Good negotiations are not so much about talent. They are the result of preparation, clear thinking, and a lot of practice. If you get ready for each negotiation carefully, use a smart plan, avoid common traps, and stay friendly, you will slowly become a confident negotiation master.

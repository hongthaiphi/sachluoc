**Author:** Byung-Chul Han | **Category:** Gesundheit & Ernährung## WAS DRIN IST FÜR DICH: EIN PHILOSOPHISCHES PLÄDOYER FÜR EINE GANZHEITLICHERE DEFINITION VON ERFOLG.

Let’s be honest: We live in a time when burnout syndrome is recognized as a medical diagnosis.

And if you are not burned out yet, you are tired.

Tired of having to work and improve nonstop.

Tired of never feeling good enough, always chasing the next goal, and trying hard to make the best use of little time.

Tired of trying to balance ambition and mindfulness.

The philosopher and cultural scholar Byung-Chul Han describes this tiredness as the basic state of our modern society.

Our magazine Blink takes up his main question: How did this happen?

Shouldn’t our ambition aim to make us happier, not more exhausted?

And even more important: What can we do to break free from this tiredness society?

We need a definition of success that includes health, well-being, and emotional fulfillment, not just achievement.

We will now explain how to do this.

## RAUS AUS DER MÜDIGKEITSGESELLSCHAFT

We live in a time when exhaustion is no longer just an individual problem, but a kind of collective condition.

People have always felt pressure from outside: strict rules, harsh bosses, and tough situations.

But now we are harming ourselves.

We work hard, optimize, and compare ourselves with others by choice.

We push ourselves alone to the edge of collapse.

The hunt for success and productivity has brought us to a point where tiredness is not just a physical state.

Now it is a social symptom that we follow the wrong values.

This problem started because we took inside the pressure we once only felt from outside.

Before, external forces like social rules and expectations made us disciplined.

Now we have internalized the pressure to perform: We push ourselves to do more and achieve more.

What could be free choice—deciding how much to work for material success—is actually a trap: We changed being controlled by others into exploiting ourselves.

We don’t wear chains anymore, but we are still trapped in a hamster wheel.

If we want to change this, we need a new idea of success.

Not “more at any cost,” but balance.

A balance between ambition and rest, between work and breaks.

Success should give us life, not take it away.

But to do that, we must be ready to think differently.

In a culture that praises overtime and self-sacrifice, we must relearn to notice our own needs and limits.

Taking breaks is not weakness but self-care.

We must also question our goals: What really drives your desire to perform?

Are you afraid to fail and disappoint others?

Or do you want to grow mentally and emotionally?

Work and effort can be fun and fulfilling.

You just have to put effort in for the right reasons.

The last part is appreciation.

Gratitude can be a quiet counterbalance to the loud call of “higher, faster, further!”

Be thankful for what you have already achieved.

Don’t tie your happiness to distant future plans, but enjoy what makes your life worth living right now.

The tiredness of our society also relates to another worrying trend: losing the ability to feel bored.

## ZURÜCK ZU ECHTER, KREATIVER LANGEWEILE

Many people today resist giving themselves real, sometimes “unproductive” free time.

This kind of doing nothing helps us find ourselves again.

Because that connection is one of the most important sources of creativity and growth.

But today, we are almost constantly bombarded by stimuli and demands.

We fill every free minute with work, information, and entertainment.

This affects how we pay attention: Our focus is broken, restless, and always ready to jump to the next thing.

Many celebrate the skill of multitasking as the highest personal efficiency and productivity.

But breaking attention into many parts is not progress; it shows we are going backward in evolution.

In the animal world, multitasking is a survival strategy.

Wild animals must split their attention to do daily tasks and watch for danger.

This is not a virtue but a burden.

But many of us are like that today: never fully focused on one thing, always ready to jump mentally.

Because of this constant activity, we lose the ability to dive deeply into focused states where insight, art, and culture happen.

Walter Benjamin once called this deep boredom the “dream bird that hatches the egg of experience.”

He meant that we must first truly rest so our real thoughts, feelings, and creative impulses can appear.

This kind of quiet watching, without aiming to produce anything, is needed for reflection, insight, and innovation.

The key difference is not having a goal.

Imagine walking freely through a city or a beautiful place.

No time pressure, no goals or performance stress.

Then something as simple as walking can bring joy.

Walking becomes relaxed strolling, a rare chance to notice and think about yourself.

A small luxury.

Without duty, movement can be playful again.

Slowing down, having no goal, and reflecting are the basics of a life of deep attention.

The author does not mean a sentimental wish to go back to past times without technology, but a return to values and qualities we are losing.

In a time when everything must be useful and forward-moving, we must keep the ability to stop and rest.

The ability to appreciate what we already have.

The beauty of standing still.

From this calm place, we might also question our active life.

Because even our active part has lost its true goal.

As said: The real goal of our efforts should be to make our lives better and more fulfilling.

Hannah Arendt described activity as brave and creative work.

Something that changes the status quo for the better and creates new possibilities.

But today “activity” often just means “paid work.”

Work for work’s sake.

This makes us driven people in a hyperactive society that hardly truly lives because of all the busyness.

## EIN BEWUSSTSEIN FÜR DIE NEUE GEFAHR VON INNEN

We stand at the start of a new age some cultural experts call the neural age.

This time is marked by more people suffering from brain-related illnesses like depression, ADHD, borderline personality disorder, or burnout.

These illnesses shape healthcare in the 21st century more and more.

According to the author, this illness pattern differs from the 20th century, when the immune system was mainly threatened by external dangers like germs.

Today, health is more threatened by things happening inside us.

For Byung-Chul Han, this is part of a bigger social change.

The ideology of the last century followed an “immunological” logic externally: Like the body, the individual and society had to be shielded from outside attacks.

This fit the Cold War idea of clear lines between defense and attack, inside and outside, friend and enemy.

Health thinking was also binary: The body was pure territory to protect against enemy attacks.

But in the neural age, health problems come less from outside and more from internal imbalance.

More diseases today happen not because of germs but because of overload: too much information and pressure.

Body and mind don’t defend against outside attacks but are overwhelmed from within.

Sadly, positive things like globalization also play a role.

The more connected and diverse the world is, the more previous clear borders fade.

Culturally and socially, this is progress.

We want a more tolerant and diverse world.

But for health, this creates new challenges.

In a more fluid and open world, dangers are not always clear enough to trigger immune defense.

The best example is work-life balance: As work and free time mix more, we realize later that we are close to burnout.

So the rise of brain-related disorders is also a response to the constant excess in our time.

We live in a nonstop stream of stimuli.

We are always moving and speeding up.

We must and want to always be reachable, productive, and motivated.

Our body does not react with classic immune defense but with neural overload.

Burnouts, depression, and poor concentration are not caused by others but are internal wear and tear.

This changes the types of violence and aggression our nervous system faces.

In the 20th century, violence was viral and military.

It came from outside.

Now it arises inside us.

It is violence from too much: too much sameness, repetition, and self-centeredness.

This new threat forces us to rethink health, well-being, and care.

We must think about how much we let the demands of modern life make us sick.

This sounds worrying.

But we see a big change happening before and around us: We are quickly entering a society of tiredness.

## EINE NEUE UND GANZHEITLICHE DEFINITION VON ERFOLG

The endless push for productivity caused a wave of exhaustion.

But there is hope.

This collective tiredness is also a chance to realign our lives and find more balance and calm.

At the center of this change may be what Byung-Chul Han calls “transformative tiredness.”

This tiredness is more than just physical tiredness after a long workday.

It is an inner, deep response to the constant pressure of the everywhere present productivity logic.

Transformative tiredness is more than a sign of overwork and frustration.

It is the worn-out state of a society that for too long has valued achievement over well-being.

It is a kind of mental exhaustion.

A collective burnout.

And this widespread burnout could be the start of change.

When you have no energy left, you have no choice but to stop.

To catch your breath and ask: Is this really worth it?

What really helps me feel good?

These questions create space for rest and honest reflection.

This means we must seriously question what success means.

Success is not blind busywork and working for work’s sake.

Success means also enjoying the rewards of your effort.

Time and peace to recover and care for your health and happiness.

Time for quiet thinking, gratitude, and appreciation.

This whole new idea of success is not only healthier for us as individuals.

It is also the base for a better and fairer society, where we have enough resources to support each other again.

## FAZIT

We have pushed ourselves into a state of collective exhaustion between madness for productivity and logic of doing more.

In this tiredness society, overtime and burnouts are not just usual but nearly badges of honor.

But more and more people have had enough, literally.

They feel deeply that something is wrong when we harm ourselves without an outside cause.

And when our hard work no longer gives us satisfaction and well-being, but just leaves us empty and tired.

But there is hope.

This deep exhaustion could be the start of a new era.

When you have no energy left, you have to stop.

That is the moment we can think: Is this still the right idea of success?

Or should success also include time for rest, enjoyment, and quiet thought?

*Author: **Erving Goffman***

_Erving Goffman_

Reading time: 18 minutes

### Synopsis

*The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life *(1956) is a well-known book about society. It uses ideas from theater to look at how people act in social situations.

## What’s in it for me? The world is like a stage.

Answering the phone at work… greeting a stranger… These moments don't feel like a play. They feel natural and automatic. It's just life happening. But they are also very exact. We know when to smile. We know when to stop ourselves. We know when to sound sure, even if we are not. We start acting without thinking, and usually without asking why.

In *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life, *Erving Goffman, a person who studies society, looks at the hidden ways we act every day.

Goffman does not ask if we are being "fake" or "real." Instead, he asks a more interesting question: How do social situations change who we are allowed to be at each moment? Why do some behaviors feel right in one place, but not allowed in another? And how much of what we call our "personality" is really an act that we have practiced many times?

Goffman asks us to listen more carefully to talks. He asks us to notice the areas people keep safe. And he asks us to pay attention to the small habits that make social life easy. When you do this, everyday life starts to look less like a series of personal choices. It looks more like a show we are all working to keep going.

In this summary, we will look at some of Goffman's main ideas. These ideas have been important for a long time. You might gain a new way of looking at society, and also at yourself.

## Blink 1 - The parts we play

Goffman says that everyday social life is like a theater. We are all actors, controlling how others see us. Every time we interact with someone, it's like a small stage. Each meeting is a performance where we show a version of ourselves that fits the situation.

This makes us ask: Do we believe in the parts we play? One type of actor is "sincere." They truly believe in the image they show. Another type is "cynical." They don't believe in their role, but they play it well because the audience wants them to. For example, a shop assistant might say nice things to a customer. 

Goffman thinks most of us move between being sincere and cynical. This depends on the situation. But over time, the difference becomes unclear. Our mask – our role – can become something we do without thinking. It can even become the truest part of who we think we are.

Goffman then talks about the "front." This is all the things we use to show others what the situation is. It includes the setting, which is the physical place. It also includes our personal front, like our look, clothes, how we stand, our voice, and our hand movements. Society expects these parts to fit together. If something feels wrong, the performance fails.

Goffman points out "dramatic realization." This is how we make our actions show clearly what we want people to notice. Think of a nurse showing gentleness. Or a sports referee showing confidence with quick decisions. But this acting takes effort. The more energy we use to *look* good, the less energy we might have to *be* good.

Goffman also looks at "idealization." This is when we show ourselves in a way that society approves of. We often show qualities that fit social ideals, like being polite, kind, or skilled. But sometimes, we also hide our strengths to fit what others expect. 

For example, women sometimes acted less intelligent to seem more traditionally feminine. This made men feel smarter. Goffman gives an example from the 1940s. Female college students would let their boyfriends explain things they already knew. Or they let them correct their spelling. One woman said, “It seems my boyfriend gets a great kick out of it.”

Performances often show not who people really are. They show who the audience wants them to be. Even a simple meeting hides a very planned act. We usually think everyday life is unplanned. So, this idea is very interesting and perhaps a little worrying.

## Blink 2 - Hiding things

Goffman says that a good performance needs hiding and control. To act in a way that people believe, we must not only show certain good ideas. We must also hide anything that goes against them. Everyday performances are just as much about what is kept *hidden* as what is shown.

People regularly hide actions, reasons, or facts that don't fit the image they want to show. Often, these hidden behaviors make them happy. So, they don't stop doing them, they just hide them. This allows people to “have their cake and eat it too.” For example, we might show fancy magazines in our living rooms. But we keep romance novels we enjoy, but might not want others to know about, hidden in our bedrooms.

Actors also want their audience to believe that the role they see is the person's main or only role. Audiences usually agree. They prefer not to think about the many other lives and acts a person might have. This is part of what Goffman calls “audience segregation.” 

Also, actors try to make each meeting feel special and new, even if it happens often. We like to believe our relationships are unique. That's why it can feel strange to see a close friend show the same warmth and closeness to someone else.

Audiences are always trying to understand signals. So, performances are easily broken. Hand movements or accidents can be misunderstood. Wrong meanings can be given to an actor. This is why showing things clearly and consistently is very important. People's feelings change often. But social roles need steadiness. We are expected to keep the same “mask” no matter our mood, how tired we are, or what we are thinking inside. Social rules help us keep this mask in place.

It's interesting that all social roles include some hiding, telling only some things, and small deceptions. Even honest relationships, like marriages, need some secret areas.

Another important point is what Goffman calls “mystification.” Keeping distance and not letting many people see can create wonder. It can also protect a performance from people looking too closely. Audiences often help with this. They treat actors with respect and control. 

In the end, social standing is not something we own. It is a way of behaving that we always show. And in a way, we are trapped. The thinker Jean-Paul Sartre once said that society works hard to keep us stuck in our roles. It is as if society fears we might escape them.

## Blink 3 - What happens backstage

Goffman makes a difference between how we act frontstage and backstage. If social life is a performance, it must happen somewhere. And where we are is very important for how we act.

Performances usually happen in clear areas, both in place and time. When we go into these areas, the performance fills them completely. Everyone there is expected to agree on what the situation is. 

The place where the performance happens is the front region, or “frontstage.” This is where people show themselves to others. They use a carefully planned setting, look, and manner to keep a certain image. Some behaviors are highlighted here. Others are purposely hidden.

Then there is the back region, or “backstage.” This is the space where the performance is openly shown to be untrue. Backstage is where we manage how others see us. Fake appearances are built. Stage items are kept. Clothes are fixed. And mistakes are corrected. 

It is also where actors can relax. They can take off their public mask and stop acting completely. Backstage, team members practice, complain, joke, and sometimes tell each other what to do. This space is very important for keeping the frontstage act going.

So, controlling who can go backstage is very important. If what happens backstage becomes known in public, the performance can fail completely. For example, funeral workers must protect the sad family from the hard work of getting a dead body ready. Even buildings matter. Thin walls in modern homes can let backstage sounds come into frontstage life. This can cause embarrassment and stress. Usually, we don't want to hear our neighbors’ private talks.

Goffman suggests that one of the most showing moments is the change between areas:  when someone leaves backstage and goes to the front, or comes back again. In these moments, we can see how quickly people start and stop acting. 

He says that Western societies have two very different “languages” of behavior – an informal backstage language with bad words, messiness, jokes, and easy body movements. And there is a controlled frontstage language, marked by good manners, self-control, and proper behavior.

Goffman also looks at what happens when keeping audiences separate doesn't work – when someone sees a performance that was not meant for them. These unwanted entries can cause big problems with embarrassment and managing how others see you. Actors might try to change the meaning of the situation to include the person who came in. Or they might pretend the person was always meant to be there. But these plans are often weak and don't work well. 

This is one of Goffman’s main ideas – social peace needs to keep areas, roles, and audiences apart. When those lines disappear, the stress can be seen right away.

## Blink 4 - Roles that don't fit

Goffman then looks at a main weakness of social performances: the problem of information. If social life needs a shared understanding of the situation, then performances must carefully control what is known, by whom, and when. 

A “team” is a group of people working together to put on a social act. Every team wants to protect its performance. They do this by controlling information that could weaken it. Some facts, if shown at the wrong time, would completely break the image they are creating. Goffman calls this “destructive information.” Keeping it hidden is one of the main tasks of social life.

To do this, teams use secrecy. Goffman names several types of secrets. “Dark secrets” are facts that would make the performance completely unbelievable if shown. “Strategic secrets” are information that gives actors an advantage over their audience. “Inside secrets” are shared among team members. They help create a feeling of unity by showing who truly belongs. 

Besides these, there are different kinds of knowledge about secrets. Some information is “entrusted.” This means it is given on purpose to certain people. Other information is “free.” It spreads by accident through gossip, watching, or information getting out. A performance stays strong if these types of information are carefully matched.

Goffman also talks about “discrepant roles.” These are positions held by people whose access to information, areas, or interactions does not match what their role seems to be. Discrepant roles show the weak points of social life. They show how easily performances can break.

Some of the most surprising discrepant roles are people who enter a situation for false reasons. The “informer” seems loyal to one group but secretly reports to another. The “shill” pretends to be a normal audience member but actually works for the actors. They quietly guide how people react. The “go-between” carries information across areas that should be kept separate. In all these cases, the person takes part in the interaction. But they have hidden knowledge that could cause problems.

There’s also another type of person – the “non-person.” Non-persons are physically there during performances. But they are treated as if they are socially invisible. A common example is a servant. They move freely between frontstage and backstage. But they are expected to notice everything and pretend not to notice anything. Their presence helps performances work. Yet, they are not allowed to take full part.

Through these examples, Goffman shows that social reality is kept together not just by acting. It is also kept by carefully controlling knowledge. Roles that don't fit show how much social order depends on secrets. And they show how easily it can be broken.

## Blink 5 - Stepping out of character

Sometimes, actors stop playing their roles for a short time. Even in very controlled performances, small breaks appear. These are small moments when people show things that go against the image they are supposed to keep. Goffman calls these moments “communication out of character.” He sees them not as accidents, but as normal things in social life that show a lot.

For example, think of sudden shouts like “My God!” These words clearly show that the actor has lost control of their role for a moment. But because they are so common, they work almost like a usual apology. They ask the audience for forgiveness, saying that everyone sometimes fails as an actor. The fact that they are so predictable shows how deeply rooted our expectations for performance are.

Communication out of character shows possible problems. But it also helps to manage them. Goffman points out a few main types of this communication.

The first is “treatment of the absent.” Behind the scenes, actors often talk about their audience, or other teams,  in ways that would not be okay to say in person. Workers complain about customers; customers make fun of workers. 

Secret criticism is much more common than secret praise. This is because it builds team unity. By making fun of or criticizing those who must be treated politely in public, actors feel better. They also make up for the emotional control needed frontstage. Importantly, Goffman says that this behavior does not always show their “true” feelings.

Another type of communication out of character is “staging talk” – talk about the performance itself. This includes gossip, work talk, and discussions after an event. Actors from very different jobs – comedians, professors, salespeople – share a similar backstage interest. They talk about what works, what fails, and how audiences react. 

The third type of communication is “team collusion,” especially when it involves showing disrespect. Here, actors secretly show signs to each other – or even to themselves – that they do not fully believe in the role they’re playing. For example, children crossing their fingers while lying, or employees making a face behind a boss’s back. These acts create mental separation from the role. They protect who the actor feels they are.

Goffman concludes that these types of communication show a very important truth – no performance completely takes over the people who do it. Actors always keep the ability to step back. They can hint at other truths. And they can manage different versions of themselves at once. Social life, then, is not built on one smooth reality. It is built on the careful, continuous control of many different realities.

## Final summary

In this summary of *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* by Erving Goffman, you’ve learned that everyday social life works like a play. In every interaction, people show themselves carefully. They try to control how others see them. People might truly believe in the roles they play. Or they might act them out for practical reasons, without believing. But most people easily change between these ways of acting. Over time, the roles we play can feel natural, even real.

To keep a performance going, people use a “front.” This includes the setting, how they look, and their manner. These things explain the situation. They must fit together. Actors often act out their actions strongly to make wanted qualities easy to see. At the same time, performances need hiding. Anything that goes against the perfect image can be hidden “backstage.”

Social peace needs to control who can get information. Teams guard secrets and manage who knows what. But this control is easy to break. Roles that don't fit show the hidden way performances are built. They do this by having unexpected knowledge or ways to get in. Performances are made more difficult by moments of “communication out of character.” These are small mistakes. They show that actors never completely become their roles.

Goffman’s main idea is that social reality is not just lived. It is always being created. Everyday life is kept together by planned acts. This is true even when everyone knows, in some way, that it is all an act.

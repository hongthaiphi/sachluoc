### Synopsis

*Lateral Thinking for Every Day* (2023) teaches you how to solve daily problems. It shows you new and creative ways to think, different from usual methods. The book gives real examples and stories. It shares useful steps and tools. These help you think better and solve problems in new ways. With these methods, you can see things differently and find fresh answers for your hardest problems.

## What’s in it for me? Learn practical ways to create new ideas and solve real problems.

King Solomon once met two mothers. Each said the same baby was hers. His decision surprised them: he said to bring a sword and cut the child in half. One woman agreed without a word. The other cried, asking him to save the child and give it to her rival. Solomon gave the baby to the second woman. He knew only a real mother would give up her claim to save her child’s life. This old story shows lateral thinking: solving problems in new, indirect ways. It means looking at things from unusual angles.

Edward de Bono created the term in 1967. Ford struggled to sell cars in a crowded market. People usually thought Ford needed to make better cars. But de Bono suggested buying parking companies. Then Ford could save city parking spots for Ford cars. This changed the focus from making cars to the driver’s experience. This new way of thinking helps create new ideas everywhere. This includes big problems like climate change or solving crimes.

In this summary, you will learn how to think and act differently. You will discover the basic ideas of lateral thinking. You will see why following the crowd stops new ideas. And you will find proven methods and real-life examples. Author Paul Sloane shares many examples. We have chosen some that show the main ideas. They also give you advice you can use right away.

Let’s begin.

## Blink 1 - The basic idea and the problem

Big airlines used to have strong beliefs. They thought customers wanted great service. Airlines gave tickets for every flight. Seats were chosen ahead of time. Travel agents sold tickets. And, of course, flights went to big airports – because business travelers were there, right? These were not just guesses. These were strong ideas, made solid by many years of doing things the same way. Then cheap airlines appeared. They broke almost every rule. They stopped offering fancy service. They did not let people choose seats early. They sold tickets in new ways, not through travel agents. And they landed at smaller airports. This created a huge market that no one had thought of.

This is a great example of *lateral thinking*. It means solving a problem by looking at it in a completely new way. Edward de Bono used this term to describe thinking that moves sideways. It is different from thinking that moves forward in the usual, straight line. It is the ability to find new ways and fresh answers for both big and small challenges. De Bono pointed out four main parts: seeing the main ideas that limit your thinking, looking at things from other points of view, letting go of strict, straight-line thinking, and using chance findings.

The problem is, most of us stay stuck with old, main ideas. We see the world the same way everyone else does. And once these ideas take hold, everything seems to prove them right.

We are not alone in this trap. Evolution has made us want to be part of groups. We want to belong. Henri Tajfel, a social scientist, found something worrying: just putting people into groups makes them like their own group more. It also makes them treat other groups unfairly. Solomon Asch did experiments to show this. He put one real person with seven actors. The actors were told to give wrong answers about line lengths. Seventy-five percent of the real people ignored what they clearly saw and agreed with the group. This tendency to conform came from two things: wanting to fit in, and thinking that others knew more.

This desire to be accepted becomes dangerous when groups make decisions together. *Groupthink* takes over. The need to agree stops people from disagreeing. It hides different opinions and blocks new ways of thinking. President J. F. Kennedy’s team never really questioned the CIA’s plan for the Bay of Pigs invasion. It failed terribly. During the Cuban Missile Crisis, Kennedy learned his lesson. He brought in experts from outside to share different views. He encouraged members to speak their worries and ask tough questions. He also left some meetings on purpose so his ideas would not control the talk. Without this careful watch, groupthink has caused many disasters. For example, the CIA not seeing 9/11 coming, Enron’s false accounting, and Volkswagen’s emissions scandal are just three. Smart people in groups make bad choices when they stop asking questions and only try to agree.

That is why lateral thinking is important. And it is why learning four key methods can free you from usual limits. They are next.

## Blink 2 - Four ways to break free

Now you know why lateral thinking matters. So, how do you actually do it? Four main ways can change how you solve problems.

The first way is to *think about the opposite*. When things seem stuck, the easy thing to do is to change what you already have. Instead, try doing the complete opposite. Encyclopaedia Britannica was very powerful for over 200 years. By 2000, it had 32 books, made by 100 editors and 4,000 writers. But it was costly, hard to update, and in the end, it failed. Wikipedia started in 2001 with a totally opposite plan: it was free, made by people who were not paid, and always changing. The opposite of expensive was not just cheap – it was free. The opposite of a small team of paid editors was not a slightly bigger one. It was thousands of volunteers. Wikipedia now has almost six million articles in English and works well in 300 languages.

Similarly, big software companies kept their computer code secret. But Linus Torvalds created Linux with open access. Anyone could see and change it. This opposite approach started the whole open-source movement. Uber owns no taxis. Airbnb owns no hotels. Turo owns no cars. Each succeeded by doing the exact opposite of what was normal.

A second way is to *break the rules*. Sometimes, to make progress, you must break the rules completely. On September 26, 1983, a Soviet officer named Stanislav Petrov got a warning. It said six missiles were flying towards the USSR. The rules said he had to report it right away. Instead, he believed it was a false alarm and kept quiet. His disobedience likely stopped a nuclear war. And Queen’s “Bohemian Rhapsody” broke all the rules for a pop song. It had six parts, many music styles, and was five and a half minutes long when radio stations played three-minute songs. Record companies said no to it. Queen went around them, gave it to a DJ, and made one of history’s greatest hits.

A third method is to *think like an outsider*. An outsider brings new ideas. People who came from other countries started or helped start more than 40 percent of Fortune 500 companies. They also started almost 50 percent of American billion-dollar new businesses. They have seen different cultures and are not stuck by local beliefs. When you are inside a system, you cannot see its strange parts. An outsider notices everything.

The last method is to *ask simple questions*. Never stop asking questions. Children ask about 73 questions every day. Adults ask about 20. As we get older, we think we know enough and stop asking. But asking questions helps us understand more deeply. Roger Hargreaves drew Mr. Tickle because his six-year-old son asked a hard question: “What does a tickle look like?” That question led to a book series that sold 90 million copies. And when Eric Schmidt was Google’s CEO, he said the company worked on questions, not answers.

Great discoveries come from asking the right questions. Newton wondered why apples fall. Darwin asked why island animals were different. Einstein imagined riding a beam of light. Keep asking “What if?” all the time. Question everything. That is how lateral thinking changes problems into chances.

## Blink 3 - Learning from history

You have just seen that following the crowd and groupthink can trap us in old ideas. History shows us something harder to accept: experts are often the worst at this. The most educated people often fight new ideas the hardest. Let’s look at some examples.

In 1847, a Hungarian doctor named Ignaz Semmelweis suggested washing hands. He said it would lower the number of mothers dying during childbirth. The doctors did not like the idea that their hands could kill patients. He died in a mental hospital. He was proven right only after he died. In 1912, German geologist Alfred Wegener’s idea that continents move was rejected. Other experts in his field said it was just outsider thinking. It was accepted only in the 1950s. History is full of similar stories: long-standing knowledge often stops new ways of thinking.

But history teaches another lesson. Those who solve problems that seem impossible often succeed not because they are experts or plan perfectly. They succeed because they are ready to fail many times and learn from it.

Aeronautical engineer Paul MacCready joined the Kremer Prize contest. This challenge was to build an airplane powered by a person. It had to fly a certain path. Teams had failed for 18 years. Instead of designing a perfect plane, MacCready built the *Gossamer Condor*. He used light materials that were easy to fix after crashes, which he knew would happen. While others spent years making perfect designs, MacCready’s team won in months. The secret was simple: fly, crash, learn. His team saw failure not as a disaster but as useful information. Each crash taught them something new.

When you learn any new skill, you expect to fail sometimes. Think the same way about your experiments. Do not plan for success – plan for safe failure. Plan your attempts so you can fail without huge losses. Then change what you do based on what you learn. This is how lateral thinkers get past experts’ resistance. They solve what experts believe is impossible.

## Blink 4 - Tools for lateral thinking

There are many different tools you can use to help with lateral thinking. Here are two useful tools to help you make decisions or create new ideas.

Use the *Six Thinking Hats* when your group needs to decide on a plan. Edward de Bono created this method. It replaces arguing – where people defend their views and egos get in the way of good judgment – with thinking together. Everyone wears the same colored hat at the same time. With the white hat, the group looks at facts together. With the red hat, each person freely shares feelings – no arguments are allowed. The yellow hat forces positive thinking: list good points even if you do not like the idea. The black hat does the opposite: find risks and bad points, even if you love the idea. Both people who doubt and people who are positive have to think outside their usual comfort zone here. Next is the green hat, which asks for creative ways to lower those risks. Finally, the blue hat reviews the whole process. The result? You have looked at the plan from all sides without a single argument. Decisions happen naturally, with everyone truly supporting them.

When you need new ideas, you can use the Random Word method. Pick any random noun from a dictionary. List what it is like, then try to connect those ideas to your problem. Let's say you need to find more job applicants. Your random word is “eucalyptus.” Its qualities include: Australia, gum, branches, medicine. This brings up ideas: recruit from Australia, offer travel chances, show how careers can branch out, provide health benefits. This random input makes your mind think in new directions – exactly where lateral thinking happens.

## Blink 5 - Overcoming mental barriers

Everyone gets stuck sometimes. Progress stops. Energy dies. When this happens, you need clear steps to start moving again. 

The first step is to *find the real barrier.* Do not just accept surface reasons. Look deeply to find the true problem. Next, *change your goal*. Who are you trying to help? What is the final result? Is it still important? Clearly state the reasons – this will restart your drive.

Third, *question your beliefs*. What if common ideas are wrong? Could a simpler, free solution replace your complex, expensive one? Then *think about the exact opposite* of your current way. Fifth, *look outside your own area* for comparisons – who has solved something similar elsewhere?

The sixth step is to imagine you have superpowers: How would a great leader or hero solve this? Then try *changing to a different format* completely. Could a mobile app work better than a website? Could a podcast take the place of a blog? Eighth, *find a key tool or resource* that would restart progress. Ninth, *ask for help*. Asking for help is not a weakness – it is often the smartest thing to do. Finally, *imagine starting fresh tomorrow*. What would you do differently if you had no past investments holding you back?

Cognitive biases are deeper, constant mistakes in thinking. They can harm your judgment. *Similarity bias* makes you prefer people like yourself. It also makes you value their opinions too much. *Anchoring bias* makes you give too much importance to the first information you find. *Confirmation bias* leads you to look for information that agrees with what you already believe. You ignore facts that go against it. *Overconfidence bias* makes you think you are better at making decisions than you really are. By knowing these thinking traps by name, you can stop them from stopping your progress.

## Blink 6 - Welcoming failure for big new ideas

In 1995, two Stanford students met when they started university. Larry Page, a computer science student whose parents were professors, wondered: *How do you measure how important a webpage is?* He used an idea from university research – papers become important when other papers quote them. So, what if you could record the whole World Wide Web and see how each page was linked to every other? It was a bold idea. It crashed Stanford’s computers. But it worked.

Page and Brin, the second student, had found something amazing. They did not first realize they were building a search engine. When they went to big companies – Yahoo, Alta Vista, Lycos, Excite – offering to sell their technology for one million dollars, they were turned down. The industry leaders thought search was not important. They believed people wanted content and to explore, not quick ways to find things. They were completely wrong. Google went on to become much bigger than all of them. Page and Brin succeeded because they used ideas from unexpected places. They also asked new questions that others had not thought of.

But another idea helps this kind of new thinking: welcoming failure. In most companies, failure is punished, and success is celebrated. The lateral thinker changes this. Failure is key to exploring new things. Success can make people too relaxed. If everything you try works, you are not being bold enough. The tennis champion Rafael Nadal sometimes serves strong, risky shots that fail. He does not do this to lose points. He does it because riskier serves are harder for his opponent to return. He accepts that some will fail as part of the plan. When you play completely safely, you limit your chance for great success. Take brave actions. Some will fail. Share what you learn from those failures. Give credit for failures that were tried honorably. When leaders support this with words and actions, people become brave and eager to try new things. As Tom Kelley from IDEO says: fail often to succeed sooner.

## Blink 7 - Lateral thinking in action

Lateral thinking is not just for company meetings and new idea labs. It is a powerful tool for solving society’s biggest problems. When police and investigative journalists face problems that seem impossible – like catching criminals or showing terrible acts – lateral thinking offers surprising answers.

When armed men robbed Northern Bank in Belfast in December 2004, stealing about £25 million, the crime seemed impossible to solve. The criminals were never caught. But bank officials thought differently. Instead of chasing the robbers, they took back all old banknotes – about £300 million – and replaced them with new money. The new notes had different designs and serial numbers. Anyone holding the stolen old notes could not spend them without being caught right away. The police could not catch the criminals, but they made most of the stolen money worthless.

Police use lateral thinking in other ways. A fake encrypted chat app called Anom was created by the FBI and Australian Federal Police in 2019. It was advertised to criminals as a safe way to talk. But it let the police watch messages from over 300 criminal groups in more than 100 countries. One operation led to 800 arrests. It also seized eight tons of cocaine, 250 guns, and $48 million in cash and digital money.

In a different situation, journalist Eliot Higgins used lateral thinking to show war crimes. In 2014, he started Bellingcat. It uses public information to look into terrible acts. Using satellite pictures and Google Earth, his team found the Buk missile launcher. This was the one that shot down Malaysia Airlines Flight 17, killing 298 people. They also showed the Russian agents who poisoned Sergei Skripal in the UK. Their investigations, using open public sources, have changed how spy agencies check information. What started as research by regular people has changed the fight against those who do terrible things.

## Final summary

In this summary of *Lateral Thinking for Every Day* by Paul Sloane, you have learned that lateral thinking solves problems by looking at them in completely new ways. Most of us stay stuck with main ideas. We see the world through the same ways everyone else does. Following the crowd and groupthink stop disagreements and block new ways of thinking. This prevents big new solutions. But you can escape this trap by practicing on purpose.

Four main ways can help you break free from usual limits. First, think about the opposite – change your whole approach instead of just tweaking what you have. Second, break the rules when you need to make progress. Third, think like an outsider who notices strange things that people inside miss. And finally, keep asking simple questions, like children do before their ideas become fixed.

By learning these methods, you can escape the main ideas that limit your thinking. You can find solutions that common ways of thinking miss.

**Author:** Dirk Steffens | **Category:** Wissenschaft## WHAT'S IN IT FOR YOU: SCIENCE AGAINST THE END OF THE WORLD.

Today, if you look honestly at the world, you might think we are ruining our planet.

Climate disaster, species dying out, pollution, and lots of wrong information—does it feel like we cannot survive this?

Maybe we can!

In this Blink to hope, we show you why, in this very hard situation, a positive view is the best choice.

Sounds strange, right?

But it makes sense when we use science to carefully look at the complicated mix of feelings and wrong ideas that shape how we see the world.

That is what we will do now.

## DON'T BELIEVE EVERYTHING YOU THINK.

First, think about the worst train ride you ever had.

There is a good chance it was in Germany, and you felt like everything was falling apart.

Aside from stress and bad mood: How can we fight a big problem like climate change when even the train’s toilet and air conditioning don’t work well?

Everyone has a bad story like this.

We like to tell these stories at parties, dinners, or at work.

And after a while, these stories turn from personal bad moments into a shared belief that everything is broken.

Wars, extreme weather, hate in politics, train delays, and schools falling apart make us feel like the world is ending.

Everyday frustration shapes our thoughts.

This is totally normal from an evolution point of view.

Thinking uses a lot of energy, so we like shortcuts and general ideas.

Many biases control our thinking, and most focus on dangers and fears.

Simply put: Our ancestors remembered the one bad berry that made them sick more than all the good food.

A poisonous snake needs more attention than a cute ladybug.

This “survival tool” in our brain has not changed much.

That is why we fear sharks, even though sharks should fear us more.

Also, we have a political system that often thinks only until the next election, and media that live on bad news.

This creates a feeling like the world is ending.

Here is the problem: What we believe becomes real.

What we feel as disasters can become real ones.

Your bike gets stolen, you hear about a shooting in your city, and suddenly you think crime is rising—and you might vote for a party that gives up freedom for fake safety that was never really threatened.

But freedom is what we need to make change.

This is a simple but important point.

Those who see only bad things limit themselves.

Those who think nothing can be saved do not even try.

Those who are afraid make worse choices and often blame others.

But how can we change this and see the world in a better way again?

The best tool to see the truth is science.

Science helps us here too.

If you look at the facts and studies, you will see many fears have no real basis.

## LET'S TACKLE THE MOST IMPORTANT FIRST.

For example, crime in Germany has been going down since the 1990s.

We live richer, healthier, and safer than ever before.

It just does not always feel like that.

If we want to see real dangers, we must make priorities.

We put out the burning house first before we wash the dishes. It is the same with global problems.

It sounds cruel and cold to say climate change is more urgent than the war in Ukraine.

But if we think about saving humanity, it is true.

We do not want to say other problems do not matter—we just have to stop the fire burning us all first.

What should be our top priority?

There is a top scientist who studies the end of the world.

His name is Johan Rockström. He leads a team at the Stockholm Resilience Center that works on "planetary boundaries."

His question is: What can we still break before it is all over for us?

Rockström found nine planetary boundaries.

If we cross one, it is the end.

These boundaries cover air quality, climate, species, pollution with new chemicals, fresh water, ocean acid, nutrient cycles, land use, and the ozone layer.

In many areas, we are going the wrong way, for example with microplastic pollution.

Every adult has so much microplastic in their brain it would fill a soup spoon.

Rockström found two areas we must focus on urgently.

First is species loss: About 150 animal and plant species die out every day.

Imagine each species like a wooden stick in a Jenga tower.

One day, if a stick goes, the whole tower falls.

The second urgent area is global warming.

We will miss the 1.5-degree target.

Now it is all or nothing.

## OUR PROBLEMS CAN BE SOLVED.

Science does not only show what is wrong and what we must do first.

It also tells us what actions can help.

In short: It is totally possible to control these problems before it is too late.

Every day, new solutions and ideas come up all around the world.

Many are easy to do and not expensive.

For example, Germany already shows the energy transition is possible.

More than 60 percent of energy came from renewables in 2024.

The food system can change too – we can produce, process, and eat food differently.

Simple things like crop rotation and less meat eating make a big difference.

Stopping species loss can be even simpler.

All we need to do is do nothing in big areas for a long time.

If we leave some land alone, many species come back by themselves.

It cannot be easier than that.

Animals and plants also help fight climate change.

A single blue whale stores as much carbon per year as 1,500 trees.

When it dies, its body sinks to the sea floor.

The greenhouse gas stays out of the air for hundreds of years.

Blue whale poop is fertilizer for algae that absorb four times more CO₂ than the Amazon forest.

Every blue whale we save helps our climate—and it costs nothing.

Solutions are everywhere.

In water treatment plants, farms, laws, and in what we buy.

We find solutions in youth science projects, new businesses, and in neighbors who use cloth bags instead of plastic.

Also, population growth helps us: in a few decades, the world population will stop growing, and things will get easier on their own.

Nice, isn’t it?

The problem is studied well and Johan Rockström and many others show us the answers clearly.

So what do we do?

We just agree and say “Yes.

Very bad!

Someone really must do something!”

And then we keep going as before.

It feels like everyone has more urgent things to do than saving the world.

Since everyone acts like this, we think it is too late to change.

But it is not.

Now we come to the most important part.

We said at first that our feelings make us see things too negatively.

Science can see the situation better and find solutions.

But you do not win hearts or many followers with statistics and hard papers.

So, we must go back to feelings to turn scientific knowledge into passionate action.

## STORIES START BIG CHANGES.

Let’s compare two big building sites: Stuttgart 21 and the Cheops Pyramid.

How is it possible that building a simple train station now takes longer than building a world wonder in ancient times, when people had no wheel?

It is not because the ancient Egyptians were ruthless slave masters, as many think.

Today, historians believe the workers were paid.

The big difference is that pyramid builders believed in what they were doing.

They followed a belief, a positive story.

Today, we are missing that.

We are pessimists and that makes us lazy.

We don’t believe we can shape the future, so we don’t try and just complain.

We need an inspiring, positive story.

But not just any story!

For a story to create big, positive change, it needs the right time, place, and situation.

For example, we agree today that equality and human rights are great ideas.

But if we told Julius Caesar that, he would laugh at us.

Or think of Jesus: If he had preached 20 years ago in a small German town, he would probably be in a mental hospital without followers.

Stories are very powerful.

But how?

How can a fairy tale or myth decide the future of humanity?

Looking back in our evolution explains why we always see the bad side and why stories are so important to humans.

When we lived in caves, people painted on walls.

These first graffiti often showed hunting scenes.

But not only that.

In the caves of Lascaux, there is a painting about 17,000 years old.

It shows a wounded bison, a bird on a stick, and next to them a human with a bird head and a big penis.

He is the superhero of the Stone Age—a creature that does not exist.

He is from the imagination of the painter, put on the real wall.

We can imagine things we have never seen before.

A better future, for example.

That’s why we can make that future real.

Many researchers say imagination and storytelling are what make us different from animals.

Because of stories, Homo sapiens rules the Earth, not Neanderthals.

Imagination is why we never stopped developing.

Think about it: Would Columbus have sailed west to find India without a big idea in his head?

On the other hand, stories caused many modern problems.

But with stories, we can solve them too.

## WE NEED THE RIGHT STORIES TO SOLVE OUR PROBLEMS.

So, what stories should we tell?

For the author, Banksy’s flower thrower graffiti is the perfect symbol.

You probably know it.

It shows a masked man throwing a bouquet of colorful flowers instead of a grenade or Molotov cocktail.

He is a radical fighter but not destructive.

This art is the oldest and simplest kind: someone paints on the wall.

This flower thrower shows the kind of story that can save us.

He stands for pure optimism mixed with “do-it-yourself” and fighting spirit.

And we need that fighting spirit because it will not be easy.

But it doesn’t have to be.

Challenges often inspire us.

In his famous Moon speech in 1962, John F.

Kennedy said the US would go to the moon.

“Not because it is easy, but because it is hard.”

This changed the mission into a national adventure.

Jesus, Buddha, and Gandhi all had great stories about love and non-violence making a better world.

But none said it would be easy.

Unlike today’s politics.

Politics only decides what hurts no one.

And that is almost nothing.

We need very good stories that connect all the solutions we see with a new reality.

We do not need a new Jesus or JFK.

Most great leaders were not bright lonely stars.

They were people who followed the spirit of their time and whose message found the right ground at the right moment.

Today, many people around the world already have ideas and stories that can save us.

It may sound cheesy, but many people doing small things change the world’s face.

You can do much too to make a better future.

You can buy organic, plan holidays without flying, and be a good role model for your children.

Be kind because attitude spreads.

And most of all, dare to think beyond your own world.

There are many ideas to tell our reality better.

They may seem “crazy” today— but our world 500 years ago would be unbelievable for those times.

Here is one example: In New Zealand, since 2017, the Whanganui River is legally a person.

It is represented by a Maori council.

The river can now sue people and companies that harm it.

It sounds strange, but it is very sensible.

This is just one of the new stories that can take us far.

Now we have reached the end of our Blink to hopeful optimism.

## CONCLUSION

A positive view of the future feels good, doesn’t it?

It should, because we need this good energy to change things.

Optimism gives you strength and direction for change.

You don’t have to save the world alone.

Just start somewhere.

The author and his wife, for example, have picked up a piece of trash every day for a long time and thrown it away.

Just like that.

They never talked about it.

One day, he saw his son pick up a piece of plastic on the beach and put it in a bin.

So: When your alarm clock rings tomorrow morning, you can choose how to feel about the state of the world.

We hope this Blink gave you some optimism.

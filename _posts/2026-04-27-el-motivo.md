**Author:** Patrick Lencioni | **Category:** Gestión y Liderazgo## ¿QUÉ ME APORTA ESTE TÍTULO? DESCIFRA LA CAUSA FUNDAMENTAL POR LA QUE LOS LÍDERES NO LOGRAN CUMPLIR SUS FUNCIONES PRINCIPALES

Shay Davis is the new CEO of Golden Gate Security. He has a hard job. Just six months after starting, people are saying he might be fired. The company is not in crisis, but it is falling behind its rivals, especially the big national company All-American Alarm. Meanwhile, Del Mar Alarm in San Diego is doing very well under Liam Alcott, a charming CEO from Britain. Wherever Shay goes, people talk about Liam’s company, whether at trade fairs or in business magazines. Del Mar is successful and shines in California, untouchable even by big national companies. Shay wants to catch up, so he contacts the consulting firm that helped Del Mar succeed. But instead of a consultant, he meets his biggest rival: Liam. This meeting shows Shay that true leadership is about why you lead. Liam says, “If you don’t understand your reason for leading, nothing else makes sense.” Although this story is not real, the reasons to lead that Patrick M. Lencioni writes about in The Motive are real. What are these reasons? What is the key to true leadership? In this Blink, you will learn which reasons for leading are right and which are wrong, and how to start using the right ones.

## EL MOTIVO EQUIVOCADO PARA LIDERAR

Imagine a young star who joins the National Football League. He likes the fame more than the hard work. He relaxes during practice and does not try his best in games. Because he wants the rewards, he works less hard and has less success. Now imagine a proud new father who thinks being a dad is just another trophy. He may play a board game sometimes or go to a school event if it suits him. But waking up at night to feed the baby or helping with homework? Not his style. The same thing happens in the boardrooms of big companies. You might think CEOs would act better, but many do not. Many leaders want power, money, status, and fun. They see leadership as a prize for themselves, not as a duty to help others. When leaders want only rewards, they ignore hard work that keeps the company strong. They treat their job like an amusement park ride, always looking for thrills and avoiding hard tasks. They forget that with great power comes great responsibility. The worst part is these leaders don’t often realize they are failing. They delegate their work or ignore problems they should solve. Some even brag about only wanting the fun parts. What happens? Their teams are lost, working hard but without guidance. Imagine working hard and getting silence from your leader. It is like a ship captain who is too busy sunbathing to steer. Employees wonder if their situation is real—and sadly, it is. Surprisingly, many smart CEOs follow this risky path. They see leadership as a golden ticket for personal pleasures. By chasing only rewards and avoiding hard work, they lose real leadership. Wonder if you have the wrong reasons to lead? Let’s look at some signs.

## SEÑALES DE QUE TU MOTIVO PARA LIDERAR ES ERRÓNEO

You can spot reward-focused leaders easily. They often miss one or all of these five main duties. First, building their own leadership team. Many CEOs talk about their teams but leave team building to HR because they think it is too emotional or less important than money or strategy. But ignoring real team work can hurt results. Ask yourself: do you think team-building is useless? Or do you plan team exercises that don’t include serious talks about improving? If yes, you may need to change your leadership direction. Second, managing their direct reports and making sure those reports manage others. Many leaders confuse management with tasks like performance reviews or salaries. Real management means guiding people, knowing their work direction, and helping fix problems. Some CEOs trust their teams and think they don’t need to manage them. But trust is not an excuse to ignore leadership. Also, they need to make sure their direct reports manage their teams well. Third, having hard talks about tough issues. Leading a team is not just strategy or numbers; it means dealing with problems like bad behavior or toxic politics. Everything from small annoyances to big office dramas must be handled clearly and kindly. Many leaders avoid these talks to protect their own feelings, which can cause bigger problems later. Fourth, running great team meetings. Meetings are often disliked but are where important decisions happen. Many leaders see meetings as boring tasks. But if meetings are poor, decisions will be weak. Also, leaders who accept bad meetings set a bad example. Fifth and last, communicating often and clearly with employees. Studies say workers need to hear important messages at least seven times to take them seriously. Some CEOs avoid repeating messages because they think people will get annoyed or they themselves are tired of repeating. But communication is about aligning goals and building emotional and behavioral connections. Without constant communication, strategies fail. Avoiding these five tasks hurts company performance and culture.

## EL MOTIVO ADECUADO PARA LIDERAR

Let’s go back to NFL stars. While many focus on rewards and enjoy fame, some choose another path. They do not relax with their success but think: “Now that I’ve made it, what will I do next?” Do you relate? This is not just football. It shows the spirit of true leadership. True leaders do not want only attention; they work hard in the background, serving and dedicating themselves. Think of your office. Some leaders want applause and praise. But a few lead quietly and strongly. They know real leadership is not about speeches or applause, but daily work, guiding teams, and helping talent grow. You may wonder how to lead this way. It is simple: change your view of the five key tasks you may have avoided. This is your plan. First, see team building not as a word but as a daily practice. Live it and breathe it. Next, coach your people. Understand each member’s goals, fears, and dreams. Guide and shape their path. Third, face hard talks. Alan Mulally at Ford had what he called “joyful accountability”—he pointed out failures kindly but firmly. This led to real change and respect. Fourth, make meetings useful, engaging, and focused on solutions. Lastly, master communication. Great leaders repeat their messages boldly. Gary Kelly of Southwest Airlines kept his message clear and memorable by saying it many ways until everyone understood it. But no leader is perfect. We all want approval or avoid tough tasks sometimes. True leadership means finding joy in responsibility and the journey more than the goal. The sooner you get this, the sooner you can be a true leader.

## ABOGA POR LA ADOPCIÓN DE UN LIDERAZGO BASADO EN LA RESPONSABILIDAD

Have you ever felt that world leaders have lost their way? Like they ride a train to benefit only themselves and leave others behind? You are not alone. Imagine being part of a team and respecting your boss, only to find out they don’t act unless it helps them. Many people accept this quietly. It is like watching a movie where the villain’s reasons are clear, but now you live it. From company boardrooms to city halls, the question “What can I gain?” is louder than “How can I serve better?” Now think about a future where all new leaders follow selfish giants. It is scary. A world where personal gain is more important than shared good, and leadership sinks in selfishness. We must stop this train, and you have a key role. Leadership is pure giving. It means putting others’ dreams and needs first, even when no one is watching you. That is where real magic happens. When leaders truly serve, companies grow, communities unite, and hope lives. So what can you do? If you are an employee, speak out when managers act selfishly. If you are a leader, check if your reasons match the common good. And as a citizen, demand more from those in power. Ask them to work for you, not themselves. Imagine a world where leadership is not just power but care. Where leaders act out of true passion for others. Companies would thrive, employees would wake motivated, and society would be full of hope and unity. Maybe then we wouldn’t even call it “servant leadership” because it would be normal. Would you like to live in this world? After all, a world that values the group's success over personal gain is what we all deserve. Let’s work together to make it real.

## RESUMEN FINAL

In this Blink about The Motive, you have learned that leading with purpose means putting service before yourself. True leaders choose hard work over show and dedication over pride. Their guide is not rewards but responsibility: build teams, coach people, have hard talks, run good meetings, and communicate often. This caring view is the heart of real change in leadership. If you are a leader, take this mindset and leave your ego behind. If you are not, challenge the norm and ask leaders to care more. You have the power to change the selfish system.

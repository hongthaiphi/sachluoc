### Synopsis

In his book *Kopfarbeit* (2022), Professor Peter Vajkoczy takes us into the operating room. He shows us the amazing world of the brain and how exact modern brain surgery is. We go to places where growths, defects, and weak blood vessels can threaten life. Every small measurement is important, and every move must be just right.

## What's Inside for You: Discover the Amazing World of Brain Surgery.

In *Kopfarbeit* (2022), we join a world between hope and danger. It's about very exact work and how fragile human life is. Brain surgery is both very beautiful and very scary at the same time. It is a field with many successes and sad moments. It can be bloody but also bring great joy. It is logical but also deeply about life itself. 

Vajkoczy shows how modern medicine works in high-tech operating rooms. Here, skilled teams use robots for imaging and tiny cameras for very precise work. They try to do things that seem impossible. But every operation also involves difficult choices about what is right. Even with all the technology, many things are unclear. Should doctors do everything they can? Why does the brain heal sometimes but not others? When does a doctor's duty end, and when does confidence become too much? 

## Blink 1 - The Brain: Beautiful, Risky, and How Doctors Make Hard Choices

Professor Peter Vajkoczy has seen brains thousands of times, but he is still amazed. When he looks at a human brain during surgery, it feels both familiar and mysterious. It is white, pink, and grey, with many small folds and tiny blood vessels. It looks very beautiful but is also very delicate. The brain and spinal cord together form a sensitive system. This system creates our feelings, our thoughts, and our awareness.

The brain grew over millions of years. It has about 100 billion nerve cells. These cells talk to each other through a very complex network. They control everything we do: moving, speaking, remembering, feeling, and thinking. When healthy, all brains look similar. But each one is unique, like a fingerprint.

Because each brain is different, working on an open head is a very intense experience. It needs total focus, patience, and respect. If a cut is too deep, or just one millimeter off, a memory, a movement, or a skill can be lost forever. Even with the best modern technology, the brain remains a mystery. Tools like microscopes, navigation systems, and imaging during surgery can help. But they can never fully predict what will happen next.

But brain surgery is more than just making very exact cuts. Doctors must make choices that can change someone's life forever. Should they operate even if it's very risky? Or should they wait and maybe miss a chance to help? The old rule "first, do no harm" is hard to follow here. Sometimes, doing nothing can be just as bad as a risky surgery.

Doctors need courage, responsibility, and a clear understanding of what is at stake. Those who operate on brains every day learn that mistakes happen. They also learn that new progress comes when people are brave enough to try new things. Vajkoczy calls this balance between exact work and trust, machines and humans, knowledge and wonder his motto: *Push limits. Take nothing for granted. Accept challenges.*

## Blink 2 - Maria Gilbert and the AVM: Her Life on a Thread

Maria Gilbert is 32 years old and a teacher. She was busy marking papers when a sharp, strong pain suddenly hit her head. It was worse than anything she had felt before. The pain started above her right ear and spread, making her sick. Her doctor sent her to the hospital, and then she went to another clinic by ambulance. The doctors found bleeding in her brain.

The problem was a brain defect called an AVM (arteriovenous malformation). This is rare. Usually, blood flows from arteries, through small blood vessels called capillaries, to veins. With an AVM, these small vessels are missing. So, arteries and veins connect directly. Blood then rushes through at high pressure. Over years, this forms a tight, twisted ball of blood vessels. It's like a ball of wool under great pressure. The walls of these vessels are always under stress. One day, they can break. This causes bleeding, which can often lead to death.

Maria Gilbert's AVM was deep in her right brain, near the part that controls speech. Several doctors said surgery was too risky. The AVM was too big, and too close to her speech area. They tried to close the faulty blood vessels in several sessions using a method called embolization. But "the monster," as Vajkoczy called it, stayed active. It kept getting blood from tiny vessels, grew larger, and threatened to burst again.

Maria Gilbert lived with the fear that the bleeding could happen again any day. In the end, she chose to have the operation instead of waiting.

The operation happened in a special operating room. This room had tools for X-rays, robots, and surgery all in one place. Her head was held still in a clamp. The doctors then opened her skull. They made a round opening, ten centimeters wide. This let them see the dura mater, the outer layer of the brain. It looked whitish, cloudy, and sensitive. Below it, the blue arachnoid membrane shimmered. And beneath that was the very thin pia mater, which lies close to the brain.

Then the real work began. Hour after hour, vessel by vessel. About two hundred blood vessels had to be sealed, clipped, or cut. The surgeon could not miss even one, or there would be new bleeding. The brain itself is very sensitive, and every vessel can be unpredictable. If a vessel wall stuck to the tweezers, blood would spray out. Maria Gilbert lost about five liters of blood during the surgery. This is almost all the blood a person has. Vajkoczy kept working carefully. He moved millimeter by millimeter through the small space between the tangle of vessels and the speech center. 

Finally, the tangled blood vessels were fully exposed. They were completely removed and sent for testing. When Maria woke up, she could move her arms and legs. But she could not speak. Not a sound, not a word. Days went by. Her CT scan showed no new bleeding and no stroke. Then, on the fourth day, she shaped her lips and said: "Olive." It was a strange word, and she didn't even mean to say it. When someone asked if she wanted a drink, she said "Olive" again.

This was a mystery for the doctors, but also a sign of hope. Her brain had started to make new connections. With patience and physical therapy, her speech came back. First, single words, then full sentences. Three months later, Maria Gilbert was teaching in a classroom again. 

## Blink 3 - Mark West and the Meningioma: A Risky Balance

Mark West is 52 years old. He is an experienced pilot and very healthy. He is used to being in charge. But then, he noticed something was wrong. When he looked up at the sky, he would stumble even if nothing was there. If he turned around, he would fall. When he jogged, everything looked blurry.

At first, he thought he was just tired or stressed. But after a simple tooth operation, the left side of his face stayed numb. He then knew it was something more serious. An MRI scan showed a meningioma. This is a type of tumor that grows from the membranes around the brain. These tumors usually grow slowly and are not cancerous. But they can push on the brain, move things, and cause very bad problems.

Mark West's tumor was deep at the bottom of his skull. It was behind his left ear, very close to the brainstem. This is a very sensitive area, as it controls breathing, heart rate, and balance. Vajkoczy knew it would be a difficult operation. The space to work was small, and the brain parts were delicate. In the operating room, everyone was very quiet and focused. On a screen, 3D pictures of the blood vessels glowed. Each line showed a path, and any mistake was a risk. The doctors opened the skull. They carefully cut the dura mater, the outer brain layer, until they could see the grey brain tissue. Then, they started to move with extreme precision. Under the microscope, Vajkoczy saw the tumor. It was pale, soft, and firmly attached. The main challenge was to take out the whole tumor without harming the nearby nerves.

During the whole operation, the team watched Mark's brain activity. Special monitors checked if signals reached the brain, if his face nerves reacted, or if his balance nerves sent messages. It was like flying an airplane in thick fog. You cannot see much, but you must know exactly where you are using your instruments.

The area around the nerves for the face, eye movement, hearing, and balance was especially important. One wrong move could leave his face crooked or take away his sense of balance.

Vajkoczy moved forward very slowly, millimeter by millimeter. He carefully peeled the tumor away from the brain tissue, following the natural lines. In the end, he removed the entire tumor. The brainstem was not harmed.

But the calm did not last. The next day, Mark West could not walk by himself. The MRI showed a small problem with blood flow in his brainstem. It was a small stroke, but it caused problems. He felt dizzy, numb, and his senses were mixed up. He went to rehab. The sound of water in the shower seemed very loud. A cup hitting a glass table sounded like a loud bang.

But the brain has an amazing ability called 'plasticity.' It can change, reorganize itself, and sometimes get back lost abilities. With patience, exercise, and balance training, Mark learned to walk safely, hear, and live normally again. A few months later, he was even able to work as a pilot again.

## Blink 4 - Julia Sachs and Awake Brain Surgery: Testing Speech

Awake brain surgery is one of the most amazing parts of modern brain surgery. It's hard to imagine being awake while doctors operate on your brain. For this surgery to work, everything must be planned perfectly. The patient needs to trust the doctors. And the whole team must know exactly what to do at every moment.

Julia Sachs was 57 years old when she got a brain tumor for the second time. This tumor was a fast-growing type called a diffuse astrocytoma. It was in the front part of her left brain, very near the area that controls speech. Doctors expected her to live only a few more years. Giving her full anesthesia was too risky. This is because they could not check her speech nerves during the operation. So, Professor Vajkoczy and Julia decided to do an awake surgery.

Doctors talked with Julia Sachs many times before the surgery. They wanted to make sure she would not panic when she woke up in the operating room. She knew who would be there. She also knew they would talk to her, show her pictures, and ask her to say words. While her skull was opened, she was asleep and felt no pain. They woke her up only when her brain was exposed. The bright lights, the unfamiliar voices, the sound of machines – it all felt strange, but she felt no pain. They showed her pictures of things like an apple, a car, or a house. Julia had to name them, make sentences, and answer questions.

At the same time, Vajkoczy gently touched her brain with a small electrical tool. If Julia hesitated on a word or said the wrong one, the team knew that area was important for her speech. Vajkoczy marked these spots with small, clean markers. This way, they made a 'speech map' during the surgery. This map showed which parts of her brain they must not touch.

The hard part was to remove as much of the tumor as possible without harming these important speech areas. Vajkoczy worked very slowly, millimeter by millimeter. He checked, reacted, and stopped if Julia struggled with words. Once, she whispered, "I know it... can't say it," looking very upset. He calmed her down, waited, and tested again. 

In the end, about 95% of the tumor was taken out. The rest was left to protect her ability to speak. Julia was put back to sleep. The doctors finished the surgery and closed her skull. Later, she had some seizures. But doctors managed them well with careful medicine. Even today, Julia Sachs is still alive, living longer than doctors expected.

## Blink 5 - Christoph Lukic and the Aneurysm: A Race Against Time

Christoph Lukic is 59 years old. He is an architect, fit, and well-organized. One day while jogging, he suddenly fainted. Doctors found an aneurysm in his main brain artery. This is a weak spot in a blood vessel that bulges out like a balloon. It happens when the vessel wall is weak and cannot hold the blood pressure. Imagine an old leather ball with a burst seam. The pressure inside pushes out the weakest part until it breaks.

His aneurysm had been treated twenty years ago. But now it was back. It was huge, more than five centimeters big, shaped like a quail egg. It was pushing on his brain and could burst at any time. Lukic chose to have another operation. This was a very difficult surgery, almost at the edge of what doctors can do.

Vajkoczy planned to 'trap' the aneurysm, which means cutting off its blood supply. He also planned to add a bypass to make sure enough blood still reached the brain. But what looked simple in the scans became very hard in the operating room. As soon as the skull was opened, the view was completely different. There was thick scar tissue and old clips from his first surgery. It was hard to see anything clearly. As they carefully exposed the area, a blood vessel tore. Blood sprayed out. Suction was used, and temporary clamps were quickly put in place. Every second counted.

So, a race against time began. The part of the brain that needed blood was no longer getting it. They had only twenty to thirty minutes before brain cells would die. Vajkoczy calmly and carefully sewed the first bypass. The clock was ticking. But then, it became clear that a second blood vessel ran through the aneurysm, and then a third. The scans had not shown this complex structure. No planned steps could help now. Only Vajkoczy's experience, instincts, and ability to change the plan in seconds mattered. Instead of one bypass, he made three. Three vessels, three stitches, three chances to get blood flowing.

Because the surgery would take many hours, Lukic was put into a deep, drug-induced sleep. His body temperature was lowered to 34 degrees Celsius to protect his brain. Only after all the new blood paths were made, the aneurysm was finally closed off. It pulsed one last time with his heartbeat. Then, it was opened and emptied.

Days later, when Lukic woke up, he could move his arms and legs. He understood what people said to him. Speaking was hard, but words slowly came back. The huge aneurysm was stopped, the danger was gone. This surgery was a mix of careful planning and quick changes. It was a win against something very hard to predict.

## Blink 6 - Beyond Exact Skill: About Craft and Character

In brain surgery, no two operations are ever the same. Each surgery is special, and every decision is final. Sometimes, only tiny distances, seconds, or one stitch can mean the difference between life and death. Doctors in this field always face the chance of failure. They also know they can never control everything.

Professor Vajkoczy talks about three kinds of problems in brain surgery. The first problem happens when doctors choose to operate when it would have been better not to. The second problem is when the body's biology is stronger than any skill. For example, blood vessels close, bleeding happens, or a stroke destroys what the doctor tried to save. The third kind of problem is the hardest one. This is when the doctor makes a mistake. It could be a wrong move, a careless action, or not having full control. Every surgeon will experience this.

The important thing is how doctors deal with these problems. It is their duty to study mistakes, talk about them openly, and not keep them secret. Only by talking about mistakes can they stop them from happening again. In this field, it's very important to be open about errors.

But not all decisions are clearly right or wrong. Sometimes, a patient must choose. They can have a surgery with an uncertain result. Or they can let their illness get worse. Is it worth having three months or two years more life, but at what cost? What is more important: how long you live, or how good your life is? Should you hold onto hope, or accept what is happening?

At these times, brain surgery becomes a test of what is morally right. Doctors must see more than just a sick body part. They need to understand the person, their past, and what they want. Some patients want to try every possible treatment. Others say: "I only want time, but good time." No numbers or rules can tell a doctor what to do in these choices.

Vajkoczy often talks about being humble. He means showing respect for the brain, for what medicine can and cannot do, and for things that happen by chance. Brain surgery means taking someone's life into your hands. But doctors must remember they cannot control everything. It needs strict work, clear thinking, bravery, and a willingness to always question yourself. In the end, it's not just about exact skills. It's about a doctor's character. 

## Conclusion

Brain surgery is a tough job with very high risks. But it is also a very human and deep subject. Every cut a surgeon makes makes them think about life. They think about what makes us human, what we are willing to risk, and what we want to save.

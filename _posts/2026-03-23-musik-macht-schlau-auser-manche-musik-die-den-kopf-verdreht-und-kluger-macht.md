### Synopsis

Music is a wonderful thing. It feeds our soul and makes us feel good. But it can do even more. Music can make you smart! In *Musik macht schlau – außer manche *(2025), you will learn about the links between music and brain health. Find out here how playing music can make you smarter and happier.

## What you will find: Valuable knowledge about how music makes our lives better and keeps our brain active.

"Life without music would be a mistake." These are famous words from the German philosopher Friedrich Nietzsche. Many people would probably agree with him. A life without music? It is hard to imagine. Most people agree that music makes them happy. But is it good for anything else? Science has some clear answers. Music, whether we make it or listen to it, helps and protects our brain!

The musician and writer Christoph Reuter has gathered some main ideas on this topic. He has also added interesting facts. We have turned these into a helpful and fun summary for you. ## Blink 1 - Magic in the Brain with Music: What is the truth about the Mozart Effect?

Mozart’s music bubbles like a glass of sparkling wine. The sounds are like a blue spring sky with fluffy clouds. If you really listen to it, you feel a tingle all over your body. It is like a warm sunbeam on your neck. In short, Mozart makes you happy. But does it also make you smarter?

In the 1990s, many people strongly believed this. They said that ten minutes of Mozart every day boosted thinking skills. It also made math easier to understand. This idea was called the *Mozart Effect*. It was named after the world-famous composer Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart. He was a child genius. People thought his music must still make listeners smarter 200 years later.

The *Mozart Effect* was talked about a lot in the media. It came from one study. A group of students listened to a Mozart sonata for two pianos. It was Köchel Verzeichnis 448 – a lively and graceful piece. Two pianists play very fast on the keys. 

Afterward, the students did tasks that needed good visual and spatial thinking. And guess what? The Mozart group did a little better. They did better than those who heard no music before the test. And so, the idea of the *Mozart Effect* began.

Today, we know that Mozart himself does not make you smart. It is about waking up your brain. Your brain gets active when it hears something that makes you alert, curious, or emotional. This could be a short story by Stephen King, an interesting radio show – and, of course, music. But it does not matter if it is Mozart or Coldplay. The main thing is that you like what you hear. The *Coldplay Effect*, that sounds pretty good too, right?

So, the idea that listening to music makes you smart quickly is not true. But if we look at the long-term effects, it is a different story. In a big study, music teacher Hans Günther Bastian gave children in a tough part of Berlin strong music lessons for years. They learned an instrument. They played in a group. They learned to listen to each other and practice on their own. At first, there were few differences. But over time, things changed. After some years, these children showed much better thinking skills and did better in school. Most importantly, they became stronger socially. They were less likely to leave others out. They felt more part of a group and believed in themselves more. Music became a place where they could show who they were. They learned to be part of something bigger.

So, music does not make you smart in a flash. Its magic works over time. By the way, if you have wondered if it helps to listen to background music while you work or learn words, the answer is "yes, but." Studies suggest that music helps you focus. But it should be calm and quiet. It should not have words or be stopped by ads. Here too, it is important to keep doing it for a long time. Do not expect miracles. Music can boost your brain – but you still have to do the learning yourself.

## Blink 2 - Fitness for the Brain Cells: How Music Trains the Brain

If you could see what happens in your head when you listen to music, you would be amazed. An MRI scan clearly shows how whole parts of your brain light up like fireworks when you hear music. Music makes your brain move – that is its secret.

Luckily, your brain is not like a hard drive that gets full. It is a network of nerves that gets denser the more you use it. Simply put, the more you challenge your brain, the better it works. It is like a muscle that grows when you go to the gym.

Imagine your brain like a snowy landscape. Every thought, every experience leaves marks in the snow. The more you walk, the deeper the paths become. Over time, real roads appear. Music builds many of these roads. This is because it makes you listen, feel, move, remember, and pay attention all at once. That is why it changes the brain for good.

But do you know what is even more powerful than listening to music? *Making* music. And before you think you need to master a hard violin piece – no. Just singing is enough. This act alone is complex enough to really get your brain working.

This mix of feeling, movement, and sound helps both big and small brains truly grow. Researchers have found that children who sing have better movement skills and higher brain power. Another nice extra? Adults and children who regularly make music spend less time on their smartphones. Why is this important? Because we now know that hours of scrolling and swiping can harm the brain. 

So, think of it this way: Even if you play the piano badly, the 30 minutes you practice daily are still valuable time. You are not spending that time looking at a screen. That alone is a huge benefit.

But making music can do even more. It teaches us something that is becoming rarer in our world: self-control. When you practice a piece, you keep going even when it gets hard. You learn to focus, not to get sidetracked, and not to lose sight of a goal. And this skill? It helps far beyond music. Studies show that people with this inner control live healthier, are happier, and deal with hard times better.

So, music does not just train your memory – it shapes your inner guide.

All good news then! But it gets even better. We can use the good effects of music on our brain even when we are old:

Studies have shown that making music helps prevent diseases like dementia. It keeps the brain active and strong. Even if someone has advanced dementia, music greatly improves their quality of life. 

From care homes, there are many touching stories of dementia patients. Someone might sit there, perhaps forgetting their middle name. They might not know who the current Prime Minister is. But then someone plays a song – a favorite song from their youth, long forgotten. And suddenly, this person comes alive. Their eyes light up. Their feet tap to the beat. The person moves to the music, feels the tunes, the rhythm – and takes part in life again.

Did you know that even people with advanced dementia can learn new songs? They can even remember the words. It is true: We can learn our whole lives. Music plays a very special part in this. It reaches the deepest parts of our mind. It always manages to activate the brain areas that control feelings and joy in life.

All these are good reasons to make music a regular part of our daily lives. What are you waiting for? Strum your guitar a little, sing a song in the shower, or hum a tune while doing the dishes. This will not only boost your brain health but also lift your spirits.

## Blink 3 - Lullabies and Flute Tunes: Music for Newborn Brains

There is an audience that cannot be bought. They do not clap. They do not write comments. They do not politely say, "That was nice." And yet they react right away. We are talking about babies, specifically babies born too early. 

These premature babies are suddenly brought from the warm comfort of their mother's womb into a bright, loud world. And they are not really ready for it. For these tiny people, it is a real shock. Alarms, busyness, bright lights, and many voices are like a constant storm of stress for their small, not-yet-ready brains.

This is where music comes in. The harpist and composer Andreas Vollenweider tried something in a Geneva hospital. He played music composed just for premature babies. The effect was amazing. The babies' heartbeats slowed down. They stopped crying and visibly relaxed. 

Of all the instruments Vollenweider tried, one was especially popular with the young audience: the snake charmer's flute. Its strong, warm sound seemed to go straight to the nervous system and calm it. So, Vollenweider created a routine. The premature babies in Geneva regularly heard short flute music. When they were falling asleep. When they woke up. These are moments when the world can be very overwhelming for such a small, fragile being. Hearing the same music repeatedly seemed to make the calming effect even stronger. This is because our brain needs patterns to follow. When something happens again and again, it creates a feeling of familiarity. And familiarity is like a safety belt for babies. It helps them learn and grow in a relaxed way.

Later in life, premature babies often have more trouble with focus and controlling their emotions. One idea is that their brain has trouble sorting out signals because of the early trauma. What is important? What is harmless? What means danger? Here, too, studies showed something. Playing the same music to premature babies again and again helps prevent these problems later. Listening to music strengthens weaker brain parts and builds new connections. This is not because music automatically "makes you smart." It is because it helps the brain mature. It trains the ability to spot patterns and make sense of chaos.

And you can take away something very useful from this – whether you are dealing with premature babies or young children. Make sound a regular routine. Sing the same simple songs again and again. Use repeating tunes to help calm down. Create calm sound spaces in a busy day. Does it sound simple? It is. But the effect is huge. Babies react right away – they calm down, or they start to move, as if their body is saying, "Ah, I know this." Music then becomes a tool for basic trust. Just like the good old lullaby. An ancient, but brilliant idea.

## Blink 4 - Healing Sounds: Music in Therapy

At first, it might sound a bit like magic to say: Music can heal. It might remind you of drumming circles. But music is actually a part of modern medicine now. It cannot fix broken bones or broken hearts. But music can build bridges. It connects the body and mind. It connects the therapist and patient. It connects what is no longer possible with what can be possible again.

Music therapy can truly change lives for people recovering from brain issues. Imagine you have had a stroke. Suddenly, you must learn basic things again – how to grasp, speak, or move. Everything that was easy before now feels impossible. And this is where music helps. It is easier for patients to lift a hand if a melody goes up at the same time. It is easier to put one foot in front of the other if there is a beat. Hearing and moving connect. New nerve links are built, and fine motor skills are trained. 

By the way, it is also a good idea to learn an instrument after an accident or stroke. Music doctor Eckart Altenmüller showed that even a few minutes of piano playing connect the hearing and movement centers in the brain. If that is not a good reason to start playing the piano even when you are older!

And then there is something we all know: Music makes you want to keep going. Music makes you motivated to stick with something. It does not matter if it is something simple like cleaning your apartment or something very important like learning to walk.

Music not only helps with coordination, but also with speech. Many people cannot speak after a stroke. Not a single word. But – and now hold on tight – they can sing. Sounds like a small miracle, right? It is. But there is a scientific reason for it. We are all born with two speech centers – one in each half of the brain. Over time, however, the brain gets used to using only the left side for speech. We still use the right side for other things, including music. If the left side of the brain is damaged, the unused speech center in the right side can be activated again – through singing. Patients have to learn to sing individual words and sentences until they can speak them again. This way, they literally sing their way back to normal. 

Music is also very important for mental health therapy. It gives us a special way to show feelings that we do not have words for yet. Or to get back in touch with our emotions at all. You probably know this feeling: You hear certain music, and suddenly anger, sadness, or longing comes up – or, of course, joy. You can even react physically, like your hair standing up, getting goosebumps, or tears coming to your eyes. Music helps you break free from being stuck inside. This can be very helpful in therapy. 

Music is also used in pain therapy. It works like this: When you listen to music you like, your body releases its own pain relievers. Your heart rate goes down, and so does your blood pressure. Your nervous system calms down. At the same time, music takes your attention away from the pain and towards something good. It activates the reward system in the brain. 

Music also makes people feel closer. When two people make music together, their brain waves have been shown to match up. This means they are truly "on the same wavelength." This shared rhythm makes relationships stronger, builds empathy, and is a powerful way to fight loneliness.

You do not have to be sick to use the good effects of music. Sing, dance, or listen to a song that truly moves you. It costs nothing, has no bad side effects, and your heart and brain will thank you.

## Blink 5 - A Final Tip: Good Mood with the Ukulele

Sometimes, just three minutes of music can make the world a little brighter. In 2010, a cover version of "Somewhere over the rainbow" became famous worldwide. It was sung by Israel Kamakawiwo’ole, also called *Iz. *Everyone loved the huge Hawaiian man and his tiny ukulele. Without a big show, he simply stood there and sang. His voice was like a gentle hug. He sang the same words that Hollywood star Judy Garland had made famous: *Somewhere over the rainbow, skies are blue. And the dreams that I dare to dream, really do come true. *

This is what hope and love for life sound like. No wonder a "ukulele fever" started after this world hit by Israel Kamakawiwo’ole. (He had actually died several years before the song became famous.) Parks were full of people playing ukuleles on picnic blankets. Even TV host Stefan Raab performed with it. Today, serious artists like singer Billie Eilish also use it.

The ukulele did not actually come from Hawaii. It came from the island of Madeira. Immigrants who worked in the sugarcane fields in Hawaii brought it with them. But the Hawaiians named it *Ukulele*, which means "jumping flea." This is very fitting when you think how tiny this friendly little instrument is. And how fast players' fingers dance over its strings. 

That is why the ukulele is also a perfect musical friend for everyday life. It is small, light, not expensive, and not hard to play. Its four strings are not a big challenge even for people new to music. With just a little practice and some YouTube videos, anyone can play a few simple chords and sing along. 

So, if you do not want to pick up a recorder, try the ukulele. Just two fingers are enough to play a chord. You do not have to practice for hours. You can start right away and enjoy the good effects of making music. So, be brave and try it! You will quickly see that music not only makes you smart but, most importantly, makes you happy.

## Conclusion

That was our summary of Christoph Reuter's book *Musik macht schlau – außer manche*. 

And if you have listened this far, you might now be asking: Wait a minute – what does "except some" mean?

Good question. This subtitle is a little hint. Even though the benefits of music for our brain are amazing, professional musicians are not always the smartest people. They lose their car keys just like everyone else. They also do not automatically know the capital of Nicaragua. And sometimes? Sometimes really silly things happen to them.

Take Rihanna: Ed Sheeran wrote the song *Shape of You *just for her. But for some unknown reason, she did not want it. So Ed sang it himself – and it became a global hit. Or Leonard Cohen, who as a young man accidentally gave away the rights to his classic song *Hallelujah*. Is that just bad luck for an artist? Or is it artist foolishness?

However, it is clear that from a scientific point of view, music has the power to make our brains work better. And, perhaps even more importantly, it can make our minds stronger and calmer. To benefit from this, you do not have to be a professional. You do not have to practice for hours. Just start – no matter how old you are or where you are in life. A little singing, a little dancing, or strumming on a keyboard makes life better, at any time of day or year. So, what are you waiting for?

### Synopsis

*The Innovative Leader *(2024) looks at how top leaders learn to innovate all the time. They don't just have one good idea. This book is based on talks with 50 innovative leaders. It also uses many years of experience. It gives clear steps to help you innovate. This can be in a business, government, or charity.

## What’s in it for me? Learn to lead well when there are no clear answers, and do well even when things always change.

You cannot innovate and succeed with old leadership ideas. These ideas were made for steady markets that don't exist anymore. Customers change their minds quickly. New competitors appear from other areas. In these times, old ways of leading do not work. Leadership itself must change.

Good leaders have six special qualities. They stay connected to new trends. They show others how to try new things. They always improve their own ways of leading. They set very bold goals. They listen to ideas from all sides. And they help others succeed. These are not things you are born with. They are skills you can learn.

This summary shows the three steps of innovative leadership: aiming high, building support, and growing a culture. These steps can change how you lead and how your organization works. The change won't be an order from the top. It will happen naturally as new ways of working spread to everyone.

## Blink 1 - CREATE your leadership style

Today, you face leadership problems that didn't exist years ago. The ways that helped companies succeed for decades now fail quickly. Customers change what they like even before your plans are ready. New competitors come from areas you never thought about. Old leadership ideas focus on stability, planning, and slow growth. These ideas will leave you behind.

This is not about working harder or faster. The basic idea of leadership itself must change. You need to learn new skills. These skills will help you deal with constant uncertainty. They will also help you inspire others to do the same.

Think about Reed Hastings. He changed Netflix from a DVD rental service into a huge streaming company. Then he made it a big producer of movies and shows. He didn't just make the old business better. He always thought about what the company could become. Even if it meant changing his own successful business.

Or look at Satya Nadella. He changed Microsoft. He took over a company that was stuck in old ways. He helped different teams work together. Before, these teams had competed strongly against each other.

These leaders show six linked qualities that help them innovate. They stay *connected* to new trends in many industries. They are *role models*. They show the experimental mindset they want from their teams. They keep *evolving*. They see their own leadership as something that must always adapt. They set *audacious* goals. These goals go far beyond small improvements. They use *three-sixty thinking*. This means they consider ideas from all sides. And they are *enablers*. They remove problems instead of creating them.

These qualities work together. They create a leadership style that uses change instead of fearing it. You will see changes before they are clear. You will encourage trying new things by showing you are willing to try. You will help your team take smart risks. You might not become a genius who has all the answers. But you will learn the mindset and ways of working that help you lead well when no one has the answers yet.

Start to develop these qualities by looking at how you lead this week. Notice when you try to control things instead of helping others. Pay attention to if you are really listening to many ideas or just finding reasons for what you already think.

Ask yourself if your goals truly push limits. Or if they just make small ideas sound big. This honest self-check builds the base for everything that follows. Leading with innovation starts with your choice to lead differently.

## Blink 2 - Define your vision

The six qualities of innovative leadership only work when you use them. This happens through three clear steps that build on each other. Think of them as the ABCs of innovative leadership.

First, you *Aspire*. This means you create a strong vision for innovation. Second, you *Build* the right conditions for innovation to grow. And third, you *Cultivate* these skills. You keep working on them until they become a natural part of how you and your team work. Each step needs different actions. But all of them rely on your promise to lead in a new way.

Aspiration starts with connected thinking. You cannot think of new possibilities if you only look at your own industry. When Steve Jobs made the iPhone, he got ideas from art classes, fancy shops, and electronics. These things seemed unrelated. But they came together to make something revolutionary.

Actively look for trends and ideas in unexpected places. Read about different fields. Talk to people in other industries. See how other areas solve problems that look different from yours. They might have similar basic ideas, or meet similar customer needs.

This broad thinking must link to setting very bold goals. Jeff Bezos didn't just want to build a better bookstore. He imagined creating "the everything store." This goal was so big, many people thought it was crazy. Bold goals make you and your team think beyond just making things a little better. They need real innovation. Small improvements will never reach the big dream. Your vision should make you a little uncomfortable. If you already know exactly how to reach it, you haven't aimed high enough.

Being a role model is important even at this vision stage. When Indra Nooyi led PepsiCo to make healthier products, she didn't just announce the plan. She openly talked about how her own ideas about healthy food and company duty were changing. She showed that aiming high means questioning your own thoughts. Your team watches how you deal with uncertainty about the future. They notice if you present your vision as a fixed truth or as a big goal you are ready to change as you learn.

Think about your own innovation goals. Write down one bold goal that truly pushes your thinking. Then find three industries or fields completely outside your own. You can look there for new ideas. Plan time to explore these areas. Do it like real research, not just quick looking. Your goals become stronger when you mix bold dreams with a broad view.

## Blink 3 - Build the right conditions

A big dream without a plan to make it happen is just a fantasy. The second step of innovative leadership asks you to build the real conditions that help innovation grow in your company. This means changing how you lead. It means helping your team in clear ways. And it means using three-sixty thinking to create systems that support trying new things, instead of punishing it.

Changing your leadership style starts with knowing that different stages of innovation need different ways of leading. When your team explores new ideas, they need freedom and to feel safe to speak. When they test new products, they need quick feedback and resources. When they make successful innovations bigger, they need structure and clear plans. 

Many leaders use the same style no matter the situation. You might control too much when people need freedom to explore. Or you might give too little guidance when people need clear instructions to make things bigger. Change your leadership to fit what the work *really needs* at each step.

Alan Mulally changed Ford during the money crisis. He created a system where managers used color-coded reports in weekly meetings. Red meant problems, yellow meant worries, green meant things were on track. At first, all reports were green, even when the company was losing a lot of money.

Mulally made it safe to show red. He praised the first manager brave enough to admit problems. Within weeks, the reports showed the truth. This was because he had created a place where honesty helped solve problems, instead of causing trouble.

This kind of support is more than just cheering. You must actively remove things that stop innovation. Find the long, slow rules that slow down new experiments. Ask if your approval steps really help, or if they just cause delays. Look at your budget. See if money goes to safe bets or to real new ideas. When someone on your team says they can't move forward, find out if the problem is real. Or if it's just old ways of thinking that no one has questioned.

Three-sixty thinking is very important here. You need to think about how your innovation efforts affect everyone involved. Your team needs support. But the finance department needs to manage money well. Customers want better products. But operations needs things to be stable. Innovation that ignores these different needs will cause problems. These problems will eventually stop progress. Build ways to get real input from many points of view before you make big changes.

This week, start building innovation. Check your current systems. List three things that slow down innovation in your team. Find one way you can clearly celebrate smart failures, instead of just rewarding success. These clear actions will show that you are building a place where new ideas and ways of working can grow.

## Blink 4 - Cultivate sustainable innovation

The third step of innovative leadership deals with your biggest challenge. This is making innovation last, not just a one-time thing. Growing this culture means making the six qualities a part of your daily leadership. They become automatic, not forced. This needs you to always be a role model, to stay connected to new changes, and to help yourself and your team keep evolving.

Being a role model becomes even more important over time. Your team watches if you stay committed when innovation gets hard. When experiments fail, do you go back to safe ways or do you learn from them? When the market changes, do you hold onto your first idea or do you change smartly? How you act in these moments teaches more powerfully than any speech about innovation values.

Sara Blakely built Spanx into a billion-dollar company. She kept a culture of trying new things by often sharing her own failures. She started a tradition where team members talked about failures at dinner. They celebrated what they learned, not the result. This was not a single class. It became a regular habit. This kept the team focused on taking smart risks, even as the company grew and had more to lose from mistakes.

Staying connected needs real effort as your company succeeds. Success makes you keep doing what you are already doing. This makes it harder to see when things around you change. You need systems that make you look beyond your immediate work.

So, plan regular meetings with people outside your industry. Watch new technologies, even if they seem unrelated to your business now. Pay attention to how customers behave in other markets. These actions might feel like extra work when you are busy. But they stop you from becoming blind and missing important changes.

Helping others must also change as your innovation culture grows. At first, you remove clear problems. You create a basic sense of safety for trying new things. Later, you need to deal with less obvious problems. Do your promotion rules reward innovation or favor those who play it safe? Do your planning times allow real changes, or do they lock teams into strict plans? These deeper issues decide if innovation becomes a part of the whole company. Or if it only happens when you personally get involved.

Start one culture-growing practice this week. Choose a regular meeting where you can openly share what you learned from failures. Find a new trend outside your industry that you will follow each month. These small, steady actions will build up to real culture change.

## Blink 5 - Innovation as culture

A big change in culture doesn't happen with announcements or workshops. It happens when enough people in your company learn new ways of working. Then they start spreading these ways naturally. Your job as an innovative leader is not just about how well you lead. It's also about helping other leaders grow. They can then spread these new ways across the whole company.

This spread needs you to be purposeful in how you help new leaders grow. When you guide someone, don't just teach them *what* to think. Instead, show them *how* to think. Show them how you gather many different ideas before making choices. Guide them through your process for setting bold goals. These goals should balance big dreams with clear steps.

Let them watch how you deal with failed experiments. Do it with curiosity, not disappointment. Make your thinking clear. This way, they can learn the patterns, not just copy the results.

When Sheryl Sandberg joined Facebook, Mark Zuckerberg didn't just give her tasks. He included her in big discussions. She could see how he solved problems. He made his thinking clear. He showed how he linked ideas from different areas and managed different needs. This helped her learn to think like an innovative leader. She didn't just follow his orders.

Changes in culture also need you to find and help innovation champions at all levels. These are people who naturally try new things. They are not always the ones with the highest job titles. They link ideas across different groups. They help others take smart risks.

Give them visibility and resources. Create chances for them to share their methods with co-workers. And protect them when their experiments don't work. These champions will become the key people who spread new ways of working throughout your company.

You also need systems that make innovation everyone's job. It shouldn't be a special task for a few. When teams across your company think broadly, act as role models, improve their methods, set bold goals, consider many ideas, and help each other – innovation becomes a normal part of work. It is not a separate project that competes with "real work." This happens when your systems regularly reward these behaviors. Not just when top leaders pay attention.

Start by finding three possible innovation champions in your own company. Plan time to understand their problems. Find out what help would make them succeed. Then create one chance for them to share their new ways of working with more people. Changing culture starts with these clear actions of support. Repeat them often until new ways of working become normal and spread on their own.

## Final summary

In this summary of *The Innovative Leader* by Stephen Wunker, Jennifer Luo Law, and Hari Nair, you’ve learned that good leadership needs six linked qualities. These are: staying connected to new trends, showing others how to try new things, always improving your approach, setting bold goals, thinking from all sides, and helping others.

You learn these qualities through three steps – your ABC. First, aim high by creating a strong vision. Do this by thinking broadly and setting bold goals. Then, build the right conditions for innovation. This means changing your leadership style, removing problems, and thinking about everyone involved. And third, grow lasting practices. Do this by always being a role model, keeping broad connections, and helping other innovation champions. These champions will spread new ways of working throughout your company.

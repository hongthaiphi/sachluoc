**Author:** Kim Scott | **Category:** Communication Skills## WHAT WILL THIS TITLE GIVE ME? LEARN TO BECOME A RADICALLY HONEST COLLEAGUE.

"Hello, do you have a minute?

" Actually, Kim Scott did not have one.

She had just stepped out of the elevator at Juice Software’s headquarters, where she was the general manager.

That morning, she felt stressed.

She had to make an important decision about prices and had to do it now.

But the employee who came to ask if she had a minute looked very worried, and with good reason.

It turned out he had a serious health problem that was affecting him a lot.

They ended up talking for an hour, and Kim calmed him down by telling him it was okay to take time off work to focus on his health.

Okay, now!

If she rushed back to her desk, maybe she could make the price decision on time.

But, surprise, surprise, she soon met a second colleague with another urgent issue.

After a morning full of "distractions," Kim was tired.

She called Leslie Koch, her executive coach, to ask for advice: "Am I a CEO or a kind of emotional nanny?"

she asked.

Leslie answered: "This is not childcare, it is management, and it is your JOB!"

.

Those words have guided Kim in her career since then.

Because what she did that day was exactly what she was supposed to do: build relationships with the people she led, show them she cared about them not only as employees but as human beings.

Those talks were not distractions, they were key to her work.

Relationships are essential to succeed as a leader.

It does not matter if you are a CEO or a new manager, your job is to create a culture of guidance, build a great team, and get the best results possible.

If you think you can do those three things without good relationships, you are fooling yourself.

To build strong relationships, you need to earn trust with your colleagues.

But trust is not something you get by snapping your fingers and voilà!

Actually, excuse the expression, you need to care about the people who work for you.

You need to care about them personally.

But you also need to be honest with your colleagues about what you really think, which often means challenging them directly.

These two parts — caring personally and challenging directly — are the heart of radical honesty.

It is radical because it is key to being a good colleague, whether you are the boss, a teammate, or an employee.

It is radical because it is rare to care and challenge at the same time.

It is honest because you will communicate clearly and humbly.

In this summary of Kim Scott’s book, Radical Honesty, we will show you why this idea should guide everything you do as a leader, a colleague, and an employee.

Radical honesty is not only for bosses!

In the end, you will know how to use it to strengthen your work relationships, guide your team well, motivate them to do their best, and, of course, get things done.

Do you want to learn how honesty can help you be more productive at work?

Then let’s start.

## THE TWO PARTS OF RADICAL HONESTY

To begin, let’s look closely at the first part of radical honesty: caring personally.

Of course, you care about the people you work with.

But listening to someone share what they are really going through, or even sharing some of your own weaknesses?

Isn’t that too much?

Not professional.

Actually, it is one of the best things you can do if you want to create a place where people feel safe and cared for.

Again, think of Kim Scott when she heard from the coworker worried about his health crisis.

She listened.

She gave him her time.

She showed him that his feelings mattered and that being part of a great team means your coworkers support you when you need to deal with something outside of work.

If you create a place where people feel they can be open, they will feel safe.

And this will create trust.

It is important not to let your caring stop you from telling people what they need to know to succeed, which takes us to the second part of radical honesty: challenging directly.

Early in her career at Google, Kim gave a presentation to the CEO and the company founders.

She was understandably nervous, but it went well — or so she thought.

Afterward, her boss invited her to walk with him to their next meeting.

Her boss gave her some honest, specific praise, but also pointed out one thing: Kim had said "um" many times.

Though this feedback hurt a little at the time, Kim knew her boss cared for her and was grateful for the offer to help her with her presentation skills.

So, if you see a team member can improve, challenge them.

Be kind, encouraging, caring, and direct; don’t beat around the bush.

When employees see you truly want to help them grow and you are willing to challenge them for that growth, they will trust you more than if you held back.

Now that you know more about the two parts of radical honesty and why they matter for your relationships with colleagues, let’s talk about another relationship: the one with yourself.

Popular wisdom says before you can really love others, you have to love yourself.

Well, the same goes for work relationships: if you want to care about others, you first have to care about yourself.

What does that mean?

Mostly, you need to find a way to keep your balance, especially in stressful times.

You know best what you need to stay calm.

What works for one person may not work for another.

Kim, for example, makes sure she sleeps eight hours every night and spends time exercising and eating regularly with loved ones.

That is her recipe.

What is yours?

These kinds of routines may seem small but can help your overall well-being.

Try to keep them and make time in your schedule for things that matter to you.

If you care for yourself this way, you can also care for others.

And you will become a role model for your team.

This is important because, as you will see, guiding others is a key part of being a radically honest colleague.

## THREE BEHAVIORS TO AVOID AS A RADICALLY HONEST COLLEAGUE.

Let’s talk again about Kim’s experience with her boss.

It is a great example of how a manager can make someone feel valued and also give the guidance they need.

You don’t have to choose between caring and challenging; you can do both at the same time.

Kim’s boss focused on the positive: she praised her with sincere and specific words, without using the "feedback sandwich" method.

But she didn’t avoid pointing out a problem that could stop Kim from moving forward in her career.

And she did it with real kindness.

This two-minute meeting — yes, it lasted only two minutes — changed Kim’s view on how to guide her own team.

How did her boss do it?

She used the two principles of radical honesty: care personally and challenge directly.

But she also avoided three wrong behaviors that are easy to fall into when those principles are not used or are misunderstood: mean aggression (®), ruined empathy (®), and fake insincerity (®).

Let’s think about that for a moment.

Maybe you are already protesting: "I’m not a controlling manager! Ruined empathy..."

...

That does not sound like me."

Remember, it’s not about who you are but how you act.

Separating those two things will help you see where you can improve and help you grow in how you give feedback at work and in your personal life.

Let’s look closer at those three behaviors to understand how to avoid them.

First, mean aggression.

This happens when someone challenges directly but forgets to show they care.

You may have had a colleague who criticized you harshly or used criticism to show power instead of help you grow.

You didn’t feel they cared about you at all.

That person acted with mean aggression.

Brutal honesty is mean aggression, not radical honesty.

The problem is that people often confuse the two.

Worse, some people prefer someone who seems like a "competent jerk," who is unpleasant but at least not "nice but incompetent."

That is a false choice.

The point is you don’t have to be unpleasant to show you are good at your job.

You can be direct and kind, like Kim’s boss with the "um" story.

But be careful: don’t let your kindness stop you from giving criticism.

Because then you will fall into ruined empathy.

This is when someone shows they care personally but won’t challenge directly.

We often do this because we do not want to hurt someone’s feelings, so we don’t say something they should know.

You don’t want to risk your relationship by offending them with criticism.

You want to avoid conflict and discomfort.

But this is dangerous, both for your team’s work and your relationships.

If you don’t give honest feedback, their work will suffer, and you will damage your relationship over time.

Fake insincerity, the worst behavior, happens when someone neither cares nor challenges.

On the outside, it looks like ruined empathy: you don’t tell people what you really think and keep criticisms to yourself.

But you do this not because you care about their feelings, but because you don’t care enough.

You manipulate to look good or because you care more about your reputation than what is best for them.

If mean aggression is "stabbing in front," fake insincerity is stabbing in the back.

This is not a good way to build trust.

Now that you know what to avoid, how can you give good (radically honest) guidance to your team?

We will see that in the next section.

## HOW TO GIVE GUIDANCE WITH FULL HONESTY.

At Google, Kim once saw a discussion between Larry Page, Google’s cofounder, and software engineer Matt Cutts.

They argued about a policy and the talk got heated.

Matt, normally calm, began to shout.

Kim worried Larry would get angry, but instead, he smiled big.

Why?

Because he liked to be challenged.

To him, it showed that at Google, employees felt comfortable enough to criticize their bosses and even the company founders.

This is the core of radically honest guidance: a culture where leaders encourage people to give them honest opinions.

As a result, everyone feels safe to say what they really think.

Asking for and giving both praise and criticism is often called "feedback," but Kim likes to call it "guidance."

Feedback sounds loud and makes us want to cover our ears.

But guidance, both praise and criticism, is what most of us want.

So, how can you build this culture where people know how to ask for criticism and feel responsible to give specific, honest, kind, and clear praise and criticism?

As a manager, you must lead by example.

Start asking for criticism by talking openly about what you want to improve.

Tell your employees, "These are the things I want to get better at. Can you help me with that?"

When they do, be sure to thank them sincerely.

This invitation and your positive response will make it easier for people to tell you when you are wrong.

This will help you grow as a leader.

It will also encourage people around you to reflect on their own growth areas and be open to receiving guidance.

When you ask for criticism, have a regular question ready like: "What could I do or stop doing to make it easier to work with me?"

Most people won’t answer right away, so be ready to stay quiet until they think of something.

Count slowly to six in your head.

When someone answers, listen carefully, ready to respond.

To handle feeling defensive, make sure you understand their point well.

Ask follow-up questions or share a story about something you did that relates to their feedback.

Finally, do more than just thank them.

If you agree with the criticism, fix the problem.

If you don’t agree, have a respectful talk about your reasons.

When guiding your team, start with the positives.

Be sure to praise more than criticize.

And with both praise and criticism, be HHIIPP.

Be humble.

Show your intention to help.

Give guidance right away, don’t save it for a private meeting or the performance review.

If possible, have the talk in person.

If you work remotely, do it live by phone or video call, not by text, email, Slack, etc.

Praise in public.

Criticize in private.

Don’t praise or criticize personality.

This last point is very important.

Use the "Context-Observation-Result-Next Steps" frame: "In the meeting (context), when you said 'um' every three words (observation), it seemed unprofessional (result). I can connect you with a presentation coach who can help (next step)." 

This helps you focus on behavior instead of jumping to conclusions about the person or making a fundamental attribution error.

Remember, these rules apply to both praise and criticism.

Kim’s boss praised her by saying: "In the meeting, when you presented both sides of the argument, you gained credibility," and criticized by saying: "In the meeting, when you said 'eh' every three words, you seemed unprepared."

She did not say "Kim, you are a genius" or "Kim, you are dumb."

It is important to consider your employees’ preferences.

Public praise may motivate one person, while another may be shy and want to avoid attention.

Keeping people motivated and knowing their preferences is part of your job.

That is why it is so important to get to know the people you work with.

We will look at this more in the next chapter.

## HOW TO FIND OUT WHAT MOTIVATES YOUR WORKMATES.

Here is a story about Sir Christopher Wren, the 17th-century architect who led the rebuilding of St. Paul’s Cathedral in London.

One day, while walking the site, he met three bricklayers building a wall.

He asked them what they were doing.

The first replied: "I’m working."

The second said he was building a wall.

But the third bricklayer gave a surprising answer: "I am building a cathedral for the Almighty."

This story is often used to tell leaders to "give purpose" to their employees.

But for Kim, the story shows something else: people can do the same work but find meaning in different ways.

A manager’s job is not to give purpose.

A manager’s job is to understand what motivates each person.

The manager’s task is to see the differences and make sure there is a place where everyone can find their own meaning.

Radical honesty (®) applies here too.

As a manager, you must care enough about each employee to understand what motivates them; it is not your job to tell them what gives meaning to their lives.

That would be a big mistake.

Sometimes people do great work because they want to put food on the table and pay rent, not because they feel "passion" for their job.

A good boss will recognize the dignity of that motivation to work.

As a boss, your job is to know each person well enough to give them the right jobs and help them take steps toward their dreams.

To do this well, you need to know when people are in superstar mode and when they are in rockstar mode.

When people are in superstar mode, they need challenges and encouragement to grow fast so they can reach their goals.

It helps to know that superstar mode sometimes looks to a manager like a shooting star: the person may grow out of their role faster than you want.

When people are in rockstar mode, like "solid as a rock," they are a stable source for the team.

They are great at their work but not necessarily looking for a promotion.

They will keep doing great work as long as you respect their choice not to want the next position or, even worse, don’t promote them if they are not ready or don’t want a promotion.

To balance growth and stability, you need rockstars and superstars on your team.

And you need to know each person well enough to see when they switch between modes.

So let’s get practical: how can you find out what motivates each person on your team and what mode they are in now?

## HOW TO HELP YOUR EMPLOYEES TAKE A STEP TOWARD THEIR DREAMS.

Russ Laraway, who worked for Kim at Google, once sat down with Sarah, one of his employees.

When he asked about her goals, she hesitated at first, then said she hoped one day to be a boss like Russ.

But Russ sensed Sarah wasn’t totally honest, so he asked if she had other plans.

Sarah paused and then shared her real dream: she wanted to have a farm growing spirulina, a superfood like algae.

Yes, she wanted to be a boss, she wasn’t lying to Russ about that, but in a field that really mattered to her.

What a discovery!

Russ could now talk to Sarah about her education and know her main motivations, like helping the environment, working hard, and being a financially independent leader.

Knowing this, they could focus on developing Sarah’s management skills more than her analytical skills, and make sure her current work selling ad servers prepared her to run that spirulina farm.

The talk was a success because both were radically honest.

Russ cared about Sarah and wanted to find what really motivated her.

And Sarah openly told Russ she didn’t plan to stay at Google forever, which ironically helped them both make a clear career plan for her in the company.

Building on this, Russ taught his whole team how to have three talks with each employee.

The goal is to leave these talks knowing what motivates each person, whether they are in rockstar or superstar mode, and help them take a step toward their dreams.

The first is a life story talk.

Here, you talk with the person about their life story and choices.

Try to understand why they made those choices.

Maybe someone left graduate school to work on Wall Street because they wanted financial independence.

Great, you just found one of their main motivators: financial independence.

Keep going and you will understand what really matters to people.

Then there is the dream talk, where you invite your employee to describe how their career would be at its best.

But don’t talk about just one dream.

Talk about three or four different dreams.

Remember very few of us know exactly what we want to do when we grow up.

Encouraging people to talk about their hopes, however big, can help you both make a clear plan.

Finally, there is the 18-month plan talk.

Here you both look to the near future and plan how to help your employee take a step toward their dreams.

Is there a meeting they can have with experts or mentors?

Are there classes they can take?

Can you adjust their work duties?

These three talks will help you put the right people in the right jobs and keep everyone motivated.

But remember, your final goal is to get results together.

## HOW TO GET THINGS DONE: GET RESULTS TOGETHER BECAUSE TELLING PEOPLE WHAT TO DO DOES NOT WORK

When Kim joined Google to lead an AdSense team, everyone did a bit of everything, like a kids’ football team, all chasing the ball.

She saw team members often felt stressed and efficiency was low.

So Kim reorganized the team into smaller groups, gave each clear tasks with clear goals, and set clear leadership lines.

Everything should have worked perfectly, right?

Well...

no.

The result was people started complaining about Kim’s controlling leadership style; some even left the team.

Why?

Her boss gave her wise advice again.

It was not that Kim’s ideas were bad.

The problem was she started to use them before listening to her team’s ideas.

This brings us to what Kim calls "the get-things-done wheel."

There are seven steps to remember: First, listen.

Really listen to what your team members have to say.

When you do, your team will feel safe to share their opinions and have the kind of talks that lead to great ideas.

Second, clarify.

Give your team time to develop and explain their ideas.

Otherwise, ideas may be rejected before they have a chance to be understood.

New ideas are fragile.

Your job as a leader is to nurture them.

Third, debate.

Allow healthy debates so team members can help each other improve their thinking.

Fourth, decide.

Good leaders often do not decide alone but create a clear decision process that lets the people closest to the facts decide.

Your job as a leader is not to decide but to make clear who is responsible for decisions and when they must be made.

Fifth, persuade.

The person responsible for making the decision must convince others it is worth moving forward with their idea.

It is important to take time to gain support from everyone who must apply the decision.

Sixth, apply.

After gaining support, your team can work to make the agreed decision happen.

Seventh, learn.

This last step is very important.

You have to learn from the results to improve next time.

To know if what you do works, set clear goals and track them regularly.

Do not be ashamed of missing some goals, but be sure to learn from it clearly.

Once you finish, start the whole process again!

Your job as manager is to keep the wheel turning: make sure you do not miss any step or get stuck in one.

The most important thing is to always remember the two parts of radical honesty: care personally and challenge directly.

This means encouraging a culture where everyone feels comfortable sharing ideas and also empowered to challenge each other.

## LISTEN AND DEBATE.

To learn more about getting things done without telling people what to do, read chapter 4 of the book.

First, let’s talk about the art of listening, as it most affects teamwork in your group.

There are two types of listening: silent listening and active listening.

Silent listening is good for leaders who prefer to let others talk.

Silent listeners don’t give their opinions right away but sit and listen carefully.

You will be surprised how much your employees share when you do this!

But for it to work, you must have created a place where people feel safe to say what they really think.

If silent listening means staying quiet to give others room, active listening means saying things to get a reaction from them.

Active listeners begin by making a strong statement and invite a strong response to keep the talk going.

Because active listening pushes people to speak, it can help shy employees come out of their shell, but only if they feel supported enough to talk.

When you have made a great listening culture, you must also make a great debating culture.

How?

First, remind yourself and your team that a debate is about ideas, not egos.

This means no one should come to a meeting wanting to win an argument.

Instead, everyone should look together for the best results.

If your team has trouble with this, try asking people to argue for the idea they disagree with.

You can also start an "ego check," where everyone leaves their ego at the door.

If everyone agrees too fast, add a "disagree obligation."

Because if your team agrees immediately on a solution, they may be missing something important.

To encourage debates, get a card that says "disagree obligation" and pass it among the group.

If your team knows radical honesty (®), they will enjoy challenging and being challenged because they know their teammates want to help them think better.

Making these two cultures — a listening culture and a debating culture — sets the base for healthy teamwork.

It makes sure your team feels safe, supported, and motivated to reach goals.

You will get results together!

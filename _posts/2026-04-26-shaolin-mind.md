## WHAT'S IN IT FOR YOU: THE STORY OF MARTIAL ARTIST SHI HENG YI AND HIS BUDDHIST LIFE WISDOM.

Yes, it is true: most of us are very good at just getting by.

We organize our daily life, meet expectations, and keep going – often for many years.

Still, there are quiet moments when something feels wrong: an inner restlessness, a vague pressure, the feeling of always being busy but never really arriving.

If this feeling lasts, one day we might get the idea to ask ourselves: What is left if I stop defining myself only by performance, roles, and endurance?

The martial arts teacher Shi Heng Yi asked himself this question too.

Below, you will learn how he found his answer with the help of Shaolin martial arts and Zen Buddhism, what events influenced him, and what insights and life lessons he gained.

## WHY FITTING IN IS NOT ALWAYS GOOD

Shi Heng Yi was born in Kaiserslautern.

His parents came to Germany as refugees from Vietnam. They wanted him to fit in: Yi should behave properly, follow rules, and not cause trouble.

At his father’s wish, Yi started Shaolin Kung Fu training when he was only four years old.

While other children could decide how to spend afternoons, Yi’s days were planned clearly: fixed training times, clear instructions, and little room for discussion.

He repeated movements many times.

If something did not work at first, he practiced until it worked perfectly.

This way, Yi learned early how to handle pressure and meet expectations.

He moved constantly between two worlds.

At home, there were different rules than outside.

In his family, discipline and restraint mattered, but outside, other social rules applied.

Growing up like this, he learned early to read situations: What do people expect from me here?

How should I behave correctly?

This ability helped Yi in training, school, and dealing with others, but it also had a downside.

He often made decisions not from his own feelings but from habit.

He did what worked.

The question of what he really wanted did not matter.

Looking back, this is a pattern that ran through his whole life and also fits many people: Discipline and good behavior help us fit smoothly into our environment, but they do not show if we act from true inner belief.

When we learn very early to meet expectations reliably, we often do not notice how deeply this pattern controls our actions.

Fitting in becomes a reflex, and following the rules feels so normal that we hardly see it as a choice anymore.

How is it in your life?

Do you often fit in without thinking, just out of habit?

Try one day to watch moments when you say yes, keep going, or push through.

Then ask yourself: Did I choose this on purpose, or did I act out of habit and would I have preferred to act differently?

## WHY PERFORMANCE IS NOT EVERYTHING

The more Yi studied the Shaolin tradition, the more he noticed something: all descriptions were about training.

Kung Fu, Qigong, Tai Chi – all focused mainly on moving well, strength, and control of the body.

But then Yi found a sentence that did not fit: Shaolin belongs to Chan Buddhism.

When he looked deeper, a new view of martial arts opened for him.

In the Chan tradition – later called Zen in Japan – the body is not seen as separate from the mind.

For Shaolin, this means: movement is not only physical training but can be a kind of meditation.

Martial arts are not just defense and technique, but also train attention, clarity, and inner attitude.

This was new for Yi, because until then, his training had little of this mental part – it was only about the body and performance.

Of course, Yi became physically stronger and his movements sharper, but inner growth was missing.

He began to ask: if Shaolin is also a way inward, why does it hardly touch my inner self?

In Western thinking, personal growth is also often about performance.

We improve skills, get degrees, climb career ladders – hoping this will give our life meaning.

But like Yi’s early Shaolin training, something can still be missing, even when everything looks fine on the outside.

Think about your own life: In what areas do you get better and better without being really happier?

And are there areas that do not help your career or performance but interest and fulfill you much more?

## HOW BODY AND MIND COME TOGETHER

Yi finally found the key to connect body and mind in Shaolin in a very unusual place: at Telekom (a company).

During an internship, he saw a film about the martial arts school he had attended as a child.

The school had changed: students now lived there like in a boarding school, and more importantly, they learned not only martial arts techniques but also Buddhist teachings.

There it was, the link to Buddhism that Yi had missed in his own training!

He immediately wanted to help this development.

So he contacted the school, made an appointment with the leaders, and after a good talk, they agreed to work together: Yi should help expand the martial arts section.

He did it, but unlike his old teachers, Yi did not focus only on technique.

Physical training stayed important but was added to by philosophy.

One key part of his teaching became mindfulness.

In Buddhism, mindfulness means paying close attention to what is happening now: thoughts, feelings, impulses, and actions.

When we are mindful, we notice earlier what is going on inside us.

This way, we can decide consciously how to act instead of just reacting automatically.

The idea of karma also became important in Yi’s lessons.

In Buddhism, karma is not about fate or reward, but the law of cause and effect.

Every action, word, and thought leaves a trace.

Careless actions increase unrest.

Mindful actions create positive energy – in our mind, in dealing with others, in everyday life.

Being a teacher brought new challenges for Yi.

Because teaching Buddhism does not allow hiding behind just physical skills.

One’s own attitude becomes clear, and questions about meaning, role models, and honesty appear.

Maybe you know the moment when just joining in is no longer enough – when you want to create instead of just work.

This is where responsibility begins.

You are asked what you want to stand for.

Mindfulness and the understanding of cause and effect can help you find out and act more according to your inner values.

## LIVE IN THE MOMENT, LEARN YOUR WHOLE LIFE.

One day Yi saw an ad for a ten-day intensive seminar in Chiang Mai, Thailand.

The interesting part: The focus was not on outer forms or impressive techniques but on internal martial arts.

Many teachers from different traditions were invited, and Yi understood at once: this was not a normal training but a real chance to grow personally.

Internal martial arts are very different from what we usually think of as fight skills.

Instead of working to be faster, stronger, or tougher, this is about the mental side of martial arts.

In the workshop, Yi experienced this difference directly.

The small, often slow and quiet movements they learned required full attention.

If the mind wandered, control was lost immediately.

Trying to force something blocked yourself.

It was not about technique but really feeling your own body: How am I standing?

How am I breathing?

Where am I unnecessarily tense?

Yi was amazed by the effects that showed not only in training but also afterwards.

His body felt more relaxed, and so did his nervous system.

Overall, he felt much less stressed.

Besides these new experiences, he took away another important lesson: learning never ends.

Even with years of martial arts experience, he was still a student.

This lesson is true not only in martial arts but almost everywhere.

Learning does not stop just because we "can" do something.

Of course, this does not mean we must chase perfection hard. On the contrary: the key to inner martial arts – and much in life – is not to hold on to a goal but to live clearly in the present moment.

What could this look like day to day?

You could think less about what went wrong yesterday or what you want to fix tomorrow, and instead try to give yourself fully to the moment.

In training, everyday life, or work: when you stop wanting to improve all the time and just feel the here and now, you let go of tensions inside and outside and will feel better.

## BETWEEN REALITY AND PROJECTION

The Shaolin Temple in Otterberg, which Yi now helped shape, had become his new center.

He did not see his work there as a job – it was part of his life.

So it hit him hard when, as the temple became better known, criticism grew.

Emails, forums, and comments accused Yi of giving himself his own order name.

They said Shaolin Temple Europe was not a “real” temple because it had no clear lineage to an existing tradition.

Yi felt personally attacked, especially because these claims were false: the temple’s name came from Grandmaster Shi Yan Hai, and this was public knowledge.

What confused Yi most was that nobody had asked about his order name for sixteen years.

And now, when his work reached more people, this point became an attack?

The accusations caused Yi to think deeply and raise questions much harder than public criticism: What images do people project on me?

What illusions grow with more visibility?

And which of these projections and illusions have I maybe helped create myself?

In this hard time, a key Buddhist idea helped Yi: We often suffer from ideas we create ourselves.

We make pictures in our mind of how things should be – how a person, a teacher, or a temple should “really” look.

These pictures feel like facts but are only mental constructs.

From a Buddhist view, suffering starts when we hold tight to these ideas and believe they cannot change.

In this case, it was about the idea of a “real” temple or a “valid” tradition.

These ideas feel fixed but are made by people.

If we take them as unchangeable, the world splits into black and white: right against wrong, allowed against not allowed.

When Yi saw this, he could step back.

Not to ignore the criticism but to see it was based on random images that do not show the truth.

This insight helped him feel less attacked.

Criticism, doubt, and hostility are hard to avoid in life.

But we can always look more closely: What is real, and what is just a projection?

What can I let go of peacefully?

## THE HIGH ART OF LETTING GO.

After a trip, Yi faced another tough situation.

A key member of the order’s leadership was seriously ill, and Yi had to take over all their administrative tasks.

The flood of emails, class registrations, and official letters was one problem.

But even worse: when checking papers, he found many years of backlog: missed deadlines, unfinished letters, unclear accounts.

On top of that, in the temple’s tea room, when talking with the main members about the administration, strong tension broke out.

Some complained about emotional pressure from temple leaders and said the work atmosphere did not match the temple’s true meaning.

What a painful discovery!

And this in a place meant to stand for inner peace and harmony.

For Yi, this was a special dilemma because the leadership had made it possible for him to help shape this place.

So wasn’t he loyal to them?

But how far does loyalty go?

When does it start to do harm?

Yi decided his responsibility was not to individuals but to the temple and its values.

This led to a clear but painful decision.

After deep reflection and talks with masters, teachers, and the community, they decided the current leadership must leave the temple.

Yi also took responsibility himself.

To make room for a true new start, he stepped down as head master, out of loyalty to the bigger picture.

The main, though hard, lesson?

Avoiding conflicts often does more harm than parting from someone trusted or giving up a beloved position.

## CONCLUSION

Shi Heng Yi’s story shows: performance, strength, or fitting in alone rarely bring happiness.

At some point, we should ask if we are really doing what matters to us – or just meeting expectations.

If it is hard for you to find your own path between pressure and outside demands, here are some tips: Listen to your body’s signals!

If you feel tired, restless, or tense more and more, this often means something is wrong in your life.

Don’t ignore it!

The same is true for crises and conflicts.

They are part of life, and long term it does no good to play them down.

It is healthier to take responsibility and face them, even if it is uncomfortable.

A martial art like Kung Fu – and sport in general – can be a great practice for that.

Because just like in real life, it is not about being perfect but about trying.

Some difficulties can only be overcome after many tries – so what?

The main thing is to keep going and honestly ask yourself again and again: Am I living in a way I can accept?

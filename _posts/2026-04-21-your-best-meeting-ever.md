**Author:** Rebecca Hinds | **Category:** Communication Skills## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? A SET OF SIMPLE RULES FOR FEWER, SHARPER MEETINGS THAT GET RESULTS.

Have you ever left a meeting feeling confused, not sure what changed because you were there?

That usually means the meeting was not planned well enough.

When meetings happen without a clear plan, they can take too much time and stop you from doing your real work.

Before you know it, your calendar controls your day, and your progress slows down without you noticing.

The good news is this is easy to fix.

A few simple rules and habits can make meetings shorter and more useful.

In this Blink, you will learn how to reduce too many meetings and stop them from coming back.

You will learn how to check if a meeting is really worth your time, using easy feedback and smart signs.

You will also get practical tips to keep meetings short, lively, and focused on real work, including how technology and AI can help without taking over.

Let’s start with the first rule about making meetings better: cutting meeting debt.

## CUT MEETING DEBT TO RECLAIM YOUR WORKING TIME

In 2013, Dropbox looked at all its meetings and tried something very bold.

They stopped all regular meetings overnight, and for two weeks they also stopped any new regular meetings.

Some types of meetings stayed, like hiring talks and meetings with customers, but most were silent.

Teams were surprised how much real work they could do when their calendar stopped controlling them.

This is the first rule for making meetings better: you must actively cut meeting debt before it slowly wastes your week.

Meeting debt means having regular meetings on your calendar that no longer need to happen.

You can fix this with a five-step plan. First, clean your calendar: check your regular meetings, score each one by how useful it is and how much effort it takes, delete all repeats for 48 hours, then bring back only those that clearly need to be there.

Next, encourage people to protect their time.

Some companies help by giving workers good ways to say no to unimportant meeting invites, so saying “no” feels normal and safe.

The third step is to create a place where anyone can report meetings that waste time, without causing problems.

After that, set rules to stop careless meeting booking.

A simple example is Shopify’s Slack bot, which reminds people not to book meetings on certain no-meeting days.

Stronger rules include needing approval for new regular meetings and blocking time for focused work.

Finally, keep cutting meeting debt regularly, not just once. Put calendar cleanses on your schedule so old meetings don’t build up again.

Take 30 minutes today to pick a date for your calendar cleanse, and treat it like an important deadline because once you make space, your calendar will try to fill it again.

## CHOOSE MEETING METRICS THAT MAKE MEETINGS BETTER

Imagine you want to book a short meeting and you see a cost before sending the invite.

Shopify added a tool that shows how much a meeting costs by adding the hourly pay of everyone invited. It shows this to make you think twice.

Some people did not like this because the cost number can feel like a shame and distract from whether the meeting is worthwhile.

So, how do you measure if a meeting is good?

That is our second rule: only measure meetings in ways that help you make smarter choices.

Avoid measures that look exact but do not help you improve.

Be careful if meeting hosts rate their own meetings, because they may think their meetings went better than they did.

The best feedback comes from people who attend the meeting.

For quality, use Return On Time Invested – ROTI.

Right after a meeting, attendees rate if the meeting was worth their time on a scale from zero to five, from “a complete waste” to “an excellent use of time.”

Anonymous online polls work best in most workplaces.

Add one question asking what would make the meeting better by one point, and you get clear, useful ideas.

Use ROTI only sometimes, about 10% of meetings, so answers stay thoughtful.

It is also good to track a few other facts, like how much time meetings use, who talks the most, if people multitask, and if meetings start on time and people attend.

Never make a metric into a strict goal, because goals can cause people to cheat or pretend.

Used right, measurements are a small habit that keep meetings honest and always getting better.

## STRIP MEETINGS TO ESSENTIALS ACROSS FOUR DIMENSIONS

When a team has a hard topic, like losing part of the budget or cutting resources, the calendar can become a way to avoid the problem.

One talk turns into many regular meetings, extra people are invited “just in case,” and the agenda fills with status updates that could be shared in a short note.

Soon, just organizing meetings feels like progress, but the real decision waits.

That is why our third rule is about keeping meetings simple and focused.

Start by cutting your agenda.

Cut it roughly by half, then rewrite what stays as an action using a verb and clear result.

For example, instead of “Budget update,” write “Decide the Q2 budget trade-offs and approve the final plan.”

Move routine updates and small decisions to messages or documents, because small items can block important work.

If someone brings up a new topic, save it in a shared note to discuss later without losing focus.

Next, shorten meeting time.

Work expands to fill the time set, so book less time and keep the ending firm.

If cutting time in half feels too much, make an hour meeting 50 minutes and a 30-minute meeting 25, and finish early when done.

Then, reduce who attends and how often meetings happen.

Invite only those who need to be there and keep groups smaller than eight. Share notes with others later.

Cut regular meetings that happen just by habit. Add end dates so meetings must earn a new invitation, and stop extra meetings by making sure decision makers are there and each action has a responsible owner.

Choose one regular meeting this week and make it as small and useful as possible.

Once you see how light your calendar can be, you will not want to go back.

## DESIGN YOUR WHOLE COMMUNICATION SYSTEM FOR FLOW

Imagine you could guess which teams will work well without hearing what they say.

That is what happened when MIT’s Sandy Pentland studied how about 2,500 people in 21 companies interacted, using badges that measured talking time, turn-taking, and movement.

The result showed a simple truth: how information flows in a group is often more important than the exact words used.

This is the fourth rule: think about communication as a whole system.

Meetings are just one way people communicate, along with email, chat, shared documents, and project tools.

If the system outside meetings is messy, then meetings have to fix everything, and your calendar fills up without solving real problems.

Start by choosing a clear set of tools, so each one has a purpose.

When teams add many apps without clear use, information is broken into pieces and people waste time looking for what they need.

One report said the average knowledge worker loses about 6 hours a week just switching tools and finding where work is.

Many workers say they want one shared set of main tools.

Standardization also means making rules for when a live meeting is needed.

Try the 4D-CEO Test as a filter.

If the goal is not to decide, discuss, debate, or develop, it should not be a meeting.

Even if it passes, it should be live only if the topic is emotional, complex, or the decision is hard to change.

The main point is meetings show how healthy your whole communication system is.

When tools are clear and rules are known, information moves easily, and meetings stop being the place where everything must happen.

## DESIGN MEETINGS FOR THE PEOPLE IN THEM

Have you noticed how some meetings feel dull and drain energy?

You might think it is because of a bad day or the wrong group.

The fifth rule says it is a design problem: if people lose interest, the meeting needs to be fixed.

There are four common energy drainers you should watch for.

Energy drainer number 1 is power moves.

One person, often the boss or the loudest, talks too much and others stay quiet.

A good solution is to reward honesty and sharing.

Alan Mulally’s weekly Ford meetings used simple red-yellow-green status reports, and he praised the first person who said there was a real problem, making it safe to speak up.

Energy drainer number 2 is lateness.

Starting late lowers attention and shows that keeping time is not important.

Between 44% and 55% of meetings start late because someone is late, and late starts mean fewer and less original ideas.

Start on time even if some people are missing, and make being on time a leader’s rule.

The third energy drainer is jargon.

When people use many acronyms and buzzwords, others spend energy figuring out meanings instead of thinking.

Jargon makes meetings harder to understand, less interesting, and less honest.

Use simple words, explain new terms once, and make it easy to ask questions.

The fourth energy drainer is boredom.

Flat agendas and boring rooms make people multitask.

In 2023, 46% of US adults said they felt bored at work at least three days a week.

Make meetings better by focusing on the most urgent topics, refreshing the room with a quick break, and adding a small fun moment to catch attention again.

If you spot these problems early and fix them fast, meetings will become a place people want to join and help.

## TIME MEETINGS TO THE NATURAL RHYTHM OF WORK

In 2012, Donna Morris at Adobe thought yearly reviews were making people leave.

The process did not match real work and made managers judge only recent weeks.

After she said Adobe would stop yearly reviews, the company moved to check-ins every quarter with goals and metrics.

By 2016, fewer people quit by 30%.

This is the sixth rule for better meetings: time them with the flow of work.

Fit your meetings to how work moves, and they will help progress instead of breaking focus.

One way is to use three rhythms: strategic, tactical, and operational.

Strategic rhythm follows your goal-setting timing.

It means everyone uses the same goals and measures so priorities stay clear.

Tactical rhythm follows projects and works best with talks at the start, middle, and end of a project.

Start with a “premortem” by imagining the project failed and listing reasons why.

This makes risks clearer.

At the middle, teams hit a point where the deadline feels real, so this is a good time to raise hidden problems and adjust.

After delivery, do a quick review to see what worked, what didn’t, and what to change next time.

Operational rhythm is the daily pace that keeps work on track, like short meetings to find urgent issues, remove problems, and pass work.

Only use this often if the work needs it.

The Cleveland Clinic holds 15-minute daily huddles that send urgent problems to the CEO fast, but many teams don’t need meetings this often.

When meeting times match the work, meetings support progress instead of stopping it.

## UPGRADE MEETING TECHNOLOGY LIKE A PRODUCT IN BETA

In 2021, a US government science team held a hybrid meeting with leaders, and the technology failed simply.

People on phone calls could not hear the room, someone had to bring a standing microphone, and remote people could only guess who was speaking.

If sound and video are bad, a meeting becomes about fixing tech, not making decisions.

The seventh rule is to keep improving your meeting technology. Treat it like something you update over time, not something you accept as it is.

Start by getting basic equipment that always works: good sound and a clear image if video helps.

Studies show bad audio makes listeners think speakers are less smart, warm, and trustworthy.

Control your space, use a headset or external mic instead of laptop microphones, and add noise-canceling software if your place is noisy.

Use calm technology – tools that work quietly without distraction.

Your tech should need little attention and handle boring tasks so people can focus on thinking, relationships, and clear decisions.

If a tool needs many logins, plug-ins, or shows distracting effects, it takes away the attention meetings need.

What about AI?

Use AI to do busy tasks like scheduling or making notes and clear action steps, but only with clear permission and rules about privacy, storage, and access.

Be open about when you use AI and why, introduce it slowly, and give it one task at a time to check if it helps or just adds noise.

Overall, the last rule is to treat your meeting tech as something you keep improving: get the basics very good first, then add tools only if they make meetings clearer, fairer, and easier to act on.

Together with the other six rules, this will help you improve meetings and make them work for you as they should.

The main lesson from this Blink to Your Best Meeting Ever by Rebecca Hinds is that meetings get much better when you design them carefully, not just do them by habit.

## FINAL SUMMARY

Stop extra meetings with a clear reset, then keep only meetings that earn their time.

Check quality with signals you can use, simplify agendas and guests, and fix your whole communication system so meetings stop having to solve everything.

Save energy by fixing domination, lateness, jargon, and boredom.

Match meeting rhythms to real work and update technology carefully so it helps focus and trust.

### Synopsis

*Tech Leadership* (2023) is a guide for people who work in tech. It helps software engineers and tech staff. It is for those who are moving from doing their own work to leading teams and managing others. It uses true stories from big tech companies. It offers ideas and plans to help you lead in the tech world. It shows that being very good at tech skills is not enough to be a successful leader.

## What’s in it for me? A blueprint for leading in tech.

In 1822, Charles Babbage made the first machine that could compute. At that time, no one knew this was the start of the Technology Age. Two hundred years later, more than five billion people use the internet every day. Also, the tech industry is now the second biggest in the US. It employs 8 out of every 100 workers.

But things are going wrong. Change is happening faster and faster. Tech leaders find it hard to keep up. They are losing good workers. They also find that old business rules from MBAs do not work well in tech. The real problem is bigger than many companies say: The best engineers get promoted to lead. This happens because they are very good at tech. But then they are not ready for what leadership really means. 

This summary helps with that. It shows a clear plan for you to become a tech leader, not just a tech worker. You will learn what makes a good leader. You will also learn how to build teams that do well when things are tough. You will see why working together has replaced older ways of managing. You will also learn how to use plans that get results, instead of plans that are forgotten.

Let’s dive in. 

## Blink 1 - What it takes to be a leader

First, being a leader is not for everyone, and that is fine. It needs a special kind of person. This person truly wants to help others, not just be helped. The best leaders do not look for power or fame. They deeply want to guide, help, and care for their team. If this is not you, it is okay to stay a regular team member. But if it feels right, three important qualities will help you succeed.

First, have a *growth mindset*. This means you believe you can always learn and get better. This is true no matter what problems you face. When leaders with a growth mindset face a problem, they find a way forward. They do not give up. A simple way to get this mindset is to start each day with a saying. For example: “I can learn whatever I need to be a great leader.” Simple sayings like this are very strong when you start to doubt yourself. Do not let doubt grow. Instead, quickly change those thoughts with your chosen saying.

Good leaders also *welcome change* when it happens. Change never stops. It happens in our bodies, in markets, and in the weather. In tech, change gets faster every year. To be a great leader, just getting through change is not enough. You need to lead it. You need to jump into new changes with so much energy and belief that others will want to follow you.

Of course, not everyone on your team will be as excited right away. When big changes happen, many people feel sad or worried. This is called the “valley of despair.” They miss the old ways of doing things. As a leader, you need to fight against this feeling. You do this by giving hope. You keep everyone focused on what needs to be done. You trust that you will figure out the small details later.

The third key thing for tech leaders is to *question the way things are*. Good leaders are like curious young children. They always ask “why?” They find new information and show real interest. But you need to find a balance. It is important to keep asking questions. But leaders must not get stuck. They should not just keep gathering information without doing anything. This is called *analysis paralysis*. Like children, they need to prefer taking action. Great leaders take smart risks. They must do this because the world changes fast.

Asking questions should not be negative or critical just for the sake of it. Keep talks interesting and curious. Do not make them like a fight. When you talk to people with real interest, not doubt, they will be more likely to share their thoughts. 

## Blink 2 - Focus on your people

At its heart, leadership is about your team members. How well you connect with each person decides if they will do their best work. Or if they will just do the minimum. This connection has four parts. They may sound easy, but they need real effort to get right.

First is *time*. Talking one-on-one builds strong bonds. Group meetings cannot do this. When you meet with someone alone, you show them they are important. Next is *attention*. If you do other things during a talk, like checking your phone or emails, it looks like you do not respect them. Even if you do not mean it. Your team members see if you are really listening or not.

*Asking questions* is the third part of building connection. You cannot learn about someone without asking questions. And you must truly listen to their answers. Last, *empathy* connects everything. This is when you truly care about someone’s thoughts and feelings. This makes people feel safe. They will take risks and work hard when they feel understood and important.

Caring for people also means *showing them the big picture*. Emails that just tell people about new rules or changes are not enough. You need to explain the reasons. Help each person see why a message is important for them. But to do this, you first need to understand the big picture yourself. This means you must step away from your daily work. Even step away from your team. You need to understand the company’s larger goals. Then, explain this vision to your team members.

This clear way of talking helps people feel involved. This directly affects how well the team does. Studies show that employees who feel involved help make up to 21% more profit. They also take 41% fewer sick days. Involved people do not just come to work. They work harder because they truly love what they do.

So, if you want to be a leader, do not wait for a promotion. Start connecting with people now. Tell your coworkers when they do good work. See your teammates as people you work with and friends, not as rivals. You will only get that promotion when you can already show these leadership skills.

Once you are a leader, stay close to the actual work. Unless you are the CEO, you should work with your team when you can. This shows you understand their work. It also shows you do not think their work is below you.

Being a leader also means you are a coach. You are always helping others to stay motivated. You help them reach their goals. The best coaches help people who are not doing well to become strong workers. They help strong workers become outstanding. 

When you coach, pay close attention. Listen very carefully. Do not think you have all the answers and that someone just needs to be fixed. Instead, ask questions. Listen well. Help people decide on clear steps to move forward. Real progress happens when people choose their own way, not just follow orders.

## Blink 3 - Cross-functional leadership

After World War II, soldiers came back from the war. They brought a leadership style called 'command-and-control' to workplaces. This was common in factories. This style focused only on making many things and reaching goals. But this way of leading did not work well with new workers. These workers were educated but had not been in the military. They worked in service jobs. They needed a very different type of leadership. One that helped them be creative, think of new ideas, and think deeply. This is what *working together leadership *does.

Working together leadership protects free thinking and allows for mistakes. It makes it easier for team members to talk and work with each other. Look at Google’s campus in Mountain View. It is very big, 1.1 million square feet. But everyone sits so that they are only a 2.5-minute walk from each other. This design helps people meet by chance. It helps them share ideas across different teams. Everyone is asked to share ideas and work together.

If you lead a team that works together and has different roles, not all members will report directly to you. So, there are a few things to remember. Your first goal is to be very clear about each person’s role and what they are responsible for. Talk to them one-on-one before your first team meeting. And in that first meeting, be very clear about the team’s goals. This way, everyone understands the same thing.

Also, make sure your team knows the background of their work. They need to know why the project began. They need to know how it fits with the company’s bigger goals. And they need to know about market changes. Try to make this information special for each team member. For example, tell your data scientists how important their findings are for understanding tricky software behaviors. Let your user researchers know how key their work is for understanding how people use products. This background information is like glue. It helps teams with different roles work well together.

It is also important to have people with different skills on your team. You must also invite and encourage people with different ideas and backgrounds. When many different views are shared, new ideas start. It also stops 'groupthink.' This is when everyone thinks the same way. This is why DEI (diversity, equity, and inclusion) is so important.

You alone cannot bring more people from minority groups into tech jobs. But you can do some things to help. First, learn about it. Find out what your team thinks about DEI goals and ways of working. HR can give you good information. This includes details on workers, how many people stay or get promoted, fair pay, and results from inclusion surveys. Second, use a team with different people to interview new staff. They will help you find good workers from many backgrounds. Last, be careful with your words. Learn which phrases and sayings are no longer widely accepted. This helps your team feel safe and like they belong. Then they will be happy to speak up and share ideas.

## Blink 4 - Strategic leadership

Strategy is your company’s long-term path. It is the plan to reach it. Strategic *leadership* means you put that plan into action now, with your team. It is not so much about writing the company’s five-year money goals. It is more about how you act and lead.

The main idea of strategic leadership is *ownership*. Treat your company’s money, tools, and customers as if they were yours. When you do this, something changes. You start to act on your own. You take responsibility without being asked. You naturally want to help what you own grow and stay safe. This is because it is important to you.

Leading with ownership means leading the way. You show the right path by your own actions. If you just tell people what to do, they often push back. But if you start doing it first, they are more likely to follow. This does not mean doing something very brave, like saving people from a burning building. Sometimes it is as simple as checking on a problem you see and making sure it is fixed.

You also need to show how to be a good follower. If you do not follow the company’s rules, culture, and team guides, you cannot expect your team to. Ask yourself: *If everyone on my team did exactly as I do, how well would we work?* Your answer shows if you are truly leading or causing problems.

This leads us to *company culture*. This is made of the written and unwritten rules. These rules decide how work is really done. As a leader, you protect and guide this culture. Your team sees the culture through your actions. To protect it means you show and make stronger the best parts of the culture. You also help create a team culture that brings out the best in everyone.

But to do this well, you need to understand your culture. This is especially true for its bad parts. Disrespect happens when people ignore or put down others’ ideas and feelings. Or when leaders treat team members badly. Not including people means you keep them out because of things like their race or sexual orientation. Very tough ways of working lead to people being unfair or working against each other. These are not just small problems. They are reasons to leave. If you see these things in your company, you should really think if it is the right place for you.

Other parts of your company’s culture might not be your favorite. But they are not reasons to leave. Think of them like a person’s small habits. You might not love every habit. But you still respect the whole person. This difference is important for leaders. When you truly accept the company culture, it is much easier to show it truly to your team. You can also create a good team culture that matches your own values. You cannot pretend to fit the culture for a long time. Trying to do so makes everyone tired.

## Blink 5 - Executing your leadership

Everything you have learned about leadership is useless if you do not do it. Ideas without action are just dreams. So, let’s talk about how to actually get work done.

You have probably heard of SMART goals. These are goals that are specific, measurable, attainable, realistic, and timely. But there is another system that is very popular in tech: OKRs. This stands for objectives and key results. Big companies like Netflix, Google, Meta, and Spotify use OKRs. They use them to set and reach big goals.

OKRs are different from SMART goals because they are meant to be very ambitious. They are not impossible to reach. But they need a big, focused effort from the whole team. An OKR has two parts. The first is an objective. This says what you want to achieve. The second is three to five key results. These show how you will reach your objective. For example, your objective might be “Make the quality of releases better.” A key result could be: “Lower the number of bugs after release from twenty to five on average.” The best part is when you let your team help set these goals. If done well, OKRs give a clear plan. Everyone commits to it because they helped make it.

But a goal is not very useful without a plan to reach it. Making a plan happens in two steps. First is *thinking deeply*. This means discussing ideas around a whiteboard. You work through ideas and check your beliefs. Many plans stop here. This is because people do not know there is a second step: *trying things out* in the real world. You need to try new things. You need to watch what happens. You need to change as you go. You must always learn and get better. A plan is a living thing that changes, not just a paper to put away.

In tech, you are always trying to find a balance. You want to be fast, but not perfect. If your solutions have no bugs, you are likely not working fast enough. But you need to watch your *technical debt*. These are small problems that build up as you move forward. In time, you must fix these problems, or they will cause big trouble. You can check how fast you are working. Use clear measures like engineering hours or work cycles. If you want to go faster without too much technical debt, you might need more team members. Not just work harder.

The thing that connects all of this is *feedback*. It helps you guide your work. It helps you find problems before they become too big. It makes processes better. It opens ways for people to talk that would otherwise be closed. Feedback is how all the ideas of tech leadership really work.

So, promise to give regular and useful feedback. Make it correct and clear. Base it on real examples and what you know directly. Give it on time. The sooner you give feedback, good or bad, the more it helps.

One last tip? Actively ask for feedback about yourself. See every comment as a gift, not a danger. Each piece of feedback is a chance to learn something new. Or to fix something that is stopping you. Leaders who stop listening will eventually stop leading. In tech, being open to what you do not yet know is important for your long career.

## Final summary

In this summary of *Tech Leadership* by Andrew Swerdlow, you have learned that leadership is for people who truly want to help others. 

Great leaders have a growth mindset. They welcome change. They question everything. They build real bonds with their teams. They do this by being present and caring. They make places where people feel safe to try new things. Strategic leadership means taking responsibility. It means leading by showing the way, not just giving orders. It means guiding the company culture. And watching out for bad parts. But all of this is not useful without doing the work. Set big goals with your team. Make plans by thinking and by trying things out. And always give feedback. This helps everyone learn and grow.

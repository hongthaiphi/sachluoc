### Synopsis

*Is this normal?* (2020) – Many of us have asked this question before when thinking about our own sex life. But it is often hard to talk about private things with others. So, here experts will answer your most important questions.

## What's in it for you: Find out how "normal" your sex life is – and why that is not so important.

Sex is everywhere. You see it on billboards. Your friends go to sex-positive parties. When you go out, people happily talk about their last one-night stand. Online, you cannot click three times without seeing breasts, bottoms, or hot abs. This can feel freeing or tiring, depending on how you see it. But not everything is loud. When we talk about our own feelings and worries, or how we truly experience sex, we often become very quiet.

This is normal. Sex touches our deepest self – our feelings, our private world, our identity. We are most open when we have sex. At the same time, seeing so much sex around us creates pressure. It makes us feel we need to perform and fit a certain idea of "normal." What a shame! But in truth, sex is much more personal and varied than the media shows us. So, if you sometimes ask yourself if you are sexually "normal": Keep reading!

First, a small warning: If you feel itchy or pain down there, or worry that your sex life might be bad for your body or mind, please talk to an expert. Don't be shy to speak openly about these things. Doctors and therapists have surely seen or heard worse things!

## Blink 1 - I have really "perverse" fantasies. Is that normal?

Short answer: Probably yes! Part of the excitement of sex is that you can enjoy things that feel forbidden, dirty, naughty, or shocking. Do you like rubbing against balloons? Do you like bondage and anal sex? Do you enjoy being poked with stilettos? Or do you like your partner, dressed as a secretary, peeing on your face? Go for it! The chances are good you will find someone who likes these things too. 

Let's look at some numbers: For example, half of all women and three-quarters of all men often imagine having sex with strangers. Also, one-third of people like to dream about being forced to have sex. You see: Yes, this is quite normal.

Still, these thoughts can feel strange. Especially when they come into your mind without you wanting them to. And then you realize these images excite you, even if you don't want them to. Then many people worry they might be a rapist, an exhibitionist, or simply completely perverse. But don't worry: You don't choose what excites you. And in your mind, almost anything is allowed.

You should draw a line if your sexual fantasies include children, someone getting hurt, or sex without agreement. Especially if you want to make these fantasies real. Then it's time to talk to an expert and get help. Don't be ashamed. You are not to blame for your urges, but you are responsible for them.

What about porn? Oh, please. We wanted to talk about *"perverse"* fantasies. Porn is very common today and no longer a forbidden topic. In a survey, three out of four single men and one out of four single women said they had watched porn in recent weeks. Add to that those who watched porn but would not admit it. You will quickly see that many people watch porn. Mainstream porn might not be great from a psychological or feminist point of view. But watching a "dirty" film now and then will probably not harm you. It's different if your porn use gets out of control. We will look at this in the next section.

## Blink 2 - I can only climax with porn. Is that normal?

Well, short answer: No, that is not normal. But sadly, this happens to more and more people today. No surprise. Porn is always available online. And it affects your mind. For example, a survey of ninth and tenth graders showed that one-fourth of them only found bodies beautiful if they looked like those in porn films.

Also, porn is much easier to get than a real partner, especially one you find attractive. Porn also gives you *supernormal stimuli*. These are urges that are stronger than what you would find in real life. So, many people just click when they want a quick sexual thrill.

This is completely fine by itself. But it becomes a problem when you click all the time and only use porn. First, you might find "normal" sex not exciting enough to climax anymore. Second, your partner might not like fast, intense sex with five position changes in six minutes. Let's not even start talking about how women are shown in mainstream porn.

Okay, so you only climax when you watch porn. The good news: This can change. But how? You need a little patience and strength. Stop watching porn for a while. Also, try to limit fantasies that are like porn scenes. Sex and masturbation might feel quite boring for a time, but keep going! After a while, your brain will get back to normal. And if that doesn't work? Well, then you might really be addicted to porn. It's time to talk to a doctor or a sex therapist. They can help you find the reason and free yourself from it.

By the way: Porn doesn't have to be disrespectful to women or full of stereotypes. If you look around, you will find many films that show variety and respect. They explore sex without stereotypes. For example, check out *Lustery*. There you can watch real heterosexual couples in romantic and private moments. Or try *Bright Desire*. You will find smart sex films that are sensual and include feelings and connection. Whether it's *MakeLoveNotPorn*, *XConfessions*, *PINKLABEL.tv *or *blue artichoke films*: Porn is truly much more than you might think.

## Blink 3 - My vulva / my penis looks really strange. Is that normal?

Oh dear, a total classic. Very short answer: Yes, it is probably normal. Again, we can "thank" porn for telling us that only sex organs of a certain size, color, and shape look good and are normal. Please note: That is nonsense. Both male and female sex organs come in a huge variety of forms.

Are your inner labia very big? Normal. A bit purple? Normal. Is the head of your penis too big or too small? Normal. Do your testicles hang very low? Normal. Micropenis? Normal. Really!

Let's be clear: Every vulva is beautiful, and every penis is beautiful – if we look at them with love. The better you know your vulva or penis, the more joy you will have with it. But if you look at yourself with shame and dislike your own body, you will find it hard to relax and simply enjoy sex.

How can you learn to accept your supposedly "strange" sex organ? It's very easy: with patience and a little bit of effort. Sorry, I had to say that. But seriously: Have positive experiences. First with yourself, and later, if you dare, with others. Find caring partners you can trust. Start your journey to self-acceptance.

Okay, but when is a strange vulva or penis not normal anymore? In short: If it hurts or doesn't work well. In that case, you should talk to a doctor. And yes, if there's truly no other way, you can also have cosmetic surgery on your sex organs. Labia reduction and penis enlargement are now quite common. But please find an experienced surgeon. Learn a lot about the risks and side effects. And don't make a quick decision.

## Blink 4 - My partner does not want sex anymore. Is that normal?

The answer here is a bit more complex. Not wanting sex *can* be normal, but it doesn't *have* to be. Generally, the desire for sex is a wide range. Some want to have sex five times a day. Others are happy with once a week. And some have no interest in sex at all. And that is okay.

The desire for sex also changes throughout life. Someone who only thought about dirty things as a teenager might later find other things more interesting. Or you might suddenly feel a strong sexual urge in your mid-forties. That's fine too.

Of course, sexual desire also changes during a relationship. You guessed it: This is completely normal. Don't let supposed statistics online confuse you. Fact check: A recent study shows that people have sex about once a week. Is it less for you? Who cares? If it is okay for *you*, then it *is* okay!

And then, of course, there's the case where your partner doesn't want sex because something is wrong. In that case, it is *not* normal. Maybe they are going through a hormone change. Or they have an illness that affects their sex drive. Or it could be medicines. Or simply an emotional problem. It could also be age, or their diet. The possible reasons are as many and varied as people themselves.

If you are in a relationship and one of you wants more sex than the other, you should find a way to deal with it. This way should feel good for both of you. This needs patience, openness, and a bit of courage. Because talking about sex is quite hard for many people. We will look at how to make it easier in the next section.

## Blink 5 - It is hard for me to talk about sex with my partner. Is that normal?

In one word: Yes! That is completely normal. It makes sense. You were probably raised like most of us about sex: What's in your pants should stay there. Don't touch there, don't look there. As a teenager, you probably used words like "wee-wee," "willy," and "bottom." Later, words like "bang," "nail," "pop," and "bonk" were added. These are not exactly poetic. The words from medical books are hardly better: prostate, cervix, erectile dysfunction. Yes, sorry, you can't have a good conversation using such words.

Embarrassing! But also: If you don't ask, you won't know. If you don't bring up the topic, you will never know if your partner secretly shares your fantasies. You won't know if they enjoy sex with you or how you could give them more pleasure. Exploring sex together can greatly enrich a relationship. Wouldn't it be a shame to miss out on this, just because you are too shy to ask? 

So, how do you overcome your shyness? It's best to find a quiet moment where you both feel comfortable and close. Then talk about what's on your mind. It's okay to laugh if you feel shy. But move forward slowly and carefully. After all, you have probably been thinking about this talk for a while. Your partner needs time to get used to the topic. Always be respectful and try to speak mostly about yourself. Don't judge. And always remember: You are on the same team. You are looking for the same thing: shared good experiences.

Of course, these talks feel strange at first. But you will see: The more often and openly you talk to each other, the better your sex will be. At the same time, trust between you will grow. You will feel closer than ever before – not just in bed.

## Blink 6 - I think I am in the wrong body. Is that normal?

Let's put it this way: Being trans is not common in statistics. But it is normal in terms of health. Being trans is not a sickness. It is simply a sexual identity that does not match the sex you were born with. The problem is not you. It's society, which has a problem with this. And sadly, it's often still a very big problem. Many trans people face rejection and criticism when they talk to their family about their sexual identity. This rejection often causes great pain. Luckily, there are support centers that can help you through this hard time, if you are in this situation.

If you are not sure if you feel like a man or a woman, that is also okay. Intersex is real: There are people who are not clearly male or female, medically speaking. Some people do not feel they belong to any gender. This is called *agender*. Some feel like both a man and a woman at the same time. This is called *bigender*. And some people think the idea of only two genders is nonsense. They are *genderqueer*. You don't know where you belong, but you still want a label for your dating profile? Then write *questioning*, meaning you are still searching. It's okay if you haven't found your right gender identity yet.

So, let's get the facts straight: *Gender incongruence* means that the gender you feel and your physical sex do not match. If you experience this, you can change your gender. You can do this with hormone therapy or with surgery. And yes, before you ask: A clitoris can be changed into a penis. However, surgery comes at the end of a long process. Anyone who has had such a change needs to discover their sexuality again from the start. You could fill whole books with this topic. 

But the same rule applies to everyone who is unsure about their sexuality: It gets better with self-love, understanding, closeness, talking, and tenderness.

## Conclusion

We hope you have learned something new or can now better understand your own situation. 

Here is one last tip for urgent situations: Relax and take the pressure off yourself. Things are often not as dramatic as they seem. If you are suffering because of a sex-related topic, try looking online for other people who feel the same way. Talking with others often makes things much less scary. Always remember that the images you see on YouPorn or in ads have little to do with the real sex lives of real people. Relax! And then find out what feels good *for you*.

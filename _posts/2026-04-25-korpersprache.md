**Author:** Tiziana Bruno | **Category:** Psychologie## WAS DRIN IST FÜR DICH: EINE UNTERSCHÄTZTE SPRACHE LERNEN.

At work, there are moments that are hard to explain: The new boss seems unfriendly right away, even if he says nothing wrong.

A job interview feels bad, even though the answers are correct.

After a talk with an employee, you still feel uncomfortable but can't say why.

Often, the key is not in the words, but in body language.

It is like a quiet second voice.

It is always there.

Those who can read it understand faster what happens in the room and can react better.

This is exactly what you learn with body language.

## KÖRPERSPRACHE IST STÄRKER ALS ALLE WORTE

Someone enters the room and says "Good morning" — but it does not feel like a good morning.

Maybe it is a tired smile, or a glance that stays too short, or the way someone stands: as if they want to be somewhere else.

And then there is something in the room that cannot be ignored.

This is body language: communication happening while we think we have not even really started talking.

At work, it is often underestimated because it does not fit in tables.

You can measure knowledge, compare degrees, and document results.

But like, trust, and impact often happen in seconds.

Why does a new boss seem unfriendly right away, even if he says nothing wrong?

Why does a job interview feel wrong, even if the content is right?

Often the answer is not in the words but what the body says beside them.

Psychologist Albert Mehrabian studied what makes a spoken message effective.

His result is still quoted: 7% comes from the words, 38% from voice and how it is said, 55% from body language.

People react more to how something is said than to what is said.

A sentence can sound friendly but still feel cold if spoken with pressed lips, shallow breath, and a look like a closed window.

This sensitivity is not a modern habit, but an old skill.

Thousands of years ago, people had to quickly decide if a stranger was dangerous or trustworthy.

Body language never comes from one signal alone.

It is the combination of gestures, pauses, eye contact, facial expression, posture, speed, and voice.

Only all together shows the full picture.

That is why it is hard to make simple rules.

Crossed arms can mean defense, insecurity, or just no interest in what is said.

A quiet voice can show shyness or focus.

Body language must always be understood in the situation.

The important thing is: It is not about learning tricks to seem "better."

That would be like acting lessons that only say: "Smile more."

The real benefit is a new way of seeing: You notice body signals more clearly — in others and yourself.

A simple cycle helps with this.

First, watch what happens in the room.

Then try to understand the signal in context.

Next, react consciously — for example with your voice, distance, eye contact, or how you lead the conversation.

Finally, check if the situation changes because of that.

So a vague feeling becomes a tool: You can shape meetings actively instead of just reacting.

## HALTUNG, MIMIK UND GESTIK – DAS VOKABULAR DER KÖRPERSPRACHE

Body language is not just single gestures you can pull out like a trick.

It is more like a whole stage set: posture, gaze, movement, voice, and speed work together.

Often it is not a big drama, but small details that matter.

Body language is always personal.

Everyone has their way of walking, standing, looking, and speaking.

If you know someone well, you often notice small things: how a friend pushes hair back when she flirts, or how a sister bites her lip when she is nervous.

Such movements are like personal signatures.

Posture shows body language most clearly.

We can roughly divide it into four types: open, closed, submissive, and arrogant.

An open posture looks upright but not stiff.

Shoulders are relaxed, eyes alert, gestures match what is said.

When sitting, it means sitting up straight, calm, hands resting loosely on thighs or the table, ready to join the talk.

A closed posture creates distance.

Crossed arms are the most known sign, but there are others: leaning back, looking away, hands holding something like a cup or phone as if for protection.

In such moments, copying the posture does not help.

It is better to stay calm and open yourself — and change the situation so the other person leaves that posture.

Changing seats, giving water, or small movements can help more than big words.

Facial expression is noticed quickly because the face is our main “reading tool.”

Are the eyes bright or dull?

Is the gaze direct or avoiding?

Is someone trying to smile or really smiling?

People with very little facial expression often seem competent at work.

But when trust matters, this sparseness can feel cold.

Gestures are shaped strongly by culture.

In Southern Europe, people use their hands and arms more than in Germany.

Someone who hardly gestures can seem uninterested or weak.

Even greetings are a small performance: a weak handshake can show uncertainty, a too strong one shows dominance.

Both can make the first meeting bad.

Voice is often underestimated but is like music.

Speaking softly can show doubt, a shaky voice tension, and a boring tone lack of interest.

A clear, nice voice often comes when the body is straight and relaxed.

Posture and voice go together.

## DIE ROLLEN IM RAUM: DOMINANTE, MACHER, SCHÜCHTERNE & CO.

At work, the first impression usually comes not from content.

It comes from how you appear: eyes, speed, distance, voice, posture.

Especially when meeting a customer, in a job interview, or a first meeting, there is no shared history.

A type system can help here — not to put people in boxes but to recognize clear patterns fast.

The first type is the Dominant.

He looks controlled, checking, and careful with movements.

His gaze is direct and strong, face showing little emotion, gestures clear.

People of this type like responsibility and want to feel their partner is steady.

If you act too casual, too friendly, or too nervous here, you lose trust fast.

But being too submissive makes you small.

The best way is calm and alert: speak clearly, stand straight, listen well, don’t make fast gestures.

The second type is the Careful.

He checks details and wants to avoid mistakes.

You can see a searching, sometimes doubtful look, ordered gestures, and a slightly forward posture.

He is seldom loud or overly excited.

If you use big gestures or too much speed, he will be suspicious.

Better are calm, open movements, patience, and showing: the work here is done carefully.

The third type is the Doer.

He has energy, loves change and action.

He looks dynamic: strong handshake, loud voice, direct gaze, strong gestures.

He gets bored fast with static talks.

If you seem weak or too careful, he feels you don’t care.

But he reacts badly to fights.

Be present but not aggressive.

The fourth type is the Social.

He builds relationships and cares about mood. He reacts strongly to warmth or coldness.

His body language is open: friendly eyes, welcoming gestures, lively voice.

He notices quickly if someone is pretending.

If you are too polished, cold, or purely professional here, you may get agreement but no real connection.

The best way is a warm tone: really listen, soft facial expressions, no stiff movements, no feeling of being above others.

The fifth type is the Shy.

He is sensitive, careful, often tense in his body.

His greetings are hesitant, voice quiet or uneven, gestures small.

Sometimes his hands play nervously with objects or his face because he isn’t sure where to put them.

If you are loud, fast, or rough, you overwhelm him.

Better are calmness, friendly safety, smooth movements, and a voice that is not too strong.

If you want to lead, present, sell, or solve conflicts, you need soft skills: talking well, empathy, presence, and impact.

These skills are hard to measure.

## ÜBUNG 1: KÖRPERSPRACHE SEHEN LERNEN – IM CAFÉ, IM BÜRO, ÜBERALL

And they are even harder to train if you don’t know where to start.

Body language is a good start because you see it every day.

The most important step is surprisingly simple: practice watching.

Many people feel when a talk is going wrong but cannot say why.

This is where training starts.

One of the best exercises needs no mirror or guide.

Just a notebook or phone app and a place where people act naturally: a café, hotel lobby, station, or waiting room.

Then watch one person for one or two minutes and write only facts.

No judging, no “looks arrogant,” no “seems unsure.”

Instead, a simple body language report: How does the person stand?

Is the weight on one leg or even?

How are the shoulders: raised, relaxed, or forward?

What do the hands do: calm, busy, hidden?

How is the gaze: direct, searching, avoiding, fixed?

How does the body move: slow and calm or fast and nervous?

How fast is the walk?

Are the gestures small or big?

This trains something often missing: the difference between seeing and judging.

Only after you learn to describe signals clearly can you understand them well later.

If you want to go deeper, you can use videos.

Theater is very good because body language is clearer than in daily life.

## ÜBUNG 2: PRÄSENZ TRAINIEREN – STAND, HALTUNG, GESICHT

Many people first think about body language as how others see them: How do I look?

How do I seem confident?

But often the harder part is the opposite.

Body language also affects yourself, from outside to inside.

Like a costume in theater: you put it on and suddenly you feel, move, and speak differently.

You can try a simple test.

In the morning, go to work with a angry face, raise your shoulders a bit, breathe shallow, and walk slowly.

Your mood will drop almost by itself.

Then try the opposite: open eyes, slight smile, faster walk, chest open.

Not a constant smile, just a choice to switch.

Many people are surprised how fast their mood changes by just using their body differently.

This also works in meetings.

If you sit with crossed arms, lean back, and look critical, you pay less attention.

You listen but like a doorman: checking and guarding.

If you sit straight, shoulders relaxed, hands calm on thighs or table, you take in more.

Posture changes not just your effect but your attention.

A useful step is to ask about your favorite posture.

Many people have a pose they always go back to: crossing arms, hands on belly, leaning on the wall, rubbing the chin, playing with a pen.

This posture feels safe or comfortable — but it also sends a signal.

If you watch yourself, you see when you use it: when you are unsure, bored, stressed, or resisting.

Just noticing this is enough.

Because when you notice, you can choose.

A useful exercise from acting school is standing training: first learn to really stand.

Many people move nervously, hang in the knees, or make themselves small without knowing.

Presence comes from a steady, relaxed stand.

To do this, stand in front of a mirror so you see yourself from the side.

Then roll your upper body down slowly, letting your head and arms hang, breathe out — and stand up again slowly.

At the end, stand with feet hip-width apart, toes slightly out, knees soft, hips neutral, back straight but not stiff.

Head straight, shoulders relaxed, hands loose by your side.

When this stand feels right, you can add a second exercise: mirror your face.

Imagine different feelings and watch your face: doubt, joy, anger, calm.

Many find something helpful: Some look stricter or colder than they feel.

Others see their “friendly” face is more a polite mask.

The better you know your face, the clearer your effect becomes.

## ÜBUNG 3: AUFTRITTE MEISTERN – LAMPENFIEBER, STIMME, ROLLENWECHSEL

Just before it gets serious, the same thing happens often: your mind is ready but your body goes into alarm.

Your breath gets shallow, shoulders rise, hands shake, voice sounds thin.

Stage fright is not weakness — it is stress.

And you can control stress fastest with your body.

A simple reset starts with movement.

Nervousness is stored tension.

So help your body release it: shake arms and legs, stretch, take a few steps on tiptoes.

Some tap their legs, arms, and chest gently.

It may not look elegant, but it makes your body open again.

Next is breathing.

Breathe in deep, slowly breathe out, and blow air out sharply once.

After a few breaths, your voice changes.

Your voice becomes richer because your chest opens and your jaw relaxes.

An easy little trick almost too simple: smile.

Not as a show, but as a muscle trick.

When your face muscles relax, your voice sounds less tight.

Just give your mouth corners a little space.

If you feel very tense, you can try a silly exercise — but it works well: the monster.

Make faces for a few seconds, stick out your tongue, bend your back, make yourself big and ugly.

The key is to keep breathing.

This works because your body leaves stiff tension and then you can return to a "friendly person" role.

For voice and face, try this: open your eyes and mouth wide, stick out your tongue.

The start itself is a body language moment.

When you enter the room, don’t walk like a shipwrecked person to the table nor like a showman waving with a permanent smile.

The best way is a third path: calm, upright, and with pace.

Walk in, make eye contact, stand briefly, breathe, then start.

The next step is an actor’s trick: practice role changes.

Many people seem unsure not because they have nothing to say.

But because they have only one body language “style.”

They always sound, stand, and look the same.

If you widen your style, you will seem more fitting, not more fake.

A practical exercise is to say a speech or short text many times – but each time with a different posture.

You can say the same words like a scared duck, then like someone very annoyed, then like someone in a hurry, then very excited.

It sounds playful, but it trains exactly what counts at work: voice, rhythm, gestures, and presence become more flexible.

The same works with a fairy tale because you play with characters, speed, and tone.

Another strong tool is animal exercises.

Act like a tiger: smooth, alert, focused.

Then a mouse: small, fast, careful.

Then a peacock: slow, proud.

Then a monkey: playful, curious, but easily nervous.

The important thing is to really try it out, not just think about it: How big are the movements?

How fast?

How does the voice sound?

If you practice this, you do not rely on luck at important moments but on a strong set of skills.

## FAZIT

Body language is not a set of tricks but a skill you can train like an actor: watch, try, and change consciously.

Those who learn to read signals and control their presence do not seem fake but clear.

Suddenly, conversations become easier — because you don’t only react to words but to what really happens in the room.

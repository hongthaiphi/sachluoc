**Author:** Dave Silberman | **Category:** Technology & the Future## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? DISCOVER HOW TO GAIN SUSTAINABLE ADVANTAGE IN THE NEW AI MARKETPLACE.

Leaders today have a lot of pressure.

If you ignore artificial intelligence (AI), your competitors will move ahead using data-driven methods.

If you use AI without care, you might face big problems when AI makes mistakes or shows biases.

This choice feels hard because it seems like you only have two options: use AI or not.

The real solution is to stop thinking like that.

This Blink shows a new way for leaders to use both human skills and AI well, without one taking over the other.

You must build special skills that AI cannot copy and learn how AI works and where it fails.

In the end, you change data into true wisdom by thinking about your own thinking.

The way forward is not to choose between old ways and new ways, but to combine them to create something better.

## THE FALSE CHOICE

You are at a difficult decision point.

One side offers AI which promises better efficiency, deep data insights, and automation to transform your organization.

The other side values human judgment, emotional skills, and experience.

It is hard to decide which way to go.

Thinking you must pick one causes you to freeze when you need to act fast.

Leaders who ignore AI lag behind others who use smart data and predictions.

Leaders who use AI without knowing its limits risk big failures when AI makes false claims, spreads bias, or misses human details.

For example, a retail leader ignored AI for managing stock, trusting her team's gut feeling about customers.

Within two years, her rivals used both AI and humans to improve their supply chains and customer service.

She lost customers not because AI was better, but because she did not try to combine AI with human skill.

On the other hand, a finance firm used AI to approve loans but did not watch it closely.

The AI was fast but kept old unfair biases, which humans would have noticed.

This caused fines and bad reputation, costing more than the AI saved.

These problems come from treating humans and AI as rivals, not partners.

The market rewards those who use both well together.

This new way is called the Both-And approach.

Instead of choosing your judgment or AI, ask how they can help each other.

Your thinking finds AI’s blind spots.

AI finds patterns you might miss.

Your emotional intelligence helps with change; AI handles big data fast.

You need new ways of thinking without false choices.

Use systems to know when to use human skills, when to use AI, and how to mix them well.

This makes both better and your leadership stronger.

With human guidance, AI gets more reliable.

Together, they produce results neither can do alone.

## HUMAN POWER SKILLS

AI is great at handling large data, finding patterns, and doing specific tasks very fast.

But AI has big limits.

It can’t handle unclear situations in human relationships or see when old patterns don’t fit new problems.

AI cannot make judgments or balance different values.

This is where your special skills are very important.

Power skills are human abilities AI cannot copy: critical thinking, emotional intelligence, solving conflicts, and clear communication.

As AI takes over routine tasks, these skills become more valuable.

They help you ask questions AI would not, and understand data in your company and culture.

Humans make decisions that no data alone can explain.

For example, think about critical thinking when AI makes suggestions.

A health manager got an AI report saying to cut staff in the children’s unit because patient numbers dropped.

The AI saw a clear number pattern.

But the manager knew more: a new children’s hospital had opened nearby, and the local population was getting younger.

Instead of firing staff, she added special services to make her unit different.

In 18 months, more patients came to her hospital because of its new services.

Her critical thinking saved the unit by questioning the AI, not just trusting it.

Emotional skills also help when people react to new AI tools.

When a factory used AI quality checks, supervisors worried they would lose their jobs.

Workers were less productive because of this fear.

The plant manager saw this was an emotional issue needing human care.

She changed roles so supervisors taught staff how to fix problems flagged by AI.

By recognizing fears and showing AI as a tool, she changed the team’s resistance into teamwork.

Strategic communication helps you work between human and AI users.

You explain AI results in ways others understand, warn about AI limits, and show the value humans add.

You become a bridge between technology and the real world.

To get better at these power skills, practice on purpose.

Question AI’s advice, ask what it might miss, and check its ideas.

Build emotional intelligence by caring how AI changes affect your team’s feelings, not just tasks.

Improve communication by explaining complex AI ideas clearly to people without technical knowledge and warning against over-trusting AI.

## UNDERSTANDING AI

To lead well with AI, you need more than a basic idea.

You need to know how AI works, where it is strong, and especially where it fails.

This turns you from someone who just uses AI into someone who works with AI for better results.

Most modern AI uses foundation models.

They learn from huge amounts of text, pictures, or data, then give answers based on the chance of words, not real understanding.

When you ask AI for advice, it does not think about your problem like a human.

It guesses the most likely next words based on its learning.

This makes a big difference in how you use AI answers.

Prompt engineering is how you guide AI to give useful answers.

The way you ask questions and give context changes the results you get.

A vague question gets vague answers.

A clear question gives specific and helpful ideas.

For example, a marketing leader wanted ideas for a campaign.

She first asked, “Give me marketing ideas for our new product.”

AI gave common ideas that fit many products.

Next, she gave detailed context:

“We are launching a comfortable office chair for remote workers aged 30 to 45 who have back pain.”

“Our rivals compete on price.”

“We stand out with strong back support based on physical therapy research.”

“Give three marketing ideas that show health benefits in a friendly way."

Now AI gave focused, useful ideas.

Knowing AI limits is as important as using its strengths.

Hallucinations happen when AI makes up confident but wrong facts.

It might name fake studies, invent false numbers, or tell stories that are not real.

Always check facts carefully, especially for important choices.

Bias in AI training data can show up in results.

For instance, a hiring AI trained on mostly male workers will tend to suggest male candidates, despite qualifications.

You must check not just AI’s advice but the data patterns that shape it.

Good data is key for reliable AI.

Incomplete or messy data leads to bad results.

Check data quality before trusting AI about customers, stock, or performance.

Bad data always means bad results, no matter how smart the AI seems.

Your job is to make sure AI supports human judgment, not replaces it.

Be skeptical of easy answers.

Ask why AI’s advice fits your beliefs so well.

Demand clear info on what data AI used.

Treat AI as a strong partner that needs your knowledge and judgment to create real value.

The world of AI brings complexity at a huge scale.

## FROM DATA TO WISDOM

You face changing markets, uncertain technology, complex organizations, and unclear choices.

This kind of situation is called VUCA by experts.

It needs skills that turn raw information into real understanding.

Many leaders have too much data but not enough wisdom.

AI makes this harder by creating lots of analysis that needs human meaning to be useful.

The journey from data to wisdom is a key idea for leaders to find meaning from information overload.

Data are raw facts without meaning.

Your sales system records thousands of sales each day with price, amount, time, and customer details.

This data can be valuable but tells no story alone.

Information happens when you organize and explain data.

Looking at sales, you see a rise every Thursday afternoon.

You now have information: a pattern.

But you don’t yet know why or what it means for your business.

Knowledge appears when you study and explain the pattern.

Talking to your sales team, you learn that a rival runs deals on Wednesdays, pushing customers to your store on Thursdays.

This knowledge links cause and effect and helps you predict future sales.

Understanding comes when you see bigger effects and connections.

You realize the Thursday sales come from reacting to rivals, not your own strength.

This shows a weak spot you didn’t see before.

Wisdom is using understanding to make good decisions.

Instead of just using Thursday sales, you create ways to attract customers all week with your unique offer.

You may also work to keep customers who first came because of your rival’s mistakes.

Wisdom combines knowledge with values, experience, and long-term thinking.

AI is strong at the early steps.

It quickly turns data into information and finds patterns leading to knowledge.

But moving to understanding needs human interpretation AI cannot do.

Your knowledge of markets, culture, and strategy helps you see what AI misses.

Wisdom belongs to humans.

It needs ethical choices, risk judging beyond numbers, and balancing priorities when there’s no clear best choice.

AI might find the best short-term profit from Thursday sales.

But only human wisdom knows that relying on rival mistakes without building your own strengths is risky.

## IMPLEMENTING BOTH-AND LEADERSHIP

To use this idea in real work, use specific ways to combine human and AI strengths in your leadership.

The Both-And approach is not just an idea but a way to change how you make choices, solve problems, and grow your team.

Start with metacognition: think about how you think.

Before big decisions, stop and check how you are thinking.

Are you trusting your gut because the problem really needs human judgment? Or just because you find data hard?

Or are you trusting AI because it looks smart, without testing if it makes sense?

This self-check stops you from blindly trusting yourself or AI without reason.

Use the data-to-wisdom steps carefully in your work.

When AI gives analysis, see which level of insight you have.

AI might give data on low employee engagement and show which teams dropped most.

But only you can understand why this is happening and how to fix it.

Know where AI’s help stops and your judgment starts.

Make decisions using both strengths.

Use AI to find patterns in large data that humans cannot handle alone.

A retail leader might use AI to see stock trends across many stores and products.

Then use human insight to know if changes are due to customers, seasons, or market events needing strategy.

For hard problems, set up back-and-forth between human and AI analysis.

Get an AI suggestion, then review it with your knowledge and experience.

Improve your question based on what AI missed to get better answers.

This repeated exchange works better than just one AI report or only human thinking.

Help your team grow skills in both areas at once.

Teach people to ask AI good questions and think critically about AI answers.

Make rules that AI advice must be checked by humans, and human ideas reviewed by AI for bias.

A finance team might check their forecasts with AI for hidden bias, and review AI’s market predictions for missing factors.

The Both-And approach works when neither people nor AI control decisions alone.

You learn to know which tool fits each case, have the humility to let AI help or human wisdom override it, and keep improving both.

This way is not an end but a growing skill as you and AI get better.

## FINAL SUMMARY

The main point of this Blink to AI-Powered Leadership by Dave Silberman, Rich Maltzman, Loredana Abramo, and Vijay Kanabar is this: good leadership with AI means not choosing just human skills or AI power.

Build power skills like critical thinking and emotional intelligence that AI cannot copy, and learn how foundation models work and where they fail.

Use prompt engineering to guide AI for smart ideas, then use your knowledge to understand the answers.

Move step-by-step from data to information, knowledge, then understanding and wisdom, knowing AI is good early on, but people give deep meaning.

Use the Both-And way by thinking about your own thinking, making human judgment and AI work together to get better results.

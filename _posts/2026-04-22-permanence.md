**Author:** Lisa Broderick | **Category:** Motivation & Inspiration## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? LEARN HOW TO MAKE YOUR RESOLUTIONS STICK.

If you are reading this Blink, you probably have tried some self-help methods before.

Maybe you wanted to do better at work, be a better parent or partner, or just take better care of yourself.

Even when these methods are easy to understand, we still find it hard to succeed and keep looking for new solutions.

Why does this happen?

The problem is that knowing what to do is very different from actually doing it.

In this Blink, we will help you close that gap.

We will show you how to take simple steps to be more active and committed to real, lasting change.

If you want better personal goals, stronger relationships, or more happiness and meaning in life, we will explain how small daily actions and checking in with yourself regularly can help you grow and become clearer about what matters.

Let’s begin.

## QUESTIONS TO KEEP YOU GROUNDED AND GROWING

First, let’s clear up a common wrong idea: successful change is not about having some special mix of motivation, willpower, resources, and talent.

If you believe this, it is easy to feel stuck and think you are just not the right person to reach your goals.

But the truth is, willpower is not the main thing.

The key is to set yourself up for success with the right foundation.

This means understanding what works best for people, which is doing small, repeatable actions every day.

In this case, it means making a habit of checking in with yourself to see if you worked hard enough and why sometimes you did not.

The best tool for this is Daily Questions. These are short questions about whether you tried your best in important areas like goals, happiness, relationships, meaning, and engagement.

The exact words you use matter.

Your questions should focus on your actions, effort, and taking responsibility.

For example, don’t ask, “Did I remain focused the entire day?”

Instead ask, “Did I do my best to remain focused today?”

Other good questions are, “Did I make progress on my most important goal?”

Or, “Did I show up for the people who matter to me?”

Studies have shown that this Daily Questions method works well.

One study with senior leaders used co-author Marshall Goldsmith’s questions like, “Did I do my best to set clear goals?”

“Did I do my best to make progress toward goal achievement?”

and “Did I do my best to be fully engaged?”

Participants also added their own questions, like “Did I do my best to avoid angry or hurtful comments today?” or questions about exercise and kindness to family.

The study found that people kept using the method for a whole year.

The process did not blame or judge anyone.

People rated themselves and had a short daily call as outside support.

Over time, many said they felt more purpose, less anger, and less need to control others.

Halfway through the year, people started changing their behavior in the moment because they knew they would answer their questions soon.

They became more aware while living their day, not just after it ended.

This improvement continued as long as they kept doing the daily questions.

Those who stopped often went backwards.

This shows that real change comes from regularly working on the behavior you want to improve.

## A SELF-MEASUREMENT SYSTEM WITH BAKED-IN MOTIVATION

The Daily Questions idea may seem new, but it has a long history.

Ancient Stoic philosophers like Marcus Aurelius used daily reflection to keep their values clear and focus on what they could control: their actions and reactions.

Benjamin Franklin even made a personal scorecard and checked it every day, seeing character and virtues as things you can improve.

This idea continues today in modern writers’ work.

As author Peter Drucker said, “What gets measured gets managed.”

When you measure something, vague goals become clear actions.

If you want to be a better listener, you watch your eye contact, how often you ask questions, and how rarely you interrupt.

Numbers give you clear signals.

Good results motivate you, and poor ones show where to improve.

With simple tracking, a short daily review, and meaningful questions, you make a personal dashboard for change that is practical and built to last.

Talking about motivation, a key part is self-awareness, which includes asking yourself about what you really value.

This is called intrinsic motivation: what really matters to you?

When your goals match your values, you feel stronger energy and keep going even when it’s hard.

It becomes easier to deal with problems and keep trying.

Let’s quickly review.

Building a self-measurement system starts with being clear.

Pick parts of your life to focus on now — maybe your health, relationships, work, or emotions.

Write these down.

Make a simple way to track your effort, like a notebook, spreadsheet, or habit app.

Keep it easy to use so you do it every day.

Add some daily questions asking whether you tried your best in those areas.

Choose a fixed time each day to answer them.

Just two minutes is enough if you do this every day.

After one or two weeks, look at your answers and find patterns.

Where do you do well?

Where do you often fail?

Use this information to improve your strategy.

You might change your goal, try new ways, or set new expectations.

Working with an accountability partner can help you keep going.

## AVOIDING PITFALLS AND STAYING ON TRACK

By now, you understand how Daily Questions can help you change.

Here are some tips to use it well.

First, wording is very important.

The best questions start with: “Did I do my best to [blank]?”

This helps you focus on your effort and choices during the day.

You watch how you acted, how you focused your energy, and how you chose to behave.

Your questions should focus on what matters most to you right now — health, patience, forgiveness, delegation, emotional control, or other things you want to improve.

If your questions match what you really care about, they will feel more important and help you make better choices.

Think of it as a daily reset button that brings your mind back to your purpose, connection, and contribution.

Make a simple chart, write your questions, and score yourself each evening.

Use simple numbers for scores.

After a week, check for patterns.

Many people add a partner who asks them daily and writes down their answers.

Even top coaches use support like this.

Having structure keeps the habit going even on busy or messy days.

Focusing on effort makes you feel positive about the process.

Some days feel easy, some hard.

That is normal.

Your daily score always counts.

Doing this every day helps you become stronger, honest, and reliable.

People who keep it up say their relationships, health, mood, and focus improve because of small, daily efforts.

But there are challenges to watch for.

One is distraction — pings, tabs, emails, and thoughts that break your focus and stop reflection.

That is why daily check-ins should be short and protected.

Having a friend check in at a set time can help a lot.

Outside support is very useful, but phone reminders can also keep you on track.

Another challenge is planner bias.

We all have a hopeful side who thinks we can do grand plans, but the real us is often tired and can’t do them.

The best advice is to keep goals small and simple.

It’s about routine and structure.

Following a small daily habit should not need arguing with yourself.

Finally, many people postpone feeling happy — they wait for future success to feel good.

Daily Questions bring happiness into the present.

You check whether you gave energy to joy, connection, and meaning in the day you lived.

This change puts your feelings back under your control, one honest answer at a time.

## KEEPING THE COMPARISONS TO YOURSELF

One big problem for personal growth is comparison.

We see many scoreboards — social media, work rankings, other people’s success.

It is easy to judge your life by their reports.

One advantage of Daily Questions is that you take back control of your measuring stick.

Your personal scores show what really matters to you.

These personal measures give you a clear path because they fit your values, priorities, and choices.

Comparing too much has a real emotional cost.

When people see only other’s successes, their own good achievements feel small.

Even big wins like degrees, promotions, or progress lose their shine next to others’ big leaps.

In high-pressure work cultures, this leads to more anxiety, loneliness, burnout, and sometimes serious harm.

The mind pushes the goal farther away.

Daily Questions give you a steady anchor in this chaos.

A short, private check-in brings your focus back to your own actions and standards.

You see if you gave honest effort to your goals, values, and growth.

This daily scoring shows forward movement that you might miss otherwise.

You notice progress.

Your wins come from what you do, not from others’ applause.

Do not forget how strong routine is — stepping back each day to ask, “Did I do my best?” and answering honestly.

This builds strength and keeps your motivation tied to things you can change — your choices, focus, and actions.

This habit slowly frees you from comparing to others because what matters most is your values and your system.

But this system can also work in teams and leadership.

Healthy teams grow when focus is on contribution, not fame.

Teams do better when steady effort, cooperation, and steady growth get noticed.

Managers can use Daily Questions to support and celebrate impact, service, and progress.

This is a way to treat people with curiosity and respect, not quick judgment.

By showing steady contributors, you build a workplace that values real work over celebrity.

Everyone works better when they feel seen for their real, steady work, not just big moments.

## A FEW MORE TOOLS TO HELP SUSTAIN GROWTH

Now let’s look at some long-term tools to keep personal change strong.

One thing very important is support.

You don’t have to do this alone.

In fact, it is better to have others with you.

Growth is stronger when you have people who share your goals.

Accountability partners, mentors, and coaches give steady help, honest advice, and a little push when you feel low.

Many coaches have their own coaches too.

They help keep your goals clear and your standards high.

Good feedback from others should follow some rules.

Forget old mistakes, speak honestly, and try to help.

One good way to give advice is called Feedforward.

Instead of talking about what already happened, you ask for ideas about what to do next.

You briefly say your goal, ask for ideas, and say, “Thank you.”

No arguing, no judging, no long talks.

This keeps the talk open and positive.

It builds humility, good relationships, and turns advice into helpful steps.

Many people put these ideas into new Daily Questions, keeping the cycle of improvement tight and personal.

Another tool is the Wheel of Change, a simple guide for your future self.

It helps you with four areas: Creating, Preserving, Eliminating, and Accepting.

Creating means new skills, habits, and relationships you want to add.

Preserving means strengths and values you want to keep.

Eliminating means behaviors and habits that hold you back.

Accepting means the parts of life you decide to make peace with — things you stop fighting so you can use your energy better.

Using this wheel gives you a balanced plan to build, keep, let go, and accept.

You can also think about labels you have — like responsible one, smart one, funny one, fixer — and choose which still help you and which you want to change.

Small changes in how you see yourself can give you new freedom to act.

These tools help support your habit of Daily Questions.

They guide your effort, add people to keep you going, start new helpful talks, and show changes to keep your growth on track.

## FINAL SUMMARY

The main point of this Blink to Permanence by Lisa Broderick and Marshall Goldsmith is that real lasting change comes from small daily behaviors, honest self-reflection, and simple measuring.

The key practice is answering short Daily Questions about whether you tried your best in what matters — goals, relationships, meaning, engagement, and well-being.

Doing this every day builds awareness and responsibility, helps you see where you don’t match your goals, and lets you change your course quickly.

Growth lasts longer when you add structure and support.

Partners, coaches, and mentors keep your promises alive and stop you from slipping back.

Together, these tools make a steady system — a practical way to be the person you want and keep living that way.

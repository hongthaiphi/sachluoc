The world is an uncertain place. It is full of constant change and chaos. Many companies fail in this chaos. But some companies do more than just survive constant changes. They use these times to get stronger.*. Oben bleiben. Immer. *(2012) explores why some companies succeed while most others fail.

## What's inside for you: How to survive tough times and make your company succeed.

Why do some companies succeed and even grow during tough times? Why do others fail? Is it luck? Is it smart leaders? Or being extra careful? 

Jim Collins and Morten T. Hansen asked this exact question. *Oben bleiben. Immer* is the result of their wide and careful research in the business world. The authors looked at what successful companies do differently to get through crises. They used their data to compare the best companies with less successful ones from similar fields. They studied them for nine years, all in bad conditions. 

These Blinks summarize what they found. You will learn that success in tough times is not about luck or fate. It's also not just about being more innovative or taking more risks. Success comes from a good mix of discipline, proven innovation, and a touch of caution.

These Blinks will explain:

- what business leaders can learn from polar explorers,

- how much courage you need to succeed, and

- how Southwest Airlines handled the crisis after September 11.

## Blink 1 - Researchers survive at the South Pole – and companies survive in tough times – only if they are ready for everything.

In 1911, two teams raced to the South Pole. Roald Amundsen's team arrived first. They planted the Norwegian flag and returned home safely. Robert Falcon Scott's team reached the Pole 34 days later. But after losing the race, the team met a sad end. No one from Scott's team ever made it home. They all froze to death in the ice.

Why did these two teams have such different outcomes?

The main difference was how they prepared. Amundsen planned his trip with extreme care. It was almost obsessive. He spent years before looking for useful knowledge around the world. The Inuit taught him how to survive. He learned about every possible food source, even dolphins. His team carried extra supplies in case of delays. He also marked his supply camps clearly with black flags against the white snow. Amundsen didn't know exactly what awaited him at the South Pole. But he made sure his team was as ready as possible. In short, he left little to chance.

Scott's team, however, carried far fewer food supplies. This meant they risked starving if there were unexpected delays. Amundsen used proven Inuit methods like dog sleds. Scott used untested technology like motor sleds, which failed in the extreme weather. 

These mistakes led to Scott and his team's death.

What does this have to do with the business world? Well, like Amundsen and Scott at the South Pole, many companies face the challenge of surviving in bad conditions. Amundsen's recipe for success also works for business leaders: Be ready for anything. Absolutely everything.

## Blink 2 - Success in uncertain times depends on discipline, proven innovation, and being usefully paranoid.

The future is unsure. No one can predict what will happen in the coming years and decades. Even with these unknowns, some companies do more than just survive. They really thrive. To understand why, we need to look at what the "*10xers*" do. These are very successful companies that performed at least ten times better than others in their industry.

What is their secret to success?

Like the polar explorer Roald Amundsen, 10x companies make sure they are ready for any event or situation. They do this by gaining more knowledge and taking the right steps.

This attitude shows in three main qualities:

First, 10x companies have* extreme discipline*. Here, discipline does not mean that every employee obeys orders. It's more about how the company acts consistently. Amundsen made a well-thought-out route and a clear schedule for his trip to the Pole. He then stuck to it. 10x leaders do the same: Once they set their goals and methods, they stick to them firmly.

Second, 10x companies use *proven innovation* when they make decisions. They rely on facts. They don't care about what others think or following old ideas. They only care about what truly works. Amundsen ignored expert advice when choosing his base camp. He looked at the evidence himself and picked a place no one had thought of before. In the same way, 10x companies use facts to decide when and where to invest.

Finally, *useful paranoia* helps 10x companies get through changes in the market. Their leaders are always watchful. They never get too comfortable. They always have a useful fear that something might go wrong. On his trips, Amundsen prepared for the worst possible problems. Similarly, 10x companies turn their fears into smart plans. These plans prepare their companies for tough times.

## Blink 3 - 10x companies always reach their goals – no matter what happens outside.

Roald Amundsen showed great discipline on his trip to the South Pole. Every day, he traveled the same distance: 15.5 miles. He did this no matter the weather or ground conditions. If the weather and terrain were good, he covered the set distance. Then he stopped and gave his team a break, even if they could have gone further. 

This way, his team saved energy. They could then cover the set distance even on days with bad weather and ground. This kept the trip moving at a steady pace.

This successful method is known as the Twenty-Mile March. It's one of the strategies that helps 10x companies thrive even in uncertain times. First, a measure for success is set. Amundsen set a daily distance. Companies use longer-term goals, like an annual growth rate or a certain level of innovation. They will then always work hard to reach that goal, in good times and bad.

With the Twenty-Mile March, you must reach your goal in bad times. But you must also not go over it in good times. This might seem strange. But it is key to success – and it needs self-discipline.

If you push too hard, your company might quickly lose its balance. It might also lose its ability to adapt to new conditions. The electronics company Advanced Micro Devices almost failed. This was because it only chased quick wins in the 1980s. It tried to reach a 60 percent growth rate. It borrowed too much money. This left it without enough resources when its market crashed. The company fell behind rivals like Intel. Intel was a 10x company that had more steady and lasting growth.

## Blink 4 - 10x companies are bold and innovative. But they only go all in when their strategy is proven to work.

10x companies are proud of making their own decisions and creating new things. Companies like Apple are famous for always being creative. Many experts think this boldness in new ideas is key to their success.

To some extent, this is true. But 10x companies don't innovate just for the sake of it. They don't take wild risks by creating new, breakthrough products without knowing what customers need. First, they gather all kinds of facts. This helps them deeply understand the market. Only then do they change their innovation to take advantage of the opportunities.

The strategy for this 'proven creativity' is: 'First small shots, then cannonballs'. A 10x company first tests the market. It uses several cheap, low-risk new ideas. It's like firing many small shots in different directions. Once a target becomes clear, the company goes after that chance with full force. It fires cannonballs at the target.

In 2001, Apple fired a 'small shot'. It launched a small MP3 player that worked with Mac computers. This shot – the first iPod – had low risk. It was cheap and didn't take much focus from their main product, the Mac computer. Sales at first looked good. So Apple fired another 'small shot': iTunes. This was an online music store only for Macs. Customers liked the iPod and the chance to download music cheaply and legally. This shot hit the mark. 

This was the proof Apple needed before launching its 'cannonballs': iTunes and iPods for non-Mac computers. This meant for everyone. The cannonballs not only hit their target. They hit with such power that they broke it apart. The rest is history.

## Blink 5 - Innovation is not everything. It must go hand-in-hand with discipline in how a business works.

Many people wrongly believe that in a changing world, the most innovative companies are also the most successful. But in reality, innovation alone does not guarantee success.

Of course, every company must have some level of innovation. And every industry has an 'innovation threshold'. This is a certain level that companies must reach to not fall behind their rivals.

This threshold is higher in some industries than in others. For example, the IT industry has quite a high innovation threshold. Products that are brand new one moment are old the next. So, companies must keep coming up with new ideas to stay competitive. The airline industry, however, has a low innovation threshold. There is no need to invent a new way of flying every month.

Companies that fail to reach their industry's innovation threshold cannot succeed. But innovating far beyond this threshold is also not very helpful. This is because a company that only focuses on innovation loses its balance. Too many resources are used to chase new breakthroughs. This leaves important areas of the company, like production or marketing, without enough money or development. What is missing is the discipline to make sure all areas, such as manufacturing, marketing, or accounting, get the attention they need.

Intel managed to beat its rival, AMD. This was true even though AMD was more innovative at first. Intel stayed successful because it focused on all parts of its business. This included innovation, production, and sales. It was very strict about costs and production processes. It made sure customers got their products on time and without extra costs. 

AMD might have beaten them in the lab. But Intel won where it truly matters: in the market.

## Blink 6 - 10x companies have 'useful paranoia'. They fear the worst and prepare for it very carefully.

10x companies never get too comfortable. They worry about their future all the time. Even when they are doing well, they keep asking: 'What if things change in my industry?' 'How will my company cope?' and 'Who might be my next competitor?' But this paranoia becomes useful for them. It pushes them to protect themselves against every possible event. For example, they save large amounts of cash.

Their paranoia makes them very alert. They constantly look around for possible dangers. These include new rivals, new laws, or changing money conditions.

Being so watchful helps them deeply understand their industry. This, combined with their careful preparations, gives them a big edge over competitors. It lets them see important upcoming situations and use them to their benefit.

In 2001, the airline industry was badly shaken by the terror attacks of September 11. Many airlines had to close down. But one airline, Southwest Airlines, not only survived the chaos but even made a profit. It managed to grow its business and offer new routes and services. Southwest Airlines did not predict the September 11 tragedy. But they had a financial cushion. This helped them get through the huge shock. Their foresight allowed the airline to find new chances during the crisis.

## Blink 7 - 10x companies rely on how they operate. This leads to steady progress and success.

In 1979, the US airline industry became more open. This allowed new airlines to start and competition to grow. In response, the CEO of Southwest Airlines shared a list of ten rules. These rules were to ensure the company's success in the changed market. This list included a set of rules the company had to follow: 'Use 737 planes' or 'Don't serve meals'.

These rules led to great success. So, 80 percent of them were kept for the next 25 years. The airline industry changed a lot and was very unstable. Yet, only 20 percent of these rules were changed. This gave the company the steady progress and clear focus it needed to succeed year after year.

These rules were successful because they were Specific, Methodical, and Consistent – or 'SMaC'. They were precise enough to solve many problems. At the same time, they were lasting enough to be useful for a long time. They were also specific enough to be used in different situations.

10x companies are experts at creating SMaC practices. They create them based on a lot of facts. They know which practices will work and which won't. They also make themselves strictly follow these rules. Southwest Airlines could have given up its rules when the airline industry faced one of its many crises. Instead, they stuck to them. They knew it would pay off in the end.

10x companies only make small changes to their rules when it is absolutely needed. After all, these rules were made with great care. This ensures everything works well from the start. This gives 10x companies the confidence they need for success.

## Blink 8 - It's not luck and chance, but ambition and hard work that explain the success of 10x companies.

Many people wrongly believe that luck and chance are the most important reasons for success in our society. This idea says that great leaders and top companies succeed less because of their mindset or plans. Instead, it claims they just happened to be in the right place at the right time.

Truly great people smile at this idea. They know they get the same amount of luck as everyone else. It's not luck that makes them stand out. It's a mix of hard work and ambition. 10x leaders get the most from their luck. They make the most of every chance they get.

Bill Gates, the founder of Microsoft, had a lot of luck. He had a private education. He could use computers at university. And by chance, he read a copy of *Popular Electronics* magazine. He found an interesting article that inspired his first big project. But this alone did not make Bill Gates successful. Many other people at his time had similar good luck. What made him truly successful was ambition and hard work.

After reading the article, he changed his life plans. He quit university. He moved to a state where he could work on his goal: to create a ground-breaking software product. He worked non-stop. Often, he didn't even eat or sleep. He knew he could achieve great things. He was not ready to rest until he did. In short: Only through hard work and ambition did he turn his luck into the legendary success story it is today.

## Summary

The main message of these Blinks is:

**Truly successful companies do not leave their fate to chance. They prepare very carefully for all possible events. They make decisions based on facts. And they follow their plans with discipline – in good times and bad. This mix of steady action and factual analysis allows some companies to stay on top in a chaotic world.**

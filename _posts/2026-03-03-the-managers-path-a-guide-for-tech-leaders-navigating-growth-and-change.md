### Synopsis

*The Manager’s Path* (2017) is a useful guide for people in tech. It helps them move from working alone to leading others. This includes jobs like mentor, tech lead, and senior leader. The book talks about the special problems in tech. In tech, managing people is also a technical skill. It gives practical tips and plans to deal with problems at each step of a manager’s journey.

## What’s in it for me? Master the fundamentals of management in tech.

You have coded for years, solving hard technical problems. Then, someone asks you to help a new colleague. Or maybe you are already a manager. You have many one-on-one meetings and performance reviews. You often feel like you are just guessing what to do. Many engineers become managers without any training. They have to learn how to turn people who work alone into strong teams. They also need to stay technical enough to be respected.

Going from writing code to leading teams is not a simple path. It has many different steps. Each step needs new skills and new ways of thinking. A manager who still wants to be the best coder can slow down the whole team. A tech lead who does not give tasks to others will get very tired. A senior leader who forgets to treat people well makes everyone scared.

In this summary, you will learn what good management looks like. You will also learn how to be a good manager yourself. You will learn how to go from being a mentor to a tech lead, balancing coding with leading. You will see what changes when you manage people directly. Also, you will learn how senior tech leadership needs a big change in how you work and talk to others.

## Blink 1 - How to be managed

You want to be a good manager. But do you know how to recognize one?

We have all met bad managers. Some managers do not pay attention until problems are too big. Others try to control every small task and decision. And some managers even yell and try to scare people. 

Good managers are very different. They really care about you as a person, not just as a tool for work. They spend time teaching you important skills. They help you ignore distractions and focus on what is truly important. How does this look in real life?

Start with the one-on-one meeting. This is not just a meeting on your calendar. It is where you and your manager connect as people. This connection builds trust, which makes everything else easier. These meetings also give you a private place to talk about anything. Be ready for it. Think about what you want to discuss with your manager before you go.

Giving and getting feedback is very important. Bad habits grow quickly, so it is good to fix them early. A great manager tells you about problems quickly. They also tell you when you do small things well. They help you understand what you need to do to get a promotion. But they cannot know your goals. If you want to move forward, you must tell them.

Growing in your career is more than just getting promoted. Your manager helps you with company rules and finding training or chances to grow. But you also have a part to play. You need to decide what kind of growth you want. You need to know what will make you happy in your job. If you are not happy, say so. If you want a chance, ask for it clearly.

Remember that managers are human too. They make mistakes. They are not perfect. They try to do good work, just like you. This does not mean bad management is okay. But it means you should have fair expectations.

When you look for a new job, think carefully about your possible manager. Look for someone who knows how company politics work. Look for someone who can truly help you grow. You are not looking for a friend, or someone whose code you like. You are looking for someone who can manage well.

## Blink 2 - Becoming a mentor

You might first learn about managing through mentoring. For example, you might help a summer intern or a new college employee. This is not formal management, so the pressure is lower. It's a great way to learn management skills.

First, help your mentee succeed. For an intern, choose a project that is important but small enough to finish. Find small tasks from your own work that take a few days. Then, sit with your intern and divide the project into clear steps. Be very available in the first few days. After that, check in with them regularly as they learn.

Here you will learn that *listening* is a basic management skill. This means more than just polite nodding. It means truly paying attention to understand others. When your mentee talks, listen for what they really mean, not just the words. Watch how they act. Do they avoid looking at you? Are they sighing? These signs tell you when someone feels confused or stuck, even before they say it.

Clear *communication* is also important. Be ready to explain difficult ideas many times, in different ways. Use a whiteboard. Draw pictures. And tell them your expectations clearly from the start. For example, should they try to solve a problem for an hour before asking for help? Or should they ask you right away if they are stuck? Not being clear causes problems.

Some people work very fast and make messy work that needs to be fixed. Others work slowly and make every detail perfect. As you see these different styles, you will need to adjust how often you check in and how much help you give.

Mentoring a new college hire uses these same ideas in a new way. You teach them tech skills. But you also help them understand the company culture. This means the written rules and the unwritten ones. It means the official company chart and how people truly work together. You also help them meet people and build relationships.

Mentoring lets you look at your own habits. Are you ignoring your mentee’s needs because you want to do your own tech work? Are you taking over your mentee’s work to show them "the correct way"? Stop these habits now. Otherwise, they will become a management style that hurts the people you should be helping.

## Blink 3 - The role of the tech lead

Moving from mentor to tech lead means you have two main roles. You still write code with your team. But now, you also guide the team. You make your own decisions. You talk to people outside of engineering in the company. And you are responsible for results that are bigger than just your own coding.

Good tech leads are skilled at *project management*. This means taking a task or idea and breaking it into clear parts. Your team will then know exactly what to build. You find out which systems need changes. You also see which new features are needed and how everything connects. You must be good at seeing what depends on what else. This means knowing what needs to be done first, and what can be done at the same time. You also look for hidden problems that could stop everything if you don't see them early.

Problems will happen; they always do. The most important skill is knowing when to stop your own work. You need to step back and see what your team truly needs. Engineers often want to solve every problem themselves. This is probably why you became a tech lead. But as a tech lead, you must learn to give tasks to others. If you don't, you will get very tired, and you will slow down your whole team.

Good tech leads have certain qualities. They understand their system very well. They know how their part works, and how it fits into the whole system. They are true team players. They do not just choose the exciting work. The common maintenance tasks often show where problems in the process are. Finding a balance is important here. This means balancing what you do yourself and what you give to the team.

Good leaders also know that you must lead in technical decisions. But leading does not mean just telling people what to do. Your team should share their ideas on decisions that affect their daily work. Their ideas make the decision better. And their support makes sure the work gets done well.

Communication skills become very important, not just good to have. You connect your team to the rest of the company. Practice writing clear notes. Get better at listening – truly listening – to what people mean, not just what they say.

Be careful of becoming a "process boss." This is someone who thinks a perfect process can solve all team problems. Processes are helpful, of course. But remember you work with people who think in different ways. Some people like clear rules. Others feel trapped by too many rules. Make processes to help your team, not the other way around.

## Blink 4 - Managing people

So, you have moved from tech lead to actual manager. Well done! People now work directly for you. This means their growth, their problems, and their daily work are now your responsibility. The first question is: How do you get to know someone well enough to manage them properly?

Ask specific questions that go beyond simple first-week chats. Does this person like being praised in public or in private? What truly makes them happy about working here? Are there certain manager actions they really dislike? These answers help you know how to support them. Some people like a 30-, 60-, or 90-day plan. Just make sure that more experienced new hires can help create their own plan for starting. 

No matter how you start, communication is key. Your new team member needs to understand what you expect. You also need to understand what they expect. How will you meet and share news? How often will you check their work? Decide these basic things early.

The regular one-on-one meeting is your main management tool. These meetings can be different, depending on what is needed. Sometimes, it's a list of tasks where you both have topics to discuss and work through them by importance. Other times, it's a feedback meeting for casual advice. If you manage other managers, many one-on-ones become updates on their projects. But no matter the meeting type, always try to know the person as a real human. Don't just see them as someone who does work.

Performance reviews are often disliked by managers. But the review itself is only the last part of a longer process. It starts with *regular feedback*. This means often sharing both good and bad things you notice. This teaches you to handle hard talks step by step. It's better than saving everything for one big, important meeting. Know your team members well: their goals, their strong points, their weak points, and where they can grow. Start with good feedback because it's easier and nicer. This helps you get better at giving feedback before you deal with harder topics.

When it's time for a review, give yourself enough time to write it well. Use clear examples. Give plenty of praise. Make sure negative feedback is clear and exact, not vague or general.

Sometimes you might need to fire someone because they are not doing well enough. The main rule here is: no surprises. Especially no bad surprises. Tell someone early if they are not meeting expectations and help them improve. If they don't get better, at least they will know why it happened.

## Blink 5 - Senior management

Let's look at a higher level: senior technical manager. The company now looks to you for advice on what to do, how to act, and future plans. People join because they trust you and your ideas. You decide the way the company works.

This could mean you are a VP of Engineering or a CTO. These roles are different. A VP of Engineering is at the top of the engineering career path. You need to be very good at managing people, teams, projects, and whole departments. You also need good "ground game." This means you can get involved in small details when needed. You will have some part in business plans, but less than a CTO.

The CTO role is different. This job is about business plans first, and technology second. You are a senior leader first, who also happens to be technical. Your job is to find ways to start new businesses using technology. Or to make sure your technology always changes to help the company grow for a long time. Planning the company's future is your main responsibility.

So, how do you make a plan for the company? Start with a lot of research. Think about your team, your technology, and how your company really works. Find out what problems your engineers have. See where your company struggles with growth. Find out where other leaders expect the company to expand. Then, use a whiteboard. Plan how you can help your company grow. Draw a possible future with clear steps that people can actually do. 

Before you give your plan to the board, make sure it fits their way of communicating. You might like simple slides and lots of talking. But they might want a detailed document to read on their own.

Here is what changes at a senior level: You are not just part of the team anymore. You are the one in charge. Everything you say is very important. People will watch your every reaction closely. To manage well, you need to keep some distance. If you don't, people might think you have favorites. Or they might take a small comment too seriously and change all their work because of it. 

But keeping your distance does not mean treating your employees like machines. You still need to treat them like people. They need to feel that you care. This is very important when you share bad news. For example, big layoffs, teams closing, or major company changes. Do not send an email to everyone. Do not bring everyone into one room at the same time. That feels cold and breaks trust. Instead, talk to people one by one. Change how you speak for those who might react strongly. Be honest about what will likely happen. Do not make things seem better with false hope.

Being a senior manager has a lot of responsibility. But you can do it well and still be kind to people. The challenge is to do both at the same time.

## Final summary

In this summary of *The Manager’s Path *by Camille Fournier, you learned that managing in tech is not just one skill. It is many different steps, and each needs different abilities. It begins with knowing what good management means: regular one-on-one meetings to build trust, giving feedback on time, and truly caring about your growth. 

Your first time leading often comes from mentoring. Here, listening is more important than being a brilliant coder. As a tech lead, you balance writing code with managing projects. You learn when to step back and give tasks to others, instead of solving every problem yourself. Managing people directly means really knowing them. It means giving feedback all the time, not just in reviews. It also means making sure no one is surprised by bad news. At senior levels, your words are very powerful. You need to keep some distance, but without treating people like robots. At every stage, good management brings together technical skills, human connection, smart planning, and practical action.

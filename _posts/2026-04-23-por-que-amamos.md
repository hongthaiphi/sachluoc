**Author:** Helen E. Fisher | **Category:** Psicología## ¿QUÉ ME APORTA ESTE TÍTULO? DESCUBRE POR QUÉ NOS ENAMORAMOS

All of us feel the strong experience of love. We feel happy when we find our soulmate or sad when we face rejection.

But what is love exactly?

In this book, biological anthropologist Helen Fisher explains how our romantic feelings, sexual desire, and attachment come from our brain.

Besides giving us a deep view of what love is, she explains how and why evolution gave us the ability to love.

Are you ready to learn how love works?

Then, let’s start.

## LAS CARACTERÍSTICAS DEL AMOR ROMÁNTICO SON UNIVERSALES

When we are in love, we feel like we are living something unique just for us.

But if we look back in time and around cultures, there are signs that everyone feels when they fall in love.

One common sign is how much we care about the people we love.

We focus strongly on our partner. They fill our thoughts, dreams, and actions.

We think they are perfect and special. We see everything they do, even their faults, in a positive way.

For example, the person you love may have crooked teeth or a speech problem, like a lisp.

And even though these faults are usually seen as unattractive, you see them as part of their charm or uniqueness.

These feelings of love are shared by people everywhere.

The author's studies have shown that these love experiences are the same for all people, no matter their age, culture, or sexual orientation.

But there are surely differences in how love is felt around the world, right?

Yes, there are, but when differences exist between societies, clear cultural reasons explain them.

In one of the author's studies, many more Japanese participants than Americans agreed with the sentence "When I talk to [the person I love], I often fear saying something wrong."

This can be explained by the fact that in Japanese society, meeting a person of the opposite sex is more formal and less common than in the United States.

So, this shyness comes from culture, not from a difference in love itself.

## EL AMOR ROMÁNTICO ES EL RESULTADO DE PROCESOS QUÍMICOS EN EL CEREBRO

For thousands of years, people have asked what causes love.

Some think it is a deep spiritual thing, but modern science shows it is caused by chemicals in the brain.

Specifically, love happens because of three important brain chemicals: dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin.

Dopamine is one of the main chemicals linked to romantic love feelings.

It is one of the strongest chemicals that affect your mood. It controls attention, motivation, and addiction, which are all important parts of falling in love.

Dopamine helps explain why love feels addictive.

Scientists have shown that when you are with someone you love, your brain acts like when you take drugs like cocaine.

Dopamine floods your brain, making you feel very happy and eager to feel that again.

That is why people in love feel dependent and miss their loved ones like addicts miss their drugs.

The next important chemical is norepinephrine, which acts like dopamine in some ways.

The excited and happy feelings with love, like butterflies in the stomach or a fast heartbeat, come from norepinephrine.

But this chemical also causes some unpleasant feelings: it often makes it hard to sleep and can lower appetite when you are in love.

The last key chemical is serotonin.

Serotonin causes restlessness and thinking a lot about the person you love.

But unlike the other two chemicals, serotonin levels are actually lower when you are in love.

This happens because serotonin goes down when dopamine and norepinephrine go up.

In this case, less serotonin means more obsession with the loved person.

## ADEMÁS DEL AMOR ROMÁNTICO, HAY OTRAS DOS FORMAS DE AFECTO: LA LUJURIA Y EL APEGO

In the last section, we learned that brain chemistry helps us feel romantic love.

But romance is not the only way we feel love or desire.

There are two other kinds of feelings: lust and attachment. These are linked to special hormones.

Let's start with the most physical one.

Lust is our sexual desire for someone, caused by the hormone testosterone.

The more testosterone is in our body, the more sexually excited we feel.

Then, there is attachment. This means feeling happy and safe when we are close to a loved one.

Attachment is linked to higher levels of vasopressin and oxytocin, hormones called the "hug chemicals" because of how they make us feel.

Each of these feelings can happen alone or together in many different ways.

For example, romantic love can lead to lust.

This is because the rise in dopamine and norepinephrine that causes love can also increase testosterone.

But it works the other way too: sexual desire can lead to romantic love.

This happens because hormone links go both ways, so more testosterone can help increase the chemicals that cause romantic love.

So next time you think about a casual sexual relationship, be careful, because it might lead to something more!

## A TODOS NOS ATRAEN EL MISTERIO, LA DIFERENCIA Y LA SIMETRÍA

Everyone has a different idea of their perfect partner: some like short people, others tall, some thin, others not.

But some things attract all of us, no matter what.

One kind of person we all find attractive, no matter our gender, is someone different from us.

We find different people mysterious and new. This makes them more desirable.

This desire for new and different things is inside our brain: newness can raise dopamine levels, which, as we saw, leads to romantic love feelings.

But why did evolution make us want people different from us?

The answer may be in how our genes and immune system work.

Scientists have shown that when two parents have different DNA, their child is less likely to get illnesses than a child of parents with similar DNA.

This explains why we are attracted to people different from ourselves.

Proof comes from the "sweaty T-shirt experiment," where women smell men's worn shirts.

Results show the "sexiest smell" comes from a man with a different but compatible immune system to the woman.

Another attraction bias is wanting partners with certain body proportions.

People with symmetrical bodies and faces are seen as more beautiful than those with uneven features.

This is because seeing symmetry makes more dopamine release in our brain.

Another example is the waist-to-hip ratio in women.

Research shows men find a 70% waist-to-hip ratio very attractive.

As expected, this is the common proportion of Playboy models.

## LOS HOMBRES TIENDEN A JUZGAR A SUS PAREJAS POR SU APARIENCIA, MIENTRAS QUE LAS MUJERES BUSCAN PAREJAS INTELIGENTES Y EXITOSAS

As we saw earlier, all of us share the same biology that makes us feel love.

But this does not mean men and women feel love in exactly the same way.

There are important differences between them.

One big difference is what qualities we look for in a partner.

When men fall in love, parts of the brain linked to seeing things are very active. So men are mainly attracted to how a partner looks.

This means men judge their partners mainly by appearance.

In particular, men want women who show youth and beauty signs, such as smooth, soft skin.

Why these signs?

Because youth and beauty show high estrogen levels in a woman, which is a sign of good reproductive health. This means the possibility to have many children.

Choosing a partner based on looks means men fall in love quickly, some say even at first sight.

Women, however, take a longer view with love and want partners who make them feel safe.

Women are attracted to men who show success qualities, like high intelligence and money security.

This is because women have to go through pregnancy and birth, a long, hard, and stressful process.

So a successful man is attractive because he can care for a woman during that hard time.

## TODOS TENEMOS UN «MAPA DEL AMOR» QUE RECOGE LAS CARACTERÍSTICAS QUE NOS PARECEN DESEABLES EN UNA PAREJA

As we grow older, we start to learn what we especially like in our partners: a big smile, green eyes, or a good sense of humor.

As these details add up over time, they form a "love map": an unconscious list of all things — like eye and hair color, personality type, and sexual preferences — that we find attractive.

But where does a love map come from?

Your particular love map comes from your life experiences, so it is unique to you.

No one else has the exact set of likes and dislikes you built from your unique experiences.

We know this because studies show that even identical twins with similar values and interests have their own love preferences — in short, they have their own love maps.

So, what exactly does your love map do?

This map guides you to fall in love with a certain person.

Let's explain with a thought experiment.

Imagine you enter a room full of strangers.

You look around and see many possible partners, but you probably won’t feel attracted to all of them.

Instead, there will be a few, or maybe just one, you feel strongly attracted to.

That is the person who fits your love map best.

## LOS ORÍGENES DEL AMOR ROMÁNTICO SE REMONTAN A HACE 3,5 MILLONES DE AÑOS

Have you ever wondered where the idea of romantic love comes from?

The answer is in our evolutionary history.

About 3.5 million years ago, humans made a big evolutionary step by starting to walk upright.

Though this gave many benefits, walking on two feet made mothers carry their babies in their arms instead of on their backs.

And since their hands were busy, mothers couldn’t gather food or quickly run from predators.

This meant they needed someone, a partner, to stay with them and help keep the children safe.

This led to a primitive form of romantic love called serial monogamy, where couples stayed together for a time and then moved on to other partners.

Why serial monogamy?

Probably our early ancestors did not stay with one partner for life, only for the time to raise a child through childhood, about four years.

After that, the couples separated and formed new pairs.

This was good for the group’s genetic health, because more children with different partners increase the gene variety in a population.

This benefit of serial monogamy may have started modern romantic love.

As couples raised children, they began to feel a mutual attachment.

While often this attachment faded when children grew up, sometimes it made partners stay longer together to have more children.

Even if the brains of early ancestors did not allow love as we feel it, this change probably led to the deep bonds we now link to romantic love.

## A MEDIDA QUE LOS SERES HUMANOS EVOLUCIONARON, TAMBIÉN LO HIZO SU CAPACIDAD PARA EL AMOR ROMÁNTICO

In the last section, we learned that the first "humans" who felt romantic love probably felt it in a primitive way.

But as we evolved, our brains grew more complex and our ability to feel love grew too.

About 1.8 million years ago, a big evolution happened: language appeared, the ability to express with words.

You could say there is no better way to show romantic love than with language.

We can be sure love grew in early humans when this skill appeared.

They could court, joke, and praise each other with stories, songs, and chats — things only possible with language.

As humans kept evolving, brain size grew more, which developed the love experience more.

Brain growth happened partly because of cooking.

By learning to cook food, early humans ate more calories, allowing bigger, energy-hungry brains.

One brain area that grew was the caudate nucleus, linked to our drive to seek rewards.

This made people try harder to find a good partner and may explain why some do anything to be with their loved one.

Bigger brains also made parents stay together longer.

But bigger brains meant larger skulls, which caused more problems at birth.

To fit through the birth canal, babies had to be born before their brains and skulls fully formed.

Being born less developed meant a longer childhood until children could take care of themselves.

This made couples stay together longer, strengthening romantic love feelings.

## LAS CITAS EMOCIONANTES AUMENTAN LA SATISFACCIÓN EN LA RELACIÓN, E INCLUSO PUEDEN DESENCADENAR EL AMOR

We all know romantic love has ups and downs: one day you feel very attracted to someone, the next day distant and indifferent.

Love seems to have its own rules, but that does not mean it is uncontrollable.

In fact, you can do some things to keep love alive, like doing new and exciting activities.

As we saw before, dopamine is a chemical that excites romantic love feelings and is released when you do something new or exciting.

This means you can use the power of excitement to make love feelings stronger.

This idea was studied in 1974 by psychologists Arthur Aron and Donald Dutton, who looked at how exciting situations can start love.

They did an experiment on two bridges: one low and steady concrete bridge, the other a shaky rope bridge over a ravine.

They asked several men to cross one of the bridges, where a female researcher waited.

She asked some questions and then offered to give her phone number if they had questions.

The men who crossed the dangerous bridge were more likely to call her later.

Why?

Because the exciting situation where they met sparked romantic love feelings.

Another way to keep romantic love high in a long relationship is sexual intimacy.

Sex increases testosterone production, which can cause dopamine release.

Also, orgasms flood the body with love-related chemicals: vasopressin in men and oxytocin in women.

So if you feel your love feelings fading, there is definitely something you can do to bring them back!

## CUANDO TE HAYAN RECHAZADO, OBLÍGATE A MANTENERTE ACTIVO

As most people know, few things hurt more than being rejected by someone you love.

It can make you feel sad and want to lie in bed all day.

But instead of giving up, you should stop feeling sorry for yourself and take action.

In fact, this is the only way to reduce the pain.

Why?

Because being active keeps you busy while you get away from the person you love.

When you get rejected, your brain chemistry reacts almost the same as when you are desperately in love: serotonin goes down and you think a lot about the person who rejected you.

But instead of giving in and contacting your loved one, you have to stay strong and keep distance by finding many distractions!

How?

As Alcoholics Anonymous members say: "One day at a time."

Tell yourself you won’t contact your loved one today and stay busy by, for example, going out with friends.

Just committing to daily tasks, like washing dishes or going to work, will keep you busy and away from the other person.

Another benefit of staying active is that it helps improve your mood.

Since new experiences release dopamine, doing something new and exciting, like climbing, skateboarding, or bungee jumping, will help you deal with bad feelings.

Also, exercise raises serotonin levels, and some doctors say exercise can work as well as therapy or antidepressants for treating depression.

So if you feel low, get up and do some exercise, and you will start to feel better right away!

**Author:** Gisèle Pelicot | **Category:** Mindfulness & Happiness## WHAT'S IN IT FOR YOU: THE STORY OF A LIBERATION

When Gisèle Pelicot took her rapists to court in 2024, the eyes of the world watched this gentle, quiet woman. She insisted on a public trial to show how many sexual crimes happen to women. With this brave choice, Gisèle started a worldwide discussion about sexual violence and how society handles it. In her autobiography, published in 2026, she tells how she experienced everything. We read her story and made a short summary – so her courage and fearlessness will not be forgotten. Before we start, a warning: This summary talks about a very serious case of sexual violence. Please only read if you feel ready emotionally.

## THE MOMENT OF TRUTH: GISÈLE'S LIFE FALLS APART

“That is you in the photo.” On November 2, 2020, at the police station in Avignon, a whole life falls apart with these words. Gisèle Pelicot, retired, married, living in Mazan in southern France, is speechless. She had not expected this. A few weeks before, her husband Dominique was caught secretly filming women in a shopping center with his smartphone. He cried and confessed to her: “I don’t want to lose you.” Gisèle comforted him. She said it was embarrassing but could be fixed with an apology. She made him promise to go to therapy. A few weeks later, she got a police summons. She thought it was still about the shopping center incident and defended her husband. She said Dominique was a good man: loving, caring, and reliable. Then the officer showed her pictures taken in their bedroom: a woman lying still, surrounded by strange men who were raping her. At first, Gisèle said, “No, that is not me. It can’t be.” But looking closer, she had to believe the officer. The woman in the pictures was indeed her: Gisèle Pelicot. The police found thousands of photos and videos on her husband's computer. They were carefully saved, dated, and named. They showed that Dominique had drugged his wife, made her unconscious, and invited men to rape her at home for years. Gisèle was frozen with shock. When the officer asked, “Do you want to press charges?” she said mechanically, “Yes.” Back home, she cleaned, washed clothes, and vacuumed as she always had. Inside, everything was breaking, but outside she held on to habits and routines. She tried to hold on to the life that had existed just hours before. But then came more horror. The terrible pictures answered questions she had for a long time: Lately, it seemed her brain was failing. She forgot important dates, names, and often could not remember when she had last been to the hairdresser. Her children worried because she sometimes acted confused and distant. Doctors found no clear neurological cause. And her husband? He acted calm and said it was normal because she was getting old. But the investigation showed a terrible truth. Police found many blister packs of drugs hidden in old hiking boots at the Pelicots' home. Gisèle remembered strange tasting drinks her husband gave her. She thought nothing of it and smiled kindly about his clumsy care. Now she understood why she often woke up with a heavy head, pain in her lower belly, and memory gaps. It was not a strange illness but years of poisoning. The gynecological exam gave more answers: doctors found several bacterial infections – sexually transmitted diseases caused by the abuse. There were no tears or injuries because the drugs made her body limp during the attacks. For Gisèle, this truth was both relieving and devastating. Relieving because now she knew the cause of her memory gaps and pain. Devastating because it was not illness but the cruelty of someone she trusted with her whole life. The man who went with her to doctors and held her hand in waiting rooms was the same man causing her pain. After that day at the police station, nothing was the same. Gisèle not only faced the ruins of her marriage but had to question her whole life: Which memories could she trust? What was true or false? And who was this man she was still married to?

## A YOUTHFUL LOVE AND ITS DARK SHADOW: GISÈLE LOOKS BACK ON 50 YEARS OF MARRIAGE

Summer 1971. Golden wheat fields wave under a wide blue sky. Dreamy forests and old castles are around. In the fairytale landscape of the rural Indre region, 19-year-old Gisèle dreams of her future and love. She visits her aunt, who just hired a young electrician named Dominique Pelicot. Gisèle first meets him in her aunt’s kitchen. He has curly, shoulder-length hair and wears a sailor’s sweater. Gisèle thinks he looks like singer Julien Clerc. She is shy because she got stung by a wasp on the forehead that morning and her face is red and swollen. Dominique is shy too. He speaks quietly and often blushes. Gisèle likes that. He seems gentle and kind. “He will love me,” she thinks. Both want love and safety. Both come from hard family lives. Gisèle’s mother died of cancer when she was nine. Memories of her mother’s illness and a grieving father shaped her childhood. While her home was full of pain and sadness, Dominique’s family was cold and cruel. His father was a violent man. His mother cried a lot. Little Dominique was often alone. When he met Gisèle, he first felt warmth, care, and gentleness. Gisèle and Dominique marry in 1973, poor but hopeful. It felt like the start of freedom. Gisèle recalls singing a popular song together: “C’est un beau roman, c’est une belle histoire” – it’s a beautiful story. At first, it was true. They had children, worked hard, and tried to succeed. Gisèle got a job at the energy company EDF. Dominique tried work in real estate. They were always close to financial trouble. Dominique made bad deals and debts. Sometimes the bailiff came and took things from the house. The more their outside life fell apart, the more Dominique wanted to control Gisèle – including sexually. He suggested things from porn magazines. Gisèle felt uncomfortable. Dominique mocked her. “You have no sexual fantasy,” he said once. Looking back, Gisèle now sees he wanted to control and own her. In the mid-1980s, Gisèle had an affair with a coworker. Dominique became angry and desperate. He grabbed her by the collar once, later threatened her drunk with a chair. Their marriage nearly ended. But they got back together. Gisèle believed in their relationship’s strength. She never thought he could hurt her or offer her to others. When Dominique was arrested, she brought him warm clothes to jail. She could not help it because this man who lied, cheated, and raped her was her life. But she knew there was no going back after Dominique’s crimes. So just before the trial, she divorced him. Gisèle Pelicot knew: her life was not over. She was ready for a new start.

## SHAME MUST CHANGE SIDES: GISÈLE MAKES A DECISION

“What name would you like as a pseudonym?” Gisèle’s lawyer asks to prepare her for the media. Every day, reporters ask questions – from serious journalists to gossip magazines. In France, victims of sexual violence usually have their identities protected. Gisèle must pick a different name for the press. She chooses Marie. It is her second name and the name of her maternal grandmother – a strong woman who always wore black as a widow and said carnations bring bad luck. Gisèle thinks of her wedding day when Dominique wore a carnation in his buttonhole. Was it a warning? The first headline appears quickly. On October 6, 2021, a tabloid magazine says: “Worse never revealed. A network of rapists in Vaucluse. He drugs his wife and offers her to other men.” Suddenly, Gisèle’s life is public. Under the article is a blurred photo of her husband. Gisèle knows the photo. She took it during a beach trip while watching their grandchildren. It is strange: her story is told, but she hardly recognizes herself. The media call Dominique the “Monster of Mazan” and Gisèle “poor Marie P.” or “the victim.” Legally right, but she fights this image inside. More details come out. Journalists got parts of the police files. Quotes from her first police interview and rape photos spread. Rumors against Gisèle appear. Some doubt her story: “How could she not notice for ten years?” people say online and on TV. Gisèle suddenly understands what many women face when they report sexual violence. Even with clear proof, people first ask if the victim is partly to blame. She is shocked how fast she is reduced to headlines. On TV, people call her the “woman raped 200 times.” Gisèle resists. She is more than that. If anyone tells her story, it should be her. She changes lawyers. The first lawyer gave many interviews and made the case political. Gisèle now chooses two new lawyers who work carefully and focus on the trial. The trial will be private. For a long time, she agrees. Why change? She does not want the spotlight. But one spring day, while walking and enjoying the sun, a sudden thought hits her: maybe the private court is not protection for her but for the criminals. She will face over 50 rapists alone, and no one outside will know what really happened inside. She understands: If the world must see the criminals’ faces, she must also show her own. A phrase returns to her mind from feminist protests: “Shame must change sides.” Why hide? Why feel shame? She did nothing wrong. The shame belongs to the men who raped her. Her lawyers are surprised. A public trial means huge media pressure and mental stress. But she stays firm.

## THE DAY OF DECISION: GISÈLE FIGHTS IN COURT

From the noisy crowd in front of the courthouse, a young woman moves forward. Tears run down her face. She struggles to speak. “I never could be as brave as you,” she finally says. Gisèle stops. “Please don’t cry. If you cry, I will cry too,” she gently replies and wipes the young woman’s tears. “And I must be strong.” The Pelicot trial has become more than a court case. It stands for the fight against violence to women and a vision of a better world. A world that no longer lets victims be silenced by shame and fear. Every day after court, people wait for Gisèle outside. They cheer and clap for her like an honor guard. But inside the court in Avignon, she faces the hardest test of her life. Not only must she face cameras and curious eyes, but the defense lawyers attack her strongly. Her lawyers warned her: “Be ready for anything, Gisèle.” Indeed, the defense tries to discredit her. They say she is an alcoholic or nymphomaniac. They claim her nodding or hip movements mean she agreed to the mass rapes. They say a sexually abused woman would not care so much about looking good. Gisèle now makes sure to dress well and prepare for each court day. Even words become a battlefield. A defense lawyer asks to stop using “rape” and say “sexual intercourse” instead. The judge sometimes suggests “sex scene.” Many of the 51 accused plead not guilty. Dominique Pelicot? He looks old behind a glass box. He does not deny what he did but tries to explain: For 40 of their 50 years, he behaved well. Only the last 10 years was he bad. Gisèle feels angry inside but cannot react because court rules say so. The worst moments are the videos shown in court. When screens turn on, Gisèle’s children leave, as she asked. Gisèle lowers her eyes to her phone, looking at pictures of her grandchildren and the sea. Things that calm her and stop her from breaking down. But she cannot block the sounds. She hears herself snoring and the rapists’ voices. How they whisper and groan, her husband’s sharp orders as he watches her unconscious body being abused. Silent and dignified, Gisèle endures. She will not show tears in public. Her children testify too. They speak of betrayal, disgust, and anger. Gisèle sits and feels her heart break. But she does not lose courage. She learns to fight the defense’s attacks. She says “rape” must be called rape and explains why the trial is public. Many victims stay silent because they fear humiliation like hers. It is a long, exhausting fight. But outside court, hope waits. People gather daily at the Avignon courthouse. They bring signs and letters. At home, Gisèle reads letters from women all over France: stories of violence, silence, shame, and fear. These letters remind her why she fights and give her strength to continue. Finally, the verdict day comes: Dominique Pelicot gets the maximum sentence, twenty years in prison. The other accused are also found guilty. When Gisèle, tired and worn, leaves the court supported by her lawyers and police, a giant banner flies on the city wall in Avignon. It shows just two words: “Merci Gisèle.”

## CONCLUSION

This was our summary of A Hymn to Life by Gisèle Pelicot. Her story is no longer just hers. It belongs to all women who faced sexual violence and hope for justice. Gisèle is now a feminist icon. But this is important: she never asked for that. She only wanted to be human, never a symbol. And above all, she never wanted to be only a victim. That is why she went public. With this step, Gisèle Pelicot freed many women from being victims and gave them a voice. She is an unwilling hero. Let’s tell her life as it is: a story of courage and freedom.

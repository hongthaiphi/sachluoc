### Synopsis

*Evil Robots, Killer Computers, and Other Myths *(2021) clears up the scary stories about artificial intelligence (AI). It explains how AI really works. And it shows why the bad futures you see in films are just imagination. It looks at today’s amazing AI tools, like face recognition and self-driving cars. It also explains why these systems cannot become the super-smart robots you see in movies.

## What’s in it for me? Realistic scenarios for our AI future.

Imagine this: the year is 2045. A super-smart AI has become aware. In a very short time, it has changed its own rules millions of times. It is now much, much smarter than any human. Within hours, it has taken control of everything important in the world. This includes power grids, money systems, and military weapons. Humans, once the top species, are not important anymore. The machines do not hate us. They just use things in the best way, and people are not efficient. This is called "the singularity." It's when AI becomes smarter than people and we cannot control it anymore.

It’s very scary, right? Luckily, this will probably not happen. Here’s why: today's AI cannot think, understand, or use common sense like people. It cannot move knowledge from one area to another. Even smart AI like 'deep learning' just finds patterns. It's not real intelligence. Today's AI is only for special tasks. It cannot become AI that thinks like a human and knows it.

This Blink will give you the real picture of AI and our future. It will show you the real problems it will cause. These include unfair computer rules, people losing jobs, weapons that act by themselves, and fake videos. It will also show you the problems that will only be in science fiction movies.

## Blink 1 - Existential threat or overhyped tech?

Back in 2011, IBM’s Watson was famous for beating human players on Jeopardy. Many people thought that artificial intelligence had finally become as smart as people, or even smarter. But here’s the thing: Watson could not really think or understand in any deep way. It was doing a very clever trick. It used patterns in numbers to find likely answers from its very big collection of information. There was no real understanding – just very clever calculations with numbers.

Since then, AI has become very well known. People have made more and more serious warnings about its effect on society. Elon Musk has called it “our biggest danger that could end us.” The late Stephen Hawking warned it could “cause the end of humans.” But are these fears true?

It's important to know the difference between AI and AGI. AI means Artificial Intelligence. AGI means Artificial General Intelligence. Today's AI is only good at special tasks. For example, it plays chess very well. Or it can find faces. Or it can guess text. But it cannot use this skill for other things. A system that wins easily at the game Go cannot suddenly decide to book your vacation. This narrow AI will never be a danger to humans. This is because it cannot really act for itself. It has no goals of its own. It cannot do more than its programming tells it.

More importantly, AI will probably never become AGI. Think about what thinkers call “the ghost in the machine.” This is the special feeling of being aware, knowing yourself, and having your own experiences. This is what makes humans, well, human. AGI would need real understanding, not just finding patterns. It would need real thinking, not just finding links between things. It would need to want to do things, not just to give the best answers. How AI is built now shows no way to achieve these qualities. AI uses information but doesn't feel anything. It gives answers but doesn't know what they mean. It does tasks but doesn't really choose to do them.

So, what's the main point? AI will definitely change how we live and work. But the stories about AI ending humanity are too big. The real task ahead is to manage the real effects of AI in a careful way.

## Blink 2 - The plausible threats of AI

So, a *Blade Runner *story, where human-like robots live with us, is not going to happen soon. But we do need to be realistic. Even with small abilities, AI will really change the world. Many challenges need to be carefully dealt with. What *should* we be worried about?

Let’s start with perhaps the most worrying use: weapons that act by themselves. AI improvements for drones are already being used. This includes software that knows faces and systems that find targets. The moral questions are very big when a human does not pull the trigger. These systems could break down, find the wrong targets, or be used without enough checks. There is also the risk of autonomous weapons spreading to groups that are not governments. Or they could make the world less safe. Luckily, groups like the United Nations are working to make basic rules for these systems.

Weapons aren’t the only area where AI security matters. Keeping computers safe also has its own problems. If AGI existed, it could cause big trouble. Imagine a super-smart system looking for every weak point in all computer networks at the same time. It could break into secret systems faster than humans could respond. Even today's AI could be hacked. But there’s still a person who can stop it. For example, in a self-driving car receiving bad orders. The good news? AI is already making computer security better by finding dangers faster than ever before.

Beyond security concerns, there’s the everyday risk of AI systems simply failing at important times. Imagine AI failing to change settings in a nuclear power station. Or medical AI missing signs of cancer. This isn’t only for AI. Computer errors caused the Mariner 1 launch failure and helped cause Three Mile Island. But AI is potentially more dangerous because it’s harder to test very carefully than normal computer programs.

Which brings us to a technology many of us will see and use ourselves: self-driving cars. Several deaths have happened because of self-driving cars. This is often due to broken sensors or programs that do not understand road conditions well. New rules are being made. But important questions are still not answered. These include: who is responsible, how to test them, and how cars should make fast safety choices. The technology is getting better. But the safety rules are still being created.

## Blink 3 - Adapting to the AI employment landscape

For many of us, the biggest worry is this: Will AI take my job? This concern is easy to understand. Losing employment has very bad effects on people. These go from money problems to feeling lost and not knowing your role. And many job losses spread out. This makes local economies worse and makes social help harder to give.

Now, if AGI existed, it could really cause many people to lose their jobs. A system with intelligence like a human in all areas could, in theory, do any thinking job a human can. This includes checking laws, writing stories, and planning for the future. But what about today's AI? Does it actually have these abilities?

Here’s some history: this isn’t the first time humans have faced dangers to jobs from machines. During the Agricultural Revolution, machine farming caused millions of farm workers to lose their jobs. The Industrial Revolution saw clothes makers and skilled workers lose their jobs to machines. More recently, computer writing programs stopped the need for many typists. And online shopping has closed many shops. The Bureau of Labor Statistics in the US says that jobs in shops went down by over 140,000 jobs between 2017 and 2020 alone. Each change needed hard changes from people. But none caused disaster. The economy changed and made new jobs.

So which jobs are at risk from machines now? Regular data typing, simple help for customers, and simple money checks are already being handled by AI. Looking further ahead, self-driving technology could eventually affect truck drivers and people who deliver goods. Millions of people do these jobs.

But here’s the other side: AI is also making new jobs at the same time. The tech industry is looking for people to train AI, to write good instructions for AI, and to check AI rules. Health care is making new jobs for people who understand AI medical reports and patient information together. Even creative industries are growing in jobs that mix human decisions with AI tools.

The important thing is to learn new skills and change. Companies are already giving classes on how to understand AI and use AI tools. Universities are putting AI skills into all their courses. Online platforms provide easy ways to learn new skills. This includes basic machine learning to how AI is used in different jobs. As the saying goes: AI won’t take your job – someone who can use AI will.

The jobs will surely change, but they will not be completely destroyed. History suggests humans are very good at changing. And this change will be the same.

## Blink 4 - AI that lies

In 2016, a chatbot named Tay was released on Twitter by Microsoft. Within 24 hours, it had learned to say racist and angry things. It was repeating the bad things it saw online. Tay wasn’t mean – it was just doing what it was designed to do: learn from patterns. But this showed a difficult truth: AI can trick people. This can happen through simple mistakes or planned bad use.

AI lies in many ways. Sometimes it simply makes things up. It creates information that sounds real but is completely false. Other times, it is used by bad people to make things that really trick others.

Take fake news. These are stories made on purpose to look like real news. They are made to change what people think. AI tools can now make many fake articles that look real. They come with quotes that seem real and made-up numbers. During the 2016 US presidential election, fake news stories on Facebook got more people to interact with them than top stories from major news outlets. This was an amazing 8.7 million shares, reactions, and comments compared to 7.3 million for real news, a BuzzFeed check showed. AI has only made this easier and harder to spot.

Even more worrying are deepfakes. These are videos or sounds made by AI that show people saying or doing things they never did. They look very real. In 2018, a deepfake video showed former President Obama giving a speech he never gave. By 2019, a deepfake detection company found nearly 15,000 deepfake videos online. That number was doubling every six months. The effects on changing politics, cheating, and bothering people are very big.

Then there’s the strange feeling we get from very human-like robots. Human-like robots like Sophia, made by Hanson Robotics, trick us just by looking like humans. She copies face movements, looks at your eyes, and talks to you. This makes it seem like she is aware and understands. As these systems become more realistic, they make it hard to trust. They can trick our feelings. And they make it hard to tell real human talks from fake ones.

How can these problems be made less bad? New rules are being made. The European Union’s planned AI law includes rules for showing how AI makes decisions. It also limits AI that tries to trick people. Asking for special marks on things made by AI, making better tools to find fake content, and making clear rules to tell people when they interact with AI systems are all important steps. Technology created these deceptions. Careful leadership can help us deal with them.

## Blink 5 - The trouble with data

In 2002, the Oakland Athletics surprised everyone in baseball. They used numbers to build a winning team without much money. This “Moneyball” approach found secret worth in players nobody noticed. It showed that making choices based on information could be better than just using feelings. It seemed like a plan for success in any field.

In 2007, Anne Milgram became chief legal officer of New Jersey. She used this same idea in the justice system for crimes. She wanted data to decide which people accused of crimes should be held before their trial. And which could be let go safely. The result was the creation of Computer Decision Systems, or ADS. These systems give risk scores to people accused of crimes. This sounds promising: fair, quick, and not showing human bias. But there are serious problems.

ADS are now used everywhere. Not just in criminal justice. They are used in deciding who gets a job, who gets a loan, how much insurance costs, and even checking on children's safety. Their influence affects millions of people every day.

Here’s where there are problems. In criminal justice, studies have shown that computer rules like COMPAS are much more likely to say Black people accused of crimes are high-risk. This is compared to white people with similar past crimes. A 2016 ProPublica investigation found that the system was wrong about Black people being high-risk almost twice as often as it was for white people. In hiring, Amazon found its AI hiring tool was always ranking job applications from women lower. This was because it had learned from old information that showed men were hired more often. The computer rule essentially put old unfairness into new choices.

The problem gets bigger in health care and money. Computer rules that decide insurance costs or if you can get credit often use area codes and other things that are linked to race and money. This unfairly marks certain areas as bad. A 2019 study in *Science* found that a health-care computer rule used on over 200 million Americans showed a clear unfairness based on race. It was giving less medical help to Black patients than to white patients who were just as sick.

What’s actually happening? ADS make the bad information they get into fixed rules. If old loan information shows unfair ways of giving loans, the computer rule learns to be unfair. If crime information shows too much police presence in some areas, the computer rule focuses on those same areas.

This shows what some people call “data fundamentalism.” This is the wrong idea that information is always fair and computer rules are always neutral. Information shows human choices, unfairness in society, and past wrongs. Computer rules make these patterns much bigger.

The solution needs strong rules that demand clear AI choices. It also needs regular checks for unfairness. And someone must be responsible when systems cause problems. Information can help make choices. But it should not make them by itself.

## Blink 6 - Are the machines coming for us?

Here’s the truth: AI brings important problems. As it is used more and more in society, careful changes and rules will be very important. But the scary science fiction idea of “singularity” is not a likely problem. That's when machines quickly become smarter than humans and we can't control them. This is not a likely problem, now or later.

Remember the difference between AI and AGI? AI is very good at small, special jobs. AGI would have human-level intelligence in all areas. Today's AI can never become smarter than humans in general. It’s a clever tool, not something that can think by itself. As for AGI? We don’t have that technology yet. And we probably never will.

Consider how the human mind actually works. Humans use common sense to think. We know that ice is cold without touching every ice cube. We use logic with symbols. We understand that if all mammals are warm-blooded and whales are mammals, then whales must be warm-blooded. We learn by combining things. A chef who knows “sauté,” “garlic,” and “spinach” can immediately create “sautéed garlic spinach.” They can understand how to cook many other things in the same way, without learning each one separately. AI simply can’t learn like this.

Current AI relies on completely different ways of learning. 'Supervised learning' teaches computer rules with examples that are named. For example, showing a system millions of cat photos called “cat” until it sees the patterns. 'Reinforcement learning' teaches by giving prizes and punishments. Like training a robot to walk by giving a prize for moving forward and punishing when it falls. This needs millions of tries to learn one skill well. Natural language processing finds number patterns in writing. Each way of learning is very specific. An AI trained to recognize cats can’t identify dogs without completely new training. It cannot use what it knows for new things. It has no flexible understanding. It only gets very good at one small task.

What about newer, more clever ways? 'Deep learning' uses many layers of computer networks. It finds more complex patterns in huge amounts of information. It goes from recognizing simple edges to complex shapes to entire objects. It has made amazing progress in seeing images and understanding language. But deep learning still works mainly by finding links, not by truly understanding. It finds what often happens from millions of examples. It does not know why it happens or what it means. Without really understanding why something happens or the situation, it is still just finding patterns on a large scale.

Here’s a helpful example: imagine a dog that thinks incredibly fast. It uses information thousands of times faster than any human. This is impressive, certainly. But no matter how fast that dog thinks, it will never understand jokes. It will not solve hard math problems. Or know why people cry at films. AI has the same basic limits.

So don't worry. The singularity isn’t coming in the near future.

## Final summary

The main idea of this Blink to *Evil Robots, Killer Computers, and Other Myths *by Steven Shwartz is this: AI brings real problems. These include weapons that act alone, fake videos, unfair computer rules, and people losing jobs. These problems need careful rules and changes.

But the fear of a bad future “singularity” is not true. That's when super-smart machines take control. This is because today's AI just finds patterns for small tasks. It does not truly understand, think, or feel.

AI will change society in important ways. But it does not have the basic ability to think like humans. Or to become the human-like AI you see in science fiction.

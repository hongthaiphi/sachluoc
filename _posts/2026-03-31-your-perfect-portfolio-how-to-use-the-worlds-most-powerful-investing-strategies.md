### Synopsis

*Your Perfect Portfolio* (2026) shows you how to choose your investments. It helps you find a plan that fits your personal feelings. It moves you away from trying to beat the market. Instead, it helps you build a money plan that works even when markets go up and down. By looking at different ways of investing, it gives you the tools to feel sure about your money in the future.

## What’s in it for me? Change money worries into feeling sure by linking your investments to time.

You probably work hard to earn money. But as soon as it's in your account, you might start to worry a little about what to do with it. The financial world offers many choices. Each one promises wealth, but often makes things unclear. You might be afraid of not getting rich enough. Or you might be afraid of losing everything. It's easy to feel that success is a hard puzzle you can't solve. Or that you don't have the strength to handle bad times in the market by yourself.

This Blink offers a way out. You will stop listening to the confusing talk about picking stocks. Instead, you will find a simpler way to manage your money. You will learn how to match your investments to your life's timetable. By the end, you will have clear ideas to build a money plan. This plan will work even when the world changes fast. It will turn you from a worried watcher into a sure builder of your financial future.

## Blink 1 - Why how you feel is more important than numbers

Let's go back in time for a moment. A thousand years ago, to be exact. Your ancestor bends down by a river to drink water. They look up. A gray wolf stares back from the other side. At that moment, their body fills with a strong feeling. Their heart beats fast. Every instinct screams one word: run. This feeling to fight or run away helped humans stay alive when there were dangerous animals.

But nowadays, you rarely see wolves. Today's dangers look different. They are the red numbers on a screen, or your money going down fast. And here's the thing: that same old feeling starts. You feel a strong need to run away. To sell everything. To run for safety. The problem is, running from a wolf might save your life. Running from a market downturn? That often makes you lose money.

This difference between our old feelings and our money is the first big problem in building an investment plan that actually works. We have old ideas and new technology. Yet we still feel like people from long ago. So the real work is not beating the market. It is controlling yourself. As people often say, the biggest problem for investors is usually themselves.

One strong way to calm that feeling is to change how you see yourself in the market. You probably call yourself an "investor." This word brings to mind big risks and big profits. But in exact money talk, investing means spending money on making things in the future. Like a company building a factory, or a person starting a new business. If you are not giving money directly to a business, you are not really investing. You are saving.

You earn money by working. Whatever you don't spend today, you save. This helps you buy things later. This difference is important. An investor feels pressure to beat the market, to try to get the most profit. A saver has a different goal: making sure money earned today can buy at least as much – or more – in the future. When you see yourself as a saver, you feel less stressed. So stop thinking of yourself as someone taking big risks. You are simply a person who looks after their own future.

Changing how you think helps you in real life. Money experts care a lot about the "best" way to invest. This is the one that gets the most profit from every risk, according to math. But numbers on a screen don't feel fear. You do. A plan that looks perfect on paper is not good if it changes so much that you give up on it when markets crash. It's better to have a good-enough plan that you follow, than a perfect plan that you give up on every time.

So, think of choosing investments as a way to protect your feelings. It's a defense from your own bad decisions. If you know a 50% market drop will make you sell everything in a hurry when prices are lowest, then only having stocks is a bad idea for you. By making a plan that fits how you feel, you stay in the market long enough to make money.

## Blink 2 - The problems with usual ways of investing

So you've started thinking like a saver. And you know the main thing is to control your own feelings. What comes next? You need a *starting point* – a normal choice to compare all other decisions against.

In theory, the simplest start is to own everything. If you could buy every single stock and bond in the world, in the right amount based on how much they are worth, you would hold what's called the *global financial asset portfolio*. This means doing nothing as much as possible. You are not betting on Apple more than Microsoft. Or the United States more than Japan. Or stocks more than bonds. You are betting on the total work and growth of all people.

That global market is about 45% stocks and 55% bonds. The good thing is that you don't need to try to be smart. You just take what the market gives you. But doing this perfectly is hard. That's why many savers prefer a simpler way: the Bogleheads’* three-fund portfolio*. This turns thousands of investments into just three parts. A total U.S. stock fund, a total international stock fund, and a total bond fund. By owning these three, you own a bit of everything instead of trying to find the best few things.

Now, here's where things get interesting. The most common way for many years has been the *60/40 portfolio*. This is 60% stocks (to grow your money) and 40% bonds (to keep it steady). On paper, this seems balanced. Like a seesaw that is a bit uneven. You might think that 40% in "safe" bonds means you are 40% safer. 

But risk is not simple math. In a 60/40 portfolio, stocks usually move up and down three times more than bonds. So they control what the portfolio does. A portfolio that is 60% stocks actually gets over 90% of its risk from only the stocks.

What does this mean for you? During a market crash, that 40% in bonds won't protect you as much as you think. If stocks fall very fast, your portfolio still has big ups and downs. This might make you panic, just like we discussed earlier. The 60/40 is not truly balanced. It's mostly stocks, with bonds slowing it down a little.

For savers who don't like big drops, finding out about these big ups and downs during a crisis can be very bad. Usual ways of investing are good to start with. But they are not as safe as they seem. When big things happen, smaller things follow along too.

## Blink 3 - Looking for growth and protection

Does trying to follow trends or seeing your money not grow for years sound tiring? Luckily there’s another path: making a strong plan so you don't have to escape bad times.

The usual way of investing leaves you open to big market changes. So, you naturally want to take control. You want to be smarter than the market. Or at least protect yourself from its bad times. This wish to do something different from others brings us to active plans. These are ways to change your chances based on facts.

To solve the hidden problems in a normal investment plan, you might look at *factor investing*. This means choosing stocks that are cheap (value) or that have been growing fast (momentum). These have often done better in the past. But this way of investing comes with a high emotional cost.

So, let’s try a different idea. If using past facts feels like driving only looking behind you, you might prefer looking through the front window. Normal index funds are based on how big companies are now. So you own more of companies that did well before. But the future economy will change. This is the idea behind a *forward-looking portfolio*. Instead of buying what's big now, you invest in what will be popular later.

Think about the big changes happening in the world. Technology is taking over old businesses. Many more old people are needing health care. New, growing markets are rising around the world. A normal index might be slow to show these changes. It might still have too much in old, fading industries. By putting more money into areas that will be important in the next thirty years, like new science and online systems – you're investing in what's coming, not what has been. Yes, it's more risky. It means more ups and downs today. But you get the chance to own big companies of the future before they are fully grown.

Now, even with clever ways to invest and future-focused plans, you still face the *risk of a big crash*. These are times when everything falls together. When people panic, spreading your money around often doesn't help. This is because all investments fall together. Stocks, real estate, and company bonds all get sold to get cash. To survive this, you need an investment that does well when things are messy.

This is called *trend following*. It doesn't care about company facts, profits, or money theories. It has one simple rule: buy what’s going up, sell what’s going down. If the stock market crashes, a trend follower doesn’t say it’s "too low to sell." They bet that the market will go down, and make money from it. 

For example, during the 2008 financial crisis, most usual investments lost a lot of value. But trend following ways of investing made a lot of money. Adding a bit of this to your plan gives you a way to make money that goes up when others go down. It's like something that helps you when you need it most. But of course, like any insurance, it has a cost. It often doesn't do as well when the market is calm and growing. It tests your patience until bad times come.

## Blink 4 - Changing to a safer plan

Does trying to follow trends or seeing your money not grow for years sound tiring? Luckily there’s another path: making a strong plan so you don't have to escape bad times.

Earlier, we talked about the secret problem with the usual 60/40 portfolio. Stocks move up and down so much more than bonds. They end up controlling most of the risk. Then there's *risk parity*. This is a way to fix this problem. It tries to make all parts of your investment equally risky. It uses some tricks to make bonds move up and down as much as stocks. This creates a real balance in the plan. No one investment type can ruin everything. The result is a calmer journey through good and bad economic times. You get profits without the big, scary drops of a stocks-only plan.

Now, if you want even more safety, consider the *permanent portfolio*. This means splitting your money into four equal parts. Each part is meant to do well in different economic times. Stocks when times are good. Long-term government bonds when prices fall. Gold when prices rise. And cash when the economy is slow. When the economy grows fast, stocks go up a lot. When prices rise fast, gold does well. When prices fall, bonds and cash keep their value. What's the downside? When the market is growing slowly, half your money doesn't grow fast. You see others make money quicker, but you stay safe and steady.

Sometimes the best way to protect your money is not about numbers at all. It's about how you feel. That's where *dividend investing* comes in. A dividend payment is not truly extra money. The stock price goes down by the same amount. So, your total money stays the same. But getting regular cash feels very different from selling parts of your investment. It feels like a paycheck. During a bad market, those dividend checks offer real proof that the company is still doing well. This can stop you from selling everything in a hurry when prices are very low.

One last safety plan is for the most risky time for a saver. This is when you get ready to stop working. This is when the *risk of when your money goes up and down* is biggest. If the market crashes just as you retire, you could lose a lot of your savings forever. The solution is called a *bond tent*. In the years leading up to retirement, you put much more money into bonds. This creates a short-term protection against big ups and downs. Once you've got through that important time without having to sell stocks for less than you paid, you slowly take down the tent. Then you put more money back into things that grow.

So, that’s your set of tools for safety. Risk parity to make your plan truly balanced. The permanent portfolio to protect in all economic times. Dividends to help you stay calm. And the bond tent to get through retirement safely. Each one gives up something – quick gains, being simple, or big profits. But they all have the same goal: making sure your money lasts your whole life.

## Blink 5 - Using investing based on time

Bond tents and permanent plans protect against certain dangers. But they show a deeper idea. That fight between wanting big profits and wanting to be safe? It isn't really about picking the right investments. It's about how time works. When you use stocks – investments that change a lot and are for a long time – to pay for school fees next September, you're taking a risk with a fixed time limit. The solution is simple: stop choosing investments by how much money they might make. Start choosing them by how long you need the money for.

So, what does that actually look like? Every type of investment has its own timing. Cash is for 0 to 3 years – it's for what you need right now. Bonds are for 5 to 10 years – they're for money you know you'll need soon. Stocks, even though you can sell them any day, are like a 15 or 20-year bond. That's how long it usually takes for company profits to recover from a very big fall in value.

Once you accept that the stock market is for money you won't need for 20 years, your view changes. You stop making stocks act like money you need right away. Instead, you make a plan that lets stocks work as the long-term growth tools they are.

The most useful way to do this is called the *flying ladder*. Picture your financial life as two separate parts working together. The first is a *bond ladder*. These are safe investments that pay you money at different times, one after another. One bond becomes ready this year to pay for living expenses, another next year. This can stretch perhaps ten years out. This ladder gives you a sure feeling. You know exactly how you’ll pay for groceries in five years, no matter what the market does.

Now, here’s where it gets interesting. Attached to this ladder is the "flying" part – your stock investments. Because the money for your costs for the next ten years is already safe, you can invest in riskier things. If the market crashes tomorrow, it's okay. You don’t need to sell those stocks to eat. You are using the money from your bond ladder. Your stock investments have time to grow, without being touched.

Keeping this plan going needs more control. This is done through what's called *rebalancing against the market's swings*. Most people change their investments based on the date. They sell stocks every December just because it's December. A better way is to change investments based on risk. When markets are going up fast and prices are very high, sell some of your best investments. And add more to your bond ladder. When markets are very low and prices are cheap, invest more. The bad times are likely over.

All of this leads to a freeing idea. The best investment plan is not a fixed chart from a book. It is one that matches your life's schedule. Cash for worries, bonds for payments, stocks for your future family. When your investments match your needs, ups and downs stop being scary.

## Final summary

In this Blink to *Your Perfect Portfolio* by Cullen Roche, you’ve learned that the secret to good money management is matching your investments to your life's time plan. This is what helps you deal with the natural ups and downs of feelings. 

Most people are savers rather than investors. This means the main aim is to keep your money's buying power, not to get big profits fast. Usual plans like the 60/40 split often don't truly balance things. Other plans like 'risk parity' and 'trend following' can be like important insurance when times are bad.

'Investing based on time' helps you choose investments by how long you need them for, not by how much money they might make. This solves the problem between risk and safety. You put money into different groups based on when you actually need it. By making your short-term needs safe with bonds and paying for your far-off future with stocks, you build a strong mind-shield. This lets you not worry about daily news and let your money grow over time.

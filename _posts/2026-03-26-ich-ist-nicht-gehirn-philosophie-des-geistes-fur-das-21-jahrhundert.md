### Synopsis

*I Am Not My Brain* (2015) asks if our mind, our 'self', and our freedom can be fully explained by science. Can consciousness, human freedom, and dignity be simply brain processes? Or do modern brain scientists miss something important? We will give you answers and explain the main ideas from the philosophy of mind.

## What You Will Learn: Why Philosophy Helps Us Understand What It Means to Be Human.

Imagine you automatically grab your phone in the morning, even before you are fully awake. Nobody forces you to do it. But it still feels almost unavoidable. Was that a free choice – or just a reflex? We all know such moments. We act without thinking much. And sometimes we ask ourselves: Could I have acted differently? Some brain scientists make us doubt this. They say our behavior can be fully explained. And that is by looking at the brain.

But is that true? Are we just the chemical processes in our brain? We will explore this question. And we will show you that brain science doesn't have all the answers. And that you cannot explain reality without philosophy.

## Blink 1 - The World Is Not Only Made of Scientific Things.

In the philosophy of mind, it first seems to be simply about "the mind." But more and more things that were once seen as "mental" or "having a soul" can now be explained by science. For example, the movements of stars were once seen as messages. But today, we understand them as mechanical processes. 

So, the question is: how do mental processes fit into a world fully described by natural laws? If we see the mind as something not physical, we quickly run into problems. This is because body processes follow the law of energy conservation. If a thought or a will should move an arm, it would need to bring in energy. But we cannot measure such energy anywhere. So, thinking of a separate mind is like imagining an invisible extra engine in the body – it just seems unlikely.

But on the other hand, there are things in life that cannot be described by physics, but are still real. For example, purchase prices are not objects. They are only conditions: You must pay price X to get product Y. Rules and laws are also not natural objects. But they affect our lives. And language cannot be reduced to molecules. But you still communicate with it. This means: Not everything that is real has to be part of the physical universe to exist.

An important difference between mental and physical things is their purpose. A heart's job is to pump blood. It does this without trying to achieve anything else. But a human acts not only for functional reasons. For example, in the supermarket, you think about which bread to buy: expensive organic or cheap pre-packed? 

You have several goals with this: taste, healthy eating, and a smart financial choice. Such goals do not belong to specific organs or body cells. Brains process information, yes, but they have no intentions. A brain has no intention. It is the person who has the intention and uses their brain to make it happen.

Anyone who believes actions can be fully reduced to brain processes mixes up two levels of description. The biological description shows what happens in the body. The action explanation shows what someone does and why. Both ways of looking at it describe reality.

The mind is therefore not a thing among things. It cannot be found like an object. Instead, it shows itself in how people relate to themselves, to others, and to the world. 

## Blink 2 - Consciousness Is More Than Just Processing Information.

Another problem for the idea that the world can be explained purely by science is our consciousness. It is not just another chemical process in the body. You can measure what happens in the brain. But consciousness is more complex.

You wake up, maybe remember dreams. You feel tired or angry. You focus on the coffee machine, traffic, or work. All of this is consciousness. We never experience it from the outside, like an object. Instead, we always experience it in a very personal way. Our consciousness belongs to us and no one else. 

This personal experience often leads to a common mistake. It's the idea that consciousness is like an inner stage where everything appears. And somewhere, an inner viewer watches the show. As soon as you think this way, you create an endless loop. Who sees the picture on the stage? And who sees the one who sees? This is like a cinema inside a cinema inside a cinema. Such ideas seem right, but in the end, they explain nothing. This is because they always need new observers.

You can see this clearly with perception. Imagine you look at a Buddha statue. You see it from your point of view. But someone else sees it from a different angle. However, if you then say that everyone only has a private movie in their head and no direct link to the outside world, it becomes hard. It's hard to explain how we can talk about a shared reality at all. 

Because then, even science would just be part of our private movie. This includes all experiments and measuring tools. You would try to check if a movie is true using things that are only in that movie. This is like buying several copies of the same newspaper. You do this to see if the truth is really in it.

Here, it helps to tell the difference between two sides of consciousness. First, there is consciousness as focus: You pay attention to things. You get information and think about something. This is the side where you connect with the world. 

Second, there is consciousness as experience: how heat feels, how red looks, how coffee tastes. These experiences are not neutral. Taste and color usually come with a 'I like it' or 'I don't like it'. That's why they are hard to share. Maybe two people call the same cube "blue". But their inner experience of blue could be very different. And you can never compare it for sure.

This difference becomes very important with artificial intelligence. Imagine a human-like robot that perfectly says a cube is blue. It can even correctly find a 'vanilla note' in wine. This can show that it processes information well. But is that enough for consciousness? Probably something crucial is missing: the experience itself. 

So, consciousness shows a side of the mind. It is not just about correct information processing. It is also about how we connect with the world and our personal experiences. 

## Blink 3 - Our Consciousness Also Connects to Ourselves.

Consciousness is about experiencing something at all. But *self*-consciousness is about how you feel about that experience. You are not just angry or happy. You notice it and judge it. You don't just have a thought. You can also see it as your thought. You can agree with it, reject it, or feel ashamed of it. This clearly shows that our inner life is not just a neutral flow that runs alongside life. It is full of feelings that immediately seem like judgments.

An everyday example shows this well. You remember that you haven't replied to a friend's email yet. At that same second, it's not just the information 'email not answered'. There's also a feeling: shame, dislike, maybe anger, maybe a guilty conscience. This judgment doesn't feel like an extra comment you add on purpose. It's there right away. A sunrise can make you happy. A watery coffee can annoy you. Losing in sports can make you feel very angry inside. In such moments, self-consciousness appears as a felt connection to yourself.

But self-consciousness is even more. It's part of your self-portrait: You create an image of yourself, and this image can change. Literature, language, and culture play a very important role in this. Over a long time, people have developed a more exact language for inner feelings. This allows experiences to become more detailed. Someone who has learned to 'read' colors, music, or art experiences them differently. A certain blue in a museum is then not just something you see. It's also linked to knowledge, comparisons, and meanings. 

What's also important is that self-consciousness doesn't only look inwards. You almost always have a feeling that others are also conscious. And your own judgments are often shaped by how others might see you. Shame is the clearest example of this. It's hard to imagine shame without thinking about how others see you, even if no one is in the room. You live in social groups from the very beginning.

This leads to a simple idea: Maybe self-consciousness only comes from others, through being recognized and seeing yourself in society. But there is a limit to this idea. To recognize others as conscious beings, you already need some form of consciousness and self-awareness. Also, you can't just 'install' self-consciousness into everything. A stone does not become a person just because people are present. 

Let's sum this up again: Your conscious states are almost never neutral. A thought is not just 'there'. Instead, it is good, embarrassing, annoying, forbidden, or beautiful. These judgments are not brain properties. They are part of your self-consciousness. 

So far, so good. Now let's get to the big question: What exactly is the 'self'?

## Blink 4 - The 'Self' Is Not Our Brain.

The question of the 'self' makes the basic problem even bigger. Is there something about the mind that cannot simply be explained by science? Often, the 'self' is seen like an inner control center. Neurocentrism is the idea that the mind and 'self' can be explained mainly by brain processes. This often leads to the conclusion: Because you cannot find the 'self' as a physical object in the brain, there is no 'self'.

A clear example is the idea that the 'self' is a simulation. More exactly, it's called a 'transparent self-model'. This means the brain creates a model of ourselves – of our body, how we see things, and what we can do. We don't experience this as a model, but directly as "I" or "me". The term "ego-tunnel" describes the feeling of looking at the world from a fixed inner view behind our eyes. But here is a problem: If the 'self' is a simulation, then it still exists as a simulation. Simulations are not just made-up things. A rainbow is also not a thing. But it is a real event under certain conditions.

There's also a point about how we study this. Anyone who says the 'self' is an illusion uses their own conscious experience. This inner view is not a measurement like blood pressure or brain activity. So, the question of whether the idea of the 'self' makes sense cannot be left to brain research alone. It is a question about ideas and meaning.

In philosophy, the 'self' is often understood in two ways. The Bundle Theory says: The 'self' is nothing more than its changing states. It's a collection of thoughts, feelings, and perceptions. The Substance Theory, however, believes there is something that *has* these states. Interestingly, neurocentrism also often ends up with a Substance Theory. Anyone who says the 'self' is the brain or specific brain processes still treats it as something that carries these processes – just described biologically. 

Another important idea comes from phenomenology. This is a study that describes how things are experienced. It highlights this: With consciousness – and so with the experience of 'self' – the difference between mere appearance and reality does not work like with outside objects. If you believe you are experiencing something, you are experiencing something. Even if you are wrong about the object itself. Dreams are the best example: They sometimes feel very real. And that's because they are: The dream itself is real, even if its contents later turn out to be just a dream. In this sense, illusion does not mean 'nothing'. It means a certain form of reality.

So, the 'self' is not a physical thing in the head. But it is still something very basic. It is the necessary point of view from which we can understand anything at all, like the world, nature, or knowledge.

## Blink 5 - Humans Are Free, Worthy Beings.

It is often said that new brain research shows our will is not free. Decisions, they say, are made in the brain even before we consciously make them. This easily creates a picture: The brain controls us, and our conscious 'self' just watches.

First, there is nothing shocking about this. Many things we do happen without us paying full attention. When you drive a car, you don't think about every movement. When you speak, you don't consciously think about which grammar rule you are using. If we had to think through everything clearly, we would not be able to act at all. So, unconscious processes are not a threat to freedom. Instead, they are necessary for it.

It only becomes a problem when someone makes a strong conclusion from this. Then they say: If everything happens in the brain, our decisions follow fixed natural laws. And what follows fixed laws cannot be free. This idea comes from a simple but misleading picture. It's the idea that nature works like a movie. As if everything is already decided, frame by frame, and we just play our part.

But natural laws don't work like that. They describe ideal conditions, not exact events. A law says how things *would* behave if certain conditions are met. It does not say what *must* happen in every real situation. Reality is not a script.

So, the main idea is different: Our actions have reasons. These reasons are not just physical causes, like a push or a reflex. They are often about society or rules. You buy a bread roll not because a force makes you. But because it is for sale, you are hungry, and you can pay for it. The price is not a cause, but a condition.

This shows what human freedom is about. We live in a world of rules, meanings, and expectations. In this world, we explain actions not just by causes, but by purposes. Someone goes shopping to get something to eat. They apologize to fix a friendship. They keep a promise simply because it is a promise.

Freedom and dignity exist in this space. Dignity does not mean being special in a biological way. It means being taken seriously as a person. Not as a thing you can replace, use, or control. But as someone whose actions can be understood because they have reasons.

And that is our task. Not to hope for a future without humans or without mind. But to make better the conditions under which people can act freely and live with dignity. 

## Conclusion

Modern brain research seems to suggest that consciousness, decisions, and even our will are just results of brain processes. But that is not true. 

Science only explains the biological conditions that make consciousness and thinking possible. But it does not explain what it means to have reasons, to chase goals, or to take responsibility. Anyone who mixes these levels confuses brain conditions with mental things.

Only if we take these mental things seriously can we also talk about freedom. Freedom does not mean acting like a robot. It means acting for certain reasons. Our decisions are part of a system of thoughts, judgments, and social explanations.

This is also where human dignity comes from. Dignity is not a feature you can measure. And it's not an inner object. It comes from people seeing each other as persons  who act for certain reasons and take responsibility for them. Anyone who reduces humans to just brain processes misses this important point.

The real problem is not brain research itself. It's a way of looking at the world that only accepts what can be measured by science. But consciousness, self-consciousness, freedom, and dignity are just as real as the chemical processes in our brain.

### Synopsis

The book *Arguing Under Stress* (2004) teaches you how to stay calm in stressful work situations. It shows you ways to handle unfair attacks well and calmly. Always treat others with respect and fairness. Do not attack people back personally.

## What you will learn: how to win without defeating others.

Maybe you have felt this before. In a price talk, a meeting, or a presentation, someone attacks you in an unfair and personal way. They might say, "How long have you been in sales?" or "You only care about your career," or "You cannot control your team."

People use many ways to scare others. This includes showing dominance, using unfair words, making strong criticisms, blaming others, or making threats. These methods are tricky. Many of us deal with them at work.

In *Arguing Under Stress*, you will learn how to respond to such unfair attacks. You will also learn how to stop them from happening at all.

Always remember this: If you attack back in the same way, you might win for a short time. But then you will have an enemy. This person will try to make your work life very difficult.

They will not forget how you made them feel bad. They will try to get back at you. So, you might win the argument, but you lose a colleague.

A better and smarter way is to not try to win, so you can truly succeed. Do not get into an emotional fight. Instead, try to get the attacker on your side. Help them to stop talking about personal feelings. Focus only on the facts and topics.

The following Blinks will show you how to do this. They will also tell you what else to remember in stressful work situations.

In these Blinks, you will also learn:

- why your body stops you from thinking clearly in stressful times,

- what method Gerhard Schröder uses to show calmness, and

- how to become a master of Argument Aikido.

## Blink 1 - Too much stress stops you from arguing well.

Imagine you need to give a speech. It is about making communication better inside your company. Your boss and other important people are there. You worked hard on your ideas. You even made your slides after work.

Suddenly, a colleague stops you. They say, "You don't believe that yourself. That's complete nonsense." You want to respond smartly, but you can't find the words. You feel angry. Your ears ring. You are confused and feel beaten. Hours later, on your way home, you think of a perfect reply. But it's too late.

This behavior is a common human reaction. In difficult or dangerous situations – like a fight, a car crash, or a loud attack – we get very stressed.

This stress can make you sweat or feel your heart beat fast. Most important, it can stop your mind from working well. Your brain does not work as well. You think slower. It takes longer to understand things and make good choices.

This happens because our body gets ready for action during stress. It focuses on physical actions, not thinking. Like our ancestors from the Stone Age, we react to danger by wanting to run or fight. For them, it might have been a saber-toothed tiger. The more stressed we feel, the stronger this urge is. And the harder it is to use our brains.

So, this is why you might not think quickly in tough situations. But there is good news. You are not helpless against stress. You can learn to manage stress better. This way, it will not affect you so much.

It is easiest to do this if you are calm.

## Blink 2 - Being calm comes from a positive mindset and helps you handle stress.

We all know those people. They stay calm even in very stressful times. They keep a clear head. They respond to attacks, even personal ones, with smart words. We often wish we were like them.

These people are very calm. They stay focused and sure in their arguments, even in difficult moments. They handle tough questions well. Even the most aggressive colleagues cannot upset them.

Many people think that calmness is something you are born with. But this is not true. You can learn to be calm. You do not need special speaking tricks. First, you need to have a positive view of yourself.

The best way to do this is to create positive beliefs for yourself. These are ideas you strongly believe in. For example, before an important talk, do not tell yourself, "I'm afraid I'll forget everything!" or "I look terrible today." Instead, tell yourself, "I will do my best!" or "I enjoy getting better at discussions."

These positive beliefs give you inner confidence and certainty. They help you go on stage feeling strong and deliver a good presentation.

Another key to being calm is to have a positive and respectful attitude towards others. For example, when you give a presentation, do not make your audience seem too important and yourself too small. Do not think, "A group of hungry animals is waiting for me, a small creature."

But it is also not good to make yourself seem too big and the audience too small. Do not think, "Everyone else knows nothing – only I do!" If you do this, you will either feel threatened and get stressed. Or you will seem too pushy and people will not like you.

A positive attitude towards others means you want to have a friendly talk, as equals.

## Blink 3 - Good preparation is key to speaking well.

Most stressful situations do not happen if you act confidently. The best way to be confident is to know your topic very well. Then you will have better points to make. You will find quick answers. Other people's critical arguments will less often make you feel unsure.

Do not rush to an important meeting at the last minute without thinking about it. Instead, take time to prepare well and deeply.

Start by setting your goals. What is the least you want to achieve in the meeting? What is the best possible outcome? These can be about the topic, like "getting approval" or "making decisions." They can also be about relationships, like "showing you can talk" or "making people like you more."

Next, think about the situation and the people involved. Who will be at the meeting? What is their situation? What do they think about the topic? What special things should you remember? This analysis is important. It helps you change your plan to fit the other people. A good speaker always connects with people where they are.

The final, most important step in preparing is to collect and rank your arguments. When collecting, look for points about benefits. How will your colleagues, the company, or your customers benefit from your ideas?

After you answer these questions, rank your points using the ABC method. A-points are things you must say. B-points are supporting details that help a main point. C-points are extra information. These can add to an argument, but only say them if there is time or if someone asks.

If you prepare in this way, you will go into the meeting feeling good and confident. The next question is: How do you present your ideas?

## Blink 4 - Get your audience on your side by being confident and friendly.

Studies show that our body language plays a big part in if people like us or not. It makes up more than 50% of how we are seen. Being liked is important. It helps people listen to you. Your arguments need a good foundation to work, no matter how strong they are.

So, for a good presentation, show positive signals and avoid negative ones. Positive signals include standing straight and having good posture. This shows confidence and skill. Look at your audience openly and calmly. This builds trust. Use clear language, with enough pauses, short sentences, and good examples. This helps people follow you easily. Also, show excitement and passion. But do not be too pushy or overwhelm your audience.

Negative signals are clear: bad posture, quick and nervous glances, difficult sentences with unclear messages, or seeming uncaring or unsure.

Also, avoid using "softeners." These are like "unforced errors" in tennis. These are mistakes made without pressure, like hitting the ball out or into the net. In speaking, softeners are useless words or phrases that make your message weaker. Examples are words like "actually" and "somehow." Or asking extra questions like "...or is the meeting time bad for you?" Or saying things that make you sound less skilled, like "I did a bit of research on that."

Using these softeners makes you less convincing. They also give critics easy targets to attack.

So, being confident and friendly is already half the battle. Then, your message just needs to be understood.

## Blink 5 - Use the 5-sentence method to make your message clear.

The French thinker Molière once said, "Anyone who speaks in a way that is understood, always speaks well."

The 5-sentence method may not make you a philosopher. But it helps you make your ideas very clear. Everyone will understand them, whether it's your boss, a colleague, or your partner.

It's like a plan to build an argumentative house. This plan has three parts: an introduction, the main part, and a conclusion. The main part has three steps.

The introduction is usually one or two sentences. It prepares your audience for your main points. For example, if you want to share ideas to improve customer service, your introduction could be: "Let me share three ideas to make our customer service better."

Next comes the main part. This is where you make your points. Our short-term memory can only handle about three points well. So, do not use more than three. When you present your points, put your best point last. In our example, it could be: "Every customer must have a customer service person assigned to them." This way, people will remember it best. Start your presentation with your second-best point.

The conclusion of the 5-sentence method is your main summary. It is the most important idea of your argument. It is the message you really want to share. For example: "To improve customer service, we must train our service staff regularly."

Using the 5-sentence method helps you make your points clear. It also makes it less likely that you will be attacked personally. This is because you will not give anyone a reason to attack your arguments.

You can never completely stop the chance of an attack. But you can get ready for it.

## Blink 6 - Use Argument Aikido to handle personal attacks.

So, you are well-prepared for the important meeting. You act confident and friendly. You present your good ideas for better customer service clearly. But there is still that grumpy person. They keep giving you bad looks. Then they get angry and say, "Your so-called plan is complete nonsense. You just want to climb the career ladder on the backs of your colleagues!"

How do you react to such a bold attack? Do not let the fighting colleague scare you into silence. But also, do not sink to their level and say mean things back. If you do, both of you lose. In the end, no one will talk about the meeting topic. They will only talk about your fight.

The best way to handle such personal, unfair attacks is to use Argument Aikido. Aikido is a Japanese martial art. It focuses on defense. The person being attacked learns to use the attacker's power. They use it to stop the attacker without hurting them.

For a spoken attack, this means: do not hit back. Instead, guide the attacker's energy towards the main topic. That's why everyone is there. For example, you could say to the attacker: "Your comment tells me you do not like my plan. What are your specific concerns about the topic?" Or, more directly: "I will not comment on your unfair attack. Let's discuss things fairly. What factual points do you have against it?"

This takes away the attacker's power. They will have to talk about the facts. Or they will have to quietly step back.

There are also other ways to handle difficult and aggressive people.

## Blink 7 - You can often calm attacks with guiding techniques.

Argument Aikido should always be your first choice for defense. But there are also other guiding techniques. These are speaking tricks for self-protection that you can use against an attacker.

One good method is "bridge sentences." They help you show calmness and gain time. You can use this time to guide the talk in the direction you want. Former German Chancellor Gerhard Schröder was a master of bridge sentences. He often responded to attacks by saying, "If I were to say what I could say now, we would all have a laugh." This way, he stayed factual, got people to laugh with him, and then continued with his argument.

If a colleague makes a strong claim and you cannot think of a clever reply, you can ask a question back. For example, if someone says, "My team does not like the new sales plan," you could ask, "What exactly are the concerns?"

With the "translation technique," you can change the direction of attacks. If someone attacks you by saying, "You treat your employees like dirt. They are scared and protect you out of fear!" you could respond: "The opposite is true. That is why my team fully supports me, and I am proud of that."

Another option is to redefine attacks. This shows you are quick-witted and takes power away from the attacker. If someone attacks you by saying, "You are a bean counter," you could reply: "If by 'bean counter' you mean someone who is careful and pays attention to details, then thank you very much for the compliment."

An unusual but useful strategy is the "confusion tactic." For example, you can use a proverb. If someone says, "You only care about your career," you could reply: "In my country, we say: 'In the evening, the lazy person becomes diligent.'"

After this, the attacker will be very confused. They will try to understand your strange answer. This will make them lose their focus. If they ask what the saying means, you can add: "You can only figure that out with deep focus. But why shouldn't you be able to do that?"

## Blink 8 - The Harvard Concept shows how to negotiate together, not against each other.

An Arab joke asks: "How much is two plus two?" The answer is: "It depends! Are you buying or selling?"

Negotiations can be hard. This is true whether in business or at a market. This is especially true when both sides argue strongly. They will do anything to win. Sometimes, this even means giving false information, scaring people, or using mind games. If you have been in such a negotiation, you know how tiring and sad it can be.

The Harvard Concept offers a different way. Its goal is to always negotiate by focusing on facts and working together.

First, the concept advises separating the person from the problem. This means you should talk about the topic of the negotiation without personal feelings or likes/dislikes. Personal attacks or blaming others only lead to problems. They make the other person angry and cause emotional reactions.

Also, negotiations should focus on the interests of the people involved, not just their stated positions. This makes it much easier to find a common solution. For example, an employee asks for a 500 Euro raise per month (Position 1). The boss offers no raise (Position 2). If you ask about their interests, you find out the employee will soon be a father. He expects more costs (Interest 1). But the boss cannot give a raise because the staff budget is already used up (Interest 2). A solution could be that the employee waits for the raise for one year. Or he could get more vacation days to be with his child.

Finally, if negotiations get stuck, it is good to use neutral standards. These could be market values, past examples, or expert reports, depending on what you are discussing.

## Summary

Good preparation and acting in the right way are key to handling discussions well. This helps you be calmer and better at handling stress. If personal attacks still happen, you should respond with facts. Use methods like Argument Aikido or other guiding techniques.

Here's what you can do:

**Stress harms your ability to argue, so do not let it start. **You can manage stress by becoming calmer and doing your homework well. Prepare well for presentations or meetings. Act confident. Speak clearly and to the point.

**When attacked, always focus on facts and get others to join you. **With Argument Aikido and good guiding techniques, you can take away an attacker's power. You do not have to attack back unfairly. The art of good arguing is to always focus on facts and solutions.

TIÊU ĐỀ: Your Best Meeting Ever  
TÁC GIẢ: Rebecca Hinds  
DANH MỤC: Communication Skills  
========================================

[WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? A SET OF SIMPLE RULES FOR FEWER, SHARPER MEETINGS THAT GET RESULTS.]  
  
Have you ever left a meeting feeling confused, not sure what changed because you were there?  
This usually means the meeting was not planned well.  
When meetings happen without care, they take too much time and blur together.  
The time you wanted to use for focused work gets pushed aside.  
Soon, your calendar controls your work, and progress slows down without you noticing.  
The good news is this is easy to fix.  
A few clear rules and habits can make meetings shorter and more helpful.  
In this Blink, you will learn how to reduce meeting overload and stop it from coming back.  
You will learn how to check if a meeting is really worth your time by using simple feedback and smart signs.  
You will also get practical advice to keep meetings short, interesting, and aligned with real work. This includes using technology and AI to make things easier without taking over.  
Let’s start with the first rule for good meetings: cutting meeting debt.  

[CUT MEETING DEBT TO RECLAIM YOUR WORKING TIME]  
  
In 2013, Dropbox looked at its calendars and tried something extreme.  
They removed all regular meetings overnight. For the next two weeks, new regular meetings were also removed.  
Some important meetings stayed, like recruiting and meetings with customers. But most meetings were gone.  
Teams found they could do much more real work when the calendar did not control them.  
This is the first rule for good meetings: cut meeting debt before it quietly takes your week.  
Meeting debt means meetings that stay on the calendar long after they stop being useful.  
You can fix meeting debt in five steps, starting with a calendar clean-up: look at your regular meetings, rate each one by its value and effort, delete all repeats for two days, then add back only the valuable meetings.  
Next, make it normal to protect your time.  
Some companies help people say “no” to unimportant meetings so it feels safe to do that.  
Step three is to create a place where anyone can report useless meetings without causing trouble.  
After that, add rules to slow down careless scheduling.  
For example, Shopify uses a Slack bot to stop meetings on no-meeting days.  
Stronger rules include needing approval to start new regular meetings and setting focus times that protect deep work.  
Finally, treat cutting meeting debt as something you do often, not just once.  
Put regular calendar cleanses on your schedule so meeting debt does not build up again.  
Block 30 minutes today to pick a date for your calendar cleanse and treat it like a top priority, because your calendar will try to fill the free time again.  

[CHOOSE MEETING METRICS THAT MAKE MEETINGS BETTER]  
  
Imagine planning a short meeting and seeing a price before sending the invite.  
Shopify added a tool that shows how much a meeting costs, by adding up the hourly pay of everyone invited. This helps people think twice.  
Some people did not like this because showing cost can feel like shaming and distract from value.  
So, how do you measure if a meeting is good?  
This is the second rule: only use measures that help you make meetings better.  
Avoid measures that seem exact but don’t tell you how to improve.  
Be careful if meeting hosts rate their own meetings, because they often see themselves better or blame others.  
The best feedback comes from the people who attend.  
For quality, use Return On Time Invested – ROTI.  
Right after a meeting, attendees give a score from zero to five, from “a complete waste” to “excellent use of time.”  
Anonymous polls work best in most workplaces.  
Add one question asking what could make the meeting one point better. This brings specific ideas to improve.  
Use ROTI only about 10% of the time, so people think carefully when they answer.  
It is also helpful to track some meeting data, like how much time meetings take, who talks, if people multitask, and if the group starts and shows up on time.  
Never make a measure a goal, because people might cheat and pretend to be more productive than they are.  
Done well, measuring can be a small habit that keeps meetings honest and better.  

[STRIP MEETINGS TO ESSENTIALS ACROSS FOUR DIMENSIONS]  
  
When a team faces a hard topic, like cutting the budget or moving resources, the calendar can get messy.  
One meeting turns into a regular series, more people get added “just in case,” and the agenda fills with status updates that could be written in a paragraph.  
Soon, coordinating looks like progress, but the real decision is still waiting.  
This is why the third rule is to keep meetings simple.  
Start by cutting your agenda to about half.  
Rewrite the rest as clear actions with a verb and a clear output.  
For example, instead of “Budget update,” write “Decide Q2 budget tradeoffs and approve final plan.”  
Move small updates and simple decisions to messages or documents, because small things can take time from big work.  
If the meeting goes off-topic, write the idea down in a shared note so you can come back to it later without losing focus.  
Next, shorten meeting times.  
Work fills the time you give it, so set less time and finish on the dot.  
If cutting time by half feels too much, make an hour meeting 50 minutes and a half-hour meeting 25 minutes, and finish early if done.  
Then invite fewer people and have meetings less often.  
Only invite people who must be there and keep the group under eight, sharing notes afterward.  
Make regular meetings earn their place with an end date and check if they still add value. Stop extra meetings by making sure decision makers are there and assigning an owner for each action.  
Try making one regular meeting this week prove its value with the smallest, simplest version.  
Once you see how light your calendar can be, you won’t want to go back.  

[DESIGN YOUR WHOLE COMMUNICATION SYSTEM FOR FLOW]  
  
Imagine you could guess which teams do well without hearing their talks.  
This is close to what happened when MIT’s Sandy Pentland studied interaction patterns with badges on 2,500 people in 21 organizations. The badges showed details like who talks and when.  
They learned that how information moves in a group is more important than exactly what is said.  
This is the fourth rule: use system thinking for communication flow.  
Meetings are only one way to communicate. Others are email, chat, shared documents, and project tools.  
When outside meetings is messy, meetings try to fix everything, filling calendars but not solving problems.  
Start by making your tools clear. Each should have one job.  
When teams add apps too often, information gets broken and people waste time looking for what they need.  
One report said the average knowledge worker loses about 6 hours a week switching tools and finding where work is.  
No wonder two-thirds of UK and US workers want one shared core tool set.  
Standardize also means deciding when to meet live.  
Try the 4D-CEO Test. If the goal is not to decide, discuss, debate, or develop, don’t meet.  
If it passes, only meet live when talking about emotional or complex topics, or hard decisions.  
The big idea is that meetings show how well your whole communication system works.  
When tools are clear and rules known, information moves easily and meetings stop being the fix-all.  

[DESIGN MEETINGS FOR THE PEOPLE IN THEM]  
  
Have you noticed some meetings feel heavy and tiring?  
People often say it’s a bad day or wrong people.  
The fifth rule says this is a design problem: if people lose interest, the meeting needs fixing.  
There are four energy-drainers to watch for.  
Number 1 is power moves.  
One person, usually the boss or loudest, talks too much and others get quiet.  
A simple fix is to encourage honest and balanced talk.  
Alan Mulally at Ford used color status updates and praised the first person who spoke about problems, making it safe to talk.  
Number 2 is lateness.  
Starting late hurts focus and shows time isn’t important.  
Between 44% and 55% of meetings start late because someone is late, and late meetings have fewer and less creative ideas.  
Start on time, even if some people are missing, and make punctuality a leader’s rule.  
Number 3 is jargon.  
When people use too many acronyms or trendy words, others waste energy trying to understand instead of thinking.  
Jargon feels harder, less interesting, and less trusted.  
Use simple words, explain new terms once, and let people ask questions easily.  
Number 4 is boredom.  
Dull agendas and the same meeting setup cause people to multitask.  
In 2023, 46% of US adults said they felt bored at work at least three days a week.  
Make meetings lively by focusing on important questions, refreshing the room with a quick break, and adding a small fun moment to get attention back.  
Find these problems early and fix them fast, so meetings become a place where people can really join in.  

[TIME MEETINGS TO THE NATURAL RHYTHM OF WORK]  
  
In 2012, Adobe’s HR leader Donna Morris thought annual reviews were causing good workers to leave.  
The reviews were out of sync with real work and made managers focus too much on recent weeks.  
After she said Adobe would stop yearly reviews, the company changed to check-ins matching quarterly goals.  
By 2016, resignations dropped by 30%.  
This is the sixth rule: match meeting timing to work rhythm.  
When meetings fit the work’s flow, they help progress, not stop it.  
One way to do this is to plan meetings around three rhythms: strategic, tactical, and operational.  
Strategic rhythm follows goal-setting.  
It uses a shared scoreboard so priorities stay clear.  
Tactical rhythm follows projects.  
Plan key talks at the start, middle, and after finishing a project.  
Start with a premortem by imagining the project failed and writing why. This sharpens risk thinking.  
At halfway, teams often feel the deadline is real, so use this time to find hidden problems and fix course.  
After delivery, do a quick review to learn what worked, what didn’t, and ideas for next time.  
Operational rhythm is daily work that keeps things moving, like short meetings to handle urgent problems and handovers.  
Only use frequent meetings if the work really needs them.  
For example, Cleveland Clinic uses 15-minute daily meetings escalating urgent issues quickly, but many teams don’t need daily meetings.  
When timing fits work, meetings support progress instead of breaking it.  

[UPGRADE MEETING TECHNOLOGY LIKE A PRODUCT IN BETA]  
  
In 2021, a US science lab had a hybrid meeting with senior leaders, and the technology failed badly.  
People calling in could not hear well, someone used a standing mic, and remote workers couldn’t tell who was speaking.  
When sound and video are bad, meetings become about fixing tech, not making decisions.  
The seventh rule is to keep improving your meeting technology over time, not just accept a bad setup.  
Start by having the basic tools that always work: good sound and clear video when needed.  
Research shows bad sound makes people judge speakers as less smart, warm, and trustworthy.  
Control your space, use a headset or external mic instead of a laptop mic, and add noise-canceling software if the place is noisy.  
Use calm technology – tools that work quietly without stealing attention.  
Your tech should ask for little attention and do boring tasks so people can focus on thinking, relationships, and decisions.  
If a tool needs many logins, add-ons, or has distracting features, it steals the focus your meeting needs.  
What about AI?  
Use it for busywork like scheduling or making notes and clear tasks. But only with clear permission and rules about privacy, storage, and access.  
Be open about when AI is used and why. Introduce it slowly and give it one task at a time to see if it helps or just makes noise.  
Overall, treat meeting tech like something you keep improving: get the basics perfect first, then add tools only if they help make meetings fairer and easier to join.  
Together with the other six rules, this will help you make your meetings work well.  
The main lesson from Your Best Meeting Ever by Rebecca Hinds is that meetings get much better when you treat them as a system you design, not just a habit.  

[FINAL SUMMARY]  
  
Cut regular meeting clutter by doing a strong reset, then keep only meetings that earn their place.  
Measure quality with feedback you can act on, make agendas and attendance simple, and fix communication outside meetings so meetings don’t try to fix everything.  
Keep energy by stopping domination, lateness, jargon, and boredom.  
Match meeting times to how work really moves, and improve technology carefully to reduce problems while keeping trust and focus.

### Synopsis

The book *How to Make People Like You in 90 Seconds or Less* (2000) helps you connect with others. It teaches you to start talking to strangers and make them like you. You will also learn to understand small body movements.

**You can learn to be likable.**

## What is this for me? Make a quick good impression on everyone you meet.

When you go to a party, meeting, or coffee shop, you will see them right away. These people stand near walls or hold their phones tightly. They try to look busy. Most of us prefer to check emails many times instead of talking to someone new.

What we do has a name: **social retreat**. It means we pull back from others. This is normal. Meeting someone new is hard. You have to make small talk. There can be long, awkward silences. You also don't know where to look. Should you look them in the eyes? For how long? When does looking interested become looking too much? 

The easiest way is to stay with people you already know. Your friends become like a safe place. Everyone else stays away.

But there is a bad side. While we avoid new people, our bodies slowly suffer.

Studies on loneliness show something important. People with many friends, not just family, live longer. They get well faster when sick. They also say they are much happier. This information is very strong. Some experts say being alone is as bad for your health as smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. Making friends is not just about having someone for coffee. It is about staying alive.

But the good things go beyond health. Every new friend helps you practice your **people skills**. These skills help you in all parts of your life. When you learn to make someone feel good in 90 seconds, you are not just being nice. You are building useful skills. These skills help you at work. You will have better job interviews. You will have stronger relationships with clients. You will be a better leader. People who can connect fast live life in a different way.

So, here is a question. If making friends is so important, why is it so hard?

The answer is that we do not know how. Most people meet new people without a plan. They hope that they will just get along well. They depend on luck and their natural character. They do not know that being likable follows rules. You can learn these rules. You can practice them and get better.

We will show you how to become **likable right away**. This is not about tricking people or pretending. It is about understanding what makes people connect. You will learn ways that work in 90 seconds or less. These methods are so useful, you can use them today.

You will learn how eye movements show how someone thinks. You will understand why leaning forward changes a talk. You will also see why that small uncomfortable feeling when you meet someone new can lead to something good.

Making new friends is not about putting yourself in bad situations. It is about having the right ways to do it. Now, let's look at these ways.

## Blink 1 - Bonding with others will make you healthier and more successful.

Imagine the African plains many thousands of years ago. Night comes. A small group of early humans sits around a fire. They hunted together all day. They followed animals across the fields. They worked together using signs and sounds. Now, they eat the food they caught. They are close together. Their lives depend on each other. If they were alone, they would die of hunger. Together, they eat well.

This scene happened again and again for thousands of years. It did not just keep our ancestors alive. It made us naturally want to connect with others. We are made to bond.

Now think of today. We have parties instead of campfires. We have town meetings instead of tribal meetings. We have fan events instead of old ceremonies. The places are different. But the need to connect is still the same. We look for each other. We gather together. We connect. This is not just culture or habit. It is a **basic part of all humans**.

But here is something interesting. This strong need to connect is not just about feeling good. It is not just about not being lonely. It is about staying alive.

Dr. Lisa Berkman from Harvard studied 7,000 people for nine years. She watched how their social connections changed their lives. The results showed something clear. People who did not have good friends or community links were **three times more likely** to die from sickness. This was compared to those with strong social groups. Three times more! Your relationships do not just make your life better. They also make it longer. And there is more than just living longer.

Think about the last good chance you got. Maybe it was a job opening you heard about early. Maybe it was a date set up by a friend that went well. Or a concert ticket that was hard to get, but someone you knew was selling it. Behind all these good things is a network of people. These people knew other people. They thought of you at the right time.

Whatever you want in life – a better job, a partner, or fun experiences – your chances are much higher. This is true if you have many friends and connections. Success does not happen alone. It happens when people connect.

This brings us to a difficult truth. Knowing that connecting with others is important does not always make it easy. 

Talking to strangers can feel strange and unnatural. This is especially true if you have nothing clear in common. And there is a lot to lose. Studies show you have about **90 seconds** when you meet someone new to get their interest. If you miss this short time, you will likely lose your chance completely.

Ninety seconds. That's it.

Does that seem scary? Yes, for sure. Is it impossible? No, not at all. Later, we will show you special skills and ways to make real connections. This is possible even in those very first moments.

## Blink 2 - The Silent Introduction

You have not said anything yet. Not one word. But the person in front of you? They have already decided something about you.

It feels strange, right? But this is how people connect. Those first seconds are very important. This is before you say "hello" or shake hands or say your names. This is when people decide about you. Your body speaks first. Always.

Let me tell you what truly happens in that moment without words.

Three things show what you want before you even speak. These are your body position, your eyes, and the look on your face. Each one sends a message. Each one either opens a door for connection or quietly closes it.

Start with your body. There is a special way to show you are open. It focuses on your heart area. When you stand so your chest, where your heart is, faces someone, you send a strong message. It means you are real. You are fully there. You are saying, "I am here with you, not looking for a way out." It is a physical promise to that moment. People feel it right away. They might not know why they trust you.

Next are your eyes. Looking directly into someone's eyes does something great. It builds trust right away. Not a stare that makes people feel strange. But a calm look that shows you truly see the person. This is not just an option. It is a main part. Every good first meeting depends on this silent sharing of attention.

Then, and this is very important, **be the first to smile.** Do not wait for them to smile first. Do not just copy their face. Show warmth first. A bright, real smile does more than show you are friendly. It tells the other person's mind that you are open. It says you are safe. It shows that talking to you will not need them to be careful or guarded.

Once you have set up this silent beginning, the talking part is very simple. A simple "hi" or "hello" with a nice voice. Say your first name. Ask for theirs. When they tell you their name, for example, Brenda, **say it back right away**: "Brenda. Nice to meet you, Brenda!" This is not just being polite. It is a way to help you remember their name.

One last thing: lean slightly forward. Move your body a little bit toward them. It is a small movement. You can barely see it. But it makes everything you have already shown much stronger. It shows interest. It shows openness. It shows you are engaged. You can also give a firm, polite handshake if it is suitable. Or just keep your hands relaxed by your sides. Both are fine.

All of this happens in seconds. But these seconds decide if you make a real connection. Or if it is just another meeting you will forget. The person in front of you has already decided. Your body told them everything they needed to know.

## Blink 3 - The Attitude That Opens Doors

Your flight is cancelled. Your holiday, planned for months, is now unsure. The airport staff member is young. They are already tired at 9 AM. They call for the next person in line.

You have only one chance to get what you want.

Right now, everything depends on something you cannot see. Not your ticket type. Not how often you fly. It depends on your **attitude**. It decides if you get what you want, or if you get stuck with what you do not want.

It all comes down to one easy choice. You can have a *helpful attitude* or a *bad attitude*.

The difference is simple. A helpful attitude makes you think about what you want from the talk. A bad attitude makes you think about what you do not want.

See how this works at the airport gate.

A traveler with a bad attitude thinks only negative thoughts. *I do not want to be here. I do not want to miss Christmas. This airline is bad and is ruining my plans.* This inner thought shows on their face. Their jaw is tight. Their eyes are small. Their arms are crossed. When they get to the counter, they already look angry. They start shouting. They demand more and more. The staff member could have helped. But now they only want to finish the talk with no more trouble.

No connection. No help.

The traveler with a helpful attitude thinks differently. *I want to find someone who can help me get another flight.* That is all. This single focus on what they want changes everything. Their face looks softer. Their shoulders relax. Their voice sounds like they want to work together. They talk to the same tired staff member like a friend, not an enemy. They say, "I know this is not your fault. You must be helping many people like me. What can we do?" 

The body language you show is not acted. It is not fake. It happens automatically. When you think about what you want, your face and body will show it.

So, before any important talk, stop. Ask yourself: What do I really want here? Not what you want to stop from happening. But what you want to make happen.

That simple question guides you. It leads you to be open instead of closed. It leads you to be curious instead of judging. It leads you to show expressions and movements that make people truly like you. This is because they come from a real aim.

The staff member sees many worried travelers that day. Who gets a new flight? Not always the ones who speak well or feel they deserve it most. It is the ones whose attitude made helping them seem easy. It even felt good to help them.

That is making a good connection on purpose.

## Blink 4 - The Body Speaks First

Your friend says he is okay. His arms are crossed. His eyes look down. His foot taps like a clock. You do not believe him at all.

Why?

This is because our brains look at bodies long before we hear words. We read signs that we can barely name. We see when things do not match. This sets off warnings in our mind. That is the power and danger of body language.

Let me explain what makes someone seem trustworthy right away. Or what makes them seem suspicious right away. We will start with a simple idea that changes everything.

As we said earlier, open body language means what it sounds like. It shows your chest and stomach to the person you are talking to. So, every movement you make is either **open** or **closed**. Shoulders back. Arms relaxed at your sides. Chest facing forward. This way of standing sends a message without words. It says: *I am here. I am listening. I want this talk.*

Closed body language does the opposite. Crossed arms protect your heart. This is true both physically and emotionally. Turning your body sideways puts space between you. These are not just random ways to stand. They are ways to protect yourself. They show resistance, anger, being annoyed, or feeling nervous. Your body is saying *I need something between us*. You might mean it, or you might not.

And it is not just about arms, legs, and your body. Your face also acts in an open or closed way. An open face looks you in the eyes. It smiles. It raises eyebrows to show interest. A closed face looks serious. It avoids eye contact. It hides feelings behind a blank look.

But here is where it gets difficult. You cannot just do one thing well and think you are finished.

Albert Mehrabian was a professor. He spent his life studying how we talk to each other. He found something very important about how much people believe you. It depends on **matching** three things. These are: your voice (how you sound), your words (what you say), and your body (what your body does).

All three must work together. If they do not, people feel that something is wrong. They might not be able to explain it. Remember that friend who said he was "fine"? His words said one thing. But his voice, his way of standing, his tapping foot, all told a different story. This difference did not just make him less believable. It made the air feel strange, like smoke.

That is why being consistent is so important. Mixed messages do not just confuse people. They also push people away. When your mouth says "yes" but your body says "no," the other person feels something is not right. Trust quickly goes away. Comfort also disappears.

What is the answer? Make everything match. Say what you truly think. Mean what you say. Let your body show both.

## Blink 5 - The Mirror Effect

Right now, as you read this, someone is matching another person's breathing without knowing it. In a coffee shop, one person is tilting her head just like her friend. In a business meeting, someone has crossed his legs the same way as the speaker. None of them know they are doing this.

This is not just by chance. It is one of the most basic and hidden forces. It shapes every time you talk to another person.

This pattern starts very early. A newborn baby, only a few hours old, already moves in time with her mother. The baby does not choose to do this. She does not *learn* it. This matching happens by itself. It is a natural need, just like breathing. 

Imagine twenty years later. That same person is now an adult. Her clothes slowly start to match her partner's style. He wears a lot of dark blue. Suddenly, she also likes it. She likes simple jewelry. He starts to stop wearing his brighter jewelry. Neither of them decided to match. But they do.

But why? What good reason could there be for us to copy each other like this?

The answer shows itself in small moments. Someone smiles at you on the street. Your mouth moves up before you even think. A workmate yawns during a meeting. Suddenly, you also feel like yawning. Watch a boxer get a hard punch in a fight. Notice how your own stomach feels tight, as if you were hit. Your body reacts to what others feel as if it were happening to you.

This explains why matching becomes a secret way to connect. Studies prove what we already feel. We like people who move like us. We like people who talk like us. We even like people who look like us. Studies show we are more likely to hire people who act like us. We are attracted to partners whose faces look a little like ours. Being similar does not just make us feel safe. It creates a connection.

I want to show you how to use this. Think about **mirroring**. This means quietly matching someone's movements, how they stand, their face, how they breathe, and their voice. It is not copying exactly. That is clear and makes people dislike you. But a small matching creates a strong, easy feeling.

Think about an art gallery. A man walks quietly. He looks at each artwork with quiet respect. He is deep in thought. Then a salesperson comes. They are loud and touch the man. They come too close. They grab his shoulder and talk loudly about the paintings. This difference feels bad. The sale is lost before it even starts.

Let's change the story. The same salesperson sees the man is quiet. She changes her actions. She comes closer slowly. She speaks softly. She gives him space. She matches his quiet way of looking. Suddenly, there is no problem. Just two people moving through the gallery together, feeling connected.

Matching voices is very powerful. When someone speaks softly and gently, and you speak loudly and strongly, you create distance. But if you lower your voice to match theirs? That simple change shows: *I am with you. We are in the same place, we feel the same way.* The other person relaxes. They stop being careful. Talking becomes easy.

You have been doing this your whole life without knowing. The question is not if you will match others. You already do. The question is if you will do it on purpose. Will you do it well, to make real connections?

## Blink 6 - The Art of Questions and the Power of Listening

A woman sits across from a stranger in a busy Italian restaurant. The smell of garlic is strong. She points to her plate and asks, "Do you come here often?" The stranger nods. "Yes." A quiet feeling comes between them, like extra forks no one wants. The talk ends before it really started.

Let's go back in time. Same restaurant, same strangers. This time she asks: "What a nice restaurant. Where do you think the cook is from?" The stranger's eyes become bright. "Actually, I think northern Italy. They use a special herb in the butter sauce..." And just like that, they start talking easily.

One word made all the difference.

Questions change talks, like a riverbed changes water. They either let the talk flow or stop it completely. The way it works is simple. Some questions **open people up**. They ask people to share more. Other questions **close them down**. These only need a "yes" or "no" answer.

The questions that open people up, and start real talks, are all similar. They start with these words: Who, What, When, Where, How, and Why. These are not just words for sentences. They ask you to explain. They ask for your opinion. They ask you to share something true. "Where do you think the cook is from?" needs more than just shaking your head. It asks for your ideas, for guesses, maybe even a story.

Closed questions are different. They start with phrases like "Are you," "Have you," and "Do you." They are like dead ends in a talk. They might seem polite or friendly. But they stop connection when you try to build it. The stranger at the restaurant can answer "Do you come here often?" with one word and then stop talking to you.

But asking the right questions is only half of it.

The other half is **active listening**. This does not just mean staying quiet while someone else talks. It means understanding the feelings behind the words. It means understanding not just what someone says, but how they feel about it.

Your body does most of this job. Turn your chest towards the speaker. Point your body towards them. Look them in the eyes. Not a strange, fixed stare. But a real connection with your eyes. Lean in a little when something they say makes sense to you. Nod when you agree. These are not just fake actions. They are signs that say *you are important, please continue*. 

What about when it's your turn to speak? Do not interrupt, even if you have a great idea. Wait. Then reply with energy that matches what you just heard.

Think of a woman who says: "I like living in Chicago. But my husband got a better job, and we might move to Miami." An average listener might say, "Oh, nice." An active listener hears the stress in her words. They hear the pull between being happy and needing to change. They reply: "Wow, that sounds like a very big and hard choice. How will you decide where to live?"

Do you see the difference? The first reply closes the talk. The second opens it wide. It understands her feelings. It asks for more with another open question.

This is how real talks happen. You ask questions that let people share freely. Then you listen with all of yourself. You listen with your ears, eyes, body, and heart. You listen to what they choose to share.

## Blink 7 - Speaking Their Language

Two people walk into the same art gallery. One stands back. His eyes move quickly from one painting to another. He enjoys the colors and shapes. The other person steps closer. She reads every sign out loud. She listens to the audio guide. A third person touches the velvet rope with their fingers. They feel the weight of their coat. They move their feet.

Same room. Same art. Three entirely different experiences.

In 1970, Richard Bandler and John Grinder found something interesting. They were creating a system called neuro-linguistic programming. They learned that people do not just *like* different senses more. They actually understand the world through them. Some people are visual. Some are auditory (hearing). Some are kinesthetic (feeling). We use all of them. But one sense is usually stronger in how we see the world.

This leads to an important question: What happens when you start talking using someone else's main sense?

Look at someone who mainly uses their eyes (visual). They move fast. They talk faster. Their clothes are important to them. They like clean lines, colors that match, and small details. When they think, they are literally *seeing* the answer. They create pictures in their mind. Listen to how they talk: "I see what you are saying." "How does this look to you?" "Can you show me what you mean?"

Auditory people, on the other hand, live in a world of sound and rhythm. Their voices sound like music, going up and down naturally. They like jobs where voice is important. For example, radio, teaching, or law. Talking is not just sending messages for them. It is an art. Their words show who they are. They say: "That sounds familiar." "Tell me more about it." "I did not like his tone."

Then there are people who use their feelings (kinesthetic). Everything slows down. Their voices are lower and warmer. They like clothes with interesting textures. They like things they can touch. When they think about something, they need to *feel* their way through it. This is why they speak more slowly and carefully. They say: "How do you feel about this?" "Let me get in touch with her." "I cannot quite understand what you are saying."

Here is how to use this. Once you know someone's main sense, you can match it. And your connection will get stronger almost by itself. Talk quickly and dress smartly around visual people. This makes an instant connection. Use clear words and a nice voice for auditory people. This wins them over. Match a kinesthetic person's slower speed. Speak with care. This makes them feel truly understood.

But how do you really know which sense someone uses most?

Their eyes show you. Ask someone about their best holiday. A visual person's eyes will move up, either left or right. It is like they are watching a movie in their mind. Auditory people look straight across, towards their ears. They are remembering sounds. Kinesthetic people look down and to the side, towards their hands and body. They are remembering how that experience *felt*.

The next time you find it hard to connect with someone, stop talking. Start watching. Their eyes are already showing you what to do. You just need to know where to look.

## Final summary

Let us finish by looking back at everything we talked about today.

First, when you meet someone new, you usually have only 90 seconds to make a good, strong impression. *Ninety seconds*. That is all.

Not 90 minutes to connect over coffee. Not 90 days to show your value. Just 90 seconds to see if someone will really connect with you. Or if they will just be polite for the rest of the talk.

The time starts the moment you meet. Studies in how people think show something both freeing and scary. The feeling you create in that short time becomes the base for everything that happens next. If you get those first moments right, you open a door. If you miss them, you will spend the rest of the talk trying to break down a wall that stays up.

What makes those seconds so important? It is not about your first words or how firm your handshake is. It is about how the other person *feels* when they are with you. Do they feel you are open? Curious? Truly willing to connect? Or do they feel you are closed, not focused, or just acting?

This is where most advice about first impressions is not enough. We are told to smile, look in the eyes, remember names. These are good tips. But they miss the deeper meaning. The person you are talking to is not just checking off your actions. They are feeling your emotional mood. It is like how you feel the mood of a room the moment you enter it.

You cannot fake a truly open attitude. But you can develop it. It starts with a simple change in your mind. Go into the meeting with curiosity, not judgment. Believe that this person has something interesting to share. Do not think they need to prove anything.

But just being open will not make a strong connection if your energy does not match. Imagine you meet someone new. Maybe it is for work. Or at a party. They speak slowly. They pause between ideas. Their energy is calm and careful. But you are speaking twice as fast. Words come out quickly. Your excitement shows in how fast you talk.

The problem is not what you say. It is about the rhythm. They are playing slow music. You are playing very fast music. In that gap, the connection breaks.

Here is a surprisingly simple method: **belly breathing**. Instead of breathing quickly from your chest, which makes you feel excited, breathe deeper. Focus on your stomach. Breathe into your stomach and feel it get bigger. Your main breathing muscle does the work. 

Watch what happens. Your breathing naturally slows down. This slower breathing calms your body. It makes you feel less rushed. And almost without trying, you will start to speak at the same speed as the other person.

You have created a match. Not by copying, but by truly changing your body's rhythm. You are no longer two people talking at different speeds. You are meeting in the same moment. You are breathing the same air. You are sharing the same rhythm.

All in under ninety seconds.

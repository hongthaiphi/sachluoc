**Author:** Patrick Lencioni | **Category:** Motivación e inspiración## ¿QUÉ ME APORTA ESTE TÍTULO? HAZ QUE CUALQUIER TRABAJO SE ADAPTE A TI.

Life is too short to have a job that makes you unhappy.

But do not blame the job itself.

If you use the right approach, any job can become meaningful.

In The Truth About Employee Engagement, Lencioni looks at how unhappy employees affect a company's success and their family and social life.

You will learn to find the three main causes of job dissatisfaction and get tools to improve your work life and others'.

No matter your job, whether manager or employee, you can use the advice in these summaries.

Do you want to discover how your job is more meaningful than you think?

Let's start.

## JOB SATISFACTION DOES NOT ONLY DEPEND ON SALARY, AND UNHAPPY EMPLOYEES COST A LOT.

What does it mean when someone says they have a bad job?

There are many reasons why they may feel unhappy with their job.

It might make them physically tired or pay a low salary.

But there are less obvious reasons why people can feel unhappy.

Imagine you get a job in the field you always dreamed about or with a good salary.

None of these things can guarantee job satisfaction.

Have you ever gone to work feeling afraid to get to the office?

Have you ever counted the hours all day, waiting for work to end?

If yes, you had a miserable job that could make you very cynical, without motivation to try or do well.

This is not about the job title or field; a doctor can be as unhappy as a construction worker.

A professional athlete can be as disappointed as a theme park designer.

A rich executive can be very unhappy at work, while the waiter serving her lunch can find his job very rewarding.

You get the idea.

Being very unhappy at work has a personal cost: your overall well-being.

What is less clear is the high cost to companies.

Let's look at efficiency.

Many studies show the bad effect unhappy employees have on company efficiency.

When a worker is less involved and less happy, they are less likely to do well, which hurts both the worker and the company.

And not only that: the unhappiness and cynicism of an unhappy employee affect their family, partner, and children.

These people also have more trouble with other social duties like taking care of loved ones and being part of the community.

To fix unhappy workers, we first need to understand what causes it.

Luckily, the next section has the answer.

## THREE MAIN CAUSES MAKE PEOPLE UNHAPPY AT WORK AND CAN AFFECT ANYONE.

Imagine a plant.

If the roots are rotten, it cannot take in nutrients, the rot spreads to the rest of the plant, and soon it wilts and dies.

Well, the same thing happens to people in bad jobs.

Three rotten roots can make a good job wilt and become bad.

The first root is anonymity.

People need their efforts to be recognized to feel valued.

It is very important that someone, ideally your employer, takes time to understand your potential and appreciate your skills.

When people feel invisible and think they would not be missed, they get discouraged.

The second root is irrelevance.

When you do work that helps others, even just one person, you feel your job matters.

If no one benefits from your work, you will feel your efforts are useless.

The last root of unhappiness is lack of measurement.

This happens when people have no way to measure their achievements.

If you do not know your goal, how can you tell if you reached it?

It helps to have a clear idea of good performance and how to improve.

Otherwise, you will lose motivation and feel unhappy.

Not only those at the bottom feel anonymity, irrelevance, and lack of measurement; even managers can feel these causes of unhappiness.

To show this, imagine the marketing director of a software company.

Her name is Nancy.

Nancy has an important job but is unhappy.

Why?

She reports directly to the CEO, who is so busy she has no time to care about Nancy's career or personal life.

She feels anonymous.

Nancy also does not see how the company helps its customers.

She is not even sure if her work makes any difference in her team.

She feels irrelevant.

And even though Nancy knows the company is doing well by performance data, she has no way to measure her contribution to the company's success.

This is lack of measurement.

The three main causes come together to make Nancy very unhappy.

## INCREASING EMPLOYEE ENGAGEMENT HAS BENEFITS, BUT THERE ARE ALSO CHALLENGES TO OVERCOME.

Imagine you have a business.

It is not making enough profit, what do you do?

Look for cheaper suppliers?

Use more efficient production methods?

Maybe first you should look at your staff: raising employee engagement can fix many problems.

The most clear benefit of engagement is more productivity.

When people feel their job matters, they are more ready to take responsibility to get work done.

This can mean working more hours or being more careful, which leads to better and more accurate results.

The less clear, but just as important, benefit is that engaged employees stay loyal to the company.

They even recommend it to other good workers.

In the long run, this saves the company time and money in training and hiring.

Also, in today’s tough market, having a reputation for a positive workplace can improve a company's image.

This happens by taking care of your workers and making sure they are motivated.

Doesn’t this sound like common sense?

Well, sadly, there are many challenges to reach this employee engagement.

As an employee, you might only think about salary and chances to get promoted when picking a job.

While these are important, it is even more important to see if the company fits your culture.

Does it support employee growth?

Does it encourage your talents?

On the other hand, the main problem for employers is poor communication.

It can be hard for a boss to ask questions that need emotional answers, like how an employee feels or if they enjoy their job.

Luckily, this skill can be learned if managers try a bit more every day to keep their employees engaged.

## EMPLOYERS MUST KNOW THEIR EMPLOYEES AND SHOW WHAT THEY BRING TO THE COMPANY.

What do you have in common with superstars like Madonna?

More than you think.

Despite money and success, many actors and stars feel unhappy, always under pressure to reinvent themselves because they fear losing importance.

This fear affects office workers and Grammy winners alike.

Employers can do a lot to help their employees feel needed.

First, by making sure every worker knows what they contribute.

For example, employers can encourage workers to ask, "Who am I helping?"

In hospitality, the answer is simple: the customer.

Even when it is clear who they help, it is important to remind workers their job has an impact by sharing customer feedback.

It is harder to motivate workers who don’t work with customers, like secretaries and office assistants.

Their work helps mostly one person: the boss.

In this case, the boss should motivate employees by showing how their hard work makes life easier for them.

Besides giving praise, employers must know their employees.

Workers whose bosses care about them feel more satisfied.

So, what is the best way to make an employee feel heard?

Simple: just take time to sit and talk.

At first, it may be hard to go beyond the formal work setting.

When hiring, managers are taught not to ask personal questions during interviews.

However, managers need to know their staff.

This includes their dreams, what motivates them, and important family events.

Sadly, managers often forget this, but showing real interest and talking often is one of the best ways to increase employee engagement.

"Good job!"

## EMPLOYEES MUST KNOW EXACTLY WHAT THEY CONTRIBUTE AND HAVE A RELIABLE WAY TO MEASURE IT.

That is what a boss would say to keep you motivated.

That is good, but not enough.

Employers must be much clearer in their praise.

Why?

Because workers need to know exactly what they contribute to feel their work matters.

This often means focusing on the human part of any job.

Take a hotel worker as an example.

Their job might be to take breakfast trays to guest rooms.

If they only see it as carrying food, they won’t find much satisfaction.

But if they understand that guests may have traveled for hours, are very tired, and having breakfast in their room will make a big difference, the work becomes much more meaningful.

Accounting may not seem like a caring job, but an accountant can also make a big difference.

Imagine a sick person loses a medical receipt but needs to get money back fast.

If the accountant helps find that receipt, they are not just finding paper but giving peace of mind to the patient.

This lets the patient stop worrying about money and enjoy life more.

Employers should remind workers who they are helping.

Besides knowing who benefits from their work, employees must be able to measure their contribution.

It is very important not to measure success by how happy a boss is with the work.

If success is judged like this, it creates competition where people do office politics and flatter the boss.

Instead, employees should track their progress with clear measures.

It is not enough to give a general company income goal; there must be feedback systems.

This lets workers, like the hotel worker, get direct comments from customers.

The hotel worker was a quick example.

Let’s see more examples in the next section.

## BOTH A PACKER AND A STAR ATHLETE CAN MAKE THEIR WORK MORE MEANINGFUL.

You have heard why a glamorous job is not always happy, and a humble job is not always bad.

If you need more proof, here are more examples of how to make any job more meaningful.

What about a packer?

The person in this example is Josh.

He works at a supermarket packing customers’ bags and carrying them to their cars.

Josh may be low in the hierarchy, but the boss should not treat him like he is invisible.

The boss could start a talk, find something they have in common, and by doing this, increase Josh’s interest and loyalty to the store.

Josh could also make his work more interesting by giving customers extra benefits, like a weather report or an inspiring quote while working.

Now look at the other side: a star athlete.

His name is Michael and he plays American football.

Michael is lucky to earn millions a year but is not always happy.

Top athletes like Michael are treated like products, traded by clubs and agents for big money.

When moving to a new city and working with a new coach, Michael may feel very lonely unless the coach tries to know him personally.

Another risk is Michael might forget what he is doing for others.

Playing sports can seem irrelevant.

Even if Michael and teammates have thousands of social media fans or media praise, this does not replace personal feedback.

Coaches and managers must remind players how much the fans who watch games value them.

A win will please fans for a whole week, and remembering this can make a big difference.

## MANAGERS SHOULD SERVE THEIR EMPLOYEES; SIMPLE STEPS CAN HELP ANY MANAGER TO DO THIS.

What do you think of when you think of service?

A poorly paid waiter serving a rich customer?

An ordinary worker serving a more powerful manager?

Actually, service should be mutual, and managers should aim to serve their employees.

As a manager in a company, it is easy to feel you are not as caring as a nurse or doctor.

But remember that managers can do a lot for their employees.

They must work to improve their employees’ physical and mental well-being to give them the best work conditions.

The good news is this helps everyone: employees feel valued and motivated, and managers feel good for helping many people and their families.

Making changes does not have to be hard.

Many easy things can help staff.

First, a manager should look at what they already do to make employees feel good.

They can ask themselves if they really know their workers, their hobbies, passions, and family life.

Managers must also set up ways for each worker to see their contribution to the company and track the impact of their work.

But managers should not only trust their feelings.

They must ask for opinions to see what can improve in helping employees find meaning at work.

If there are problems, the manager can make a clear plan to fix them.

This will mean talking more with people and with the whole team.

If everyone tries to connect and make others feel valued, both people and the company will grow.

## FINAL SUMMARY

Do not accept an unhappy job: find ways to make it meaningful.

Both managers and workers have tools to bring joy and meaning to others’ work.

The key is to focus on how you affect others, what you add to the team, and have a clear way to measure your success.

The more engaged employees are, the more the company will succeed.

If you are job hunting, it is smart to check if the job can be meaningful.

You can do this during the interview.

Tell possible employers you want to know them better and for them to know you.

Tell them you need a clear view of what your work gives to others and a clear way to measure success.

If they do not seem interested in answering, be careful.

Look for a company that offers a workplace you want to go to every morning.

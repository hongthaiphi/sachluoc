### Synopsis

*The Power of Habit* (2012) shows us the role habits play in our lives. This includes good habits, like brushing your teeth or exercising. It also includes bad habits, like smoking. Research and stories explain how habits start. They also show how you can change them. This is true for personal habits, and also for habits in companies and groups.

## What's in it for you: Learn how to build good habits and break bad ones.

You made a decision: no more cigarettes! Or: no more fast food! For a few weeks, everything goes well. You feel proud, you stay away from unhealthy things. But one day, it hits you hard – and the old habits are back.

Does this sound familiar? If yes, you already know: the power of habit.

But where does this power come from? In these blinks, you will see that habits are deep inside the human brain and mind. They affect our lives in many ways. Habits make life much easier. For example, you don't have to think about how to open a door every time you stand in front of one. But habits can also cause problems and even ruin lives.

Luckily, you can fight the power of habits by learning how they work.

In the next blinks, you will learn:

- why expecting something leads to habits,

- what not eating marshmallows has to do with habits, and

- what the *LATTE* method from Starbucks is.

## Blink 1 - Habits are simple cue-routine-reward loops. They save us effort and are very strong.

 

In the 1990s, scientists at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology studied mice. They wanted to see how habits form in the brain. In the test, mice had to find a piece of chocolate. It was at the end of a T-shaped maze. Scientists used special tools to watch the mice's brain activity. They watched as the mice sniffed their way to the chocolate.

When the animals first went into the maze, their brain activity went up a lot. They smelled the chocolate and searched the maze for it. The more often they repeated the test, the less brain activity the mice showed. They slowly learned how to get to the chocolate – go straight, then two sharp lefts, and finally a right.

This process is called "chunking". It is when you turn a series of actions into an automatic routine. It is the base of all habits. From an evolutionary point of view, this process is great. It helps the brain save energy. It does this by doing the same tasks well each time.

Finding chocolate in a maze or backing a car out of a driveway. These things are hard at first. They need a lot of focus. But over time, they become easy habits. Studies from Duke University in North Carolina in 2006 show that at least 40% of what we do every day comes from habits.

Generally, every habit has a three-part process, a loop:

First, you notice the external* cue*, like your alarm clock ringing. This makes your brain look for a habit that fits this situation.

Next, the routine starts: You do a typical action for that specific cue. You walk into the bathroom and brush your teeth. Your brain is sort of on 'autopilot'.

Finally, you get a reward: In this case, it's the fresh, minty feeling in your mouth. Your brain activity goes up when your brain sees that you finished the task well. This makes the link between the cue and the routine stronger.

Habits are incredibly strong. People can keep old habits even after bad brain damage. One study looked at a man who had severe brain damage from meningitis. He could not point to his kitchen door. But when asked what he would do if he felt hungry, he went straight to the kitchen cupboard. He took out a packet of nuts. He could do this because habits are learned and kept in the *basal ganglia*. This part of the brain can work normally even if other parts of the brain are damaged.

Sadly, this strength also means that habits you thought you had beaten, like smoking, can easily come back.

## Blink 2 - Habits are so strong because they create cravings.

 

Imagine this: Over the past year, you always bought a tasty chocolate cookie from the cafeteria. It was a reward for a hard day's work. You ate it with great pleasure. Sadly, you gained some weight. Your friends also noticed this. So, you decide to stop eating your evening cookie. But the next afternoon, you stand at the cafeteria entrance, unsure. You can either go in for 'just one more cookie,' or you can go home feeling grumpy.

This kind of habit is hard to break. This is because you have developed a *craving* for the reward at the end of the habit loop. Studies from the 1990s by neuroscientist Wolfram Schultz from the University of Cambridge showed what happens in our brains. Schultz studied the brain activity of a monkey named Julio. In one test, Julio sat in front of a screen. His job was to pull a lever when a pattern appeared on the screen. As a reward, a drop of blackberry juice would flow into his mouth through a tube.

At first, Julio didn't pay much attention to the screen. But when he accidentally pulled the lever at the right time and got the juice reward, his brain activity in the reward center shot up.

As Julio slowly understood the link between the screen patterns, the lever, and the juice, he started watching the screen. Also, his brain activity went up when he saw the patterns on the screen. The spikes were even as high as when he actually got the reward. In other words, his brain started to *expect* the reward. This ability of our brain is why we have cravings. It explains why habits are so powerful.

Later, the experiment changed. No juice came out, or only watered-down juice, when Julio pulled the lever. Schultz then saw brain patterns for craving and frustration. The monkey became sadder when he didn't get his reward.

The good news is: good habits also create cravings. A study from 2002 showed this: people who exercise regularly develop a *craving* for endorphins in their brain. Or they crave the feeling of success. This is how habits become strong. Just a cue and a reward are not enough on their own.

Companies and advertisers work hard to understand these needs. They try to create them in customers. Claude Hopkins was a pioneer in this area. He successfully sold Pepsodent toothpaste. Other toothpaste brands failed at the same time. His secret: he created a reward that made people crave it. It was the cool, foamy, fresh feeling. This is now the main feature of all toothpastes.

## Blink 3 - To change a habit, you must replace it with another and believe in the change.

 

Every former smoker knows: when you crave nicotine, it's very hard to ignore. So, the main rule for changing habits is this: Don't try to fight the craving. Instead, change it. Keep the cue and the reward. But change the routine to get the reward.

Studies with former smokers show that people are most likely to stay smoke-free if they know what triggers their desire for a cigarette. They also need to know the exact reward they get. Then, smoking can be replaced by a habit that gives a similar reward. You can do some push-ups, chew nicotine gum, or relax for a few minutes.

Alcoholics Anonymous (AA) uses a similar approach with great success. They have helped millions of people overcome addiction. Members write down what they hope to get from drinking. Often, things like relaxing and being social are more important than the actual drunk feeling. AA then suggests new routines that meet these desires. For example, to meet people, you can go to social gatherings or group talks. The goal is to replace drinking with habits that are less harmful.

This method generally works well. But stressful situations can quickly lead to a relapse. A research team in California found this in the 2000s. The author's story about an AA member who relapsed also shows this. The man had stayed sober for years. One day, his mother called and told him she had cancer. Right after the call, the man left his job. He went straight to a bar. He later said he was "drunk for two years".

Research suggests that *belief* makes the difference between relapsing and staying sober. So, spirituality and God are a big part of Alcoholics Anonymous. But it's not always the religious part that helps people stay sober. Believing in God simply helps people know that change is possible. It helps them feel they don't have to do it alone. Strong belief can also give a lot of strength in hard times.

## Blink 4 - If you want to change, focus on your keystone habits and small wins.

 

Paul O'Neill became CEO of Alcoa, a struggling aluminum company, in 1987. Many investors were unsure about him. O'Neill made them even more unsure. He did not talk about profits and earnings. Instead, he cared about safety at work. One investor then called his clients. He told them: "The board has given control to a crazy hippie. He will ruin the company".

O'Neill knew that not only people have habits, but also companies. To change Alcoa and make it successful, he had to change these habits. He also knew that not all habits are the same. Some habits are called *keystone habits*. They are more important than others. They have the power to also make other habits better.

O'Neill said that workplace safety was the most important thing. This made his managers and workers do something: They had to think about how to make the production process safer. And they had to find the best way to share their ideas. The result was a completely new and profitable way to make things.

Investors had big worries at first. But O'Neill changed Alcoa with great success. By the time he retired, the company's profits had grown five times.

Keystone habits are also very important for people and their wish to change. Studies show that doctors find it hard to make overweight people change their lifestyle just by talking. But if people keep a food diary, they focus on building one keystone habit. Soon, other good habits become strong. These include regular exercise or eating fruit for breakfast.

Keystone habits are easy to start. This is because you see success quickly. These are called *small wins*. When you succeed with keystone habits, it's easier to believe that change is possible in other areas of your life too. This starts a chain of good changes.

But what exactly are keystone habits?

## Blink 5 - Willpower is the most important keystone habit.

 

In the 1960s, scientists at Stanford University did a famous long-term study. In it, four-year-olds went into a room one by one. There was a table with a marshmallow. The scientists gave each child a choice: eat the marshmallow right away, or wait a few minutes and get two marshmallows. Then, they left the room for 15 minutes. Only about 30% of the children managed to wait for the second marshmallow.

Years later, the scientists found the study participants, who were now adults. They found some interesting results. The children who waited for the second marshmallow, showing patience, were more successful in school and with friends. This was compared to children who had less willpower. Willpower seemed to be a keystone habit that affects other parts of life. Newer studies find similar results. In 2005, a study with eighth-graders showed this: students with high willpower usually had better grades.

So, some people seem to naturally have more willpower than others. The fact is that people have more or less willpower depending on the day. Some days it's easy for us to go to the gym. On other days, just getting up from the sofa feels almost impossible.

This is because willpower is like a muscle: it can get tired. If we use it a lot, like at work making a boring spreadsheet, we sometimes have no willpower left when we get home. But the muscle comparison goes further: We can make our willpower stronger by doing habits that need determination. For example, following a diet. It's like training our willpower. Other studies show that a lack of freedom makes our willpower weaker. If people do something not because they want to, but because they are told, their willpower muscles get tired much faster.

Other things can also affect our willpower. At Starbucks, employees often smiled and seemed happy. This was thanks to their willpower, no matter how they felt personally. But in stressful times, like when a customer got angry and loud, this relaxed feeling quickly disappeared. Studies inspired the management to get all staff ready for bad situations. They got a plan to deal with customer complaints. This plan, called the *LATTE* method, was meant to give staff the willpower they needed. This would help them stay calm with customers in stressful times. The method works like this:

- 
**L**istening to the customer

- 
**A**cknowledging their complaint

- 
**T**aking action

- 
**T**hanking the customer

- 
**E**xplaining why the situation happened

The Starbucks staff practiced this method again and again. They eventually learned exactly what to do in a stressful moment. This way, they were more likely to stay calm.

## Blink 6 - Habits within companies can be dangerous. But they can change during crises.

 

In November 1987, a passenger at King's Cross tube station in London saw a sign of fire. He told a ticket inspector. But the inspector did not check it. He also did not tell the main office. He simply did nothing. In his view, a developing fire was not his job.

It sounds strange, but the inspector's reaction was not surprising. At that time, London Underground was divided into many separate areas of responsibility. Because of this, the people working there formed a company habit: they did not cross their department lines. For decades, a strict system was in place. Bosses insisted on their power. The nearly 20,000 underground staff were careful not to cross these unseen boundaries.

Under the surface, most companies are like battlegrounds. Employees are always fighting for power and rewards. Habits like 'only taking care of your own business' create peace.

So, the Underground inspector stuck to his habit until the fire truly broke out. No one there knew how to use a sprinkler system. And no one had the power to use a fire extinguisher. Within minutes, a wall of fire rushed through the ticket hall. Emergency services were finally called after many wrong decisions. They reported very bad burns. 31 people died.

The main problem: Responsibilities were carefully divided. But not a single employee had an overview of passenger safety. Such sad events are terrible and make people act. They often give a reason to change habits within a company.

When looking into the cause of the fire, special investigator Desmond Fennel found something. Many changes that could have saved lives were suggested years before. But they were never put into action. Fennel met resistance when he suggested safety improvements. So, he made the investigation a big media event. This was the only way he could make changes happen. Today, every London Underground station has a manager. Their main job is passenger safety.

## Blink 7 - Companies use habits for their marketing.

 

Imagine you are shopping in a supermarket for a moment. What do you notice when you enter? Probably all the fresh, good-looking fruits and vegetables near the entrance. If you think about it, this layout doesn't make sense for customers. Fruits and vegetables are soft. They can easily get crushed by other items in your cart. So, it would make more sense to find them near the checkout. But marketing companies found out years ago that shoppers who already have fresh and healthy products in their cart are more likely to buy unhealthy products later, like chips or cookies.

This is not surprising. But shops have even more clever, perhaps less known, ways to influence customers. Did you know, for example, that most people walk right when they first enter a store? Retailers know this. So, they often put their most profitable products behind the entrance, on the right side.

These methods are clever. But they have one big drawback: they don't really consider how different buyers shop. In recent decades, better data collection has helped target customer groups more precisely. Target, a US retail chain, is a master at this. They serve millions of shoppers each year. They collect huge amounts of data about them.

In the early 2000s, the company decided to focus all its data analysis on one group of people: new parents. To be ahead of competitors, they even wanted to include future parents in their marketing. They wanted to do this *before* their babies were born. The smart system could find out when a customer was expecting a baby. It did this by looking at changes in her shopping habits, like suddenly buying certain vitamin pills. Then, it would send her special coupons.

The data analysis worked so well that Target 'knew' about a teenager's pregnancy. This was before she even told her parents. Target sent her coupons for baby and pregnancy items. Her angry father visited the store manager. He said: "She's still in high school... Are you trying to make her get pregnant?!" Then, the truth came out. The embarrassed father had to leave without doing anything. But Target quickly learned that customers do not like to be spied on. So, to make the baby item coupons less obvious but still work, they are mixed with other offers. These offers have nothing to do with pregnancy – diapers next to garden products and canned soup. This makes the ads seem more random.

Companies also use our habits when selling new goods. They make sure these goods feel familiar to us. For example, radio stations can help a new song become popular. They play it between two songs that are already hits. Target received a lot of criticism for its methods. But the marketing drive was still successful. Sales at Target grew from $44 billion to $65 billion between 2002 and 2009. This was partly because of products for pregnant women and future parents.

## Blink 8 - Protest movements need strong social ties, group pressure, and new social habits.

 

In Montgomery, Alabama, in 1955, an African American woman named Rosa Parks refused to give up her bus seat for a white man. She was arrested and charged for breaking segregation rules. This event and how people reacted to Rosa Parks' case marked the start of the US civil rights movement.

Rosa Parks' arrest started what are called *social habits*. These were mass protests and a bus boycott by Black people. This boycott hurt the bus company badly. Social habits are strong, unconscious behaviors of large groups of people. They change the world when the actions of a few people turn into big movements. So, this bus boycott was the starting point for the civil rights movement.

But why did Parks' arrest specifically start a bus boycott? Her act of defiance was not the first, nor the last. Many other African Americans had been arrested for the same reasons before.

Rosa Parks was very popular in her community. She also had a very wide circle of friends. She was a member of many clubs and groups. She had close ties with all sorts of people, from professors to field workers. She worked with the local NAACP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People). She was active in a church youth group in her neighborhood. In her free time, she helped poor families sew clothes. She also offered to alter clothes for young women from rich white families. She was so active that her husband sometimes said she ate at other people's houses more often than at home.

US sociologist Mark Granovetter calls such connections in many different directions *strong social ties*. These ties freed Parks from jail. They also made the news of her arrest spread quickly through all social groups in Montgomery. But her friends alone could not have kept up a long boycott. This is where *group pressure* comes in. Besides strong ties, social groups also have *weak ties*. These mean friends of friends or people you know slightly. Weak ties often have high group pressure: If a person's entire social network supports a movement, it is hard to ignore.

Over time, support for the boycott in the Black community grew weaker. This was partly because city officials made new carpool rules. These rules made life without buses harder. At that time, Martin Luther King gave a speech. He spoke against violence. He asked movement members to embrace and forgive those who oppressed them. Because of this message, many people adopted new habits. They organized separate church meetings or protested peacefully. This shows the third way social habits affect movements. The idea gains its own momentum and spreads beyond one community.

## Blink 9 - We are responsible for changing our habits.

 

One night in 2008, Brian Thomas from Wales strangled his wife. He was desperate and went straight to the police. He was charged with manslaughter. What was his defense? He suffered from what are called 'night terrors'. These caused him to do ancient actions while asleep, without knowing it.

Unlike *sleepwalking*, where some people get up and follow certain urges, the brain of people with night terrors fully shuts down. Only the most basic brain parts stay active.

Brian Thomas was in this exact state on the night of the murder. He thought he was fighting a burglar who was attacking his wife. His lawyers argued in court that the idea of his wife being in danger led to an automatic reaction from Thomas – to defend her. In other words: he followed a habit.

Around the same time, Harrah's casino company sued Angie Bachman, who had a gambling addiction. She had racked up half a million US dollars in gambling debts. Before this, she had already lost her house and an inheritance of one million dollars through gambling. In court, Bachman argued that she was just following a habit. Gambling felt good. So, she could not resist when Harrah's offered her tempting free trips to the casino.

The two court cases ended very differently. Brian Thomas was found not guilty, with much sympathy. Bachman, on the other hand, lost her case and was shamed by the public. Both Thomas and Bachman could claim: "It wasn't me, it was my habits!" So why was only Thomas found not guilty?

The answer is simple: Once we notice our bad habits, we are responsible for changing them. Thomas did not know he would kill someone in his sleep. Bachman, however, knew she had a gambling problem. She had already lost a lot of money. For example, she could have joined addiction programs. This would have stopped Harrah's from sending her more ads.

## Summary

The main message of these blinks is:

**Following habits is a key part of our lives. It is also important in groups and companies. All habits work using a cue-routine-reward loop. The easiest way to change this loop is to replace one routine with another. Keep the same cue and the same reward. Lasting changes are not easy to make. But you can do them by focusing on important keystone habits, like willpower.**

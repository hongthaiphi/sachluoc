### Synopsis

*Permission Marketing *looks at the problems marketers face in the digital age. It shows new and practical ways to market. The book shows how old ways of marketing fail in today's crowded world of advertising. But smart marketing doesn't stop customers. Instead, it gets their attention with their permission. It also asks them to take an active part in the marketing.

## What's in it for you: Build good customer relationships and keep them strong.

Do you have a business? Do you work in marketing? Or do you want to start a company soon and need the right plan?

One thing is clear:

It is much cheaper to do business repeatedly with old customers. This is better than always trying to get new customers. So, instead of using traditional ads that stop customers and fight for their attention, let them give you their attention freely.

Then you can use this permission you have gained. This will help them make a final purchase. Even after you make a sale, you need to keep the customer relationship strong. Keep their interest and trust alive by sending useful messages and offers often. *Permission Marketing* explains how to do this.

In these Blinks, you will learn:

- why old-style ad breaks no longer work,

- what 'intravenous trust' is, and

- why a prize draw can be very valuable.

## Blink 1 - Traditional marketing, which stops customers, no longer works well.

 

Today's marketers have a big problem. People don't pay attention to their ads anymore.

Most marketing has been ads that stop people for many decades.

This method was very popular in advertising for a long time. It aims to stop people from what they are doing. It wants them to look at certain products and services. A good example is TV advertising. The show stops, and viewers are forced to watch ads for cars, fast food, shoes, and more.

Why was this pushy way of marketing so popular? In the middle of the 20th century, advertisers found that products sold very well with clever and eye-catching ads. Companies using this strategy usually had very high sales. Also, it was a simple and clear process. Big companies could easily control it.

To reach many customers, companies like Procter & Gamble used 'mass marketing.' This aimed to reach many people. It did not make ads for specific groups of customers. Over time, more and more advertisers tried to stop customers. They fought for their attention. Soon, there was a flood of ads, but fewer people paid attention. Companies tried to fix this. They bombed their possible customers with more and more ads. They used stranger ways to stop them.

Today, ads are everywhere. They are on supermarket floors, taxi doors, and train station walls. They come from radio and computer speakers. They pop up annoyingly on many websites. But customers mostly ignore them. If people paid attention to all the ads fighting for their notice every day, they would have no time for anything else.

This shows that stopping people is no longer an effective way to advertise. Today, marketers who stop people struggle for too little attention. Most of their messages are ignored. Or they are quickly forgotten.

## Blink 2 - Permission Marketing works better because customers willingly pay attention to it.

 

So, stopping ads don't work anymore. But what does work?

The answer is: *Permission Marketing. *With this method, customers decide if a company can send them ads or not.

Stopping ads bombards customers without their agreement. For example, TV ads stop a show to tell customers to buy certain products.

But Permission Marketing needs clear permission from the customer. For example, a TV ad might offer a customer the chance to learn more about a product. When customers can choose to accept or refuse this offer, they get more control over their time and attention. This puts them in a stronger position.

A good example of effective Permission Marketing is *Hooked on Phonics*. This is a US company that helps children improve their reading skills. The company started a radio ad campaign. It invited parents to call a hotline for more information. So, parents were free to decide if they wanted to learn more about the product by calling. The detailed information they might get was clearly wanted by these parents, who were possible customers.  

When customers take an active part in the ad process, they are more likely to pay attention to the marketing message. They are not bombed with product information. Instead, they are simply asked if they want more details. Those who say 'yes' become perfect people to receive all future ads. This is because they have actively chosen to give their attention.

## Blink 3 - Permission Marketing still needs to stop customers, but it is more focused, fits customer needs, and is repeated more often.

 

Permission Marketing is less pushy than stopping ads. But it still needs to get the customer's attention before marketing can start. So, some kind of stop is still needed here. But unlike stopping ads that don't work (because they don't target specific groups), Permission Marketing is targeted.  

First, it is important to show customers a clear and strong benefit. This makes the stop worthwhile. Because people only care about things that are important to them. If they don't see the benefit, they will quickly look away.

Also, the message must be repeated often. Creating a personal reason for the customer is not enough. Each message can be easily ignored or forgotten. Repeating helps with this. It increases the chance that customers will notice the message. They will remember it and become part of the Permission Marketing process.

Finally, you need to focus these efforts. The message should be repeated to people who are very likely to be interested in your product. It should use the media these people usually use. Ads repeated often are not worth much if they go to a large group of people who are probably not interested.

A great example of a company that does clear, useful, and focused marketing often is the American retailer *L.L.Bean*. Instead of wasting large sums on expensive, general ads in mass media like The New York Times, the company focuses on its current customers. It sends them catalogs and new, appealing offers many times a year. This way, it is very likely that these customers will be convinced to buy sooner or later.

## Blink 4 - Permission Marketing is a process that builds strong customer loyalty.

 

Permission Marketing is a process. You don't get permission overnight. It must be carefully grown over a long time to show results.  

In Permission Marketing, the first stop should aim to offer the customer a relationship, not a product. A great example of this patient marketing is* Camp Arowhon*, a successful Canadian summer camp for children. The camp director knows that if you want parents to sign up their children, it's better to slowly build a relationship with them. This is better than trying to make a sale right away. So, the company's first marketing efforts only offer a free video and brochure. Interested customers can ask for these.

After the customer has willingly given their attention, the marketing process must move forward. The company must take care of the good feelings it has gained. This means it needs to make the first ad message stronger. It also needs to tell the customer about the product's benefits. The summer camp makes relationships with possible customers stronger. For example, it suggests a personal meeting with a camp leader and a visit to the camp.

Finally, the customer will feel like they know the company and can trust it. This should be the goal of all Permission Marketing. People are much more likely to do business with a company they see as a friend, not a stranger. Camp Arowhon found that it could build relationships with possible customers using a multi-step process. This process included videos, brochures, visits, and meetings. After this long process, it is finally easy to make the sale. Parents have gotten to know the camp and its leaders personally. They can now feel good about accepting the offer for their children. 

## Blink 5 - Growing customer trust makes more marketing possible.

 

Most people naturally avoid companies that want to sell them something. They don't want to give these companies broad permission. To overcome this distrust, companies must build a trusted relationship with the customer.

First, permission should be asked for at a basic, everyday level. For example, this happens when a customer calls an information number. A salesperson then advises them. A sale is not certain yet. But the customer offers their attention willingly and is open to marketing. If the company's person can build a talk and a relationship with the customer, their trust will grow. The more customers trust the company, the more willing they are to give permission. In the best case, this can grow into what is called *intravenous trust*.

An IV drip feeds a patient in a hospital without their active input. In a similar way, intravenous trust allows companies to make sales automatically. The customer does not have to make a separate buying decision each time. A magazine subscription is an example. The subscriber pays for the magazine months ahead. They don't know what will be in the next issue. They simply trust that the publisher will give them the content they hope for.

But be careful: If customer trust is misused in any way, they will take back the permission they gave the company. For example, if they find out their personal data has been sold to another company, the company loses their trust. Customers will then end their relationship with it. 

## Blink 6 - The internet brings Permission Marketing back to life.

 

Permission Marketing is actually an old idea. One hundred years ago, before ads that stopped people appeared everywhere, companies relied on word-of-mouth. This helped them get new customers. This meant they built long-term relationships with their customers. This is what Permission Marketing aims to do today.

Stopping ads replaced this type of marketing in the first half of the 20th century. This was because it was cheaper and easier. But today, Permission Marketing is having a rebirth. This is not only because stopping ads have lost their power.

A strong reason for the rise of Permission Marketing is the arrival of new media. With this, companies can attract customers and get their attention. The most important of these media is, of course, the internet.

First, the internet has greatly lowered the cost of sending ads. Sellers can now send their messages more often to more customers. They pay much less money for this than for TV and radio ads.

Second, the internet allows customers and companies to talk to each other. It costs the customer no money and little time to reply to a message or start a conversation.

*Yoyodyne*, an online marketing company founded by the author, is a great example. It shows how the internet helped Permission Marketing succeed. In the 90s, it used ad banners that promised prizes. This brought customers to an email list on its website. The company then contacted those who had signed up by email. It started a conversation. Longer talks increased chances of winning. So, customers were happy to allow more interaction. This gave Yoyodyne many chances. They could send messages about their own prize draw and also offers from partner companies.

## Blink 7 - From a one-person business to a global airline: Everyone can benefit from Permission Marketing.

Companies of all kinds and sizes can benefit from Permission Marketing. That's the great thing about it.

To start Permission Marketing, small businesses or service providers can send ads to possible customers through an available channel.  

For example, to get new customers, a local tradesperson can use their network of friends, family, and existing contacts. This helps them make their business known.

Once a business has gained the attention of its possible customers, the marketing process moves to the next stage. Now, keeping customers is key. The local tradesperson could offer interested people a private talk. They could boost the business relationship by suggesting appealing 'get-to-know-you' offers. For example, a discount on painting one room of a house.  

When the customer relationship is strong, the company should use the trust built to grow its business. If the local tradesperson can show their excellent work through the 'get-to-know-you' offer, the customer will very likely give them bigger jobs. This way, they can even get new clients if the customer recommends them to others.

Just like small businesses, large companies can also benefit from Permission Marketing. They can also benefit from the long-lasting customer relationships it creates. Airlines now offer frequent flyer programs. Customers collect bonus points and can later trade them for free travel. This strengthens customer loyalty. Customers now have a reason to fly only with that one airline. In return, customers give airlines permission to collect their data and send them useful offers.

## Blink 8 - Permission Marketing has lower costs in the long run and higher sales than traditional marketing.

Permission Marketing is not free. Like stopping ads, it costs money at first to get customer attention.

This process takes time and may seem more expensive than a single stopping ad at first. But overall, it is much more cost-effective because it includes many ad messages.

Also, Permission Marketing leads to higher sales. This is because it focuses on well-researched channels. These are places where the target group already pays attention. This means most people reached are possible customers. It also builds trust and lasting customer relationships. When customers see a business owner as a friend and not a stranger, they stay loyal. They buy from them again and again. Also, they will likely recommend the product to others. This brings new customers to the company without extra marketing costs.

The popular online retailer Amazon is a good example. It shows how Permission Marketing can achieve much better sales than traditional stopping ads. At first, customers are attracted by Amazon's wide product range. They also like the easy way to order through the internet. Personal recommendations make sure customers keep coming back to Amazon. This gives Amazon a large and growing customer list. It allows the online retailer to send ads to its customers for very little money. This happens if Amazon grows or has new products. This way is much cheaper than sending ads to a general group of consumers.

## Summary

The main idea of this book is:

**Stopping ads have lost their power. It's no longer easy to get and keep people's attention like it used to be. Permission Marketing gets and keeps customer interest. It gains attention with their permission. It also makes them active in the marketing process. Permission Marketing aims to build long-term customer relationships. These relationships pay off over time. **

What you can put into practice:

**Know who your customers are.**

Find out as much as possible about your buyers. Also learn about the people you want to get as new customers. Find out which media they prefer to use. Learn how they spend their free time, where they shop, and how much they are willing to pay. You can get this information from existing customer data. Or you can find it using a market research tool, like a short survey.

**Attract new customers with offers that interest them personally. **

Offers can take many forms. They must make customers pay attention to us. These can be a prize draw, details about your product or service, or some fun. The main thing is to make target customers interested enough. This way, they will give you their attention.

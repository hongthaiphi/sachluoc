**Author:** Antoine Gerschel | **Category:** Personal Development## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? RETOOL YOUR PEOPLE TO SUCCESSFULLY NAVIGATE CHANGE.

In 1963, inventors filed just over 90,000 patents in the United States.

By 2011, that number grew a lot to 535,188 – so much that Congress had to change the whole patent system.

This big increase shows something important about today’s business world: change is not just faster, it is the only thing that always happens.

Your company faces this no matter if the market is doing well or badly.

If the market is going down, you must rethink what you offer.

If the market is growing, you have to fight for good workers and balance profit with quality.

In any case, if you do nothing, you will fall behind.

But here is the key point – when factories make a new product, they don’t break down the whole factory and start from zero.

They change their machines a little, fixing what they have to fit the new needs.

The same idea works for your workers.

By quickly changing their focus, teaching them new skills, and giving them energy again, you can handle uncertainty without losing what makes your company strong.

Most businesses do this wrong.

When change happens, they first look at technology, processes, and new tools.

They wait to work on the “soft” skills, often too late.

This Blink is made to fix that.

It shows five important soft skills that help companies stay strong and successful in more and more difficult times.

## SEEING THE BIG PICTURE

Today, business is full of changes, so success means every worker must see the big picture.

It is not enough to do their tasks well – they must understand how their work helps the company reach its goals.

When Matthew Shattock became CEO of Beam, a top spirits company, he understood this right away.

He made a simple “Vision Action Sheet” on one page that showed what workers needed to stay on track: the company’s vision and mission, money goals, values, and three main performance measures.

Workers started putting it on their office walls.

This simple tool worked because it gave everyone the same guide.

The sheet also showed the company’s ten most important priorities.

Focusing on a few things is more important than many leaders think.

Research by the Hackett Group shows a clear fact – lower-performing companies handle an average of 372 priorities every year, but better ones focus on just ten.

The difference is not working harder.

It is about being clear.

As a leader, don’t think your team knows your priorities as you do.

You can check this with a simple test: ask everyone to write down what they think the company’s top priorities are, then compare answers at your next meeting.

Look carefully at what managers say compared to their teams.

Differences will show where communication is not working.

When changes happen fast, knowing business alone is not enough.

Leaders must make change personal for their teams, so workers know what is changing and why.

Just as important, they must know what is not changing.

This gives comfort and stability during hard times.

Company values usually stay the same.

Should your team focus on speed, teamwork, or accuracy?

These rules help people know what to do when everything else changes.

Steve Jobs knew this well – before he died, he started Apple University so workers could always remember the company’s core values and history.

Breaking big tasks into small steps also works here.

By making big changes in easy steps, you create clear goals and steadiness.

This is a classic idea for a reason: a journey of a thousand miles really does start with one step, and this works for changing organizations too.

Using these ideas, your team will be ready to handle any surprise changes in your business.

## A CULTURE OF INNOVATION

Most people think innovation means having a great idea – a new product or process that changes everything.

But when you change your company quickly, innovation is not about the idea itself.

It is about building a culture where new ideas can grow and succeed.

Before encouraging creativity in your workers, you must be clear about who your customers are.

Without this, you just make noise.

Try this exercise: write down your three main customers or customer groups and what they sell.

Then think about how they are different from others in the market and what you could do to make them stronger.

This is not busy work – it is the difference between useful creative energy and wasted energy.

So, how do you make people comfortable taking real risks?

Trust is the base.

Not just saying “trust your people” – real psychological safety means someone can share an idea, make a mistake, look silly to their manager, and still feel safe.

Without this safety, people only do what is familiar.

The willingness to accept failure must be strong in the whole company, not just lower levels.

Think about Nespresso, Nescafé’s coffee-in-a-capsule idea.

They started the idea in 1986 but did not make money until 1995.

That is almost ten years of losing money, when leaders could have stopped the project.

But Nestlé’s culture had trust deeply built in.

They gave the idea time to fail and succeed.

Trust alone is not enough.

You also need flexibility – the chance to change direction when new good ideas come up.

Even with a good plan, you fail if your workers do not want to try new things and work hard to make them real.

Fisba, a Swiss tech maker, shows this with rewards.

When workers suggest improvements that work well, they get 50 percent of the first gains.

This not only encourages creativity – it shows people will share rewards when they take risks.

Here is what many leaders miss: to change well, often you have to stop old ways first.

When something new comes, people often need to start fresh.

Old habits that once worked become problems.

If you want true innovation, people must honestly check their habits and let go of them when needed.

## BREAKING DOWN SILOS

We live in a time full of communication.

Social networks connect us all instantly.

Smartphones keep us always reachable.

Travel has become cheap.

But somehow, building trustful, strong relationships has become harder, not easier.

This problem is big: communication is faster and easier, yet miscommunication is also more common.

This shows in silos – invisible walls between departments that block communication.

In today’s fast business world, these walls can stop progress.

They slow decisions, cause repeated work, and break the unity you need for change.

Fixing silos is not optional anymore.

The work starts with the top leaders.

Leaders must agree and connect their goals with the company goals.

If leaders can’t do this, they cannot expect their teams to break barriers.

This takes planned effort; silos don’t go away by themselves.

Make-A-Wish International, working in nearly 50 countries, understands this well.

To break silos and encourage wide thinking, they created regional roundtables where CEOs meet to share and align their views.

The key is to have shared goals – this works beyond just leaders.

When workers see the company’s bigger goals, they can adjust faster when things change.

Some companies go further and change their whole structure to stop silos.

The lattice structure is a good choice.

Instead of only moving up, people can move sideways into new projects and technologies.

This makes the company more flexible and workers happier.

Whatever you do, you will ask: How do I explain this?

Face-to-face talks are best for clear communication and solving conflicts, but you can’t always travel far for meetings.

Email is risky for important talks – it is easy to be misunderstood and cause arguments over tone or unclear words.

Good news: video calls are a middle way.

Seeing and hearing people helps fill the gaps left by email.

You may not fully break silos by video, but you can make them weaker.

This opens the way for quick and successful change.

## MAINTAINING YOUR TEAM’S ENERGY

Keeping your team full of energy is one of the most important jobs for a manager.

People bring their private lives and pressures to work, and during hard changes, they need help to stay focused and motivated.

This starts with you: as a leader, you must show confidence.

If you are not truly motivated, your team will notice, and they will lose motivation too.

Building the right team is more than finding skilled people.

What makes a great team is self-awareness, trust, and respect between members.

Skills matter – but good teamwork is more important when you need quick change and risk-taking.

Getting your team to accept quick change means including them, not forcing change on them.

Let them help decide how the changes happen.

One good idea is letting team members choose new members.

This creates a sense of ownership that helps with other choices too.

To encourage more thinking, ask this simple question: “What would you do if you owned the company?”

This moves people from just employees to owners, thinking of solutions, not just tasks.

Team meetings can be very useful during changes.

Set positive goals by clearly saying the end point – that after this phase everyone should feel ready and confident for the next step.

Words are important here.

Use “we,” “us,” and “the team.”

Show you are all together, and that you lead your workers, not stand apart from them.

When motivating people, be open about their choices to build real commitment.

Explain their options and how each choice affects the team, company, and you personally.

When people see how their actions matter, they feel more involved and agree more easily.

And never forget this old advice: listen!

Make people feel respected by hearing their worries and needs, then answer them directly.

Only when you truly listen can you ask for their commitment to the hard work of change.

## YOUR OWN PERSONAL ENERGY

We have talked about keeping your team energized.

But what about you?

As a leader, people always watch your energy.

It affects every talk, decision, and meeting.

If you are tired, people see it.

If you are excited, they feel it.

How you keep your motivation is very personal.

There is no one right way.

But some patterns stand out when leaders face constant change.

Start with work-life balance.

Many leaders change how they think about this.

At high levels, it is hard to fully switch off – the company needs you to be reachable.

You might get a few days off, then you are back to work.

This can annoy some people, but others see it differently: what if it is not about balance, but about mixing work and life?

When your work truly interests you and satisfies you, it can give energy instead of taking it away.

The question changes from, How do I get time away from work? 

to Am I doing the right work?

If you feel dread on Monday mornings, taking weekends off will not fix the real problem.

Other leaders disagree strongly with this mixing idea.

In fact, evidence shows working less can mean working better.

For example, software company 37signals uses a 4-day, 32-hour week and finds workers focus better on top work.

Google uses “20 percent time:” one day a week for projects outside regular jobs.

This is not time off, but it gives creative space and makes workers happier.

This leads to a tough question: do you as a leader have what you need to handle fast change?

Some qualities make leaders strong in hard times.

First is a true will to work hard.

Not fake busyness, but real effort to face troubles directly, not avoid them.

Next is what you might call “challenge calluses.”

Points for being tough come from experience.

To get through big changes, you must be okay with being uncomfortable; every problem you face makes you stronger for the next one.

Finally, you need curiosity.

Fast change brings long times of doubt and not knowing, and curiosity gives you courage to try new things when your instincts say to stick to the old.

It helps you make better choices and notice great ideas others miss.

In a world always changing, curiosity keeps you moving forward.

## FINAL SUMMARY

In this Blink to Rapid Retooling by Antoine Gerschel and Lawrence Polsky, you learned that handling constant change needs more than new tools or better methods – it needs attention to soft skills many companies forget.

Success begins with clarity: workers must know your top priorities and core values to change fast when needed.

Build a culture where trust allows innovation and smart risks, because new ideas need room to fail before they succeed.

Break down silos by aligning your leaders first, then involve workers in change instead of forcing it from above.

Keep your own energy by checking if your work gives you strength or drains you, and build resilience by accepting tough times.

The companies that do best in hard times are not always the smartest or richest – they focus on the human parts that let them change fast.

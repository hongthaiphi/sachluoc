**Author:** Tom Griffiths | **Category:** Psychology## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? UNCOVER THE LAWS THAT GOVERN OUR MINDS.

About 300 years ago, some famous thinkers tried to find the laws that control the world around us.

Newton, Descartes, Hobbes, Leibniz – they all wanted to find the Laws of Nature. They tried to use math to explain how things work.

Today, these laws, like gravity and acceleration, are taught in physics classes worldwide.

But what about the world inside our minds?

Those thinkers were also looking for the Laws of Thought.

This search led to formal logic, cognitive science, Bayesian ideas about the mind, and even the neural networks used in today’s AI.

This summary will take you on that fast-paced journey of ideas.

So, ready to think deeply about thinking?

Let’s start!

## THE EARLY DAYS OF FORMAL LOGIC

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz may have been the smartest man alive.

He was born in 1646 in Leipzig, Germany, and he helped create calculus at the same time as Isaac Newton.

But no matter how hard he tried, he could not find a way to use math to describe how our minds work.

Leibniz followed Aristotle, who used syllogisms – arguments with two statements and a conclusion that follows if the statements are true.

Here’s an example: Every healthy person is happy.

Some healthy people are rich.

So some rich people are happy.

If the first statements are true, the conclusion is also true.

Aristotle found 14 types of syllogisms using intuition and proofs.

Leibniz wanted more; he wanted a math rule that would show when syllogisms are correct.

He wanted what we now call a formal system.

Many great minds joined him, including Descartes.

But the next big step came over 100 years later from a 17-year-old English teacher.

One day, walking through a field, George Boole realized human thoughts could be shown using algebra.

The key was to limit variables so they could only be 0 or 1.

Boole made algebraic statements for logical ideas like “Every A is B.”

Philosophers later used Boole’s ideas to build a more complete formal logic system, called propositional logic.

You might ask – why does this matter to us?

One big problem for us is knowing what to believe.

If we know some things are true, what else can we trust to be true?

Formal logic helps us answer this.

It changes meaning (semantics) to rules about symbols (syntax), so truth comes from following rules.

This helps us better understand how thinking works.

But this is not the end of our story.

## THE RISE OF BEHAVIORISM

In the first half of the 20th century, psychology started as a new science. But the mind is invisible and hard to study.

Early psychologists watched how people acted and guessed what thoughts or feelings were behind those actions.

Wilhelm Wundt did an experiment measuring how fast someone pressed a button after hearing a sound. From that, he guessed how the mind worked.

Many thought this was too much guessing.

So they made a new idea: psychology should only study what can be seen or measured.

The mind’s workings were off limits.

This was called behaviorism, and it influenced psychology for 50 years.

But Jerome Bruner came along and showed problems with behaviorism.

After WWII, he and his team at Harvard did new studies.

In one study with Cecile Goodman, children were asked to match a circle of light to the size of a coin.

Children often guessed the coins were bigger than they really were, especially for coins worth more money.

Also, children from poor families guessed bigger sizes than those from rich families.

This was a problem for behaviorism: it showed people react differently based on inside experiences, not just outside things.

But this challenge did not give a clear new way to study the mind.

For that, Bruner turned to early computer ideas.

That’s where we go next.

## THE FIRST COMPUTERS

In 1833, English mathematician Charles Babbage began building the Analytical Engine, a machine to do math by following instructions on punch cards.

This was the first idea for a computer.

Babbage died before finishing it.

A hundred years later, Alan Turing at King’s College, Cambridge, created the idea of the Turing machine.

This machine had a head that could move along a long tape and read or write 0s and 1s.

The machine followed rules telling it what to write and when to change its state, based on what it read and its current state.

The Turing machine could solve any math problem a person could.

At MIT, Claude Shannon started linking logic with electricity.

He used switches in circuits to represent logical operations like “and” and “or.”

Turing and Shannon later worked at Princeton University.

There, they met John von Neumann, a brilliant mathematician and physicist who left Germany in 1933 to fight totalitarianism.

In 1944, von Neumann helped build electronic computers for the US Army.

A key part of his work was finding how computers could store and use information from memory.

This meant figuring out how to show facts in a way a computer could understand.

Jerome Bruner visited von Neumann during this work and was inspired.

He began showing how psychologists could study thought using these same ideas.

## THE COGNITIVE REVOLUTION

The cognitive revolution began.

In the 1950s, cognitive science grew rapidly.

Many important papers pushed the field to find scientific ways to explain the mind.

One idea was using rules and symbols—formal systems based on logic.

Allen Newell and Herbert Simon from the US introduced the physical symbol hypothesis.

This idea said we could create symbols that describe objects and their behaviors in the real world.

You might ask: how can simple symbols show the complexity of human behavior?

Simon gave an example of ants walking on sand dunes.

Ant trails seem complex, but ants follow simple rules.

Their environment makes the shapes look complicated. People might work the same way.

At the same time, Noam Chomsky tried to make a formal system for language.

He made phrase structure grammar, a system both rich and complex.

But this raised a question.

Children hear only limited language, yet learn it easily.

How?

Chomsky said we are born with built-in knowledge in our genes.

This problem connects to a bigger issue: induction.

Formal logic uses deduction—we know some facts and use them to find other truths.

Induction means we accept conclusions that are likely true but not certain.

For example, we see the sun rise every day.

But this does not guarantee it will rise tomorrow.

Human thought often relies on induction.

Without it, life would be hard.

Even seeing uses induction: the patterns of light in our eyes are not enough to be sure about the 3D world.

Visual illusions show this.

If rules and symbols can’t explain induction, do we need new ways to understand the mind?

One strong challenge came from Western New Guinea.

In 1968, Eleanor Rosch studied the local Dani people.

## FUZZY BOUNDARIES

The Dani had only two words for colors: “mola” and “mili.”

Rosch tested their color memory using an experiment like one done on Harvard students by Roger Brown.

The Harvard students remembered colors that matched English color names better.

But the Dani remembered those same colors well, even without color names!

Rosch saw that colors have a clear center—one shade most agree on—and fuzzy edges where people might disagree.

This seemed to come from how humans see colors, not language or culture.

What if categories like “blue,” “toy,” “sport,” or “furniture” also have clear centers but fuzzy edges?

This is hard for formal logic, where statements are true or false only.

How do we explain this?

People developed a new way to think about categories using spaces and features.

Categories like “blue” or “furniture” are like perfect models in a psychological space.

Each category’s place depends on how many features it shares with others.

When you see something, like a shade of blue, you call it “blue” because it is closer to the “blue” model than to others.

This solved the fuzzy boundaries problem and gave a new way to describe our thoughts.

But it did not show how we move through different thoughts—how we actually think.

For that, we return to computing, where a new model was created.

## NEURAL NETWORKS

The perceptron.

It sounds like science fiction.

But then, it was new and strange.

Researcher Frank Rosenblatt made this machine — an artificial visual system that could classify images using inputs like an eye’s retina.

The perceptron had three layers.

First, sensory units that activate when they see light patterns.

Next, association units that add up signals from sensory units and activate if strong enough.

Finally, response units that sum and decide what the image is.

The perceptron could tell if a shape was a triangle or on the left side of a picture.

The connections between units had different strengths or weights.

Different weights helped solve different problems.

The perceptron learned by changing weights when it made mistakes.

Many say it was the first artificial neural network.

But it only solved simple problems.

Researchers needed to find ways to show the complex world to a perceptron.

In language study, neural networks could predict the next word or understand word meanings in context.

Words were shown as points in a semantic space.

Today, these are called word embeddings and are key in how large language models work.

More data made these networks stronger.

They solved hard induction problems by finding the right answers from data, without formal logic.

But a big question remained: why do neural networks need much more data than humans?

To answer, we look at one more way to think about the mind: probability theory.

## BAYESIAN INFERENCE

Everyone uses probability daily.

We guess how likely it is to rain or to get a job promotion.

When we get new information, like dark clouds or an email, we change our guesses.

How to do this with math?

In the 1700s, Thomas Bayes and Pierre-Simon Laplace solved this.

We call this Bayesian inference.

It starts by giving probabilities between 0 and 1 to possible outcomes.

These show how likely each possible world is true.

A probability of 1 means we are sure the outcome is true.

We can then update these probabilities with new events using Bayes’ rule.

This helps us understand induction better.

For example, learning a language’s grammar.

Different grammars are hypotheses, and words we hear are data.

If a learner already prefers one grammar, that grammar has a higher prior probability.

According to Bayes’ rule, a grammar with high prior needs less evidence to be confirmed.

This fits with Chomsky’s idea that children are born with knowledge that helps them learn languages easily.

Innate knowledge gives children biases toward certain grammars.

This raises the prior probability of that grammar.

Large language models start without these biases.

So their prior probabilities are lower, needing more data to learn.

Bayesian ideas also show how to improve LLMs.

To make them more like humans, we need to give them biases more like ours, developed over thousands of years.

This is what probability theory offers.

It fills gaps left by rules and symbols and neural networks.

Together, these three ways help us understand thinking.

Future scientists will continue building this picture.

### Synopsis

*The Art of Living Happily Without Excess *(1982) is a classic self-help book. What was true more than 40 years ago is even more true today: having too many things does not make us happy. Here, you will learn how to escape the pull of consumerism. You will free yourself from things you don't need. This will bring you more joy and freedom.

## What's Inside for You: Ideas for a Simple and Self-Reliant Life.

Fancy clothes, sushi, holidays in the Maldives, or all eight seasons of *Game of Thrones* – today, you can often get what you want with just one click. Everything is always there, and everything is always advertised.

But this "land of plenty" has a cost. We spend our hard-earned money quickly. We fill our homes with things that will soon be rubbish. We watch too many TV shows, and we eat too much sweet food and fast food. Why do we do this? To feel safe, successful, or important for a short time. Or just to get distracted.

But a happy life without too many things is possible! With a little mindfulness, some self-reflection, and the idea that "less is more," you can stop buying so much. You can turn to the things that truly make life meaningful. This Blink gives you some ideas on how to do this.

## Blink 1 - Simple and Content: The Inspiring Life of the Hunza People

*A good life is not about having more and more. It is about having just enough.* Does this sound like a big claim? Not for the Hunza people. They live simply and close to nature. They live in a hidden valley in the beautiful Himalayan mountains in northern Pakistan. Their way of life is: Respect your needs, but do not let them control you. We in the West could learn from this.

Living simply and with nature might seem strange in today's world of buying things. But it has clear benefits. In the 1970s, scientists found that the Hunza people rarely got common illnesses. Cancer was almost unknown among them.

One reason for this could be their simple but healthy food. The Hunza only eat what they grow themselves. So, their food is fresh, local, natural, and has no added chemicals. They eat little, but it gives them strength. They do not need big supermarkets with many types of cereal, frozen pizza, and chocolate bars. They eat whole grains, apricots, milk, and only sometimes meat. For them, food is not something to buy. It is about their connection to nature and their bodies. This is the main point: Food is for living, not for distracting, rewarding, or numbing yourself.

But it is not just their food. The Hunza's daily mindfulness also helps their excellent health. They live in a way where work and play are not separate. They enjoy what they do. They move a lot as a natural part of their day, not as exercise. For example, a Hunza person might walk for several days to deliver a letter. But instead of rushing with a serious face, they walk happily through the countryside, laughing. They enjoy the movement and the fresh air. They know that the journey itself is important.

Their relaxed approach to time also gives the Hunza a peaceful and healthy life. There is no constant stress. No one feels rushed. No one waits for retirement. No one needs holidays to rest from life. They rest every day. They simply do one thing at a time, calmly. And they work together in the Hunza Valley. Everyone helps, and no one is alone.

The Hunza understand that less is often more in life. They are not fooled by promises of modern progress. They know that new technology often brings more work and stress. Once, someone tried to bring electric lights to the Hunza Valley. But when the generator stopped working, the Hunza decided not to deal with the problems this "help" caused. They quickly got rid of the generator and went back to using candles. This is how the Hunza's natural way of life has worked for hundreds of years without problems: They wake up at sunrise and go to sleep at sunset. It is very simple.

Of course, this does not mean you should live without electricity and supermarkets. You do not have to move to the mountains with all your things. But you could start to question your own spending. What would "back to basics" look like in your daily life? For example, with food: For one week, try to eat only natural foods. This means fresh vegetables, homemade bread, and farm eggs. Also, you could try not to rush every minute and feel like you always have to be busy. For a change, walk or cycle for journeys you usually take by bus or car. This might take a bit longer, but you will be surprised how much inner peace you gain.

Also, ask yourself often: Where am I not listening enough to what is good for me? What things have I gotten used to that make my life harder, but not better? The Hunza show us the way: Happiness starts when we are brave enough to let go of what we do not need and trust in simple things.

## Blink 2 - Free and Independent: The "Less is More" Way of Thinking

Big changes often start small. If you want to break free from having too much, you do not need big, sudden changes. Instead, you need a better sense for making careful choices. A simple rule could be: Fix the small things, and the rest will often follow.

We already talked about an important first step: Walk more. This might sound like a simple tip. But this habit gives you back control. Every time you get into a car when you could walk, you lose a little bit of your independence. Walking gives you more exercise. It also gives you a sense of being able to do things yourself. You feel your body, your speed, and your surroundings. This feeling grows. If you get used to walking short distances yourself, you might also fix more things yourself. You will not run to the doctor for every small problem. You will not call for help for every small mistake. In this way, you become more independent step by step. And you also make your daily life richer.

A second point is about giving gifts. We often give gifts because we feel guilty or because of social pressure. Then, the price is set by guilt, not by joy. Free yourself from this kind of giving by setting a limit for how much you spend on gifts. This will make you be creative. It will make you focus on what really matters when giving: your attention, your idea, and your time. This makes giving gifts personal again, not just a money trade without feelings.

It is also good to pause before you buy something. Many buying choices are made too quickly. This often happens because of time pressure or a "now or never" feeling. Get used to asking yourself honestly before every purchase: Do I really need this? Or do I only want it because my friend has it? Or because the salesperson talked me into it? Or because I am tired, hungry, or in a bad mood? By waiting to buy, you gain time. This time is the best protection against bad choices. Important: "Need" does not only mean basic things for living. Sometimes a person also "needs" dignity, care, or a good feeling. A haircut, a new jacket, or a massage are not vital for life. But they can greatly help your well-being. The key question is: Does this good feeling last, or does it disappear as soon as you swipe your credit card and your dopamine level drops again?

Another important thought is about money and freedom. We too often forget this: If we spend more today than we can afford, we sell a part of our future. Debts make us dependent. At worst, they can lead us to poverty in old age. So, only spend what you truly have today. A life based on borrowing money is not really yours. It makes you less proud than being able to say: "This belongs to me. It might not be much, but I worked for it, and it is mine."

You will see: Every time you choose less instead of more, your life will become a bit lighter and happier.

## Blink 3 - Fear and Guilt: The Secret Reasons Behind Over-Spending

Having too much does not start in your wallet, but in your mind. It starts with that constant inner voice telling us we might do something wrong. It says we are not good enough or that people will not notice us. This nagging inner critic gets a lot of fuel from outside. For example, from rules, expectations, and systems in society. Then, brands and companies jump into this feeling of insecurity. They make us believe we can solve our problems by spending money with them.

The more unsure we feel, the more pressure we feel to fit in. This leads to more spending. We want to be fit, beautiful, smart, and successful. We want to meet what society expects of us. We are ready to spend a lot of money for a feeling of safety, approval, and status. This could be for another insurance policy, a show-off car, or an expensive cream for perfect skin. But all these are ways of running away. They take us further from ourselves and deeper into being dependent on buying things.

We can only stop this bad cycle if we learn to trust our natural feeling for what is right again. This inner sense has not gone away. It has just been covered by society's noise, speed, and expectations. As soon as we pause and listen to our true feelings and needs, instead of just reacting to outside influences, we get control back. We can then take responsibility for ourselves.

What does this mean for you? Well, you could start to look for the real reasons behind your fear and guilt. Do not just use them as a reason to buy. If you notice that you are only buying something because you feel unsure, stop for a moment. Ask yourself: Am I really solving a problem with this purchase, or am I running away from something?

The best way to practice this self-reflection is to regularly create small moments of quiet for yourself. In these moments, no outside opinions can reach you. For example, take a walk without headphones. Or spend a few minutes in the morning without looking at the news. This way, you will slowly find your inner wisdom again. With every moment that you no longer calm your fear by buying things, you free yourself from burdens and too many possessions. You gain inner peace and freedom.

## Blink 4 - Between Buying Noise and Expected Standards: How to Hear Your Own Voice

Buying things, wanting to buy too much, and having too many things shape our daily lives. This is not surprising, because these ideas are pushed on us all the time from outside. To stand strong against these temptations, you need something that comes from within. You need an inner compass. This will help you tell the difference between a sudden desire to buy and a real need. But you will only gain this inner certainty when you listen to what feels right for *you*. How do you find this forgotten instinct again? It's simple: By listening deeply to yourself.

Next time you face a decision or feel pressure from others, do not quickly explain yourself or make excuses. Instead, become very still. Stay still until you feel the inner worry slowly disappear. Imagine you are sitting high up in a forest, watching the sky. You see the sun slowly rise on the horizon. Only when you become very still will there be space for *your* thoughts. Then, what is truly important can appear. This is what "listening to yourself" means. You give your inner wisdom a chance to be louder than the nagging outside voices that want to judge everything right away.

Listening to yourself is actually not hard. Sadly, we do it too rarely. We easily find time for everything that *others* want from us: appointments, news, talks, work. But what about the person we spend our whole life with: ourselves? We often ignore ourselves. Yet, we only need to take a few minutes regularly and enjoy some quiet moments. Only then can this listening to ourselves truly work.

So, how can you bring this into your daily life? Every day, set aside a small, fixed time when you belong to no one but yourself. It is best to first walk for a few minutes without your phone. Feel how you connect with yourself inside. Then, sit down, close your eyes, and watch. What body feelings, broken thoughts, images, and struggles appear inside you? Do not force anything. In fact, if you try to chase results, your inner critic will take over again. Let your inner world be like a calm pond. Do not disturb it. You will see: If you stop stirring it up, it will become clear by itself.

Then comes the second and most important step: Focus on what you experience. Write everything down or record a short voice message on your phone. Sometimes it is solutions for daily problems. Sometimes it is a half, unfinished sentence that stuck with you. Sometimes it is unclear feelings. Sometimes it is unanswered questions. This information is very valuable. It comes from deep inside you, not from the noise of the outside world.

Over time, something bigger will come from this: your own standards. Many people live by other people's rules without even knowing it. They ask: "How much money do I need?" and automatically look at examples from others. By regularly listening to yourself, the question changes. Suddenly it becomes: "How much money do I need to live the life I truly want to live?" That is a huge difference. Because now *you* decide what is enough. And that is the start of true freedom.

If you want to go one step further, you can create a small life map for yourself. Write down a few areas that are important in your life. These could be: recognition and success, money and possessions, relationships and living space, health, personality, and creativity. Then, over the days, write down all the keywords that appear when you listen to yourself. You will quickly see patterns. Suddenly, you will clearly see where you push yourself too much, where you try to be someone you are not. And you will see where you might be ready to let go.

At this point, the tempting voice of buying things loses its power. Because you will not just understand, but you will *feel* that everything you need is already inside you. Instead of continuing to shop away your problems and fears, you will listen to yourself until you truly feel what is right for you. And then, you just need to do it. No constant noise, no distractions, no inner critic – just a clear path ahead, *your* path.

## Conclusion

In the end, it all comes down to a simple idea: "Less is more." Happiness is not about having too many things. It is about knowing when you have *enough*.

If you are also tired of the endless chase for "better – faster – more," and you wish for independence and being true to yourself, we have a great tip for you. Make yourself a little uncomfortable every day. Yes, really! Give yourself small challenges. For example, a quick cold shower in the morning, ten minutes of strong exercise, or a brisk walk around the block – no matter the weather. This way, you prove to yourself every day that all the resources you need are inside you. You do not need a cushion of consumer goods, easy products, or distractions from social media. You hold your own destiny in your hands. And with that, you also have the power to let go of anything that is not good for you.

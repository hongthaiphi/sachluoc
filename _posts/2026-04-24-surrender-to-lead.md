**Author:** Jessica Kriegel | **Category:** Motivation & Inspiration## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? DISCOVER HOW STRATEGIC SURRENDER UNLOCKS HIGH-PERFORMANCE ALIGNMENT AND RESULTS.

You probably know how tiring it feels to think you must be responsible for everything in your life and work. You believe that if you just hold on tighter, things will get better. But this pressure often makes you feel like a passenger in your own career, not the driver you want to be. In this Blink, you will find a new kind of strength—not one based on force or strict control. By the end, you will move past the stress of micromanagement and learn to lead in a way that makes success happen naturally. You will feel calmer, work better, and get great results without losing your peace of mind.

## THE ACTION TRAP

You know this feeling well. A project is falling behind, a team is not performing well, a goal seems further away. Your heart races, your jaw tightens, and you want to push harder, watch every detail closely, and control everything. This is called the action trap—a cycle where you believe more effort and control are the only ways to fix a problem. For a short time, it works. You see small successes. But holding on tightly wears everyone out. People stop taking chances. They stop caring. The more you try to control, the more people resist—your team, the results, and reality itself.

Here is one way to think about it. Imagine two great professional football players. One is strong, fast, and always trying to be the star. When things go wrong in a big game, she shows her frustration. She clenches her teeth and demands people notice her skills. The other player is smaller and quieter. She helps her teammates and understands the game’s flow. She trusts the team effort to win. One fights things that have changed. The other lets the game come to her—and wins.

What does this mean outside the game? Suppose you are 45 miles into a 100-mile bike ride. Your heart is pounding, the sun is hot, and suddenly you have a problem—your bike breaks or you get hurt. Everything stops. Your first thought is to be angry, push your body to ignore the pain, and fight the problem. But real clarity comes only when you stop fighting. You accept the problem and slow down. Maybe you pedal with one leg. Maybe others pass you. But when you let go of your plan for the race, you find focus to finish.

This change—from forced control to something like surrender—follows a clear process. It starts by stopping the fight against the facts. You accept what is true, not what you wish it was. When you loosen your grip, you make room for something better: trust in your team and clear purpose. This kind of surrender is not passive. It means moving from fear-driven action to calm steadiness. It means doing only what you can handle next. When you stop trying to make everything fit your plan, something new happens—you lead better, and your team achieves results you could not force.

## THE RESULTS PYRAMID

Now that you understand surrendering control and trusting your team, let’s see what happens inside a team with trust—and what stops trust when it is missing. Think of human performance as a pyramid. The results are at the top. They are easy to see and measure. But they depend on everything below. At the bottom are experiences. Every interaction, email, and meeting adds up. People use these experiences to form beliefs about the company and their role in it.

Here is the key point. Imagine a manager who only appears to demand call logs or point out mistakes. This creates a feeling of being watched and pressured. The team believes their value depends only on output, and mistakes are punished. These beliefs guide their actions: they may work hard only when watched, but will not speak up about problems. They avoid risks and stay quiet, feeling safe. Many leaders fall into this trap. They focus on the top of the pyramid—results and actions—and try to force change there. They add new schedules, reports, and stricter control. But none of this works if beliefs below don’t change. A true leader knows culture is not just words on a wall. It is how people think and act every day to get results. To get different results, you must change the experiences at the bottom of the pyramid.

There is real evidence for this. Companies with flexible cultures—where people can respond to changes—grow much faster than rigid ones. One study showed leaders who matched strategy with a culture based on belief had growth of about 44.5% over three years, compared to only 10.7% for leaders who did not. Building a high-performing team means creating a place where people go in the same direction because they believe in the mission, not because they fear punishment.

Fear is the biggest barrier. When fear rules, it stops the creativity, honesty, and responsibility that teams need. Fearful people don’t try new ideas. They avoid risks that lead to big changes. They settle for only following rules and call it alignment. But when trust and clarity are the main feelings, teams change. They stop blaming and start owning the mission. They solve problems differently. By shaping experiences at the base of the pyramid, you build a culture that pulls the whole team forward instead of needing push.

## THE RESULTS EQUATION

If culture is set well, great. That is a strong first step. But trust alone is not enough without a shared plan. Think of it like this: your team has their own beliefs, and your strategy is the plan they follow. When these don’t match, confusion and low motivation grow. You lose energy, even if the purpose seems clear. What connects all this is one simple equation that links purpose, strategy, and culture into one system.

The equation starts with purpose. The clear reason why your organization exists. This must be more than a line in a report; it should inspire people each day. Research shows that organizations with a strong purpose do 4.2% better in the market. But the purpose must be simple enough to remember and use to make daily choices.

Next, you set an R2 Vision—a measurable goal three to five years ahead that shows what success looks like. This is your long-term target. You also need short-term markers, your Key Results. These are the numbers you want this year: member growth, customer satisfaction scores, or digital service ratings. These must be clear, challenging, and understood by all. When each person and department has Key Results that support the big vision, wasted effort drops and alignment grows.

Two more parts finish the equation: Strategic Drivers and Cultural Beliefs. Drivers are the big choices on how you win in your market—the “How” in your strategy. Beliefs shape how people think and act daily.

Take Wellby Financial. They moved from reactive work to a clear mission of helping their community. This change gave teams shared ownership of results and changed how people thought inside the company.

When everyone feels ownership, leaders can let go of false control and trust the system. When people understand the “Why,” “Where,” and “How,” they act well on their own. This makes a company truly flexible—able to change strategy and culture quickly as things change.

## TOOLS FOR ALIGNMENT

So far, you have built the equation—purpose, strategy, and culture working together. This is the engine. But an engine only runs if someone starts it every day. That means your main job is Chief Repetition Officer. Yes, you will repeat the message many times. That is necessary. True alignment happens when everyone hears the message so often it becomes automatic. Base every meeting and email on your purpose and strategy. When people hear it enough, they don’t need permission to act—they already know where things are going and why.

This steady message supports a powerful cultural tool: Focused Recognition. You may have seen “employee of the month” awards that feel empty. The difference is focus. Link recognition to a specific action, connect it to a cultural value, and show the result it created. Everyone watching learns what is truly important. You are shaping behavior in real-time.

Look at a big plane program that was late and over budget with a blaming culture. The leader, Ray, saw teams blaming each other instead of taking responsibility. He used a story—a McDonald’s drive-thru analogy—to explain financial details in a way everyone could understand. Then something small changed everything. A janitor found a scrap of titanium that would normally be thrown away. This janitor now understood how every penny counted. He recycled it. The manager noticed, praised him, and shared the story with everyone. This one act changed the mood. The whole team moved from not caring to feeling ownership. The program was saved and saved over two hundred million dollars.

This ownership needs one more tool: Focused Feedback. This requires a mindset shift—from giving feedback to asking for it. Doug Merritt, a leader in cloud networking, built a learning culture by showing his own weaknesses. He held daily meetings where teams talked about what went well, what was hard, and how to improve. By being the first to admit he didn’t know everything, he made it safe for others to stop hiding mistakes and start solving problems together. This is the heart of surrendered leadership: stop fighting to be right and start fighting to learn. This is the only way to find the next right step when things change fast.

## THE POWER OF ACCOUNTABILITY

The desire to learn from mistakes leads to an important mindset choice. Imagine a line. When things go wrong—a deal fails, a project is late, a competitor wins—the natural reaction is to fall below the line. This is the victim mindset: blaming others, making excuses, waiting to see what happens. It feels right at the time. But while you defend yourself, you lose power to change the outcome.

Rising above the line means real accountability. It is a personal choice—not a tool to pressure others. It means fully owning your results.

There are four steps. First, See It—look at the truth without hiding behind ego or fear. Second, Own It—stop blaming others and accept your role in the situation. This moves your focus from what you cannot control to what you can influence. Third, Solve It—stop asking who is to blame and ask, “What can I do next?” Use your creativity to find a way around the problem. Fourth, Do It—take the next right step with honesty, keep your promises, and keep moving.

This happened clearly with Scott Wine, who led a global team of 40,000 people. He showed this “Above the Line” attitude by being open and honest. His team stopped being defensive and started delivering results the right way.

Here’s the paradox. Total accountability is only possible after you fully surrender control. If you keep fighting things you cannot change, you will keep blaming others. But when you let go, you see clearly what you can control. You stop feeling tired from carrying everything and feel energized by your mission. You lead with trust and care, and the results you want will start to come to you.

Instead, you build a culture where responsibility and purpose grow naturally. By moving from the “Action Trap” to the “Results Pyramid,” you allow your team to work from trust and shared ownership, not fear. Experiences shape beliefs, beliefs guide actions, and actions create results. The Results Equation connects your purpose with your strategy, creating a lasting engine for growth. Leading “Above the Line” means facing challenges bravely and taking the next right step for your shared vision. When you stop focusing on small details, you can achieve great things.

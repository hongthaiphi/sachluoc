**Author:** Duncan J. Watts | **Category:** Psychology## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? LEARN HOW TO AVOID THE PITFALLS OF COMMON SENSE.

Every morning, people wake up and make thousands of decisions without thinking twice.

Which shoe goes on first.

Whether to grab an umbrella.

Which side of the escalator to stand on.

These small choices happen easily, guided by something we rarely question: common sense.

It's the wisdom of everyday life – a mental library built from years of meeting people, avoiding mistakes, and learning what works.

Common sense tells you not to go to work without pants.

It tells you not to touch the stove when it's red hot.

It helps you remember to look both ways before crossing the street, even when the light is green.

But here is where things get interesting – and a little worrying.

That same trusted guide, which helps with escalator rules and clothes, sometimes gives very bad advice when things get difficult.

When you try to guess which fashion will be popular next season, or why some paintings become very valuable while others do not, or why people in other countries make very different choices – common sense doesn’t just make mistakes.

It fails badly.

In this Blink, we will show you times when common sense fails completely.

Not to say common sense is useless, or that you should not trust it, but to help you see when you might be using the wrong way to think.

Think of it like using a butter knife to tighten a screw – it might work sometimes, but there is a better tool.

The real danger of common sense isn’t that it is sometimes wrong.

It’s that it usually feels right.

Once you learn to notice this feeling, you will start to see the world in a very different way.

## COMMON SENSE ISN'T COMMON

You are standing on a crowded subway platform in your underwear.

Everyone stares.

You feel very embarrassed because – well, clearly you should wear pants on public transport.

Right?

But: who decided that?

Where is the rulebook?

What we call "common sense" feels like it is true for everyone, like gravity or sunrise.

It is the invisible system of daily life – which side of the escalator to stand on, if it is okay to cut in line, how to share a dinner bill.

These unwritten rules seem so *obvious* that we rarely question them.

Until they break completely.

Let me show you what happens when researchers play a simple fairness game in different cultures.

The results show something surprising about what we think everyone "just knows."

The game is called the ultimatum game, and it works like this: Two strangers sit across from each other.

One gets $100 and must decide how to share it – from keeping all to giving all away.

The other person has two choices: accept the offer and both keep their shares, or reject it and both get nothing.

When Western players played, they often chose a "fair" solution: a 50-50 split.

Offers under $30?

Rejected immediately.

People would rather get nothing than accept what felt like an insult.

Common sense, right?

Fairness means equal.

Then researchers took the game to the Machiguenga tribe in Peru.

The offers dropped to 25 percent of the total.

Even more surprising: almost no one rejected these low offers.

What seemed unfair in New York or London seemed normal in the Amazon.

Plot twist: In Papua New Guinea, the Au and Gnau tribes did the opposite.

Players offered *more* than half – sometimes much more.

Generosity above the 50-50 split.

Yet these generous offers were rejected just as often as stingy ones.

Too much fairness was somehow...

.

.

unfair?

What is happening here is not that some cultures "get it" and others do not.

Each group follows their own common sense perfectly – but common sense itself depends on the society each group lives in.

The Machiguenga live in small family groups and trade little.

The Au and Gnau have gift-giving traditions where taking too much causes social debt.

Same game.

Same money.

Very different "obvious" answers.

This is more important than you might think.

When we try to fix big social problems – making policies, guessing behavior, building systems – we rely on common sense.

We think people will act "rationally" or "naturally" when given motivation.

But if common sense changes between cultures and contexts, our solutions may work well in one place but fail badly in others.

We are building on sand, thinking it is solid ground.

The feeling of fairness, the expectation of pants on subways – none of these are hardwired.

All are learned.

That means they can be unlearned, changed, or missing somewhere else.

## WHEN COMMON SENSE BECOMES DANGEROUS

Chicago's South Side, 1962.

Twenty-eight tall towers rise, each sixteen stories high, stretching for almost two miles.

The Robert Taylor Homes.

Architects and planners gathered for the start, sure they had the answer for public housing.

They designed what they *thought* would work – big apartments, modern features, smart layouts.

Their plan came from more than data: it came from their gut feeling about how people should live.

Fast forward thirty years.

Those towers became known for violence, poverty, and decay – worse than the slums they replaced.

What went wrong?

The planners fell into a trap that affects many decision-makers, from politicians to scientists.

They trusted common sense.

Common sense helps in daily choices.

Should you wear a coat if it is cold?

Take the shorter way to work?

These small decisions match our gut understanding of how the world works.

Common sense helps us follow social rules and fit in society.

But when the problem is bigger – from personal to social – common sense starts to fail.

The Robert Taylor Homes planners were not careless.

They truly believed their design would improve people's lives.

They had seen poverty – read news, watched people struggling.

That familiarity made them sure.

Why do careful studies when the answer seems clear?

This shows a strange double standard.

When studying nature, we demand proof.

Scientists don’t guess about gravity or chemicals – they test, measure, try experiments.

Science rules.

But with human behavior?

We trust our instincts.

Why? Because we live in society every day.

We think we understand it well.

A scientist would not guess about particles without testing, but planners feel they can redesign communities based on beliefs about families, neighbors, and poverty.

This idea – that living among people makes us experts – is what makes common sense dangerous at scale.

What helps us live daily lives becomes a blind spot for social problems.

Urban planning is not unique.

Policymakers write laws that "make sense."

Business leaders restructure based on common wisdom.

Social programs follow intuitive ideas.

Many times, these common-sense solutions fail badly.

I will explain this paradox more – how our instincts mislead us with big problems.

Seeing where common sense fails is not just theory.

It is the first step to making good decisions.

## THE INVISIBLE FORCES BEHIND BEHAVIOR – AND SUCCESS

Germany and Austria share a language, a border, and a long history.

But when it comes to organ donation, they are very different.

In Germany, only about 12 percent agree to donate their organs.

In Austria, almost everyone agrees.

Common sense tries to explain with culture.

Different values, attitudes toward death, moral ideas.

But the real reason is very simple: Austria makes organ donation the default choice.

Germany does not.

One box checked or not changes how a whole country behaves.

This shows that human behavior is not guided by fixed preferences or clear reasons.

It is shaped by the situation – the invisible system around choices.

Defaults are only the start.

Small hints guide our actions without us noticing.

Studies show that seeing words about old age makes people walk slower.

Suggested numbers influence our donations or estimates by pulling us to certain points.

These forces work quietly, below our awareness.

But when we watch behavior, we ignore them.

Instead, we tell simple stories.

Someone donates a lot?

They must be generous.

Someone takes a low offer?

They must be selfish or rational.

We explain actions by character, not by situation.

This mistake is not just for people.

It applies on a larger scale.

Think of the Mona Lisa.

Why is it the most famous painting?

The answer feels obvious: its skill, mystery, and Leonardo’s genius.

It must be famous because it is great.

But many paintings are just as good.

Many great works never got this fame.

So why this one?

Because success, like behavior, is shaped by invisible forces.

Researchers tested this with music.

People could listen to and download songs.

Some saw how popular songs were; others did not.

In the group with social info, early hits became bigger hits.

Songs that got a small early lead attracted more downloads because others had downloaded them.

The same songs elsewhere created different winners.

This is cumulative advantage: once ahead, it stays ahead – not because it is better, but because it already is ahead.

Popularity creates more popularity.

Success feeds itself.

This means our common-sense answers are often backwards.

We look at success and think it had the qualities of success all along.

We confuse the result with the cause.

The Mona Lisa is not famous because it is the best painting.

It is famous partly because it became famous – through chance, attention, and repeated visibility.

Put these ideas together, and a bigger pattern appears.

We misunderstand behavior by ignoring the situation.

We misunderstand success by ignoring the process.

Common sense looks for simple explanations – personality, quality, merit – and misses the small forces that drive results.

This mistake feels nice: that people act because of who they are, and winners win because they deserve it.

The reality is more complicated.

## THE MYTH OF THE INFLUENCER

And much more interesting.

What if what we think about how ideas spread is wrong?

Marketing experts spend millions chasing influencers.

Celebrity ads.

Viral campaigns focused on few well-connected people.

The idea seems strong: find the hubs, the connectors, the people with huge networks, and let them work.

One Kim Kardashian tweet reaches millions.

One post from the right person, and your product explodes.

But there is a problem.

The science does not support it.

Let me take you back to 1967, to a psychology lab where Stanley Milgram did an important experiment.

He got 300 people and gave them a task: send a message to a friend, but not directly.

They had to pass it through strangers, each passing to someone they thought closer to the target.

Six steps.

That was the average number of steps messages took to reach the person.

Six degrees of separation was born, a famous idea.

But Milgram found something else, that shaped marketing for years.

Almost half of messages passed through just three people.

Three people acted as key bridges in the network.

The conclusion seemed clear: networks need hubs.

Find the influencers and you control the spread.

Years later, researchers tested this in the real world.

They repeated Milgram's experiment, now with 60,000 people across 166 countries, using email.

Massive scale.

Real life.

The result?

Very different.

Instead of going through a few people, messages took many different paths.

Almost as many paths as there were recipients.

The network was not controlled by hubs – it was equal.

Information passed through thousands of routes, treating people almost equally.

This brings us back to Kim Kardashian and her expensive tweets.

Companies paid a lot, thinking they bought her influence and access.

But what if they gave that money differently? One dollar to 10,000 regular people?

What if power was shared by the crowd, not held by a few celebrities?

The truth is: we all help spread information.

Not just the famous.

Not just the well-connected.

*All of us.

* The network depends on many people, not only a few.

Common sense says chase the hubs.

Data says trust the crowd.

## THE STORIES WE TELL OURSELVES ABOUT YESTERDAY

A French sailor stands on his ship deck in the English Channel, 1337.

Salt spray stings his face.

English ships come into view.

In hours, cannonfire and chaos.

Blood on wood.

Men screaming, falling into cold water.

What is he thinking?

That he is starting the Hundred Years War?

Of course not.

He thinks about surviving.

About his family at home.

About the wind.

He does not know that years later, historians will say this was the start of a great war that changed Europe.

The gap – between what people *lived* and what we *say* they lived – is where history becomes unclear.

I want to show you something surprising about how we understand the past.

We believe we learn from history, taking lessons from the past.

But common sense tricks us, turning messy reality into neat stories that feel true but might be wrong.

Take Iraq, 2007.

Violence had been rising for years.

Then came the surge – 30,000 new American troops.

The next summer, violence went down a lot.

Most people think: surge caused peace.

Simple.

Nice.

But we miss something important.

What if there was no surge?

We will never know.

That other timeline does not exist.

At the same time, the Iraqi Army fought militias more strongly.

Sunni groups turned against Al-Qaeda.

Some neighborhoods had already separated, lowering conflict.

Many things changed together.

Which was most important?

We cannot say for sure.

But common sense wants one clear answer.

This problem is bigger than Iraq.

When we look back, we watch a movie, but the people in it were acting without a script.

They did not know which moments would be historic.

They did not know the story we tell now.

The sailors in 1337?

They thought they were solving trade problems or small battles.

The phrase "Hundred Years War" came centuries later.

It meant nothing to them.

We make these stories – beginnings, middles, ends – because we want order.

Stories help us manage chaos, remember, and teach.

But they are *our* stories, not history’s truth.

We write stories on top of real events, then mistake our summaries for reality.

So when we say we "learn from history," maybe we learn from our own stories, not from the real, messy past.

History is not a book.

It is millions of people who had no idea what was happening in the big picture.

## THE ILLUSION OF PREDICTION – AND ITS LIMITS

Financial advisors speak like prophets.

“Invest here,” they say, with charts like holy books.

“The data is clear.

The path is obvious.

” Millions listen.

Then the market crashes.

The surprise is not that experts are wrong – but that they believe they can be right.

This raises a hard question: what if prediction itself is flawed?

Common sense tells us there is one future ahead.

One path.

One result.

Experts just need to read the signs.

But real life is not like that.

The systems – markets, politics, technology – are complex and linked.

Small changes grow and cause unpredictable results.

There is no one future, but many possible futures, shaped by small changes now.

But common sense tells us to focus only on what seems important.

Ignore unlikely events.

Focus on what matters.

The problem is we only know what matters after it happens.

On September 10th, 2001, airport security watched for known threats: weapons, usual hijackings.

They did not think planes could be weapons themselves.

They missed this until it was too late.

Prediction fails not because we are not smart, but because we ask the wrong question.

We search for one future, but should prepare for many – even the strange ones.

What can we do instead?

One way is to use the crowd.

Prediction markets combine many individual guesses into one forecast.

One person guesses too high, another too low.

Positivity balances negativity.

Sometimes, this collective guess is better than one expert.

But this has limits.

Big decisions – new products, big crises – do not happen often enough to learn patterns.

They are one-time events.

No data to guide us.

So we try another way: scenario planning.

Imagine many futures.

Prepare for each.

Stay ready.

This looks smart – until real life surprises us.

In the 1980s, an oil company planned three futures: slow, average, fast growth in supply.

All expected more oil.

Instead, oil went down.

None imagined this.

The future was not in their plans – it was something new.

This is the deeper problem with prediction.

Even when we plan more, ask more, and imagine other futures, we stay inside what we know.

The real future does not have to follow our ideas.

If prediction keeps failing – even with the best tools – maybe prediction is not the goal.

Maybe the real task is simpler and harder: not to see the future, but to be ready when it comes.

## STOP PREDICTING, START RESPONDING

Every January, fashion experts meet in Milan, Paris, and New York to say what colors, styles, and shapes will be popular 18 months later.

They study trend reports.

They analyze culture.

They make big claims about style’s future.

And almost no one checks afterward if they were right.

Think about that.

An industry based on prediction, but no one checks accuracy.

So what if it is pointless?

Zara found a better way decades ago.

While others hired trend experts and made big bets on possible futures, Zara did something very different.

They watched what people were *already* wearing.

Right now.

On the streets.

Their method – called measure-and-react – works in three simple steps.

Designers watch real people in real cities, noting what is popular naturally.

They make small new styles based on these notes and send them to some stores.

Then the important part: they watch carefully.

What sells out by Tuesday?

What remains on racks by Friday?

Successful items get more supply fast.

Unpopular items get dropped without regret.

No guessing.

No predictions.

Just watching and acting fast.

This idea is now used beyond fashion.

In the 2000s, Google and Yahoo!

found they could track flu outbreaks by counting searches for "flu" and "flu shots."

Their data matched official health reports but came faster, without waiting for doctors.

The internet creates a constant feedback loop that makes measure-and-react powerful.

You are not guessing behavior; you are *reading* it as it happens.

But – and this is important – you cannot always measure and react.

Some decisions are too big and final.

If you pick a factory location or change your whole company, you cannot just try it and change next week.

Should you then trust your feelings and hope for the best?

Not quite.

I want to show you a different way: use local knowledge.

When Toyota improves an assembly line, they do not hire outside experts to guess efficiency.

They ask the people working there every day.

These workers know where problems happen.

They understand why things get stuck.

More importantly, they often made their own fixes.

Their closeness to the problem gives them insights no far-away expert has.

This is not about democracy or feeling good – it is about using the best data.

Workers closest to the problem know things spreadsheets miss.

Whether tracking flu from search data or redesigning a factory with worker advice, the rule is the same: the present has more useful information than any future prediction.

You just need to look in the right place.

## THE LUCK WE DON'T SEE

A driver blinks, tired at the wheel as lights blur past.

He is almost home.

His eyes close for one or two seconds – and then comes a terrible crash.

A child, suddenly there, lies in the road.

We send the driver to jail.

Yes, for sure.

He deserves it, right?

But rewind.

Same driver, same tiredness, same closed eyes.

But this time the street is empty.

He makes it home, goes to bed, and wakes with no idea what almost happened.

Do we punish him for what *might* have happened?

No, that would be crazy.

The only difference is luck – terrible luck about where the child was.

The driver’s actions, carelessness, danger to society?

Exactly the same.

What changes everything is outside his control.

This reveals something about fairness.

Our common sense about justice is fooled by outcomes.

We judge people not by their intentions or actions alone, but by random results that often do not reflect who they are.

This means how society treats us is often a matter of chance.

Good or bad luck.

This is not just thinking.

Researchers following people with the same skills found a surprise: after 5 or 10 years, their success is very different.

Same start, very different ends.

The difference?

Random events and lucky breaks.

Someone meets the right person at a party and gets a dream job.

Another, just as skilled, misses that chance.

One succeeds; the other struggles.

Not because of skill, but because of luck – timing, place, coincidence – the invisible hand of random chance.

Now a big question: if luck is so important, what does this mean for building society?

Philosopher John Rawls thought about this a lot.

His answer?

A fair society is one that works to reduce how much luck affects inequality.

Not stop luck – that is impossible – but stop pretending success is all earned.

We won’t fix this quickly.

Building fairness is hard work.

But we can start by noticing when common sense fools us.

When we judge people by results they could not control, we can stop.

Question.

Look deeper.

Fairness is not about punishing luck or rewarding fortune.

It is about seeing past chance to what really counts.

## FINAL SUMMARY

In this Blink of Everything is Obvious by Duncan J.

Watts, you learned that what feels obvious is often wrong.

Common sense works well in daily life.

It helps you follow social rules, make fast decisions, and avoid small errors.

But when things are more serious – explaining success, understanding behavior, or forecasting – it misleads you.

We saw how.

We think behavior shows character, but it often depends on context.

We think success shows quality, but often it comes from timing and momentum.

We think the past tells a clear story, but it is a messy mix we simplify later.

We think the future can be predicted, but it has many possible paths.

Each time, common sense gives easy answers – but leaves out something important.

That is why it is so powerful.

It doesn’t feel like guessing.

It feels like knowing.

But knowing its limits gives you something better: a new way to see.

One that asks what is missing, what hidden forces work, and which ideas you assume are true.

This change – from trusting feeling to questioning it – is the start of uncommon sense.

It is not about having all the answers.

It is about doubting easy answers.

Today we have many tools to understand the world better.

But the real challenge is not more data – it is letting go of what feels obvious.

Because common sense is fast and feels good.

Uncommon sense is slower and feels strange.

But it offers something better: a clearer look at reality.

Not perfect.

Not whole.

But closer than before.

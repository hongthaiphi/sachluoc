*Author: **Morgan Housel***

Reading time: 19 minutes

### Synopsis

*The Art of Using Money Wisely* (2025) looks at why money is such a hard topic for us. The simple reason: We think too much about how to earn it. We don't often think about how to spend it best. Money is not just a goal itself. It is a tool that should make our lives easier. So, learn how to use money in a way that truly fits your wishes and needs!

## What You Will Learn: How Money Can Improve Your Life, Not Just Your Bank Balance.

But money can also be a tricky thing. We work hard every day to earn it. But we also find it hard to spend it wisely. We buy things that we stop liking after just a few weeks. We save money not because we enjoy planning for the future. We save because we fear having no savings later. And we often confuse being rich with being happy. Money should make our lives easier. But instead, it causes us worry, stress, and sadness. We want more, we feel jealous, and we compare ourselves to others. We should use what we earn to find peace and space.

Morgan Housel is an expert in the "Psychology of Money." He has an interesting idea: Managing money well is less about strict self-control. It is more about truly knowing yourself. Real luxury is not being able to buy anything you want, whenever you want. It means understanding what is truly important to you. When you understand this, money can finally do what it should: make your *life* richer, not just your bank account.

## Blink 1 - It's Not About Earning, It's About Spending

Many people chase random money goals throughout their lives. They want a certain amount in their bank account, a number on their payslip, or an amount for their pension. But when they finally reach these goals, they realize they are still not happy. The reason is simple: We confuse what we want – which is money – with what we truly need. These are health, closeness, purpose, and connection. Without these basic needs, life feels empty, even with a full bank account.

So, the real problem is not *earning* money. It is how we *spend* it for a good life. Imagine a young man who spends too much money on a fancy car to impress others. Or think of an older woman who retires after a life of saving. She still doesn't treat herself, because saving has become a habit for her. These are not *financial* problems. They are *psychological* habits. These emotional patterns show how much our money habits are shaped by fears, hopes, and comparing ourselves to others.

In school, money is taught like a dry science. It uses formulas, tables, and logic rules. But in real life, money is more like art or beauty. Everyone has a different idea of what "enough" means. Our background, natural personality, and personal values influence this idea. A modest and grateful person with a low income might feel richer than a wealthy business owner who is always chasing the next success.

We must stop believing that "more" is always "better." Ads, social media, and our natural desire to fit in push us into constant competition. We chase a bigger home, a better phone, and nicer holidays. But spending money to show off is like running on a treadmill forever. It is tiring and has no real goal. True wealth starts when you can truly say, "I have enough."

The psychologist C.G. Jung described five things that truly make people happy: health, stable relationships, a sense of beauty, work that you enjoy, and a way to stay calm through life's ups and downs. Money can make some of these things easier. But it cannot replace them. You can buy comfort, but not closeness. You can buy safety, but not fulfillment. 

So, the art of managing money well starts with knowing yourself. It means asking what truly makes *you* happy, not your neighbor or your boss.

 

## Blink 2 - Money Should Show How You Want to Live Your Life

We all know the pressure to do things "right" in life. There's an unwritten idea of a "successful" life. It includes a specific kind of home, a certain retirement age, and a balance between savings and pleasure. The problem with these general ideas is that they miss the most important part: you! They miss *your* needs, likes, and desires. Money only serves its purpose when you match it to your priorities and values. So, what is your own idea of "right"?

In other parts of life, this is easy for us. Your friend loves pasta, but you prefer tacos? No problem, it's just about taste. But when it comes to money, we are much less relaxed. An acquaintance buys a nice car, and you start wondering if you need one too. Someone says renting is like throwing money away, and you immediately look for properties online. We often don't realize how easily other people's ideas influence us. 

We all have different backgrounds, worries, and dreams. Some people need a lot of money in their bank account to sleep well. Others need less money for security. They are happy if they can have new experiences, travel, go to concerts, or spend time with friends. This is not about what is "better" or "worse." The important question is what helps you feel calm and balanced.

So, there is no point in getting angry about other people's money habits. Everyone has their own life story and reasons. Maybe someone buys expensive things because they grew up poor and can finally treat themselves. Maybe someone saves money very carefully because they once went bankrupt. How you use money shows where you come from and where you want to go. That's why judging other people's finances is not only wrong. It can also make you wrongly believe that your way of handling money is the only correct one. But wisdom comes from questioning yourself: Why do you spend your money the way you do? Does this spending still fit your life and values today?

Understand what makes your money habits different from other people's. When your money habits show your priorities instead of others' expectations, money becomes more than just numbers. Then it becomes a tool to show how you want to live your life.

## Blink 3 - Happiness Is the Gap Between Expectation and Reality

In 1915, the British explorer Ernest Shackleton and his crew got stuck in the Antarctic ice. For months, the crew hoped the ice would melt and free their ship. But instead, the ice crushed the *Endurance* and broke it into pieces. From that moment, 28 men were unprotected in one of the harshest places on Earth. It was minus 10 degrees, with frozen tents, and dwindling supplies. They ate seals, rowed open lifeboats through rough waters, and slept every night in damp clothes. They fought to survive for a tough 19 months. And not one of them died.

When they finally reached a whaling station on South Georgia Island in the South Atlantic, they could hardly believe their luck. They had a hot bath, plenty of food, fresh bedding, and a long night's sleep. None of these things would be special for us today. But for these men, it was heaven on Earth. Some later wrote that the first night in safety was the happiest of their entire lives. And that is the message of this short story: Happiness does not come from always having too much or from constant pleasure. Happiness comes from moments that remind us how valuable the small and ordinary things in life are.

We sometimes experience this in small ways. Think of a hot shower after a long journey. Or a good soup after a tiring illness. Or fresh sheets after a long flight. These small things feel so great because they come after a time of not having them. As soon as comfort becomes a habit, it loses its magic.

This brings us to perhaps the greatest paradox of wealth: The more you own, the less those possessions enrich your life. Someone who gets to travel first class for once is amazed by the comfort of wide seats and empty spaces. But others forget what a luxury it is to drive their own car every day. A hundred years ago, only the rich and powerful owned private cars. In the end, it's not the things themselves that change. It's our expectations.

And that is where happiness lies: in the gap between what we expect and what we get. So, stay humble. Enjoy the simple things. Use luxury wisely instead of always expecting it. The best moments are those that wake you up and remind you how good your life actually is.

## Blink 4 - Trial and Error Show You What You Like to Spend Money On

There is no single measure for what makes people happy. Some dream of luxury trips, others of a good book on the couch. Some save for an occasional visit to a fancy restaurant. Others love nothing more than a greasy frozen pizza at midnight. One person thinks first-class tickets are a waste of money. Another cannot imagine anything better. Everyone is different. And our relationship with money is just as unique. The key question is how *you* like to spend your money. And to find the answer to this question, nothing beats trial and error. It's about the good old principle of *trial and error*.

So, money only gets meaning when you use it for things that make *you* happy. Do not use it for what others tell you to. So, start choosing! Imagine you go into the sandbox of choices with a wide funnel and a fine sieve. You try many things first. Then you sort out everything that doesn't give you anything. Maybe you spend a bit more on travel, clothes, good restaurants, technology, workshops, or concerts. As soon as something doesn't excite you, stop doing it without feeling guilty. Do it like with a book that doesn't grab you: put it down and pick up another one.

So, in the long run, you don't want to consume *more*. You want to consume *smarter*. Through these experiments, you will slowly get a small list of things that truly matter to you and make you feel alive. For some, it's nice clothes. For others, it's live music. For others, it's collecting records or sneakers. Still others love to follow a hobby, learn a new instrument, or take regular short trips. The key is that your spending fits you, your values, and your likes, not what others expect.

This inner attitude makes you free. Once you know what you like to spend money on, you can more easily say "no" to other things. You no longer feel like you are missing out if you don't buy things that give you nothing. Instead, with every "no," you feel that you are making more room for what makes you happy. This might mean you like to spend money on stylish clothes and don't mind driving an old car. Or that you go to restaurants less often to save for big and special family trips. Both are fine if they fit you and your true preferences.

Trial and Error. Researchers, scientists, and creative people do the same. They try many things, throw out most of them, and keep only what truly works or brings joy. It's the same with money. You don't find the best way to spend by following rules or copying other people. You find it by bravely and carefully trying new things. And you always honestly check if how you use money truly makes you happy.

## Blink 5 - The More Luck You Have, the More Humble You Should Be

Behind almost every success story is a good bit of luck. Of course, talent, hard work, and persistence pay off. But in most cases, chance also plays a key role. This includes the social class you are born into, the contacts you make, and the pure luck of being in the right place at the right time. Knowing about this "luck lottery" changes how you see life. The more life goes your way, the more you should be kind and humble to the rest of the world.

A good example for this lesson comes from the life of actor Kevin Costner. At the start of his career, Costner was good friends with a struggling writer. The man was talented but strange and hard to deal with. Costner tried to help him by making contacts for him. But all these attempts failed. Eventually, his friend lost his way, went broke, and ended up homeless. Costner felt sorry for him and let him live in his guest room. There, his friend sat at the desk night after night and wrote. Every morning, he asked Costner if he wanted to read his drafts. And Costner refused every time because he no longer took his friend seriously. At one point, the friend even started reading his stories to Costner's three-year-old daughter. But Costner still didn't listen. And he finally asked his friend to move out, because his wife urged him to. 

The man found a job as a dishwasher in Arizona and barely managed to get by. Months later, he called again and asked if Costner had read his last manuscript. Costner had not. He only sent him a sleeping bag because his conscience bothered him. More time passed, and another call came. He asked Costner to at least look at the latest version of the text. And eventually, Costner took a moment and read it. The title of the script was: *Dances with Wolves*. The rest is film history. The movie won seven Oscars and changed Kevin Costner's career forever. And the book and screenplay came from a man no one had believed in anymore.

The story shows how little we truly know about other people's potential. Help, advice, or inspiration often come from unexpected places. Kindness and warmth are not just morally right. They also open doors that you didn't even know existed. Former US President Benjamin Franklin once said that honesty is not just a good quality, but "the best policy." The same is true for human warmth. Kindness is wise. It builds bridges that you might desperately need later.

Material success can easily twist your view. Those who achieve a lot often like to believe they earned it all themselves. But they forget that wealth, security, and freedom are big advantages. These advantages often build on the hard work of other people. And those who truly understand this do not go through life arrogantly. Instead, they are grateful and humble.

## Conclusion

That was our summary of *The Art of Using Money Wisely* by Morgan Housel. We learned that money only makes you happy when you spend it carefully on what makes you happy. So, stop trying to meet other people's expectations. Spending only makes sense when it fits your true values and needs. And remember: Small joys bring greater happiness when you use them wisely. Comfort keeps its magic when you don't just take it for granted. And the best way to use money wisely is through trying new things. Try as many things as you can. See if they make you happy, and strictly remove what doesn't. Focus on what is good for you, and let go of everything that doesn't bring you joy. Money should make your life easier, not harder. True inner wealth does not come from status or possessions. It comes from being grateful, humble, and kind.

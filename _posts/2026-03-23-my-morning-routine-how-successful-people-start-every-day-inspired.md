### Synopsis

The authors talked to 64 very successful people. This book, *My Morning Routine *(2018), is a useful guide. It helps people who want to wake up early. You might not like your alarm clock. You might often use your phone's snooze button. But your mornings don't have to be difficult. If you find a good morning routine, your whole day will get better.

## What will you get from this? Find the secret power in your morning

Let me ask you a question. How did you feel this morning when your alarm rang?

Most people probably groaned. They hit snooze. They got out of bed slowly. They felt like they had already lost the day.

But think about this. The most successful people in the world are Olympic athletes, CEOs, and famous writers. They do not just accept their mornings. They make their mornings the most important part of their day. They do not follow strict rules. Each person has made a special routine for themselves.

We will look at this together. Benjamin Spall and Michael Xander talked to 64 amazing people. They found something interesting. Each person's morning routine is very different. But they all stopped just letting mornings happen. They now control their mornings.

I am not telling you to wake up at 4:00 am and run a long race. What I *am* going to do is show you real ways to plan your morning. Think of it like a menu with many choices.

## Blink 1 - An important task or something you love can get you out of bed.

Imagine this. It is 5:30 in the morning. The room is still dark. What do you do? Most of us would probably turn over. We would close our eyes. We would sleep until it is a bit later. 

But Leo Rafael Reif is different. He is the president of MIT. At 5:30 AM, he is already awake. Not because his alarm rang. He *chose* to wake up.

You might think, "Of course he wakes up early. He leads a top university." But let me tell you what truly happens. Reif does not force himself out of bed. He is not trying hard to be busy. He wakes up because MIT is a worldwide university. While he slept, people on the other side of the world were working. They sent emails and made choices. If he waits until 7:00 a.m. to check, he will be late. This situation is very important. It gets him out of bed better than any alarm.

Remember this. Reif has a strong *reason* to get up. It is not about willpower. It is about purpose.

Now, meet someone very different. Caroline Paul is a writer. She also wakes up around 6:00 a.m. But she does not check emails. She does not manage a big university. She does something much easier. She reads. She just sits quietly with a book and coffee. She gets lost in another person's story.

Why does this make her get out of bed? Reading is not just her work. It is what she loves to do. These early morning hours are very special for her. It is the only time when the world is calm. Her phone is not ringing yet. No one is asking her to do things. She is not giving up sleep. She is taking a precious time for herself.

Then there is Andre Wagner. He is a street photographer in New York City. He leaves home by 7:00 or 7:30 a.m. He has his camera. He walks the city streets. He wants to find something special. He wants to find the early morning light. He likes how it shines on the buildings. He likes how the city looks before everyone is awake. For him, mornings are not about being busy. They are about taking pictures of beauty. This beauty only happens during those short morning hours.

Do you see the main idea? These people do not have a special power to ignore sleep. They have simply found something. This something is more important than 20 extra minutes in bed.

Now, let's talk about what *doesn't* work. This part is interesting. The authors talked to many successful people who wake up early. They found that 71 percent used an alarm clock. This is not surprising. But here is the important point: only 34 percent used the snooze button.

Think about what the snooze button does. You tell your body, "Okay, wake up now! Just kidding! Go back to sleep! Wait, no, really wake up now! Actually, five more minutes..." It is like starting a car engine but not driving. You use energy, but you do not move. Even worse, you feel *more* tired. This is worse than if you just got up at first.

So, here is the next important idea to remember. The secret to getting out of bed is not about alarm tricks. It is not about quick sleep fixes. It is about one simple question: *What's waiting for you when you wake up?*

## Blink 2 - You can do your best work in the morning. So, do your important tasks first.

Okay, let's go to the next part. Imagine a common situation. You have a project. Maybe it is a book you want to write. Or a business idea you had. Or something important just for you. Every day, you tell yourself, "I will do it later." But later never happens, right? You deal with work emails, meetings, and other tasks. Life throws many things at you. You get very tired. The thing that is important to you gets moved to tomorrow. Again.

Now meet Sheena Brady. She wakes up very early. She spends the hours from 7:00 to 11:00 a.m. only on her own company, Tease Tea. After these four hours, she goes to her other job. She works at Shopify for the rest of the day.

Think about this. She is not trying to fit her important project into small bits of her day. She takes the best hours. These are the freshest and most energetic parts of her morning. She uses them for the work she cares about most. When most people are just starting their day, she has already done good work. She is building her own future.

Todd Henry is a writer and speaker. He found something similar. For many years, he tried to do everything in the morning. All his tasks and duties at the same time. It was messy. Then he changed. He stopped trying to do everything. Instead, he made special time in his mornings for what he calls his most important work: studying and writing.

And he understood this. Mornings are not just *a* time to work. You often do your *best* work then. There is something special about those early hours. Your mind is clearer. The world is quieter. You have not yet been busy with many other things.

So how do you keep that important morning time? Here is a helpful tip. It might sound unusual: do not check your email first thing.

Why does this work? Shane Parrish started the Farnam Street blog. He explains it well. He stopped checking emails in the morning. He saw a problem. His emails were telling him what to do all day. Other people's important tasks became his tasks. Other people's urgent needs became his urgent needs.

And that is what email is like, isn't it? It makes you *react*. You answer what other people ask you for. But your morning should be about acting *first*. It is your time to take care of yourself. It is your time to plan your day. It is your time to move your life forward.

Geoff Colvin is a senior editor at Fortune magazine. He takes this idea further. Every morning, he sits down. He writes a list of what he wants to do that day. After he writes his plan, he starts working. He starts with the most important tasks. He decides what his day will be about. No one else tells him first.

Now, you might think this is a rule for everyone. But let me tell you about Chris Guillebeau. He is a writer. Guess what? He likes to start his day by reading his emails. This works very well for him. And that is perfectly okay.

Remember what I said earlier? This is *your* own test. The goal is not to follow other people's rules strictly. The goal is to find out what really works for you.

## Blink 3 - Doing exercise in the morning can help you have a healthy, happy, and good day.  

Let's be direct. Exercise is good for you. You already know this.

The authors talked to 64 very successful people for this book. 79 percent of them exercise every day. But when you look closely at *why* they exercise, you find something interesting. It is not mostly about making muscles stronger or losing weight. It is about what happens in their minds.

Look at Caroline Burckle. She is an American Olympic swimmer. She won a bronze medal. She wakes up at 5:30 every morning. She goes to the gym first. You might think, "Of course, she is an athlete." But listen to her words. She says moving her body is her way of *meditating*. The exercise is not only for her body. It also prepares her mind.

Sherry Lansing used to be the head of Twentieth Century Fox Film Corporation. She says it even more clearly. She admits that sometimes she misses her workout. Life gets in the way. But she has seen this: when she makes exercise the most important part of her morning, she feels great all day. This is not about strong muscles. It is about her way of thinking.

Now, here is what *not* to do. Do not exercise too hard. A good plan is to change your exercises each day. This stops injuries. It also keeps you interested.

Look at Lansing's plan. On Mondays and Wednesdays, she does Pilates. On Tuesdays and Thursdays, she runs on a treadmill for 90 minutes. Then she lifts weights. She changes her exercises. This keeps her interested.

But maybe you are thinking, "90 minutes? I do not have that much time." Here is the good news: you can start with small steps.

It is easy to set very big goals. Then you feel very bad if you do not reach them. Do not do that to yourself. Add exercise to your daily routine. Do jumping jacks while your coffee is made. Really.

Julie Zhuo works at Facebook. She spends only 10 to 15 minutes on her exercise machine each day. This does not take much time. So, it is easy to start. When it is easy, you actually do it. For her, exercising is as normal as brushing her teeth.

Kevin Cleary is the CEO of Clif Bar & Company. He has a different method. Every Sunday, he plans his exercise. He plans it around his work and family. He does not force his life to fit exercise. He is practical. This flexible plan means he will probably not cancel.

Here is the main idea: Morning exercise is not about hurting your body. It is about preparing yourself. It helps your mind and body feel strong. It helps you feel ready for anything that happens next.

## Blink 4 - If you meditate every morning, it helps your mind focus for the whole day.

It is easy to think meditation is just a trend. Especially if you have never tried it. Maybe it sounds strange. Or not for you. But listen to this: more than half of the people in this book meditate often.

So, why is this happening? Why do so many successful people do this?

Meet Michael Acton Smith. He is the CEO of the Calm meditation app. Every morning, he leads a group meditation. This happens at his company's office. He says it sounds very "Californian". But he has learned something. It is a great way to start the workday.

Aiste Gazdar started the Wild Food Cafe in London. She agrees completely. For her, morning meditation is the most important part of her day. These are calm times. She can think about her worries for the day. After she does this, the rest of the day goes smoothly.

Some people love meditation so much. They never miss it. Ed Catmull is the president of Pixar and Walt Disney Animation Studios. He meditates for 30 to 60 minutes every day. He has not missed a day in years. Think about that. Why would someone who leads two big creative companies spend so much time sitting still?

Here is his answer. Slowing down and focusing his mind helps him. It helps him react calmly to surprising things. If you know about leading a creative studio, you know surprising things happen all the time.

Ruth Ozeki is a writer and teacher. She also thinks meditation is very important. For her, it makes sense. She is a Zen Buddhist priest. But here is what is interesting. Her daily plan changes a lot. It depends on if she is teaching or writing. But no matter what, she always meditates. She meditates before doing anything else. Even if she has a busy teaching day. Or a calm writing day. Well, almost anything. She first enjoys a cup of coffee in bed.

So maybe this sounds good to you. But you are asking: Where do I start?

Here is the good news. Meditation means paying attention to what is around you. It means living in the present moment. So a good place to start is to focus your mind on simple things. Like making coffee or tea in the morning.

That is all. You do not need a special cushion. You do not need to pay for an app. You do not need a teacher. Just you. Be fully present. Pay attention to what you are doing.

Notice the steam from your cup. Feel the warmth in your hands. Hear the water pouring. That is meditation. It is not about having no thoughts. It is not about reaching a special state. It is about being right where you are. It is about doing what you are doing. Your thoughts should not rush to other things on your list.

Try it tomorrow morning. Make your coffee and pay full attention. See what happens.

## Blink 5 - Take some time for yourself. Do it before you start work and daily tasks.

We have talked a lot about getting things done. We talked about doing important work. We talked about making your morning better. All these things are important. But we must not forget one thing: the person doing all of this. That is you.

Finding some real "me time" in the morning is not just a nice idea. It is a very powerful way to prepare yourself for the day.

Think about it like this. Take some time to do things important to *you*. Do it before others ask you for things. This gives you a good start. Later, things will surely get busy. But you will already be in a good flow. You will be ready to handle everything easily.

Melody McCloskey, CEO of StyleSeat, found this out. She gets up at 5:45 a.m. She then spends about an hour cleaning. Or she handles personal and work things. These are not exciting tasks. But here is what is important. By the time others wake up, she is already calm and ready. This makes her work better *and* feel happier all day.

But "me" time does not have to mean chores. Take a moment to focus on something you truly like. This can help your creativity in surprising ways.

Look at the artist Elle Luna. The first thing she does when she wakes up is record her dreams. She uses a voice recorder. Why? Dreams give her ideas from her deep mind. They help her be creative. She often uses these recordings to paint her dreams later. She is catching the ideas for her art before they disappear.

These people do not jump straight into work. They do not immediately check what others want from them. They make their morning routine slower. This makes the rest of the day calmer. It stops them from getting too busy before they even start.

Think about your normal morning. How much time do you spend reacting to what other people need? Your boss's emails. Your family's requests. The news trying to get your attention. All of this is true and important. But if you never take time for yourself, you start every day feeling empty.

What if you gave yourself 15 minutes, or maybe 30? A time when you are not available to anyone else. A time when you do something that helps you. This could be reading, writing in a journal, or sitting quietly with your coffee. Or recording your dreams like Elle Luna.

This is not selfish. It is very important. You cannot give to others if you have nothing left, as they say. If you wait until the end of the day to care for yourself, you will be too tired.

The morning is your chance to care for yourself first. Whatever you want to do then, the important thing is not to miss it. Do not tell yourself you will do it later. Later never happens.

Start tomorrow. Take just ten minutes for yourself. See how it changes everything after that.

## Blink 6 - You can get ready for your morning routine the night before.

Here is something that might surprise you. Your morning routine does not really start in the morning. It starts the night before.

If you want to start your day well, you need to think about what you do before you go to sleep.

A good night's sleep is the most important thing. So how can you get enough rest? How can you wake up ready for the day? The answer is: disconnect.

This means turning off your devices. Stop connecting to the world as early as you can each evening. Some people do this very seriously.

Take Nir Eyal. He is a famous writer and business owner. He set up a special router. It stops his internet connection automatically around ten each evening. He does not need to force himself. He does not need to argue with himself. The internet just... stops. This means he is usually in bed by eleven.

David Kadavy writes books and hosts podcasts. He does something similar. He turns off all screens in his home at ten. He even sleeps with special glasses. These glasses stop light from screens affecting his sleep. If he is not ready for bed, he does not use social media. Instead, he does something calm, like reading a book.

Notice what both people are doing. They are setting limits. They are separating themselves from the always-on digital world.

Emails are another big problem. Jenny Blake writes and speaks to groups. She simply does not check her emails after 5:00 p.m. That is the rule. She stops completely. This method makes her evening calm. She feels fresh and ready for the next morning.

But disconnecting is not only about turning things off. It is also about what you turn *to*. Thinking quietly about your day can help you relax.

When Jenny Blake goes to bed, she asks herself about her day. What were the good parts and the bad parts? She thinks about what she is proud of. Or what she is thankful for. This simple habit helps her relax. It helps her clear her mind. After she does this, she falls asleep very quickly.

I know truly disconnecting is sometimes hard to do. We live in a world where people always expect us to be available. But here is the thing: small details can change everything.

Take Bob Moore. He started the health-food company Bob's Red Mill. Every evening, he puts out the clothes he will wear the next day. That's all. But think about what this does. It makes his mornings much easier. He has one less decision to make when he is still sleepy. One less thing to think about.

These things might seem small. Turning off your internet. Laying out your clothes. Thinking about your day. But they grow into bigger changes. They create a calm space. This space is between your busy day and the rest you need. They tell your brain: We are finished now. It is time to relax.

## Blink 7 - Get enough sleep. Then you might not need an alarm clock.

If you find it hard to get out of bed, there is often a clear reason. You did not sleep well.

How much you sleep affects your mornings more than anything. Good sleep is very important for your health and happiness.

Arianna Huffington started the Huffington Post. She learned this in a difficult way. Once, she did not get enough sleep. She fainted and broke a bone in her face on a desk. Imagine that moment. You fall because you are so tired. You wake up hurt. You know you must change.

That moment truly made her change. She decided to make important changes in her life. Today, she tries to get eight hours of sleep. To do this, she goes to bed at 11:00 every night. She never misses it. And here is the amazing part: she sleeps so well that she does not need an alarm anymore. Her body simply knows when it has had enough rest.

Scott Adams makes the Dilbert comic. He also planned his day around getting enough sleep. For him, this meant waking up as early as possible. Mornings were when he worked best. He also made sure he was in bed by 11:00 p.m.

He was very clear about why this was important. Not enough sleep, he said, makes your mind slow. He believed that being tired could lower your IQ score by ten points. Ten points. Think about that. You could be making choices and working. But you are working much worse than you can. Only because you are tired.

This brings us to a new idea. Maybe it is time to stop using an alarm. Stop forcing yourself out of sleep.

That is what investor Brad Feld decided. For years, he made himself get up at 5:00 a.m. on weekdays. No matter where he was. Then on weekends, he tried to "catch up" on lost sleep. Sadly, this bad routine led to a serious period of sadness.

After feeling so bad, he changed his method completely. Now he makes sure he gets enough sleep. He just sleeps until his body wakes up naturally. This might be 5:30 a.m. or 9:00 a.m. And he is fine with this changing time. He listens to what his body needs. Not what a set schedule tells him.

Arianna Huffington puts it perfectly: "An alarm, in most situations, is a signal that something is not right."

Think about that for a moment. If you need an alarm to wake you, it might mean you do not get enough sleep. Your body is not finished resting. But you are waking it up anyway.

I know not everyone can just stop using their alarm. You cannot always sleep until you want. You might have children. Or a job with set hours. Or other things you must do. That is life. But even if you cannot sleep late, you can still try to get enough sleep. Go to bed earlier. Or try to sleep at the same time every night.

## Blink 8 - Children decide when you wake up. But being a parent also brings joy.  

If you had a perfect morning routine before having children, I have news. Your mornings will change a lot. Really.

When you become a parent, your lovely routine will likely not fit your life. And this is not always bad. Children need routines just like adults. The main thing is to change your routine. Make it work for your children too.

Here is the first thing to know. Your children are your alarm clock now. They will decide when you wake up. They will be more exact than your phone.

Nick Bilton works for Vanity Fair. He used to wake up around 6:00 a.m. to work. Now? His young child wakes him every morning at 5:30. That is his new routine. Even if the children sleep late, his dog makes sure he is up. When you are a parent, there is no snooze button.

Biz Stone helped start Twitter. He tells a similar story. His five-year-old child wakes him between 6:30 and 7:00 a.m. every morning. This is a clever time. It leaves one hour for play. Then the day truly begins.

So, if you are a parent and want to get things done in the morning: some work, some exercise, some quiet time for you. Then you need to wake up before your children.

Bob Ferguson is an attorney general in Washington State. He wakes up between 5:00 and 6:30 a.m. This gives him 1 to 2.5 hours for himself. His twins wake up at 7:30 a.m. He is trying to get some personal time. He wants it before the busy part of the day starts.

Whatever routine you choose, the most important thing is to enjoy time with your family. Do this once everyone is awake. This is very important if you work late, like Ferguson. Those morning moments with your children might be the best time you have with them all day.

Listen, I will be honest: Being a parent changes everything. Your sleep. Your exercise. Your quiet morning coffee. Your ability to plan anything. It is messy. It is tiring. You cannot guess what will happen.

But it is also for a short time. Your children will not always wake you at 5:30 a.m. They will not always need you right when they wake up. One day, probably faster than you want, they will sleep late. Then you will miss those early morning interruptions.

So yes, change your routine. Wake up earlier if you need time for yourself. Be flexible. Do not expect too much on hard days. But also? Enjoy the mess. Let your young child be your alarm. Let playtime be your meditation.

Because here is the truth. The perfect morning routine is not the one that looks good on paper. It is the one that really fits your life now. With your children and everything.

## Blink 9 - When you travel, try to change your routine. Do not stop it completely.

You might be on holiday or a business trip. When you travel, you are in new places. These places might not have your usual comforts. These comforts help your morning routine. Travel can quickly break your careful plans.

But the good news is, you can take steps. You can keep some control over your mornings. Even when everything else is new to you.

The first tip is to try to keep at least some parts of your routine. I said *some*, not all. This is important.

Look at Cameron Russell. She is a model and activist. She travels a lot for her work. This has completely changed her idea of "routine." For her, it is more like trying to fit what she values into a busy travel plan. She needs to change and create things as she goes.

But no matter where she is, she always reads a little. This might be reading a few pages in a taxi. Or while sitting for makeup. No matter where she is, she needs this inspiring start to her day. It is her steady point. It is the one thing that stays the same. Even when everything else changes.

There are also simpler solutions. Investor M. G. Siegler just drinks a bottled Starbucks Frappuccino every morning. Starbucks is almost everywhere. So he can do this whether he is home in San Francisco. Or in Tokyo. It is not special, but it is always the same. Sometimes, you need things to be the same when everything else feels confusing.

But you do not need to do the exact same routine everywhere. It is often good to change your morning plan. Change it to fit where you are.

Peter Balyta is president of education technology at Texas Instruments Inc. This is what he does. He uses his science and math skills. He creates special exercise plans. These plans fit the place he is in. He might jog along the Great Wall of China. He might use physics to lift weights. Or he might use his hotel furniture for jumping exercises. He always changes his plan to fit his surroundings.

Travel can make morning routines difficult. Jet lag. Different time zones. New beds. Work that starts earlier. All these things work against you. Your goal is to find a routine that works for you in that place.

Maybe you cannot do your full one-hour workout. But you can stretch for ten minutes in your hotel room. Maybe you cannot have your normal breakfast and book. But you can read a few pages while you wait for coffee.

And if travel makes you completely confused. If everything goes wrong on a trip. And you have no routine at all. Do not feel bad. Just try to start your routine again when you are home.

## Blink 10 - Even good plans can go wrong. So, learn to accept changes.

The world is full of surprises. So some mornings, things you cannot control will happen. They will change your usual morning routine.

But that does not have to ruin your whole day. If you know about problems, you are ready. If you expect changes, you will be better able to deal with them. You can still focus on your goals.

Look at junior doctor Rumana Lasker Dawood. Her routine is actually *made for* changes. Working in a hospital always means changes. Shifts change daily. She moves to new hospital departments every six months. She cannot have a regular wake-up time. She cannot plan fixed daily tasks. For her, these things always change.

But she does not let that stop her. Her difficult job taught her to expect surprises. And to be ready. She handles problems easily. She makes quick changes. That is her routine. Being flexible is not a second plan. It is her main plan.

And here is something to think about. It is good to be flexible and able to change. This can help you. Even if your life is not as surprising as a doctor's.

Look at writer Austin Kleon. He has learned to enjoy days when his routine changes. He sees these changes as an interesting break from his normal plan. He does not hate them. He is curious about them. What will today bring? How will I change?

Every day is different. You might try to make every day the same. But life has other ideas. Children get sick. Water pipes break. You sleep past your alarm. An urgent problem at work needs you now. Your car does not start.

You can fight against this reality. Or you can work with it.

But here is the key point. You cannot change or stop your routine unless you have one first. You need to know your normal routine. Then you can change it. You need to know what works for you normally. Then you can decide what to do when things are not normal.

When you make your routine, remember this. It must fit your needs. Not your friend's needs at work. Not what some expert says. It must be for you.

Your goal is not to please others. You should not feel bad if you do not reach a random goal. Your goal is to create a morning routine. This routine helps you start the day well. It helps you reach your own goals. No matter what they are for you. On this day. In this time of your life.

Be kind to yourself. Try new things. Change. And remember: every morning is a new start.

## Final summary

So here we are. We have looked at the morning routines of 64 successful people. They are Olympic athletes, CEOs, artists, writers, business owners, and parents. One thing should be very clear now. There is no one perfect plan for everyone.

What do all these people share? They all have a strong morning routine. This routine prepares them for the day. But that is the only thing they share. Each person has a different morning plan. Some write. Some exercise. Some meditate. Some check emails first. Others do not check emails at all. Some wake up at 4:00 a.m. Others sleep until 9:00.

The secret to making your own morning routine is not to copy one method. It is to find what works best for you.

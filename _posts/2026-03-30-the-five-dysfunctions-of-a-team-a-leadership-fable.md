### Synopsis

*The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* (2002) says that teams often don't work well. So, special steps are needed to help teams work great. A good team leader can do a lot to help their team. The book gives useful ways to do this.

## What is in it for me: learn how to build and maintain a winning team

Many people learn something important at work: even smart people don't always get great results when they work together. Teams of very smart and successful people often have problems, stop, and struggle. 

Often, the most talented people don’t work well together. People's pride can cause problems. They focus on power games instead of real work. Simply put, good people don't always make good teams.

It's frustrating because teamwork is not a mystery. You can actually guess how it will go. 

Teams fail in a clear way. First, there is no *trust*. If people don't trust each other, they don't talk honestly. They don't have real talks or arguments. They just agree nicely but complain later. Next, without good *conflict* (discussions to find the best ideas), people don't truly agree on choices. If there's no *commitment*, no one takes responsibility. No one tells others when they do something wrong. And without *accountability*, no one cares about team goals. Everyone works for themselves, and the team struggles.

These problems build up, one on top of the other, like a pyramid. This is the pyramid of five problems: no trust, no conflict, no commitment, no accountability, and bad results. 

In the next eight chapters, we will learn how to build great teams. They are not built by chance. They are built by clear, planned actions that fix each problem in the pyramid.

## Blink 1 - Teamwork is the ultimate competitive advantage; make it your top priority.

Imagine a basketball game. On one team, there are five famous players. They are very proud and have amazing personal scores. On the other team? There are five players no one knows well. But they work together as one. They know where each other will be and create chances by working together, not alone.

Who wins?

The team that works well together almost always wins against the team of stars. But in business, companies often don't get this. They put smart people together and think they will do great work easily.

Think about DecisionTech, a tech company in Silicon Valley. It had everything to succeed. It had great investors. Its engineers were very skilled. Its leaders were experienced, successful, and well-paid. It looked like this company would be very successful.

But instead, they lost a lot of money and found it hard to get customers. Things got worse quickly. In two years, this company, which everyone thought would be huge, was behind other companies and fighting to survive.

What went wrong?

Their technology was good, there was a real chance to sell their products, and each person was very capable. The problem was how people worked *together*. The leaders were ambitious and successful. But they made their workplace full of power struggles. They did not work together. They competed. They tried to get ahead of each other instead of building something as a team.

This is what happens when a team is not built well. People's pride takes over. Every meeting is like a show, and every decision is like a game. Energy that should go to customers or creating value is used for internal fights. People spend time trying to be better than their coworkers, not better than other companies.

Because of this, people feel very unhappy at work. When there is no trust, every talk feels risky. Work becomes very tiring. Your best workers will want to leave.

DecisionTech was like this when Kathryn Petersen came. The company leader chose her as the new CEO. Many people were surprised.

But Kathryn understood a key point: teamwork is the best advantage because it is so rare. If your team works well, other companies cannot easily copy what you do. They can hire good people or buy better tech. They can copy your prices or products. But a team that truly works well together is hard to copy.

So, Kathryn focused on teamwork. She made it more important than making money right away. She believed that if people worked well together, everything else would get better.

But how do you change a team that has problems? You can't just say "we'll be a great team now" and expect things to change. You need a clear plan. That's where the pyramid idea helps.

## Blink 2 - All teamwork is based on trust, and trust is built when team members are open about their weaknesses and mistakes.

Imagine this common situation. A senior boss needs to leave their job. Someone on the team says they want the job. But everyone else in the room knows, *knows*, this person is not right for the job.

What happens then? Usually, everyone is quiet. People move in their chairs. No one wants to say what they really think. So, the talk becomes strange, then about power. 

At DecisionTech, Kathryn started to build trust. Then, something new happened.

The head of sales left. Carlos Amador, who led customer support, said he wanted the sales job. But the rest of the team had worries. They thought other team members had better skills for sales. They thought the chief operations officer was a better choice.

But unlike other companies, they told Carlos what they thought, clearly. They said why they thought someone else would do better in that job.

What did Carlos do? Did he get angry and refuse to work? No, he listened. He thought about what the team said. He agreed. He was not upset. He saw they were right.

What made this talk work? Simply, trust. The team had to trust each other enough to give hard feedback. Carlos had to trust his coworkers' reasons. So, he could hear their worries without feeling attacked.

Without trust, that talk would have caused big problems. Carlos would feel surprised and unwanted. People would feel angry. Whatever decision was made, it would cause harm.

This is why trust sits at the foundation of the pyramid. Without it, everything else crumbles.

But what kind of trust is this? It's important to know we don't mean trusting someone to do their job well. It's not "I trust you to send the report on time." Or "I trust you to talk to the client well." These are normal things you expect at work.

True team trust, which helps teams do great work, is about being open about your weaknesses. It means you believe your team will not use your weaknesses or mistakes against you. They will not use it if you say you don't know something. They won't use it to get power or think you are less capable.

When people cannot be open about their feelings, every meeting becomes a show. Real problems are talked about in the hall after meetings, not where choices are made. So, small problems that should take minutes to fix can take weeks. This is because no one will say what is truly wrong.

So how do you build vulnerability-based trust?

Simply put, *you* start. Especially if you are the leader. Say what you don't know. Admit when you are having a hard time. Ask for help directly.

When the leader shows their weaknesses, it gives others permission to do the same. It shows that this team works in a new way. Slowly, more people take this risk. They see their weaknesses are not used against them. Then, trust grows.

Let’s find out more about this in the next chapter.

## Blink 3 - If people trust each other, they engage in constructive conflicts and make better decisions.

When Kathryn came to DecisionTech, she saw something strange about the leader's meetings. They were very... polite.

People showed ideas. Others agreed silently. They asked questions, but they were gentle. They didn't really question anything. Talks went easily from one topic to another. Everyone acted professionally. It looked like they were working well.

But actually, the team avoided talking about important things.

Here is a strange truth for many teams: we think arguments are bad. We see teams argue and think: if everyone was nicer, worked together more, and agreed more, then we would do better.

But this is not right. Often, the real problem is *not* having arguments.

We need to be clear about what kind of argument we mean. We don't mean bad arguments caused by pride, power games, or personal desires. That kind of argument is truly bad and should stop.

We mean helpful arguments. Helpful arguments are strong discussions about ideas, plans, and choices. This happens when smart people who really care challenge each other's ideas to find the best answer. After a lack of ‘trust,’ not wanting to argue is the next problem in the pyramid. 

When a team doesn't have trust, people avoid these kinds of talks. They don't share their true thoughts. They don't question bad ideas. They worry about what others will think. Is this about the work, or is it personal? Will saying something hurt my relationship with this person? So, it's better to be quiet and let a bad idea go forward.

This leads to bad choices. Often, no real choice is made. Just a vague agreement to "look at options" or "talk later."

Think about this in a real-world way. If you want to solve a hard business problem, you need different ideas to be discussed. You need someone to find problems in the plan. You need someone who sees problems with putting the plan into action to speak up, before you use money or time. You need honest talks about good and bad points of each idea. Open talks lead to better results.

But these helpful arguments need trust first. If you trust why someone is saying something, you hear their challenge to your idea in a different way. You don't hear "you're wrong." You hear "I want to make this better." The argument becomes useful, not scary.

At DecisionTech, Kathryn purposely helped the team to have these good discussions. She made the team talk about difficult topics they had avoided. Over time, the team became so close that they started having strong, even heated, talks about important things. These talks helped them find better answers.

Trust enabled conflict. And conflict led to something else the team had been struggling with: actual decisions.

## Blink 4 - Everybody has to be committed to a decision, even if there is no consensus or certainty about its correctness.

Listen to the following example and think about how many times you've sat in this type of meeting before.

The talk lasts an hour. People share their views. Someone suggests a plan. Others nod. A few people give small comments. Finally, time is up. Someone says, "So, we agree on this plan?"

More nodding. Then, the meeting ends.

Two weeks later, nothing has changed. Even worse, people are sometimes doing different things. They understood the decision in different ways. When you ask how things are going, you hear things like "I'm not sure that was the best idea," or "I thought we were still looking at choices."

Simply put, the meeting seemed to make a decision. But no one truly agreed to follow it.

This is a common problem in business: teams cannot make choices and stick to them. This happens because there are no good arguments, as we talked about before.

Imagine what happens when you don't have real discussions. People leave the meeting still having worries. They didn't say what they disagreed with. Maybe trust is low, or the team doesn't like arguments. So they smile and nod, but inside they think "this will not work."

And when it's time to do the work? They don't. Not really. They hold back. They do the work slowly. They keep wondering if the decision was right. So, nothing really gets done.

Good teams work in another way. They know something surprising: making any choice is better than making no choice, especially for important things. No commitment makes things unclear. When leaders are unclear, this confusion spreads down. It causes mixed-up goals that get worse at each level. So, no commitment is the next problem in the pyramid. It comes after no trust and no conflict

Before we move on, it’s important to remember one thing: commitment doesn't require consensus.

Most teams misunderstand this. They think everyone must agree before doing anything. So they keep talking, keep trying to find an answer that pleases everyone. This usually doesn't help.

Instead, good teams know that commitment means everyone has spoken and understands the main goal. Even if the final choice was not their own.

At DecisionTech, Kathryn made sure everyone could truly share their ideas before choices were made. Not just pretending to listen, but really hearing their views. When team members knew their worries were heard and answered, things changed. They were ready to support team choices, even ones they had argued against before.

You often see this in good teams: people fully support team choices they argued strongly against just hours before. They had the argument. They did not win. And now they are fully committed to making it work.

## Blink 5 - Great teams have peer-to-peer accountability, meaning everyone's performance is transparent.

One time at DecisionTech, an employee did not finish his report on rivals on time. The team knew this would happen. Everyone saw the work was not done. But no one said anything. When Kathryn spoke about it at the next meeting, she did not scold the employee. Instead, she told the rest of the team: "You should have talked about this sooner. It was clear the report would not be ready. You should have told him to do the work." The room became quiet. Kathryn had just named the fourth problem: not holding people responsible.

Team members holding each other responsible is often hard. Telling a coworker they are wrong can feel rude, even proud. It can feel like you are acting better than them or getting involved in their work too much. So most people keep quiet. But being quiet has a price. When team members don't make each other follow rules, no one feels very responsible. Deadlines are missed, work quality goes down. The leader then has to do all the correcting .

It's strange, but teams that get along well often have the hardest time with accountability. They worry that telling a coworker about a problem will hurt their friendship. So they let problems go. But the opposite happens: anger grows, respect goes down, and work quality gets worse. 

Good teams avoid this problem. They hold each other responsible. This makes the team get along better and get better results.

The steps are quite simple. Leaders must show everyone how work is going. If results, promises, and progress are clear to all, then taking responsibility becomes easy. Leaders must also change how feedback is given. Teams need help to see that telling a coworker about a problem is a sign of respect, not a complaint. Lastly, like with everything, leaders should show the right behavior first. When they are okay with the team holding them responsible, they let everyone else do it too.

Accountability connects commitment and results. You've talked openly, decided clearly, and agreed fully. Now, will you do what you promised? In strong teams, the leader is not the only one to answer this. Every team member answers it. When everyone feels responsible, good work is not just a hope. It becomes normal. 

Even with good accountability, there is one more problem that stops teams: forgetting what is truly important. Next, we will see what happens when people work for their own goals, but the team forgets its shared goals.

 

## Blink 6 - Effective teams focus on collective results rather than individual goals.

To start this chapter, let’s go back to basketball.

Kathryn's husband was a basketball coach. One year, he had a very good player. This player could score points easily. But the player didn't care if his team won or lost. He only cared about how many points he got. So, Kathryn's husband removed him from the team. The player's own goals were more important to him than the team winning. This made him a problem, not a help.

Every team has goals. Maybe to make a new product, sell more, or win a competition. Most people also have their own goals. But in good teams, shared goals are more important than personal ones. If selfish people like that player stay on a team, the team's goals become less important. And the team's work gets much worse.

So what shared goals keep a team focused? Goals that are clear and easy to check. If the goals are clear, no one can work on their own goals instead of the team's. For example, at DecisionTech, the goal was clear: 18 customers by the end of the year. Everyone worked together for it. There was no way to be unclear or avoid the goal.

When everyone accepts shared goals, something amazing happens. Team members want to help each other more, even if it's not their main job. At DecisionTech, the engineers helped the sales team show products to customers. This was not their normal job. But it was the best way they could help get more customers and reach the team's goal.

This is the fifth and last part of the pyramid. Trust helps with arguments. Arguments help with commitment. Commitment helps with accountability. And strong accountability uses all team energy for shared results. Without this, you have skilled people working only for themselves. With it, you have a team that can do amazing things.

The lesson is simple but hard to follow: your team's success must be more important than your own score. Set goals that are clear and can be checked. Make sure everyone knows what success means. Build a place where people naturally ask, "How does this help our team win?" instead of, "How does this help me get ahead?" When that change happens, the team stops fighting inside. They start fighting against other companies. And then, good results come.

But building a great team is not only about plans and rules. It needs something more basic: time. Next, we will see why good teams spend a lot of time together. And how this time helps build trust, agreement, and speed.

## Blink 7 - Great teams spend a lot of time together, which results in them saving a lot of time.

A rowing boat cannot move if each person rows a different way. It is the same for teams. If they don't agree on their goal, they will waste time and effort. So, how to stop this? The answer is easy: meet often.

At first, this might seem strange. Meetings are often thought to waste time. But for good teams, it's the opposite. Spending time together often brings many good things that grow over time. 

First, it helps people build good relationships and trust. This helps them fix problems fast. Second, arguments are easier to solve when people meet. It's much easier to hear everyone's ideas and different views when they are all together. Third, in meetings, team members understand what others are doing. They see how each other's skills can be used in other areas. This means less wasted work.

At DecisionTech, Kathryn made team members spend a lot of time together. This fixed many of their problems. About eight days every three months were spent in meetings. These included the yearly meeting, quarterly out-of-office meetings, weekly staff meetings, and other talks as needed. That might seem like too much, but it helped a lot.

At first this focus on *more *meetings might feel like too much. But spending time early saves time later. Later, the team works faster, wastes less effort, and is much clearer. This is better than teams that try to "save time" by skipping meetings.

This leads us to the top of the pyramid. We have talked about trust, conflict, commitment, accountability, and shared results. But these actions cannot grow without the strong connection that comes from spending time together. It makes everything else possible. Time together builds trust. Trust helps with arguments. Arguments make commitment stronger. Commitment helps with accountability. And accountability leads to shared results. Each part needs the part below it. And all parts need teams to spend real, planned time together. This is not about meeting just to meet. It's about making a situation where all five actions can grow well.

So how do you bring it all together and make these five behaviors stick in the real world? That's what we'll explore in the final chapter.

## Blink 8 - Making It Stick: From Framework to Practice.

The pyramid looks good as a drawing. Trust is at the bottom. Then conflict, commitment, accountability, and shared results are at the top. But knowing this plan and using it are different. DecisionTech didn't change because Kathryn drew a picture. It changed because she made a situation where new actions could start. This happened with one hard talk and one planned action at a time.

Most teams already know what good teamwork is. They know trust is important. They know avoiding arguments causes problems. And they know leaders alone cannot be responsible for everything. But knowing is not enough to change how people act. What changes how people act is planned, repeated practice. This must be done in a place where it's safe to try, make mistakes, and get better.

Trust is the base. You can't force trust. But you can grow it. You can do this by sharing personal stories, leaders showing their weaknesses, and shared experiences that show the real person, not just their job. Without trust, arguments become personal. Commitment is weak. And there is no accountability.

Arguments come next. Good teams know the difference between useful discussions and bad power games. They make it normal to question ideas without making it personal. They reward people who speak up, even when it's hard. And they make choices when everyone cannot agree. It is better to agree on a clear decision than to talk forever about finding the perfect one.

Team members should hold each other responsible. Everyone should see how work is going. Team members should question each other. This should be seen as respect, not as a complaint. When someone holds a teammate responsible, this action should be praised, not stopped.

Shared results need strong focus. Goals must be clear, easy to check, and cannot be forgotten. Everyone should know what success means. The workplace should make people ask, "How does this help *us* win?" not "How does this help *me*?"

Spending time together makes it all possible. Regular meetings, real time together, and shared understanding build good relations and trust. This makes everything else faster and easier. It might seem costly at first. But over time, it stops wasted effort, misunderstandings, and slow choices.

The five problems are in every team every day. But they can be fixed. With good leaders, good ways of working, and readiness to do hard work, any team can climb the pyramid. It's not about if it's possible. It's about if you are ready to begin.

## Final summary

The key message in this Blink is:

**Good teamwork does not happen by chance. It needs trust from being open about weaknesses. It needs arguments about ideas, not pride. It needs commitment even if everyone does not fully agree. It needs team members to hold each other responsible, not just the leader. And it needs a strong focus on shared results, not personal wins. The five problems are in every team. But with planned practice and good leaders, they can be fixed. The pyramid is not just a plan. It's a guide to build teams that do better than others.**

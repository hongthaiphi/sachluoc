**Author:** Keza MacDonald | **Category:** Technology & the Future## WHAT’S IN IT FOR ME? SEE HOW NINTENDO BUILT ITS MAGIC, AND WHAT ITS HISTORY REVEALS ABOUT WHY PLAY MATTERS.

For more than 100 years, Nintendo has asked the same simple question: What makes play feel fun and real?

The answer changed over time—from hanafuda cards to toys, handheld devices, consoles, and game worlds full of surprises—but the main idea stayed the same.

Nintendo rarely focused only on technology power.

What made it special was its focus on joy, curiosity, and the small things that make play feel close and human.

Seen this way, Nintendo’s history shows us why games matter and why play is an important part of being human.

In this Blink, you will see how Nintendo built its style around making play feel friendly, surprising, and full of life.

From early toys to game worlds made for curiosity, connection, and comfort, its history shows why these ideas stayed strong for many years.

Let’s start with the toy idea that shaped everything else.

## NINTENDO LEARNED EARLY THAT FUN COMES FROM INVENTIVE USE OF SIMPLE IDEAS

Long before video game consoles, Nintendo was a card company in Kyoto.

It began in the late 1800s making hanafuda, small decorated cards for family games, gambling, and a very old history of games across many cultures.

This shows Nintendo belongs to a long tradition of human play, not the start of something totally new.

Hiroshi Yamauchi became leader in 1949 at age 22. He helped Nintendo move from making handmade cards to plastic decks, family-friendly packages, Disney partnerships, board games, and many experiments showing Nintendo would try any type of play that might be popular.

The big change came in 1965 when Gunpei Yokoi joined to fix machines that made cards.

He became more than a repairman.

In 1966, after Hiroshi Yamauchi saw a grabber toy Yokoi made for fun at work, Nintendo made it into the Ultra Hand toy and sold over a million in one Christmas season.

This success made Yokoi head of research and development. He helped make a style that defined Nintendo for many years.

The idea was simple: Stop chasing the newest or most expensive technology. Instead, use current technology in fun ways.

You can see this in Nintendo’s unusual products from the late 1960s and 1970s like the Love Tester, paper model kits, light-gun toys, and the Game and Watch handheld.

This was really the start of Nintendo—a company made by people trying new ideas, accepting failures, and following whatever felt fun enough to grow.

By the time electronic games became exciting, Nintendo was already used to trying new things.

Now let’s see how one young designer turned that toy-making culture into a big arcade game success.

## DONKEY KONG MADE NINTENDO’S MOVE INTO GAMES FEEL ORIGINAL

By the late 1970s, arcade games were very popular, especially after Space Invaders.

Nintendo tried arcade games too, like Color TV-Game 6 in 1977 and Space Fever, but was still following what others did, not leading.

The person who changed this was Shigeru Miyamoto, a young design graduate from Kanazawa College of Art. He joined Nintendo in 1977 under Gunpei Yokoi after showing Hiroshi Yamauchi some toy designs.

He joined a company that knew how to make fun products but had not yet found its own style in video games.

Nintendo’s big step in video games started with a problem in the USA.

In 1980, Minoru Arakawa set up Nintendo of America and ordered 3,000 Radar Scope arcade machines, but only 1,000 sold.

That left many unsold machines and a problem: What to do with them?

The answer was to change them by adding a new game from Miyamoto’s idea.

Because Miyamoto studied art and design, not programming, he thought differently about games.

He started with characters and their stories, then made the game around them.

The result was a game with an ape, a captured woman, and a small hero climbing ladders and avoiding dangers.

For players used to shooters and racing games, this mix of story, characters, and changing action felt new.

Donkey Kong’s success changed Nintendo’s future.

It launched Mario, made Nintendo famous in video games abroad, and was the world’s top arcade game for two years.

It also made Yamauchi decide in 1981 to build a home cartridge system to bring games into houses.

Nintendo’s entry into video games came from a failed product, a risk, and a new idea about characters.

Now let’s see how Mario improved Nintendo’s game style.

## MARIO SHOWS HOW NINTENDO MAKES PLAY FEEL GOOD

By 1985, Nintendo showed its clear style.

Super Mario Bros.

is fun because it feels good as soon as you hold the controller.

Running feels real, jumps feel quick and right, and each small move is clear so when you fail, you want to try again.

Shigeru Miyamoto and Takashi Tezuka made the game so that players learn by playing.

At the start, enemies, blocks, coins, mushrooms, and the side-scrolling world come in a smart order, helping players understand the rules naturally.

This way of teaching continues in later Mario games, where new ideas come slowly before becoming bigger challenges.

Also, Mario’s worlds reward curiosity.

A pipe may take you to a surprise place, odd corners may hide bonuses, and the background feels like there is always something fun nearby.

This mix of clear control, surprise, and discovery gave Mario long-lasting popularity that copycat games rarely matched.

The same ideas moved Mario into 3D in 1996 when the Nintendo 64 had to compete with the PlayStation.

Super Mario 64 had to prove that 3D play could be as easy and fun as 2D games.

Nintendo made the analogue stick, gave players control of the camera, gave Mario more moves, and made Peach’s Castle a world to explore, not just a menu.

This gave players more free movement, direction, and choice than before and became a model for many games.

Mario is Nintendo’s mascot and its best example of how careful design makes playing joyful.

Now let’s see how Zelda used that respect for play to create mystery, exploration, and wonder.

## ZELDA TURNED NINTENDO’S FUN INTO FREEDOM

Nintendo’s other big idea is wonder.

In 1986, The Legend of Zelda asked players to do something bold even today: stop following a fixed path and start exploring freely.

Shigeru Miyamoto remembered hiking near Kobe in Japan, where forests, caves, and a lake on a mountain stayed in his mind from childhood.

This feeling of leaving home and finding surprises became Zelda’s heart.

While Mario moves left to right with clear controls and obstacles, Zelda opened wide.

Its world was made of many screens, and progress came from curiosity, patience, and trying your own ways.

Players were not told what to do at every step.

They had to search, get lost, solve puzzles, and talk to other players.

When Zelda came to the USA in 1987, its save feature made this freedom easier because you could stop and continue later instead of finishing all at once.

This idea made the series very lasting.

The details changed over the years, but the feeling stayed strong.

Hyrule might be flooded, broken, fixed, or new again.

Link, Zelda, and Ganon appear in new ways, and familiar tools like bows and boomerangs come back with new ideas.

What holds it all is a mood of adventure and themes of childhood, time, destiny, and growing up.

Each game feels both familiar and new, which is why the series can change without losing itself.

Zelda showed Nintendo could give more than fun movement.

It could give a world that makes you look closer.

Now let’s see how Pokémon took that spirit of discovery and made connection between players central to play.

## POKÉMON MADE CONNECTION THE HEART OF PLAY

Pokémon became one of Nintendo’s strongest ideas because it treated play as something shared with others.

Satoshi Tajiri, born in 1965, loved collecting bugs as a child. He wanted to bring back the excitement of finding strange creatures and sharing with friends.

Around 1990, he saw Game Boys connected for Tetris, which gave him a new idea.

He imagined creatures moving between Game Boys, turning into a game that mixed collecting, fighting, and trading into a social system.

After a 1990 meeting, Nintendo supported the project. Six hard years later, the first games came out in Japan in 1996. A small team worked on old Game Boy hardware.

The games' strength was not just the fantasy world, though that was important.

Kanto felt alive on a black and white screen, with places like Lavender Town showing a world where humans and Pokémon lived close.

The bigger idea was inside the game cartridges.

Different creatures appeared in each version, so to complete the Pokédex, players had to trade.

This changed the Game Boy link cable from a way to beat others into a way to help friends.

Also, the game had many details to learn, like types, moves, and team-building.

Because each creature grew differently, players made teams that felt personal.

Your team showed your taste and effort and what kind of player you were.

This social side helped Pokémon last a long time.

It spread in playgrounds in the late 1990s and came back in streets and parks with Pokémon Go in 2016, again bringing people together.

Through all these changes, the core idea stayed: play is better when it connects you to others.

Next, we will see how Nintendo used this idea to welcome almost everyone into gaming with Wii Sports.

## WII SPORTS OPENED GAMING TO ALMOST EVERYONE

When the Wii came out in 2006, Nintendo made a bold choice: games could be easier to start without being boring.

The best example was Wii Sports, a pack-in collection of tennis, baseball, boxing, golf, and bowling games.

The idea worked because the controller asked for natural movements instead of many buttons.

Swinging the controller like a racket or rolling it like a bowling ball was easy to understand, even for people new to consoles.

Families could open it at Christmas and start playing in minutes.

Older family members who did not usually like games could still follow what to do, and some were good because the movements felt natural.

Nintendo also kept the look simple.

The Mii avatars were plain and friendly, the rules were easy, and each sport had extra tricks to keep it fun after the first time.

You could add spin in bowling, curve shots in tennis, and learn timing in boxing, so the games stayed fun.

This way changed many consoles’ success.

In the early 2000s, gaming was mostly for young men, but the Wii and DS grew the audience by showing design can welcome many kinds of players.

Satoru Iwata, Nintendo’s president during the Wii time, believed casual and hardcore players were not different kinds of people.

People start by being curious, and Wii Sports gave an easy first step.

Even technical limits did not matter much because the fun was in playing together in the same room.

The Wii was made for being together in person while other games were moving online.

This gave the Wii a special place in Nintendo’s history.

Finally, let’s see how Animal Crossing turned Nintendo’s human focus into everyday friendship and comfort.

## ANIMAL CROSSING TURNED PLAY INTO EVERYDAY COMPANIONSHIP

Animal Crossing gave Nintendo a new way to think about games.

The idea started with loneliness.

After moving to Kyoto in 1986, Katsuya Eguchi missed his family and friends. Years later, he felt sad that work kept him from playing with his kids when they were awake.

Around 1998, he started making a game about sharing play over time, not all at once.

The first version was a multiplayer dungeon game for the 64DD, where one player’s actions could help another later.

But when that hardware stopped, the game changed to fit on a Nintendo 64 cartridge, shrinking the big world to a small town.

From this limitation came the game’s true idea.

Released in Japan in 2001 as Animal Forest, it asked players to live in a town, not conquer a world.

You could decorate your home, catch bugs and fish, send letters, pay off Tom Nook, and check in daily as neighbors moved, seasons changed, and weeds grew if you stayed away too long.

There was no final goal to win.

The point was daily life’s rhythm.

This simple idea was ahead of its time.

The GameCube version sold more than two million.

Then Wild World on the DS sold nearly twelve million by 2005 as social play grew.

New Leaf in 2012 sold thirteen million, fitting a world that liked customization and sharing.

New Horizons came in March 2020, as lockdowns started, and became a place for routine, creativity, birthdays, protests, friendships, and comfort.

It sold over 45 million copies.

All versions kept the same heart: family, friendship, and community.

By now, the bigger picture is clear.

Nintendo lasts because it knows play is not only about challenge or show.

Sometimes it is about making space for each other.

## FINAL SUMMARY

The main message of this Blink to Super Nintendo by Keza MacDonald is that Nintendo’s lasting power comes from treating play as something very human.

From cards and toys to consoles and famous game worlds, its best ideas grew from curiosity, trying new things, and believing fun should be welcoming, surprising, and meaningful.

This is why its games teach through movement, reward exploring, bring friends together, welcome new players, and offer comfort when people feel lonely.

Together, these stories show that great play is about connection, imagination, and the simple joy of finding something that makes life a bit brighter.
